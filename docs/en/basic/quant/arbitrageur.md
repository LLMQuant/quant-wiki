# An Arbitrageur Is an Investor Who Seeks to Profit from Market Inefficiencies
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Many arbitrageurs exploit price inconsistencies for the same asset across different markets by simultaneously buying at a lower price in one market and selling at a higher price in another. Risk arbitrageurs, on the other hand, attempt to profit from price discrepancies that arise during mergers and acquisitions before the deal closes.

### Key Takeaways

- Arbitrageurs exploit market inefficiencies and are considered essential for keeping those inefficiencies to a minimum.
- Arbitrageurs are typically experienced investors who are detail-oriented and comfortable with risk.
- The most common way arbitrageurs profit is by exploiting price discrepancies in assets listed on multiple exchanges.
- In such cases, an arbitrageur may purchase the asset on one exchange and short-sell it on a second exchange where the price is higher.
- Merger arbitrageurs seek to profit from the spread between a stock's quoted acquisition price and its trading price before the deal closes.

## What Arbitrageurs Actually Do

There is considerable confusion around this area of finance. For instance, many people know that arbitrageurs seek price discrepancies among stocks by buying undervalued shares on one exchange while simultaneously short-selling an equivalent number of overvalued shares on another. This gives the impression that they capture so-called "risk-free" profits, since prices on the two exchanges will converge. But this activity is far from risk-free.

It is therefore worth emphasizing: despite what may be true for certain trade scenarios, arbitrage transactions are by no means risk-free. Many arbitrageurs specialize in merger arbitrage, which carries a wide range of risks along the way.

Another fundamental misconception involves historically illegal practices that, if an arbitrageur engaged in them, could lead to serious legal consequences -- not just hefty fines, but potentially prison time. Historically, in risk or merger arbitrage, arbitrageurs would often obtain inside information to get ahead of public announcements. They might have connections with law firms, investment banks, and accounting firms, or make calls to companies that might be expanding. The aim was to obtain critical information (often for a fee) that would allow the arbitrageur to trade ahead of any public announcement.

In the 1980s, several insider trading cases -- including that of arbitrageur Ivan Boesky -- led to the passage of the Insider Trading and Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988, which increased penalties and required the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) to strengthen insider trading enforcement. In 2000, the SEC introduced Regulation FD ("Fair Disclosure"), further restricting the information available to arbitrageurs. Any material information must be disclosed broadly to the public, rather than shared privately with a select few.

This brings us to another dimension of the arbitrageur's work: contrary to the common belief that an arbitrageur's job ends when a merger is announced (and profits are collected as the relevant stock rises), in practice, the modern arbitrageur's work typically begins at that point.

So what does an arbitrageur actually do? Let us start with merger arbitrage, which has formed the basis of several Hollywood films over the years.

Also known as risk arbitrage, merger arbitrage involves exploiting the price spread between a target company's stock price and the acquiring company's offer price. When a merger is announced, the target company's stock typically trades below the offer price, reflecting uncertainty and risk factors associated with the deal. Arbitrageurs often step in at this point, believing the probability of approval is high and that the target's stock will ultimately converge with the offer price. They exploit this spread by purchasing the target company's shares at the lower market price and selling at the higher offer price when the deal closes.

Once a merger or acquisition is announced, the arbitrageur begins tracking the deal in detail. Their role falls somewhere between financial researcher and investigative journalist -- reporting exclusively to one publicly traded entity. The arbitrageur is working to determine whether the deal will go through.

Their first steps typically include obtaining the relevant companies' annual and quarterly reports as well as 10-K filings to assess their financial health. The stronger the companies' financials, the more likely the deal is to close. Arbitrageurs also gather research from brokerage firms on both companies and their industries. Online databases and services have greatly simplified this process. They also want to understand the industry and the rationale behind the deal. A strategically sound merger is not only a valuable portfolio addition but is also more likely to complete successfully.

The AT&T/Time Warner merger (announced 2016; closed 2018) and the Disney/Fox merger (announced 2017; closed 2019) presented significant arbitrage opportunities. Both deals faced legal challenges and regulatory scrutiny over concerns about media concentration. The delays meant arbitrageurs tracked developments at every stage -- a court case here, a regulatory ruling there -- while calculating the probability of each deal succeeding.

Arbitrageurs are typically financial generalists who must learn quickly, as they might focus on two manufacturing companies one day and pivot to a natural gas company with complex proven reserves and pipelines near the Black Sea the next. At every turn, the arbitrageur is assessing potential returns, risks, and the probability of deal completion. Only then can the arbitrageur determine which stocks to buy and what hedging strategies to deploy if the deal falls through.

Between announcement and completion, the following issues may arise:

- **Financing collapse:** The funding required for a deal may evaporate. For example, the financial condition of the companies involved may change, or market interest rates may rise. Even if all parties are willing, the deal cannot proceed without financing.
- **Antitrust intervention:** Regulatory agencies charged with protecting fair competition may challenge the deal in court. For example, in early 2024, a federal court blocked JetBlue Airways' (JBLU) planned $3.8 billion acquisition of Spirit Airlines (SAVE) based on a lawsuit filed by the U.S. Department of Justice, ruling that the deal would lead to higher consumer prices.
- **Shifts in the economic climate:** Broad economic changes can make a merger less viable. Market volatility, shifts in consumer demand, or a wider economic downturn can undermine the expected benefits. For instance, in February 2008, Microsoft (MSFT) planned a $44.6 billion acquisition of Yahoo!. As the financial crisis deepened, Microsoft determined that Yahoo's asking price was unrealistic under the prevailing economic conditions and withdrew three months later.
- **Discovery of fraud or accounting issues:** If fraud or material misrepresentation surfaces during the deal process, it can derail the transaction.
- **Emergence of a competing bidder:** A third party may enter with a more competitive offer, disrupting the original deal.

Monitoring these risks makes merger arbitrage an ongoing process. An arbitrageur may be tracking a court case in Delaware while simultaneously watching global economic indicators and regulatory changes that could affect the deal's success. Arbitrageurs also maintain constant contact with industry experts, legal counsel, and other arbitrageurs to stay informed about developments, market trends, and shifts in investor sentiment, adjusting strategies as needed.

## Arbitrage Across Market Price Discrepancies

Below are some ways arbitrageurs seek to profit from price differences across markets:

- **Global equity arbitrage:** This involves exploiting price differences in the same stock listed on multiple exchanges. The same stock may trade at slightly different prices on different markets due to currency fluctuations, regional market sentiment, or differences in liquidity levels. Arbitrageurs identify these discrepancies and execute synchronized buy and sell orders to profit.

**Important:** When an asset is listed on multiple markets, its price may not always be identical across them.

- **Cryptocurrency arbitrage:** Arbitrage opportunities arising from price differences between cryptocurrency exchanges are similar in nature. Traders exploit these price gaps to profit from the volatility of crypto markets.
- **Index arbitrage:** For stock index futures and options, arbitrageurs execute index arbitrage to exploit pricing discrepancies between an underlying index and its futures or options contracts. This includes S&P 500 index arbitrage and other equity index arbitrage strategies.
- **Fixed-income arbitrage:** Arbitrage opportunities also exist in fixed-income markets, where traders attempt to profit from yield differentials, interest rate differences, or credit risk discrepancies among various bonds and other debt securities.
- **Convertible bond arbitrage:** Between a company's equity and its bonds, further arbitrage opportunities may exist. In convertible bond arbitrage, the arbitrageur attempts to exploit discrepancies between a convertible bond's conversion price and the company's stock price.
- **Cross-border arbitrage:** In foreign exchange markets, traders may seek to profit from exchange rate differences for currency pairs across different markets or forex brokers.

## A Practical Example of Arbitrage

Let us consider an example of how an arbitrageur operates. Suppose Company X's stock trades at $20 on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) while simultaneously trading at $20.05 on the London Stock Exchange (LSE). A trader can buy the stock on the NYSE and immediately sell the same shares on the LSE, earning a profit of 5 cents per share, less any transaction costs. The trader exploits this arbitrage opportunity until NYSE specialists run out of Company X's inventory or until prices on the NYSE or LSE adjust to eliminate the discrepancy.

The rise of cryptocurrency has provided another avenue for arbitrageurs. As Bitcoin's price climbed, arbitrage opportunities emerged from price discrepancies across global exchanges.

For example, Bitcoin traded at a premium on South Korean cryptocurrency exchanges compared to U.S. exchanges -- a spread known as the "Kimchi Premium" -- driven largely by high regional demand for cryptocurrency. Crypto traders profited in real time from the price differential between these two locations.

## The Impact of Arbitrage

Arbitrageurs play an important role in the functioning of capital markets because their efforts in exploiting price inefficiencies help make prices more accurate than they would be otherwise. When arbitrageurs buy an asset in a cheaper market and sell the same asset in a more expensive market, their actions tend to push prices toward convergence. This results in prices being largely equalized across all markets.

In short, arbitrageurs help resolve pricing inefficiencies and inject liquidity into the market.

## What Technologies Do Arbitrageurs Use?

Arbitrageurs typically use trading software specifically designed for algorithmic trading. This software can process real-time market data, analyze it, and execute trades rapidly upon processing. In addition, many arbitrageurs use custom-built systems tailored to their specific trading strategies. These systems generally feature powerful processors and high-speed internet connections to minimize trade execution time. Cloud computing and data analytics are critical in this process, as traders searching for arbitrage opportunities across global markets often need to store, process, and analyze large datasets.

## How Do Arbitrageurs Mitigate Risk?

Arbitrageurs face the risk that expected price convergence may not materialize or may take longer than anticipated, potentially leading to losses. Delays or failures in executing trades can also erode profit margins. Additionally, arbitrageurs face liquidity risk -- the possibility of being unable to buy or sell an asset quickly at the expected price due to thin trading volumes. To mitigate these risks, arbitrageurs typically diversify their investments across different arbitrage opportunities to avoid significant losses in any single area. They also employ sophisticated risk management tools and techniques, including real-time monitoring systems and stop-loss orders to limit potential losses. Many arbitrageurs also engage in hedging strategies to offset potential losses from market fluctuations.

## What Is the Role of High-Frequency Trading (HFT) in Arbitrage?

High-frequency trading plays a significant role in modern arbitrage strategies. HFT involves using sophisticated algorithms to execute large volumes of orders at extremely high speeds. For arbitrage, HFT allows traders to exploit fleeting price discrepancies that may exist for only fractions of a second across different exchanges or securities. These algorithms can analyze market conditions, execute orders, and close positions in an instant -- often before the market has the chance to correct these inefficiencies.

## Conclusion

Arbitrageurs search for market inefficiencies and seek to profit from them. These inefficiencies often involve pricing discrepancies for the same asset on different exchanges. However, they can also involve deep research into market developments, such as mergers and acquisitions, to profit from pricing spreads in those transactions.

Arbitrageurs typically work for large financial firms with substantial resources, and their activities help improve liquidity and equalize prices across markets.
