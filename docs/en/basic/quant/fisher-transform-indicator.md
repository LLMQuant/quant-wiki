# What Is the Fisher Transform Indicator?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Fisher Transform is a technical indicator that converts prices into a Gaussian normal distribution. It highlights when prices have moved to an extreme and can help identify turning points in an asset's price based on recent price action. The indicator can also be used to reveal trends and isolate price waves within a trend. The Fisher Transform was created by John F. Ehlers.

### Key Takeaways

- The Fisher Transform normalizes asset prices, making turning points easier to identify.
- The formula is typically applied to price data but can also be applied to other indicators.
- Some traders look for extreme readings to signal potential reversal areas, while others watch for a change in direction of the Fisher Transform.
- Asset prices are not normally distributed, so attempts to normalize prices through an indicator may not always produce reliable signals.

## Fisher Transform Formula and Calculation

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{Fisher Transform} = \frac{1}{2}*\ln \left( \frac{1+X}{1-X} \right)\\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &\ln \text{ is the natural logarithm}\\ &X = \text{price transformed to a level between -1 and 1}\\ \end{aligned} $$

## Understanding the Fisher Transform Indicator

As noted above, the Fisher Transform is a technical analysis indicator developed by John F. Ehlers -- an author, trader, and engineer. Ehlers began trading in the mid-1970s and created several indicators that traders still use today.

The Fisher Transform enables traders to create a Gaussian normal distribution from data -- such as market prices -- that are not normally distributed. In essence, this transformation makes extreme price swings relatively rare events, making it easier to spot price reversals on a chart.

The indicator is commonly used by traders seeking to identify changes in an asset's price direction and emerging trends. More specifically, traders tend to prefer leading signals over lagging indicators. The Fisher Transform can help traders understand an asset's price movements and broader market conditions.

## Applying the Fisher Transform in Trading

The Fisher Transform is unbounded, meaning extreme values can persist for extended periods. What constitutes an extreme depends on historical readings for the asset in question. For some assets, a high reading might be 7 or 8, while a low reading might be -4. These thresholds can vary across different assets.

An extreme reading suggests the possibility of a reversal, which should be confirmed by a directional change in the indicator. For example, following a strong price rally, if the indicator reaches an extremely high level and then turns downward, the asset's price may decline -- or may have already begun declining.

The Fisher Transform often includes a signal line, which is a moving average of the indicator's values and therefore moves more slowly than the Fisher Transform line itself. Some traders use a crossover of the indicator and the signal line as a trade signal. For instance, when the indicator drops below the signal line after reaching an extremely high level, it may signal an opportunity to sell an existing long position.

As with many indicators, the Fisher Transform generates numerous signals, not all of which are profitable. Some traders prefer to combine the indicator with trend analysis. For example, during an uptrend, the indicator might be used only for buy and sell signals rather than short signals. During a downtrend, it can be used for short-selling signals and to time exits from short positions.

**Important:** The Fisher Transform can also be applied to other technical indicators, such as the Relative Strength Index (RSI) or Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD).

## Fisher Transform vs. Bollinger Bands

These two indicators look quite different on a chart, but both are based on the distribution of asset prices.

Bollinger Bands use a normal distribution by employing standard deviation to show when prices may be stretched beyond their typical range. The Fisher Transform, by contrast, uses a Gaussian normal distribution.

The Fisher Transform appears as a separate indicator on a chart, while Bollinger Bands are overlaid directly on the price chart.

## Limitations of the Fisher Transform Indicator

Despite being a popular tool among technical analysts, the Fisher Transform has several drawbacks:

- Although it is designed to make turning points easier to identify, it can be quite noisy at times. Extreme readings are not always followed by a price reversal. Sometimes prices simply move sideways or reverse only slightly.
- Defining what constitutes an extreme reading is also difficult, as the levels shift over time. A reading of 4 may be considered high for years, but readings of 8 may eventually start appearing frequently.
- Monitoring all directional changes in the Fisher Transform can help identify short-term shifts in price direction, but signals may arrive too late to capture a profit, as many price moves can be short-lived.
- Asset prices are not normally distributed, so attempts to normalize them through the indicator may be inherently flawed and may not produce reliable signals.

## Fisher Transform vs. MACD

The Fisher Transform and MACD are two distinct technical analysis indicators. Both provide important trend signal information to traders. The Fisher Transform normalizes asset prices through transformation, while MACD relies on moving averages and can be applied to strategies involving short-term trends. The Fisher Transform is generally considered more precise because it provides a clearer picture of market direction.

## What Is Technical Analysis?

Technical analysis is a trading discipline that uses historical performance and data to identify market opportunities. Traders analyze asset prices, implied volatility, and trading volume to forecast future performance. These data are used to calculate various technical indicators, which are then plotted on charts to help traders determine entry and exit points.

## What Is a Technical Indicator?

A technical indicator is a signal used by traders in technical analysis. It relies on key asset data -- namely historical prices and trading volume. Technical indicators are commonly used to analyze short-term moves but are also valuable for long-term traders looking to identify entry and exit points. There are many types of technical indicators, generally classified into two broad categories: overlays and oscillators. Examples include the Fisher Transform, moving averages, the Relative Strength Index, and MACD.

## Summary

Indicators help technical traders find opportunities in the markets. The Fisher Transform is one such tool. It works by transforming prices to create a Gaussian normal distribution, allowing traders to spot trends and identify price waves within those trends. While the Fisher Transform is regarded as a reliable tool, traders should use it in conjunction with other indicators to obtain a more accurate view of the market and reduce the risk of potential losses.
