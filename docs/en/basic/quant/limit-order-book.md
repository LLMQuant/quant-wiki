# What Is a Limit Order Book?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A limit order book is a record of outstanding limit orders maintained by the security specialist at an exchange. A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell a security at a specified price or better. A buy limit order is placed at a preset price or lower, while a sell limit order is placed at a preset price or higher.

When a limit order for a security is entered, the security specialist records it. As buy and sell limit orders accumulate, the specialist logs all related orders in the book. When the market price reaches a preset limit, the specialist executes the orders at or better than the limit price.

### Key Takeaways

- A limit order book is a record of outstanding limit orders maintained by the security specialist at an exchange.
- A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell a security at a specified price or better.
- When a limit order for a security is entered, the security specialist records it.
- As buy and sell limit orders are submitted, the specialist logs all of them in the order book.

## Understanding the Limit Order Book

The specialist managing the limit order book is responsible for ensuring that priority orders are executed before other orders and ahead of orders at the same or worse price submitted by other traders, such as floor brokers and market makers.

The specialist profits from the spread between bid and ask prices in the order book. Advances in trading system technology have shifted this process from a manual operation to one that is largely automated.

## Tracking Limit Orders

In 2000, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) began creating a centralized limit order book that tracks limit orders electronically across exchanges. [1] This electronic order-tracking system automatically matches the best pair of orders for execution. The best pair consists of the highest bid price and the lowest ask price.

The bid price is the price at which the security specialist or exchange is willing to sell a security -- or the price at which an investor can buy. The ask (or offer) price is the price at which the specialist or exchange is willing to buy a security -- or the price at which an investor can sell.

When a limit order is entered into the trading system and processed by either the specialist managing the book or an electronic order database, it remains in the system until it can be matched with an appropriate trade and executed. Buy limit orders are placed with an upper price limit. The investor is effectively saying, "I do not want to pay more than $X for this stock." Sell limit orders are placed with a lower price limit. The investor is saying, "I do not want to sell this stock for less than $X."

## Limit Order Qualifiers

A limit order may include qualifiers. Without any qualifiers, the order is valid only during the market trading day, known as a "day order," and may expire without being filled or may be only partially filled.

If an investor's order reads "buy 10,000 shares of XYZ common at $32," they are requesting to purchase 10,000 shares at $32 or better -- that is the order's qualifier.

If an investor's strategy requires all 10,000 shares to be filled at any time at the requested price or better, the order can be entered as "buy 10,000 shares of XYZ at $32 GTC." A good-till-cancelled (GTC) order instructs the market to keep purchasing shares until the order is cancelled, even if the order is filled in increments of 100 shares over several weeks. The investor wants the order executed regardless of how long it takes.

Another qualifier is AON, or "all or none." The investor may not want the order to be partially filled and uses this condition to instruct the market to execute the order only when all 10,000 shares can be filled; otherwise, no shares are purchased.

There are additional types of order qualifiers that allow investors to ensure transactions are executed according to their specific investment objectives, defining the "limits" they impose on the market in each case.

## Special Considerations

An investor is guaranteed the specified price if the order is triggered once the market reaches the preset level. However, a limit order does not guarantee execution. In other words, the order can only be filled when the price reaches the limit. Limit orders are helpful for investors because they ensure that the price paid for a security does not exceed the originally set preset price.
