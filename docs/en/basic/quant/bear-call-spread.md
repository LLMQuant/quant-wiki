# Bear Call Spread
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A bear call spread, also known as a short call credit spread, is an options strategy used when the trader expects the price of the underlying asset to decline. The strategy involves simultaneously selling a call option and buying another call option with a higher strike price but the same expiration date. The maximum profit from this strategy is the net credit received when the trade is initiated.

A bear call spread is considered a limited-risk, limited-reward strategy.

### Key Takeaways

- A bear call spread is created by selling a call option and buying another call option with a higher strike price but the same expiration date.
- A bear call spread is considered limited-risk and limited-reward because traders can contain their losses or realize reduced profits using this strategy.
- The strike prices of the call options determine the profit and loss limits.

## When to Use a Bear Call Spread

As the name implies, a bear call spread is used when the trader has a bearish outlook on the underlying asset. Investors typically use this strategy when they expect the underlying asset's price to decline moderately but wish to limit potential losses. It is a relatively conservative approach compared to simply buying put options or directly short selling the underlying asset.

If the investor anticipates a significant decline in the underlying asset's price, a bear put spread may offer greater profit potential than a bear call spread.

**Important:** The maximum loss occurs when the stock trades at or above the strike price of the long call option. Conversely, the maximum gain occurs when the stock trades at or below the strike price of the short call option.

## How a Bear Call Spread Works

A bear call spread is an options trading strategy used when an investor expects a moderate decline in the underlying asset's price. It involves simultaneously buying and selling call options on the same underlying asset with the same expiration date but different strike prices. Here is how it works:

At expiration, if the underlying asset's price falls to or below the lower strike price, the maximum profit is realized. The maximum loss is limited to the net premium paid for the options.

## Advantages of a Bear Call Spread

The primary advantage of a bear call spread is that it reduces the net risk of the trade. Buying the call option with the higher strike price helps offset the risk from selling the call option with the lower strike price. This strategy carries far less risk than short selling the stock or security outright, because the maximum loss is the difference between the two strike prices minus the credit received when the trade was initiated. Short selling a stock theoretically carries unlimited risk if the stock moves higher.

If the trader believes the underlying stock or security will fall by a limited amount between the trade date and the expiration date, then a bear call spread could be a sound play. However, if the underlying stock or security falls by a greater amount, the trader gives up the ability to claim that additional profit. It is a trade-off between risk and potential reward that appeals to many traders.

## Disadvantages of a Bear Call Spread

As with any trading strategy, a bear call spread has drawbacks. First, because of the reduced risk, the reward potential is also limited. Specifically, the maximum gain is capped at the difference between the strike prices used. So if the strike prices are $5 apart, the maximum profit per spread is also $5.

Moreover, if the underlying asset's price does not fall as expected, the trader will incur a loss equal to the net premium of the entire spread.

Investors should carefully consider their risk tolerance, investment objectives, and market outlook before implementing a bear call spread or any other options trading strategy.

### Pros and Cons of a Bear Call Spread

#### Pros

- Risk is lower than outright short selling
- Losses are limited to the cost of the spread
- Performs well in moderately declining markets

#### Cons

- Maximum profit is limited to the difference in strike prices
- If the market rises, the trader may lose the full cost of the spread

## Bear Call Spread Example

Suppose a stock is trading at $30. You could use a bear call spread by purchasing one call option contract with a strike price of $40 for a premium of $0.50 ($0.50 x 100 shares/contract = $50 premium) and simultaneously selling one call option contract with a strike price of $35 for $2.50 ($2.50 x 100 shares/contract = $250).

In this scenario, you receive a net credit of $200 to set up the strategy ($250 - $50). Here is what can happen:

The breakeven point for this strategy is the strike price of the sold call plus the net credit. In this case, the breakeven is $35 + $2 = $37.

Here is the range of outcomes:

- **Maximum profit:** $200 (net credit received)
- **Maximum loss:** $300 (difference between strike prices minus net credit)
- **Breakeven point:** $37 (sold call's strike price plus net credit)

## Difference Between a Bear Call Spread and a Bull Call Spread

A bear call spread involves selling a lower-strike call option and buying a higher-strike call option with the same underlying asset and expiration date. This is a bearish strategy that results in a credit to the spreader. It is also known as a short call spread.

In contrast, a bull call spread involves buying a lower-strike call option and selling a higher-strike call option. This is a bullish strategy, but both potential profit and loss are limited.

## Difference Between Call Spreads and Put Spreads

Call spreads involve buying and selling call options at different strike prices, while put spreads involve buying and selling put options at different strike prices. A long (bullish) call spread is used when the investor expects the underlying asset's price to rise, while a long put spread is used when the investor expects the underlying asset's price to fall.

## How Should You Choose Strike Prices and Expiration Dates for a Bear Call Spread?

The choice of strike prices and expiration dates depends on the trader's market outlook and time horizon. Generally, select a short call (the one you sell) with a strike price above the current price of the underlying asset. This strike price should be one that you believe the underlying asset is unlikely to exceed by expiration. Choose a long call with a strike price above the short call's strike price. The difference between the two strike prices determines your maximum potential loss and maximum potential gain. A common practice is to select contracts one to several strikes apart.

Additionally, choose an expiration date that aligns with your expectation for when the underlying asset's price will decline. If you expect a short-term decline, choose a nearer expiration date. If you expect a longer-term decline, choose a further expiration date. Keep in mind that longer-dated options generally have higher premiums due to increased time value.

## Summary

A bear call spread is a bearish strategy that involves selling a call option and simultaneously buying a call option with a higher strike price on the same underlying asset with the same expiration date. If the market does decline, the trader benefits from the drop in the underlying asset's price, as both call options expire worthless, allowing the trader to keep the net credit received from setting up the spread.

The strategy limits both potential profit and loss, providing well-defined risk-reward characteristics.
