# What Was Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) was a large hedge fund led by Nobel Prize-winning economists and renowned Wall Street traders. It collapsed in 1998, forcing the U.S. government to intervene to prevent a broader financial market meltdown.

### Key Takeaways

- Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM) was a large hedge fund led by Nobel laureate economists and prominent Wall Street traders.
- At its peak, LTCM was highly profitable, attracting approximately $3.5 billion in investor capital by spring 1998, with promises that its arbitrage strategy would deliver outsized returns.
- LTCM's highly leveraged trading strategy unraveled when the Russian debt default triggered mounting losses, compelling the U.S. government to organize a bailout to prevent global financial contagion.
- Ultimately, a lending consortium of major Wall Street banks was established in September 1998 to rescue LTCM and allow it to unwind in an orderly fashion.

## Understanding Long-Term Capital Management (LTCM)

Since its founding in 1994, LTCM achieved remarkable success, amassing approximately $3.5 billion in investor capital by the spring of 1998. The fund's arbitrage strategy promised to exploit short-term changes in market behavior, theoretically reducing risk to near zero.

However, LTCM's heavily leveraged trading strategy failed spectacularly, generating massive losses. The repercussions rippled through the financial system, nearly triggering a global financial collapse in 1998. The U.S. government was ultimately forced to step in, orchestrating a bailout through a consortium of Wall Street banks to prevent systemic contagion.

LTCM started with just over $1 billion in assets and focused on bond trading. The fund's strategy centered on convergence trades -- exploiting arbitrage opportunities between securities. For these trades to succeed, the securities involved had to be temporarily mispriced relative to each other.

One example of such a trade involves interest rate changes that have not yet been fully reflected in securities prices. This creates an opportunity to trade those securities at prices that differ from where they will soon converge.

**Note:** LTCM was founded in 1994 by John Meriwether, the famed Salomon Brothers bond trader, together with Nobel laureate Myron Scholes, co-creator of the Black-Scholes options pricing model.

LTCM also engaged in interest rate swaps -- contracts involving the exchange of a series of future interest payments between two parties based on a specified notional amount. Interest rate swaps typically involve exchanging a fixed rate for a floating rate, or vice versa, to minimize exposure to interest rate fluctuations.

Because the spreads on arbitrage opportunities were narrow, LTCM had to employ extreme leverage to generate meaningful profits. At its peak in 1998, LTCM held approximately $5 billion in assets, controlled positions worth over $100 billion, and held derivatives positions with a total notional value exceeding $1 trillion. The fund had also borrowed more than $155 billion in assets.

## The Collapse of LTCM

In August 1998, Russia defaulted on its sovereign debt. At the time, LTCM held a substantial position in Russian government bonds (known as GKOs). Despite losing hundreds of millions of dollars per day, LTCM's computer models recommended maintaining its positions.

The combination of extreme leverage and the Russian financial crisis inflicted devastating losses on the fund, which faced the risk of defaulting on its own obligations. This made it extremely difficult for LTCM to cut its losses. The fund's positions amounted to roughly 5% of the global fixed-income market, and it had borrowed enormous sums to finance these leveraged trades.

**Important:** Had LTCM defaulted, it could have triggered a global financial crisis, as its creditors would have been forced to undertake massive write-downs.

When losses approached $4 billion, the U.S. federal government -- fearing LTCM's imminent collapse would spark a broader financial crisis -- engineered a rescue plan to stabilize markets. A $3.625 billion loan fund was established, enabling LTCM to survive the market turmoil and liquidate its positions in an orderly manner by early 2000.
