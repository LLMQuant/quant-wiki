# Jesse L. Livermore
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Jesse L. Livermore was one of the most prominent stock traders of the early twentieth century. Despite having no formal education, he began his career at the age of 14. Known as a Wall Street legend, Livermore influenced generations of stock and commodity traders.

Livermore was the author of *How to Trade in Stocks* and *My Life in Wall Street and How I Made Three Fortunes in the Stock Market*.

Jesse L. Livermore died by suicide on November 28, 1940. [1]

### Key Takeaways
- Jesse L. Livermore was one of the most famous stock traders of the early twentieth century.
- He started working at Paine Webber at age 14.
- Livermore's experiences were chronicled by Edwin Lefevre in *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator*.

## Early Life and Education
Jesse L. Livermore was born on July 26, 1877, in Shrewsbury, Massachusetts. Raised in poverty, Livermore received only an elementary school education.

At age 14, he took a job at Paine Webber in Boston as a quotation board boy, recording stock prices from the ticker tape onto a chalkboard.

## The Stock Trader
In his book *Jesse Livermore: Boy Plunger, the Man Who Sold America Short*, Tom Rubython described Livermore as the man who made the most money in a single day and lost the most money in a single day. Between 1900 and 1940, Jesse Livermore made and lost three fortunes. He bought and held during bull markets and sold when market momentum shifted. His strategy was remarkably precise, especially given that companies at the time did not publish financial statements or conduct fundamental analysis.

Livermore's first trade, at age 15, netted him $3. [1] By 16, he had quit his job at Paine Webber and begun trading on his own. Trades at the time were often conducted at bucket shops, where customers leveraged bets on stock price movements. After being banned from bucket shops in Boston due to his consistent success, Livermore moved to New York City. [1]

## The Bear of Wall Street
Jesse L. Livermore earned a reputation on Wall Street for predicting market collapses, earning him the moniker "the Bear of Wall Street." [2] His two most famous trades occurred during the Panic of 1907 and at the beginning of the Great Depression.

As a market bubble expanded in 1906, Livermore followed the trend until his instincts told him otherwise. In a famous trade, he shorted shares of Union Pacific and earned a $300,000 profit within two days after the San Francisco earthquake struck. When the market plunged in 1907, Livermore heeded the advice of J.P. Morgan and bought while others were selling. Other traders followed suit, and Livermore is credited with playing a key role in the market's early recovery. [3]

By 1929, Livermore was well positioned in the stock market but began looking for the first signs of weakness as another bubble approached. In several small trades, Livermore sold his long positions by probing the market with short bets, resulting in losses of nearly $250,000. He continued building short positions, however, and on Black Tuesday, October 29, 1929, he reportedly made $100 million shorting during the Great Depression. [3]

At his peak, Livermore's fortune was equivalent to approximately $1.5 billion in today's dollars. He traded freely and without regulation until the founding of the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) in 1934, which marked the end of Livermore's era. [4] By 1940, Livermore was bankrupt.

## Who Did Jesse L. Livermore Influence?
Commenting on Livermore's experiences, William J. O'Neil -- founder of *Investor's Business Daily* -- noted: "In my 45 years of experience in this business, I have found only 10 or 12 books that were really worthwhile, and *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator* was one of them." Today, many in the market know Jesse Livermore through an anonymous Twitter account of the same name that discusses and recommends Livermore's trading strategies.

## How Did the White House Affect Jesse L. Livermore's Trading?
In the unregulated markets of the era, Livermore cornered the cotton market after World War I. Using brokers around the world, he built substantial cotton positions, and within 18 months, he owned most of the cotton in the United States. President Woodrow Wilson personally asked Livermore to sell his dominant position to avoid harming the American economy. [5]

## How Did J.P. Morgan Influence Jesse L. Livermore?
During the Panic of 1907, Livermore made $1 million in a single day from short positions. When the renowned banker J.P. Morgan urged him to close his shorts for the good of the country, Livermore complied. He then went on to earn an additional $3 million as the market rebounded.

## Summary
Jesse L. Livermore's rise from quotation board boy to Wall Street legend remains instructive for investors to this day. His experiences serve as a historical record of unregulated stock trading in the early twentieth century.
