# What Is Alpha?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Alpha is a term used in investing to describe an investment strategy's ability to outperform the market, often referred to as its "edge." Alpha is therefore also known as excess return or the abnormal rate of return relative to a benchmark, after adjusting for risk.

Alpha is frequently used alongside beta, which measures the overall volatility or risk of the broad market, known as systematic market risk.

In finance, alpha serves as a performance measure indicating whether a strategy, trader, or portfolio manager has managed to beat the market return or another benchmark over a given period. Alpha is often regarded as the active return on an investment, gauging performance relative to a market index or benchmark that is considered representative of the market's overall movement.

The excess return of an investment relative to a benchmark index is the investment's alpha. Alpha can be positive or negative and is the result of active investing. Beta, by contrast, can be earned through passive index investing.

### Key Takeaways

- Alpha refers to excess returns earned on an investment after adjusting for risk.
- Active portfolio managers seek to generate alpha in diversified portfolios, with the goal of eliminating unsystematic risk.
- Because alpha represents a portfolio's performance relative to a benchmark, it is often considered the value that a portfolio manager adds to or subtracts from a fund's return.
- Jensen's alpha incorporates the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) and includes a risk-adjusted component in its calculation.

## Understanding Alpha

Alpha is one of five commonly used technical risk ratios in investing. The other four are beta, standard deviation, R-squared, and the Sharpe ratio. These are all statistical measures used in Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) to help investors determine an investment's risk-return profile.

Active portfolio managers seek to generate alpha in diversified portfolios designed to eliminate unsystematic risk. Alpha represents a portfolio's performance relative to a benchmark, and is therefore often viewed as the value the portfolio manager adds to or subtracts from a fund's return.

In other words, alpha is the portion of an investment return that is not attributable to broad market movements. An alpha of zero indicates the portfolio or fund is perfectly tracking the benchmark index, and the manager has neither added nor destroyed value.

## Alpha in Investing

The concept of alpha has become increasingly relevant with the rise of smart beta index funds tied to benchmarks such as the S&P 500 and the Wilshire 5000 Total Market Index. These funds aim to enhance the performance of portfolios that track specific market subsets.

Despite alpha's importance in portfolio management, many index benchmarks still outperform asset managers the vast majority of the time. Partly due to a growing loss of confidence in traditional financial advisors, more investors are turning to low-cost passive online advisors (often called robo-advisors) that invest client assets almost entirely in index-tracking funds, reasoning that if they cannot beat the market, they might as well join it.

Furthermore, since most traditional financial advisors charge fees, when a manager generates zero alpha, it actually represents a slight net loss for the investor. For example, suppose a financial advisor named Jim charges 1% of a portfolio's value, and over a 12-month period, Jim generates an alpha of 0.75 for his client Frank's portfolio.

While Jim did improve Frank's portfolio performance, the fee exceeds the alpha generated, so Frank's portfolio experienced a net loss. This example highlights the importance of considering fees alongside performance returns and alpha.

## The Efficient Market Hypothesis

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH) posits that market prices incorporate all available information at all times, so securities are always properly priced (the market is efficient). According to the EMH, systematically identifying and exploiting mispricings in the market is impossible because they do not exist.

If mispricings are discovered, the market quickly arbitrages them away. As a result, persistent patterns of exploitable market anomalies are rare.

Empirical evidence comparing active mutual fund returns to their passive benchmarks shows that fewer than 10% of active funds achieve positive alpha over a 10-year period, and this figure shrinks further after accounting for taxes and fees. In other words, alpha is difficult to obtain, particularly after taxes and fees.

Because beta risk can be isolated through diversification and hedging (which entails various transaction costs), some argue that alpha does not actually exist -- that it is merely compensation for taking some unhedged or unrecognized risk.

## Pursuing Investment Alpha

Alpha is commonly used to rank active mutual funds and other types of investments. It is typically expressed as a single number (e.g., +3.0 or -5.0), representing the percentage by which a portfolio or fund outperformed or underperformed the reference benchmark index.

A deeper analysis of alpha may also involve Jensen's alpha, which incorporates the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) market theory and includes a risk-adjusted component in its calculation. Beta (the beta coefficient) is used in CAPM to calculate the expected return of an asset based on its specific beta and the expected market return. Investment managers use alpha and beta together to calculate, compare, and analyze returns.

The broader investment landscape offers a wide array of securities, investment products, and advisory options for investors to consider. Different market cycles also influence the alpha of investments across different asset classes. This is why risk-return metrics are particularly important when evaluating alpha.

## Alpha Examples

Alpha can be illustrated through the following historical examples of a fixed-income ETF and an equity ETF:

The iShares Convertible Bond ETF (ICVT) is a low-risk fixed-income investment. It tracks a custom index called the Bloomberg U.S. Convertible Cash Pay Bond > $250MM Index. As of February 28, 2022, its three-year standard deviation was 18.94%. Its year-to-date return was -6.67%, while the Bloomberg U.S. Convertible Cash Pay Bond > $250MM Index returned -13.7% over the same period. Thus, ICVT's alpha relative to the Bloomberg U.S. Aggregate Index was 6.5%, with a three-year standard deviation of 18.97%.

However, because the Aggregate Bond Index is not the proper benchmark for ICVT (the Bloomberg Convertible Bond Index would be more appropriate), this alpha may be overstated, as convertible bonds carry considerably more risk than plain vanilla bonds and the excess return may be incorrectly attributed.

The WisdomTree U.S. Quality Dividend Growth Fund (DGRW) is a higher-risk equity investment that targets dividend growth stocks. Its holdings track a custom index called the WisdomTree U.S. Quality Dividend Growth Index. As of February 28, 2022, DGRW's annualized three-year return was 18.1%, exceeding the S&P 500's 16.4%, producing an alpha of 1.7% relative to the S&P 500. However, the S&P 500 may not be the correct benchmark for this ETF, since dividend-paying growth stocks are a specific subset of the broader equity market and may not include all 500 of America's most valuable companies.

## Alpha Considerations

While alpha is often called the "holy grail" of investing and receives considerable attention from investors and advisors, there are several important caveats to keep in mind.

When using generated alpha calculations, it is crucial to understand the underlying methodology. Alpha can be computed against a variety of benchmark indices within an asset class. In some cases, there may be no suitable pre-existing benchmark, in which case advisors may use algorithms or other models to simulate a benchmark for comparison purposes.

Alpha can also refer to the abnormal rate of return on a security or portfolio in excess of what would be predicted by an equilibrium model such as CAPM. In this context, CAPM aims to estimate investor returns at various points along the efficient frontier. If a portfolio's actual return is 15% but CAPM estimates it should have returned 10%, the portfolio's alpha is 5.0 -- meaning it earned 5% above what the CAPM model predicted.

## Alpha vs. Beta in Finance

Alpha measures excess return relative to a benchmark, while beta measures volatility -- or risk. Active investors employ unique strategies to achieve alpha returns.

## What Is a Good Alpha in Finance?

In finance, and particularly in trading and investing, what qualifies as a "good" alpha depends on the investor's objectives and risk tolerance. Generally, a good alpha is one that is greater than zero after adjusting for risk.

## What Does Negative Alpha Mean for a Stock?

Negative alpha for a stock means that, after adjusting for risk, the stock has underperformed its benchmark. If an investor aims to match or exceed a specific benchmark and the portfolio falls short, the alpha is negative.

## Conclusion

Investors aim to achieve the highest possible returns. Alpha is a measure of investment performance relative to a benchmark on a risk-adjusted basis. Active investors seek returns above the benchmark through various strategies. Many funds, such as hedge funds, are designed to generate alpha and charge higher management fees accordingly.
