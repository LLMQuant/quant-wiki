# What Is a Currency Carry Trade?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A currency carry trade is a strategy in which a high-yielding currency is funded by a low-yielding currency. Traders employing this strategy attempt to capture the interest rate differential, which can be substantial depending on the amount of leverage used.

The carry trade is one of the most popular strategies in the foreign exchange market. Classic carry trades involve buying currency pairs such as AUD/JPY and NZD/JPY, where the interest rate spreads are relatively wide. The first step in constructing a carry trade is to identify which currencies offer high yields and which offer low yields.

## Fundamentals of the Currency Carry Trade

The currency carry trade is among the most popular strategies in the forex market, embodying the principle of "buy low, sell high." The best way to implement a carry trade is to determine which currency offers a higher yield and which offers a lower one.

The most popular carry trades involve purchasing currency pairs such as AUD/JPY and NZD/JPY because these pairs feature wide interest rate spreads.

Mechanically, a trader profits from the interest rate differential between two countries as long as the exchange rate remains stable. Many professional traders use this strategy because gains can be amplified significantly through leverage. If a trader in our example uses common 10:1 leverage, the profit on the interest rate differential is magnified tenfold.

The funding currency is the currency that is borrowed and exchanged in a carry trade. Funding currencies typically have low interest rates. Investors borrow the funding currency and take short positions while establishing long positions in the asset currency, which carries a higher interest rate. Central banks of funding-currency countries, such as the Bank of Japan (BoJ) and the U.S. Federal Reserve, often pursue aggressive monetary stimulus that drives rates lower. These central banks use monetary policy to reduce interest rates in order to stimulate growth during recessions. As rates decline, speculators borrow the funds and aim to unwind their positions before rates rise again.

The optimal time to enter a carry trade is when central banks are raising -- or considering raising -- interest rates. Many participants pile in, pushing up the value of the currency pair. Carry trades also perform well during periods of low volatility, as traders are willing to take on more risk. As long as the currency's value does not fall significantly, traders can continue to earn the interest differential.

However, during periods of declining interest rates, carry trades offer less attractive returns. A shift in monetary policy often signals a change in currency values. When interest rates fall, demand for the currency tends to decrease, making it difficult to sell. Essentially, for a carry trade to be profitable, there must be little adverse volatility or the currency must appreciate.

- Traders employing this strategy aim to capture the interest rate differential, which can be substantial depending on the leverage used.

## Currency Carry Trade Example

Consider a trader who notices that interest rates in Japan are 0.5% while rates in the United States are 4%. The trader expects to earn a 3.5% profit -- the difference between the two rates. The first step is to borrow yen and convert them into U.S. dollars. The second step is to invest those dollars in securities paying the U.S. rate. Assume the current exchange rate is 115 yen per dollar and the trader borrows 50 million yen. Once converted, the trader holds:

U.S. dollars = 50 million yen / 115 = $434,782.61

After one year invested at the 4% U.S. rate, the trader's balance is:

Ending balance = $434,782.61 x 1.04 = $452,173.91

The trader now owes the 50 million yen principal plus 0.5% interest, totaling:

Amount owed = 50 million yen x 1.005 = 50.25 million yen

If the exchange rate remains unchanged at 115 over the year, the amount owed in dollars is:

Amount owed = 50.25 million yen / 115 = $436,956.52

The trader profits from the difference between the ending dollar balance and the amount owed:

Profit = $452,173.91 - $436,956.52 = $15,217.39

Note that this profit matches the expected amount: $15,217.39 / $434,782.62 = 3.5%.

If the exchange rate moves against the yen, the trader earns even more. If the yen appreciates, the trader earns less than 3.5% -- or may even incur a loss.

## Risks and Limitations of Carry Trades

The primary risk in a carry trade is the uncertainty of exchange rates. Using the example above, if the U.S. dollar depreciates against the yen, the trader may face losses. Additionally, these trades are typically highly leveraged, meaning even small exchange rate movements can lead to significant losses unless the position is properly hedged.

An effective carry trade strategy involves more than simply going long the highest-yielding currency and shorting the lowest-yielding one. While the current level of interest rates matters, the expected direction of future rate changes is even more important. For example, the U.S. dollar could appreciate against the Australian dollar if the U.S. central bank begins raising rates just as the Reserve Bank of Australia finishes its tightening cycle.

Furthermore, carry trades work best when market sentiment is calm or optimistic. Uncertainty, anxiety, and fear can cause investors to rapidly unwind their carry trades. The 45% sell-off in currency pairs such as AUD/JPY and NZD/JPY in 2008 was triggered when the subprime crisis escalated into a global financial crisis. Because carry trades are often leveraged, actual losses can be far greater than the underlying moves suggest.
