# What Is a Short Squeeze?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A short squeeze occurs when a heavily shorted asset's price rises sharply, forcing short sellers to close their positions to limit losses. This phenomenon typically arises when a security has a large number of short sellers, meaning many investors are betting that its price will decline.

A short squeeze usually begins with an unexpected surge in the asset's price. As short sellers scramble to cut their losses and exit their positions, the buying pressure intensifies, further fueling the rally.

### Key Takeaways

- A short squeeze happens when many investors bet against a stock and its price rises sharply instead.
- The squeeze accelerates the stock's price increase as short sellers rush to close positions and limit their losses.
- Contrarian investors try to anticipate short squeezes by purchasing stocks with heavy short interest.
- Both short sellers and contrarian investors are taking risks. Prudent investors typically have additional, well-founded reasons for shorting or buying the stock.

## How a Short Squeeze Works

Short sellers open trades with the expectation that a stock's price will decline. When a heavily shorted stock unexpectedly rises, short sellers may need to act quickly to cap their losses.

Short sellers borrow shares of an asset they believe will fall in price, intending to buy them back at a lower price. If they are right, they return the shares and pocket the difference between the sale price and the repurchase price. If they are wrong, they must buy back at a higher price, absorbing the difference as a loss.

Because short sellers close their positions by purchasing shares, this buying activity pushes the price even higher. The rapid, sustained price increase also attracts new buyers to the security. The combination of panicked short sellers and fresh buyers creates a dramatic, often unprecedented price surge.

**Note:** The term "short squeeze" derives from short sellers being squeezed out of their positions at a loss.

Short sellers typically target stocks they consider overvalued by the market. Tesla (TSLA), for example, captured the enthusiasm of many investors with its innovative approach to producing and marketing electric vehicles. Investors wagered heavily on its potential, while short sellers bet on its failure. In early 2020, Tesla was the most shorted stock on U.S. exchanges, with more than 18% of its outstanding shares in short positions. [1]

From late 2019 through early 2020, Tesla's stock surged 400%. Short sellers were battered, collectively losing approximately $8 billion. [1] Although most stocks -- Tesla included -- declined during the early March 2020 market downturn, the stock eventually rebounded, and Tesla short sellers lost more than $40 billion total during 2020. [2]

## Why Short Squeezes Happen

Short sellers establish positions on stocks they believe will decline. However reasonable their thesis may be, a positive news story, a product launch, or an earnings beat can upend the outlook.

The reversal in the stock price may prove temporary. But if it does not, the short seller can face runaway losses as the expiration date approaches. They typically choose to close out immediately, even if it means absorbing a substantial loss.

This is the essence of a short squeeze. Every buy-to-cover order from a short seller drives the price higher, forcing still more short sellers to cover.

**18%:** Tesla's short interest as a percentage of outstanding shares in late 2019. [1]

Two useful metrics for identifying potential short-squeeze candidates are short interest and the short interest ratio.

Short interest is the number of shares sold short expressed as a percentage of total shares outstanding. The short interest ratio divides the number of shorted shares by the stock's average daily trading volume. Speculative stocks tend to have higher short interest than more stable companies.

Monitoring short interest can reveal whether investor sentiment toward a company is shifting. For example, if a stock typically carries short interest between 15% and 30%, a move outside that range may indicate that views on the company have changed. Declining short interest could signal that the price has risen too quickly or that short sellers are leaving because the stock has become too stable.

Rising short interest above historical norms signals increasingly bearish sentiment, but extremely high readings can be a sign that a short squeeze is imminent -- which could push the price even higher.

Contrarian investors may purchase stocks with heavy short interest to exploit the potential for a squeeze. While a rapid price surge is attractive, the approach carries risk -- the stock may be heavily shorted for a good reason, such as a poor outlook.

Active traders monitor heavily shorted stocks and watch for them to begin rallying. Once the price starts rising, they buy in, aiming to ride a possible short squeeze to significantly higher levels.

Many stocks recover after being heavily shorted, but others continue to decline.

High short interest does not guarantee that the price will rise. It merely indicates that many investors believe the price will fall. Anyone buying in anticipation of a short squeeze should have additional -- and more compelling -- reasons to believe the stock's price will increase.

## Naked Short Selling vs. Short Squeezes

Naked short selling is the practice of selling shares short without first borrowing them. It involves selling shares whose existence has not been affirmatively confirmed. According to the SEC, abusive naked short selling is illegal. [3]

Nonetheless, naked shorting persists due to discrepancies between electronic and paper-based trading. Naked shorting can exacerbate a short squeeze because it allows additional short positions that might not otherwise exist. While proponents argue that it helps balance the market -- by pressuring prices downward and triggering sales that reduce losses -- critics contend that it distorts price discovery.

## Short Squeeze Example

Consider a hypothetical biotechnology company, XYZ, whose drug candidate is in advanced clinical trials.

There is considerable skepticism among investors about whether the drug will actually work, resulting in heavy short interest. In fact, 5 million of XYZ's 25 million outstanding shares have been sold short. Short interest stands at 20%, and the average daily trading volume is 1 million shares, giving a short interest ratio of 5. This ratio, also called "days to cover," means it would take short sellers five days to buy back all shorted XYZ shares.

Assume that because of the heavy short interest, XYZ's stock declined from $15 to $5 over several months. Then news breaks that XYZ's drug is performing better than expected. The stock jumps to $9 as speculators pile in and short sellers scramble to cover.

Every short seller who entered between $9 and $5 is now losing money. Those who shorted near $5 face the steepest losses, as their investment is down 80%.

The stock opens at $9 but continues climbing over the following days as short sellers keep covering, and rising prices combined with positive news attract new buyers.

## Real-World Case Study: GameStop

In the months following the COVID-19 pandemic, a remarkable short squeeze unfolded between traders and investors in GameStop Corporation (GME). With consumers locked down and stores shuttered, analysts and investors anticipated the company could face bankruptcy from increased competition and declining foot traffic. GME became a prime target for short sellers, with short interest exceeding 100% of outstanding shares. [4]

This attracted Michael Burry of Scion Asset Management, who developed a long-term bullish thesis, believing the company could return to profitability within a few years rather than go bankrupt. His thesis was shared and cited through Reddit forums and YouTube content creators in mid-2020. Michael Burry and Chewy co-founder Ryan Cohen also took long positions. [5]

As more investors began buying the stock in late 2020, the price started rising significantly. Retail investor buying and call option activity created a snowball effect. The price surge forced some short sellers to exit and attracted high-profile investors and public figures, including Elon Musk and venture capitalist Chamath Palihapitiya. [5]

GameStop's stock price soared from under $5 to $120 per share in approximately one month, driven by the short squeeze against major hedge funds. [6]

After the initial "meme stock" frenzy, GameStop's stock steadily declined, settling at just above $10 per share by spring 2024. However, in mid-May 2024, Keith Gill -- known as "Roaring Kitty" -- returned to social media, and the stock surged again.

Gill's cryptic posts on X (formerly Twitter) reignited interest in GameStop and other meme stocks, driving a massive spike in volume and price. On May 14, 2024, GameStop shares surged nearly 100%, having also risen 74% the previous day. [7] The sudden rally caught many investors and analysts off guard, as the company's fundamentals had not materially improved since the 2021 meme stock frenzy.

The 2024 meme stock resurgence again pressured short sellers who had re-established short positions in GME and other meme companies. According to analytics firm S3 Partners, GameStop short sellers lost more than $1.3 billion during the initial squeeze in May 2024, with losses mounting further if the stock continued to climb. [8]

Although the 2024 resurgence shared many parallels with the 2021 events, some analysts questioned whether the phenomenon would have the same long-term impact on markets and retail investor participation. Nevertheless, the sudden revival once again underscored the power of social media and retail investor sentiment in driving market trends. [7]

## What Is "Days to Cover" and Is It Useful for Identifying Short Squeeze Targets?

Days to cover, also known as the short interest ratio, is calculated by dividing the total number of shares sold short by the stock's average daily trading volume. For example, if a stock has 1 million shares sold short and an average daily volume of 100,000 shares, the days to cover is 10 -- meaning short sellers would need 10 days to cover their positions based on average volume.

Generally, the higher a stock's days-to-cover figure, the more susceptible it is to a short squeeze. If Stock A has 2 days to cover and Stock B has 20 days, Stock B is potentially more vulnerable as a squeeze target.

## Who Loses and Who Benefits in a Short Squeeze?

Speculators and traders holding short positions in a stock undergoing a short squeeze face heavy losses. Contrarian investors who built long positions in anticipation of the squeeze benefit as the stock price rises.

## Where Can I Find Short Interest Information?

Financial portals such as Yahoo Finance offer free stock screeners that generate lists of heavily shorted stocks. Drilling down into individual stocks reveals relevant short-selling data, including the number of shares sold short and the short interest ratio. Online resources such as MarketBeat provide useful short-selling data, including the largest short positions, changes over time, and the short interest ratio. Exchanges such as the NYSE and Nasdaq also publish exchange-wide short interest data.

## What Is the Largest Short Squeeze in History?

During the 2008 financial crisis, Porsche Automobil Holding SE (POAHY), as a major holder of Volkswagen (VWAGY) shares, increased its stake to approximately 75%. The state of Lower Saxony also held over 20% of the company, leaving very few tradeable shares available while the stock was heavily shorted. [9] Hedge funds and other short sellers found themselves trapped, as Porsche's move meant not all shorts could be covered. In the ensuing squeeze, short sellers were forced to buy back shares they had sold, driving Volkswagen's stock price to 999 euros. [10]

## Summary

Short squeezes may appear to erupt without warning, with prices climbing parabolically. But they are typically set up by unusually high short interest in the underlying security. When a catalyst pushes the stock price higher, short sellers simultaneously rush for the exit, buying to cover. This buying pressure fuels the rally, often producing an irrational spike. The 2021 GameStop saga vividly illustrated how a short squeeze operates in practice.
