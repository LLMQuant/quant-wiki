# What Is a Long Put?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A long put refers to the purchase of a put option, typically in anticipation of a decline in the underlying asset's price. The word "long" here does not refer to the time until expiration, but rather to the trader's action of buying the option with the hope of selling it at a higher price at a later time.

A trader might buy a put for speculative reasons, betting that the underlying asset will fall in price, which would increase the value of the put option. A long put can also be used to hedge a long position in the underlying asset. If the underlying asset's price declines, the put option increases in value, helping to offset the loss on the underlying position.

### Key Takeaways

- A long put is a position where someone buys a put option. It is inherently a bearish market strategy.
- Investors buy put options when they believe a security's price will decline.
- Investors may choose to buy put options to speculate on price declines or to hedge a portfolio against downside risk.
- Downside risk is therefore limited when using a long put strategy.

## Understanding the Long Put

A long put has a strike price, which is the price at which the put buyer has the right to sell the underlying asset. Assume the underlying asset is a stock and the option's strike price is $50. That means the put option entitles the trader to sell the stock at $50, even if the stock drops to $20. On the other hand, if the stock rises and stays above $50, the option is worthless because there is no point in selling at $50 when the stock is trading at $60.

If the trader wishes to exercise the right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price, they can exercise the option. Exercising is not mandatory, and the trader can exit the position at any time before expiration by selling the option.

An American-style put option can be exercised at any time before the expiration date, while a European-style put can only be exercised on the expiration date. If exercised early or if the option is in-the-money at expiration, the option holder will hold a short position in the underlying asset.

## Long Put Strategy vs. Short Selling Stock

A long put can be an attractive alternative to short selling for bearish investors. A short stock position carries theoretically unlimited risk, since the stock price has no ceiling. The profit potential on a short sale is also limited, since the stock cannot fall below $0 per share. A long put has a similar profit profile -- the put's value increases only until the underlying stock reaches zero. The advantage of a long put is that the risk is limited to the premium paid for the option.

The downside of a long put is that the underlying asset's price must fall before the option expires; otherwise, the amount paid for the option is lost.

To profit from a short stock trade, a trader sells shares at one price hoping to buy them back at a lower price. Put options are similar -- if the underlying stock falls, the put option increases in value and can be sold for a profit. If the option is exercised, the trader is placed in a short position on the underlying stock and must then purchase the shares to realize the trade's profit.

## Using a Long Put for Hedging

A long put can also be used to hedge against unfavorable moves in a long stock position. This hedging strategy is known as a protective put or married put.

For example, suppose an investor is long 100 shares of Bank of America Corporation (BAC) at $25 per share. The investor is bullish on the stock long-term but is concerned it may decline over the next month. The investor purchases one put option with a $20 strike price for $0.10 (multiplied by 100 shares, since each put contract represents 100 shares), expiring in one month.

The investor's hedge caps the loss at $500, or 100 shares x ($25 - $20), minus the premium paid for the put option ($10 total). In other words, even if Bank of America falls to $0 next month, the most this trader can lose is $510, because all stock losses below $20 are covered by the long put option.

## Example of Using a Long Put

Suppose Apple Inc. (AAPL) is trading at $170 per share and you believe the price will decline about 10% ahead of a new product launch. You decide to go long 10 put options with a $155 strike price at $0.45. Your total outlay for the long put position is $450 plus fees and commissions (1,000 shares x $0.45 = $450).

If Apple stock drops to $154 before expiration, your put options are now worth $1.00 each, because you can exercise them and be short 1,000 shares at $155, then immediately buy back at $154 to cover.

The total position value of your put options is now $1,000 (minus any fees and commissions), which equals 1,000 shares x $1.00 = $1,000. Your return on the position is 122% = ($1,000 - $450) / $450. Going long on put options allowed you to realize a return far greater than the 9.4% decline in the underlying stock price.

Conversely, if Apple shares rise to $200, the 10 option contracts would expire worthless, causing you to lose your initial outlay of $450.
