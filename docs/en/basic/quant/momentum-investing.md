# What Is Momentum Investing?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Momentum investing is a strategy that seeks to capitalize on the continuation of existing market trends. Practitioners buy securities that have been rising and sell them when they appear to have peaked.

In financial markets, momentum refers to the tendency of a price trend to sustain itself over time.

### Key Takeaways

- Momentum investing is a strategy designed to profit from the continuation of existing trends in the market.
- Market momentum is the ability of a price trend to sustain and extend itself.
- Momentum investing typically follows a strict set of rules based on technical indicators that dictate entry and exit points for specific securities.
- Few professional investment managers rely on momentum investing, preferring instead to use fundamental analysis and value-oriented metrics.

## Understanding Momentum Investing

Momentum investing involves taking long positions in stocks, futures, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), or any financial instrument exhibiting an upward price trend, while simultaneously shorting assets with downward-trending prices.

Momentum investors believe that trends can persist for a considerable period, and that profits can be made by riding a trend until it ends, regardless of how long that takes. For example, momentum investors who entered the U.S. stock market in 2009 generally enjoyed an uptrend through December 2018.

**Note:** While he was not the first to employ the strategy, fund manager and businessman Richard Driehaus is widely regarded as a pioneer of momentum investing.[1]

## Momentum Investing Methods

Momentum investing typically follows a strict set of rules based on technical indicators that dictate market entry and exit points for specific securities.

Momentum investors sometimes use two longer-period moving averages (MAs), one slightly shorter than the other, to generate trading signals. For example, some use 50-day and 200-day MAs. A buy signal is generated when the 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA, and a sell signal occurs when the 50-day crosses back below the 200-day. Some momentum investors prefer even longer-period MAs for signal generation.

Another approach involves going long on sector ETFs with the strongest momentum while shorting those with the weakest, then rotating between sectors accordingly.

Other momentum strategies incorporate cross-asset analysis. For example, some equity traders closely watch the Treasury yield curve and use it as a momentum signal for equity entries and exits. A 10-year Treasury yield above the 2-year yield is typically a buy signal, while a 2-year yield above the 10-year yield is a sell signal. Notably, the 2-year/10-year Treasury yield spread tends to be a strong predictor of recessions and also has implications for stock markets.

Some strategies combine momentum factors with certain fundamental factors. One well-known system is CAN SLIM, created by William O'Neill, which is notable for its emphasis on quarterly and annual earnings-per-share (EPS) growth.[2] While some might argue this is not a pure momentum strategy, CAN SLIM generally seeks stocks with earnings and sales momentum, which also tend to exhibit price momentum.

**Important:** If you intend to practice momentum investing, ensure you select appropriate securities and consider their liquidity and trading volume.

Like other momentum systems, CAN SLIM includes rules for entering and exiting stocks, based primarily on technical analysis.

## The Debate Around Momentum Investing

Few professional investment managers use momentum investing, believing that individual stock picking based on discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis and other fundamental factors tends to produce more predictable results and is a better approach for outperforming an index over the long term. As one article in the UCLA Anderson Review put it: "As an investment strategy, it's a thumb in the eye of the efficient market hypothesis (EMH), one of the central tenets of modern finance."[3]

Nevertheless, momentum investing has its proponents. A 1993 study published in the Journal of Finance documented that from 1965 to 1989, strategies of buying recent winners and selling recent losers generated significantly higher short-term returns than the overall U.S. market.[4]

More recently, the American Association of Individual Investors (AAII) found that over five- and ten-year periods, CAN SLIM outperformed the S&P 500 and performed well over even longer time frames.[5]

## What Technical Indicators Can Momentum Traders Use?

In momentum trading, technical analysts can employ several momentum indicators. Among the most popular are the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Rate of Change (ROC), Stochastics, and Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD).

## What Is the Formula for Market Momentum?

Market momentum is commonly defined by the following formula:

$$ \begin{aligned} &M = V - V_x\\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &V = \text{latest price}\\ &V_x = \text{closing price } x \text{ days ago} \\ \end{aligned} $$

## What Is the Market Psychology Behind Momentum Trading?

Momentum traders benefit from herd behavior, greed, and the fear of missing out. These forces can drive bull market rallies beyond what fundamentals alone would predict. Similarly, the fear of losses and panic can turn a sell-off into a crash.
