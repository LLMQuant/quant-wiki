# What Is an Immediate or Cancel (IOC) Order?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
An immediate or cancel order (IOC) is an order to buy or sell a security that executes all or part immediately, then cancels any unfilled portion of the order. An IOC order is one of several "duration," or time-in-force, orders that investors can use to specify how long an order remains active in the market and under what conditions it is cancelled.

Other commonly used duration order types include fill or kill (FOK), all or none (AON), and good 'til cancelled (GTC). Most online trading platforms allow IOC orders to be placed manually or programmed into automated trading strategies.

### Key Takeaways

- An immediate or cancel (IOC) order attempts to execute immediately and cancels any unfilled portion.
- IOC orders only require a partial fill and may be designated as limit orders or market orders.
- IOC is a type of duration order; other types include fill or kill (FOK), all or none (AON), and good 'til cancelled (GTC).

## How an IOC Order Works

Investors can submit either a "limit" or "market" immediate or cancel (IOC) order depending on their specific execution requirements. An IOC limit order is entered at a specified price, while an IOC market order has no price attached and transacts at the best offer price for a buy and the best bid price for a sell.

IOC orders differ from other duration orders in that they require only partial fulfillment, whereas FOK and AON orders must be filled in their entirety or be cancelled. GTC orders remain active until either executed in the market or cancelled by the client, although most brokers cancel them within 30 to 90 days.

## When to Use an IOC Order

Investors typically use IOC orders when submitting large orders to avoid having the order filled at a range of different prices. An IOC order automatically cancels any portion that does not fill immediately. For example, suppose a trader places an IOC order to purchase 5,000 shares of International Business Machines (IBM). Any portion of the 5,000 shares not purchased immediately is automatically cancelled. Traders who deal in multiple stocks throughout the day may use IOC orders to minimize the risk of forgetting to cancel an order at the close.

**Tip:** IOC orders help investors limit risk, speed up execution, and improve pricing by providing greater flexibility.

## Example

Suppose an investor places an IOC market order to buy 1,000 shares of Apple Inc. (AAPL). The order book shows 2,000 shares bid at $170.95 and 500 shares offered at $171.00. The order would immediately fill 500 shares at the offer price ($171.00) and cancel the unfilled 500-share portion.

Now suppose another investor places an IOC limit order to buy 1,000 shares of Apple at $169 at the market open, while the stock is offered at $170. The S&P 500 dips slightly in the afternoon, and a seller quotes 700 shares of AAPL at $169. However, because the IOC order was not filled earlier in the day, it has already been cancelled.

## Benefits of Using IOC Orders

IOC limit orders protect against receiving a poor fill in a fast-moving or illiquid market. On the other hand, IOC market orders ensure a full or partial execution in a strongly traded stock with high demand.

## How Does IOC Affect Market Orders or Limit Orders?

A market order is an order to buy or sell a stock at the best available price and will be executed immediately. A limit order is an order to buy or sell a stock at a specific price or better. Market orders and limit orders may include time-in-force instructions and other trading directives, such as IOC. [1]

## What Does Time-in-Force Mean for Market Traders?

Traders use time-in-force instructions when placing orders to specify how long the order will remain active before it is executed or expires.

## Conclusion

An immediate or cancel (IOC) order is an order to buy or sell a security that executes all or part immediately, then cancels any unfilled portion, combined with a time-in-force instruction. IOC limit orders protect investors in fast-moving markets, while IOC market orders ensure full or partial execution in strong markets with high demand.
