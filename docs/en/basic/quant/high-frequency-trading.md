# What Is High-Frequency Trading (HFT)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
High-frequency trading (HFT) is a trading method that uses powerful computer programs to execute a large number of orders in milliseconds. HFT relies on sophisticated algorithms to analyze multiple markets and execute orders based on prevailing conditions.

Generally, traders with the fastest execution speeds are more profitable than those with slower speeds. HFT is also characterized by high turnover rates and elevated order-to-trade ratios.

### Key Takeaways

- HFT is a form of sophisticated algorithmic trading in which large volumes of orders are executed within seconds.
- HFT adds liquidity to markets and narrows bid-ask spreads.
- HFT is criticized for giving large firms an unfair advantage in trading.
- Another criticism is that the liquidity produced by HFT is ephemeral -- it vanishes within seconds, making it impossible for traders to exploit.

## Understanding High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

High-frequency trading is a form of algorithmic trading. Traders analyze critical data to inform their decisions and complete trades within seconds. HFT can facilitate a large volume of transactions in a short period while tracking market dynamics and identifying arbitrage opportunities.

Key characteristics of high-frequency trading include:

- Trading at extremely high speeds
- Executing large volumes of transactions
- Very short investment time horizons

Given the complexity involved, it is unsurprising that HFT is predominantly used by banks, other financial institutions, and institutional investors.

HFT gained popularity when exchanges began offering incentives to firms that added market liquidity. For example, the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) has a group of liquidity providers known as Supplemental Liquidity Providers (SLPs), which compete to add liquidity to existing quotes. [1]

The SLP program was introduced after the collapse of Lehman Brothers in 2008, when liquidity became a major concern for investors. As an incentive, the NYSE pays a fee or rebate to firms that provide liquidity. With millions of transactions daily, these payments generate substantial profits. [2]

**Important:** Notable HFT firms include Tower Research Capital, Citadel LLC, and Virtu Financial.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of HFT

The primary benefit of HFT is the speed and convenience of trade execution. Banks and other traders can execute large volumes of trades in very short time frames -- typically within seconds.

HFT has improved market liquidity and eliminated bid-ask spreads that were previously too small to matter. This was demonstrated when fees were imposed on HFT activity, causing bid-ask spreads to widen. One study assessed the impact of introducing fees on HFT in Canada and found that market-wide bid-ask spreads increased by 13% and retail spreads rose by 9%. [3]

HFT has also attracted significant controversy and harsh criticism. It has displaced many broker-dealers, using mathematical models and algorithms to make decisions -- removing the human element from decision-making and interaction.

Decisions are made in milliseconds, which can cause significant market movements without warning. For example, on May 6, 2010, the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) experienced what was then its largest intraday point decline, falling 1,000 points and dropping 10% in just 20 minutes before recovering. A government investigation attributed the crash to a massive order that triggered a sell-off. [4]

Another criticism of HFT is that it allows large firms to profit at the expense of smaller players. The so-called "phantom liquidity" produced by HFT has also drawn scrutiny: liquidity appears one moment and vanishes the next, making it impossible for other traders to capitalize on it.

#### Advantages

- Large volumes of trades can be executed simultaneously
- The process is fast and simple
- Improves market liquidity
- Eliminates small bid-ask spreads

#### Disadvantages

- Removes human decision-making and interaction
- Rapid trading can cause significant market disruptions
- Traders cannot take advantage of phantom liquidity

## How Does HFT Work?

High-frequency trading is a form of automated trading. It uses algorithms to identify trading opportunities. HFT is commonly employed by banks, financial institutions, and institutional investors, enabling these entities to execute large batches of trades within extremely short time frames. Because all trading is automated, execution is seamless. HFT adds liquidity to markets, but it can also cause significant market volatility and removes the human judgment factor.

## Does Cryptocurrency Trading Use HFT?

Yes. High-frequency trading exists in cryptocurrency markets and operates in the same manner as in other markets. Using algorithms, it analyzes crypto data and facilitates large volumes of transactions in very short time frames -- typically within seconds.

## How Fast Is High-Frequency Trading?

High-frequency trading is extremely fast. Speeds can reach as low as 10 milliseconds. In some cases, executing large volumes of trades takes even less time. [5]

## Summary

Technological advances have transformed many areas of the financial industry, including trading. Computers and algorithms have made it easier to identify opportunities and to accelerate trade execution. High-frequency trading enables major trading entities to execute large orders with extraordinary speed.

Despite streamlining the process, HFT -- and other forms of algorithmic trading -- carries notable drawbacks, particularly the risk of significant market disruptions, as demonstrated by the 2010 flash crash that sent the Dow Jones plummeting intraday.
