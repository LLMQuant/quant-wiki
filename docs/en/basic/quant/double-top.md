# What Is a Double Top?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A double top is an extremely bearish technical reversal pattern that forms after an asset reaches a high price two consecutive times with a moderate decline between the two peaks. The pattern is confirmed once the asset's price falls below the support level corresponding to the trough between the two highs.

### Key Takeaways

- A double top is a bearish technical reversal pattern.
- It is not always easy to identify, as confirmation requires a break below support.
- While a double top is a bearish signal, a double bottom is a bullish signal.
- A double top typically includes an uptrend, a first peak, a trough, a second peak, and a neckline.
- Traders may initiate short positions after the price breaks below support, or trade on a smaller scale given the potentially limited profit target of a double top.

## What Does a Double Top Tell You?

A double top signals a medium- to long-term trend change in an asset class. Let us consider some historical examples.

The chart above shows Amazon.com (AMZN) stock displaying a double top pattern between September and October 2018, around a price of $2,050. The key support level in this case was approximately $1,880. Although the stock fell nearly 8% from its October peak to the $1,880 support level, the double top could not be confirmed until the price broke below $1,880. From there, shares plunged nearly 31%.[1]

In the next example using Netflix (NFLX), we can see what appears to be a forming double top. However, in this instance, support held and the stock continued to advance with the trend. Later on the chart, the stock appeared to form another double top in June and July. This time it proved to be a genuine reversal pattern, with the price breaking below the $380 support level and declining 39% to $231 by December. Note also how the $380 support level acted as resistance twice during the November rally.[2]

## Double Top vs. Double Bottom

Following an uptrend, a double top is a bearish reversal pattern. It consists of two nearly equal-height peaks with a trough between them. The pattern suggests that price has tested a resistance level twice but failed to break through. Traders typically interpret this as a signal to sell or go short in anticipation of further decline.

The near-inverse of this is a double bottom. Following a downtrend, a double bottom is a bullish reversal pattern. It consists of a peak positioned between two nearly equal-depth troughs. The pattern indicates that price has found support at a certain level and failed to break below it.

In many respects, double tops and double bottoms look similar -- the only difference is the direction. A double top produces consecutive "highs," while a double bottom produces consecutive "lows."

**Important:** Double tops can produce false signals. Even the strongest patterns can break in the opposite direction.

## How to Identify a Double Top

There are several key steps to identifying a double top. Note that not every double top instance is exactly the same, and false signals can mislead investors into believing a double top is forming.

## Key Elements of a Double Top

When identifying a double top pattern, consider the following elements:

- **Uptrend:** Before the pattern forms, the price should be clearly moving upward, making higher highs and higher lows.
- **Two peaks:** The pattern consists of two peaks at approximately equal price levels. These peaks serve as resistance levels where price stalls and begins to decline.
- **Trough:** A trough forms between the two peaks, representing a temporary pullback or consolidation period.
- **Neckline:** The neckline is a horizontal line drawn by connecting the low point of the trough. It acts as a critical support level for confirming the pattern.
- **Neckline break:** The break of the neckline is the defining component of the double top pattern. The pattern is confirmed when price falls below the neckline, signaling a potential trend reversal.
- **Volume:** Volume can strengthen the interpretation of the pattern. Typically, volume increases when price breaks below the neckline and decreases during the formation of the two peaks. A surge in volume on the break can reinforce the pattern's validity.
- **Price target:** After the break, the height from the neckline projected downward serves as a reference point, providing a rough estimate for the potential price decline.

**Tip:** The time between the two peaks can vary considerably. One double top may see peaks separated by a week, while another may take months.

## How to Trade a Double Top

There are three primary approaches to trading a double top. First, you can wait for the price to break below the neckline, which confirms the double top pattern and signals a potential trend reversal. After the breakout, you can enter a short position.

To manage risk, consider placing a stop-loss order above the most recent peak. You can also project the vertical distance between the neckline and the highest peak downward to determine a profit target.

Second, after the neckline breaks, price sometimes retests the neckline from below before continuing lower. As part of this strategy, watch for the price to break below the neckline, wait for a retest of the neckline, and then look for bearish confirmation signals (such as bearish candlestick patterns, trendline breaks, or lower highs) to enter a short position.

Multiple techniques can be used to confirm profit targets, including projecting the pattern's height downward or identifying potential support levels. Stop-losses can be placed above the most recent high.

Third, additional technical indicators or oscillators can enhance the reliability of the double top pattern. For example, check for bearish divergence on the MACD histogram or Relative Strength Index (RSI) as price forms the two peaks. When the neckline is broken and indicators confirm the bearish signal, enter a short position following the stop-loss and profit-target guidelines described above.

## Pros and Cons of Double Tops

The double top pattern is a visual signal of a potential shift from bullish to bearish momentum. It benefits traders looking to profit from changes in market trajectory by capturing new opportunities.

Additionally, two consecutive peaks at nearly the same price level typically create a well-defined resistance zone. Traders can use this level as a reference for stop-loss orders and profit targets, improving risk management and trade planning.

The neckline break also provides a clear entry point for short positions. If the price fails to break below the neckline, it establishes a fixed level that helps determine the pattern's invalidation point. The pattern's height can also be used to project profit targets, giving traders a clear exit signal.

Volume analysis can provide additional confidence in the pattern's validity. Volume typically increases when price breaks below the neckline and decreases during the formation of the two peaks. This increase in volume can further strengthen the signal.

Finally, by identifying the double top pattern, traders can determine profit targets based on the pattern's height and set potential downside targets. Since the potential profit target usually exceeds the initial risk (stop-loss), this typically provides a favorable risk-reward ratio.

However, double top patterns are not infallible. Like other chart patterns, they can sometimes produce false signals. If price briefly forms two peaks but then continues higher, a failed double top has occurred. Therefore, the neckline break and other supporting signals should serve as confirmation, and traders should proceed with caution.

Identifying double tops can involve a degree of subjectivity. The placement of peaks and troughs, as well as the pattern's symmetry, may be interpreted differently by different traders, leading to varying conclusions and outcomes.

Not all double tops exhibit perfect symmetry or identical peaks and troughs. Price range, duration, and shape can vary. This variability can make it difficult to pinpoint exact entry and exit levels or to determine precise target levels.

The downside target of a double top is typically calculated by projecting the pattern's height downward from the neckline. However, the potential profit target may be limited relative to the initial risk or stop-loss level. Depending on market conditions, price may not reach the projected target, resulting in lower-than-expected profits.

## Is a Double Top Pattern Bullish?

No, a double top pattern is not considered bullish. This chart formation is bearish, pointing toward a potential shift from an uptrend to a downtrend.

## What Does a Double Top Pattern Mean?

A double top is a technical chart pattern that typically suggests a potential transition from an uptrend to a downtrend. It forms when an asset's price reaches a resistance level twice, fails to break through, and begins to decline.

## Can Trading a Double Top Pattern Be Profitable?

Trading a double top pattern can be profitable when approached with proper analysis, risk management, and favorable market conditions. However, profitability is not guaranteed, and multiple factors can influence the outcome.

## What Is the Success Rate of Double Top Patterns?

The success rate of any chart pattern depends on several factors, including market conditions, time frame, the quality of the pattern's formation, and the presence of confirming signals.

It is important to remember that chart patterns like the double top do not always predict future price movements accurately. They can produce false signals or failed patterns, but they remain useful for identifying potential trends and reversals.

## Conclusion

Traders and analysts interpret the double top pattern as a bearish indicator. It suggests that upward momentum is weakening and the probability of a price decline is elevated.

The break of the neckline -- the horizontal line formed between the troughs -- is commonly used by traders to confirm the pattern. When price falls below the neckline, it is viewed as a signal to initiate short positions or to sell, with the expectation that the price will continue to decline.
