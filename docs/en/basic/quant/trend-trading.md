# What Is Trend Trading?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Trend trading is a trading style that seeks to capture gains by analyzing an asset's momentum in a particular direction. When price moves in a sustained overall direction -- up or down -- it is called a trend.

Trend traders enter long positions when a security is in an uptrend, characterized by higher swing lows and higher swing highs. Conversely, when an asset is in a downtrend -- marked by lower swing lows and lower swing highs -- trend traders may enter short positions.

### Key Takeaways

- Trend trading aims to profit from uptrends, where prices tend to make new highs, or from downtrends, where prices tend to make new lows.
- An uptrend is a series of higher swing highs and higher swing lows. A downtrend is a series of lower swing highs and lower swing lows.
- In addition to monitoring swing highs and lows, trend traders use tools such as trendlines, moving averages, and technical indicators to help identify trend direction and potentially generate trade signals.

## Understanding Trend Trading

Trend trading strategies assume that a security will continue moving in the same direction as its current trend. These strategies typically include take-profit or stop-loss provisions to lock in gains or limit losses if the trend reverses. Trend trading is used by short-term, medium-term, and long-term traders alike.

Traders use price action and other technical tools to determine trend direction and detect potential changes.

Price action traders focus on the movement of price on a chart. For an uptrend, they want to see price break above recent highs, and when price pulls back, it should remain above prior swing lows. This pattern indicates that even though price fluctuates up and down, the overall trajectory is upward.

The same concept applies to downtrends: traders watch for price to consistently make lower lows and lower highs. When this pattern breaks down, the downtrend is called into question, and trend traders lose interest in holding short positions.

## Trend Trading Strategies

There are many different trend trading strategies, each employing various indicators and price action methods. All strategies should use stop-losses to manage risk. In an uptrend, the stop-loss is placed below a swing low that occurred before entry, or below another support level. In a downtrend with a short position, the stop-loss is typically placed above a prior swing high or above another resistance level.

Traders often combine several of these strategies when looking for trend trading opportunities. A trader might look for a breakout through a resistance level to signal a potential uptrend but only enter if price is trading above a certain moving average.

These strategies involve entering a long position when a short-term moving average crosses above a long-term moving average, or entering a short position when the short-term average crosses below the long-term average. Alternatively, some traders wait for price to cross above a moving average for a long signal, or below it for a short signal.

Moving average strategies are typically combined with other forms of technical analysis to filter signals. This may include watching price action to confirm a trend, since moving averages produce poor signals in the absence of a trend -- price simply whipsaws back and forth around the average.

Moving averages also serve as an analytical tool. When price is above a moving average, it helps indicate a potential uptrend. When price is below, it suggests a possible downtrend.

Among momentum indicators, one approach involves identifying an uptrend and then using the Relative Strength Index (RSI) to generate entry and exit signals.

For example, a trader might wait for the RSI to drop below 30 and then rise back above it. This could signal a long entry, provided the overall uptrend remains intact. The indicator shows that price pulled back but is now resuming its upward move in line with the broader uptrend.

The trader might exit when the RSI climbs above 70 or 80 and then falls back below the selected level.

Trendlines are drawn along the swing lows of an uptrend or the swing highs of a downtrend. They indicate areas where price may pull back to in the future.

Some traders buy during an uptrend when price pulls back to and bounces off a rising trendline -- a buy-the-dip strategy. Similarly, some traders short during a downtrend when price rallies to and retreats from a falling trendline.

Trend traders also watch for chart patterns such as flags or triangles, which suggest a potential continuation of the trend. For example, if price rallies sharply and then forms a flag or triangle, a trend trader will watch for a breakout from the pattern to signal a continuation of the uptrend.

## Trend Trading Chart Example

The following Alibaba Group chart illustrates several examples of how trends can be analyzed and how chart patterns and trends can be used to identify potential trades.

Price starts in a downtrend, then breaks above the descending trendline and above the moving average. However, this alone does not mean the trend has turned up. Trend traders typically wait for price to form a higher swing high and a higher swing low before considering the trend bullish.

Price continues higher, confirming the new uptrend. It then pulls back and begins to rise again, forming the first chart pattern. Price breaks out of this pattern, signaling a potential long entry.

The uptrend continues aggressively, forming two additional chart patterns along the way. Both present opportunities to enter a long position or add to an existing one (known as pyramiding).

Price continues to rise but then begins to show warning signs. Price drops below the moving average for the first time, creates a lower swing low, and breaks through a short-term ascending trendline.

After that, price makes a new high but then drops below the moving average again. This behavior is not characteristic of a strong uptrend, and trend traders would typically avoid going long under these conditions. They would also look to exit any remaining long positions.

The chart shows price continuing to oscillate around the moving average without a clear trend direction. Finally, price slides into a downtrend. Trend traders would exit longs, avoid new long positions, and potentially look for opportunities to enter short positions.
