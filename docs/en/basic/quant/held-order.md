# What Is a Held Order?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A held order is a market order that requires prompt execution for an immediate fill. This contrasts with a "not-held order," which grants the broker discretion over time and price to seek a better fill for the client.

### Key Takeaways

- A held order is an instruction given to a broker requiring prompt execution and an immediate fill, such as a market order.
- The advantage of a held order is that the client is certain the entire order size will be executed without delay, whether buying or selling.
- A not-held order, by contrast, gives the broker discretion to work the order in search of a better price.

## Understanding Held Orders

In most cases, a trade is expected to be executed at the best offer for buy orders or at the best bid for sell orders. Market orders are a common example of held orders. When filling a held order, the trader has very little discretion in finding a price because time is of the essence. Typically, they must match the highest bid or the lowest offer to facilitate a prompt transaction. [1]

For example, if the bid-ask spread for Apple Inc. (AAPL) is $156.90 / $157.00 and a trader receives a held order to buy 100 shares, they would submit a buy order at the $157.00 ask price, which would be executed immediately under normal market conditions.

Held orders are used by investors who need to adjust their exposure to a particular stock and want the order executed promptly.

There are situations where submitting a held order may not be advisable. One such case involves illiquid stocks. Suppose a small-cap stock has a wide bid-ask spread of $1.50 / $2.25. A trader using a held order would be forced to pay the 33.3% spread ($0.75 / $2.25) to obtain immediate execution. In such a case, the trader might achieve a better price by exercising discretion -- sitting on the bid and gradually increasing the order price to attract a seller. Of course, the 33.3% spread may be a reasonable price to pay, especially if the trader is executing a breakout trade or closing a position resulting from an earlier error.

**Tip:** Held orders implicitly carry an immediate-or-cancel (IOC) condition.
