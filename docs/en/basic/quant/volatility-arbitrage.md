# What Is Volatility Arbitrage?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Volatility arbitrage is a trading strategy that seeks to profit from the difference between the forecasted future price volatility of an asset and the implied volatility of options based on that asset.

Volatility arbitrage carries several risks, including the timing of holding positions, potential price changes in the asset, and uncertainty in the implied volatility estimate.

### Key Takeaways

- Volatility arbitrage is a trading strategy used to profit from the difference between the forecasted future price volatility of an asset (such as a stock) and the implied volatility of its options.
- When considering a trade, the investor must determine whether implied volatility is overestimated or underestimated.
- If the underlying stock price moves faster than the investor anticipated, the strategy must be adjusted, which may be difficult or costly depending on market conditions.
- If a trader believes a stock option is underpriced because implied volatility is too low, they may consider opening a long call option position combined with a short position in the underlying stock to profit from the expected change.
- Hedge fund traders may study volatility arbitrage to make trades.

## How Volatility Arbitrage Works

Because option pricing is affected by the volatility of the underlying asset, a discrepancy between forecasted and implied volatility will produce a difference between the expected price of the option and its actual market price.

A volatility arbitrage strategy can be implemented through a delta neutral portfolio consisting of an option and its underlying asset. For example, suppose a trader believes a stock option is underpriced because implied volatility is too low. In that case, they might open a long call option combined with a short position in the underlying stock to profit from this expectation. If the stock price does not move and the trader is correct about implied volatility rising, the cost of the option will increase.

If the trader believes implied volatility is too high and will fall, they may decide to take a long position in the stock while shorting a call option. Assuming the stock price does not move, the trader would profit as implied volatility declines and the option's value decreases.

**Important:** Volatility arbitrage strategies are complex and carry risk for the trader, but they can be implemented through a delta neutral portfolio consisting of an option and its underlying asset.

## Special Considerations

A trader must make several assumptions, which increases the complexity of a volatility arbitrage strategy.

First, the investor must determine whether implied volatility is overestimated or underestimated. Second, the investor must accurately gauge the time needed for the strategy to become profitable; otherwise, time value erosion may outweigh any potential gains.

Finally, if the underlying stock price moves more rapidly than expected, the strategy will need to be adjusted, which may be very costly or even impossible under prevailing market conditions.
