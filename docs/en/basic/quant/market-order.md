# What Is a Market Order?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A market order is an instruction from an investor to a broker to buy or sell a stock, bond, or other asset at the best available price in the current financial market.

For most investors, market orders are the default choice for buying and selling. If the asset is a large-cap stock or a popular exchange-traded fund (ETF), there will be numerous buyers and sellers in the market. As a result, a market order is typically filled almost instantly at a price close to the latest quote the investor sees.[1]

A limit order is the other main alternative, instructing the broker to buy or sell only at a specified price.

###

- A market order is an instruction to buy or sell a security immediately at the current price.
- A limit order is an instruction to buy or sell only at a price specified by the investor.
- Market orders are best suited for buying and selling large-cap stocks, futures, or ETFs.
- For thinly traded or highly volatile assets, limit orders are preferable.
- Market orders are the most common transaction type in the stock market and are typically the default option on most online brokerage platforms.

## Understanding Market Orders

When using an online broker, clicking the "buy" or "sell" button typically brings up an order form that the user needs to fill out. The form requires the ticker symbol, whether you are buying or selling, and the number of shares. It will also ask for the price type.

The default price type is usually "market." This means the investor is not setting a specific price but instead is willing to transact at the prevailing market price.[2]

Other options include "market on close," which means you wish to execute at the very end of the trading session, and "limit," which allows you to buy only at a specified price or lower, or sell only at a specified price or higher.

The market-on-close option suits those who believe they will get the best price of the day at the close of trading. A limit order lets the investor step away from the screen, confident they will not miss an opportunity.

**Note:** If you believe a stock is about to reach an acceptable level, consider using a limit order. If you are wrong, the trade simply will not execute.

Market orders are the most common and straightforward transaction type in the market, designed to be executed quickly at the current ask price. The vast majority of stock buyers and sellers choose this option most of the time, which is why it is the default.

Market orders also tend to carry the lowest fees. Some brokers charge more for trades involving limit orders.

A market order is a safe choice for any large-cap stock because of its high liquidity. This means there is an enormous volume of buy and sell activity at any given moment during the trading day. Trades are executed almost immediately. Unless the market is extremely volatile at that moment, the price you see when you click "buy" or "sell" will be virtually identical to the price you receive.

For less liquid investments -- such as shares of smaller or troubled companies -- market orders become less reliable. Because these stocks trade infrequently, the bid-ask spread tends to be wide, and market orders may be filled slowly at disappointing prices.

## Market Orders vs. Limit Orders

Market orders are the most basic buy and sell transactions, while limit orders give investors greater control.

A limit order allows an investor to set a maximum purchase price or a minimum sale price. The order is only processed when the asset reaches that price.

Limit orders are preferable in several situations:

- When a stock is thinly traded or highly volatile, the investor can time a sale during the next price uptick (or a purchase during a dip).
- When the investor has predetermined an acceptable price, a limit order stands ready at all times. (Note: If using an online broker, do not check the "good for the day" option unless you want the order to expire at the end of the trading session.)
- When the investor wants certainty that the price will not shift in the instant the trade is completed. A stock quote reflects the last price agreed upon by buyer and seller, and the next trade may move the price.

Limit orders are commonly used by professional traders and day traders, who may seek to profit from tiny price movements by quickly buying and selling large volumes of shares.

**Important:** Trades in large-cap stocks like Apple and Microsoft are typically executed almost instantaneously without issue. Smaller, less well-known stocks may behave differently.

## Market Order Example

Suppose the bid and ask prices for Excellent Industrial's stock are $18.50 and $20, respectively, with 100 shares available at the ask price. If a trader places a market order to buy 500 shares, the first 100 will be filled at $20.

However, the remaining 400 shares will be filled at the next best ask prices offered by sellers. If the stock is thinly traded, the remaining 400 shares might be executed at $22 or higher.

This is why using limit orders makes sense for certain trades. Market order execution prices are determined by the market, while limit orders give the trader greater control. Large stock orders can also be processed in batches at the best available prices.

## Special Considerations

Every time a trader seeks to execute a market order, they are agreeing to buy at the ask price or sell at the bid price. The person placing a market order therefore immediately gives up the bid-ask spread.

For this reason, it is wise to closely examine the bid-ask spread before submitting a market order -- especially for less liquid securities. Failing to do so can result in unexpected costs. This is particularly important for those who trade frequently or use automated trading systems.

## Market Order FAQ

Below are answers to some common questions about market orders.

A market order is an instruction to a brokerage firm to buy or sell shares of an asset at the current market price. For most investors, it is the most common way to buy and sell stock.

A market order is essentially an instruction for an immediate purchase or sale at the prevailing price. It is akin to buying a product without negotiating the price. However, in financial markets, the fair price at any given moment is determined by the vast number of buy and sell orders in the market. You receive the fair price at the current moment.

Traders can choose to make it a limit order instead of a market order.

A limit order sets the maximum price an investor is willing to pay or the minimum price they are willing to accept. The limit order remains open until it is either executed or expires.

On an online buy or sell form, the "good for the day" option will cancel the order when the market closes if the target price has not been reached.

Batch orders are transactions processed by brokerage firms behind the scenes. At the start of the trading day, they combine various orders for the same stock into a single trade to be pushed through. Batch trading can only occur at market open and is limited to orders placed between trading sessions.

Each batch order will contain multiple market orders that were submitted between the previous day's close and the current day's open.
