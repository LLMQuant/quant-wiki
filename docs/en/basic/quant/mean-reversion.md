# What Is Mean Reversion?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The concept of mean reversion is widely applied across various financial time series, including prices, returns, and book values. When an asset's current market price is below its historical average, it is generally considered attractive for purchase. Conversely, if the current price is above the average, it is expected to decline. Traders and investors use mean reversion to time their respective trading and investment strategies.

### Key Takeaways

- In finance, mean reversion suggests that various phenomena -- such as asset prices and return volatility -- will eventually revert to their long-term average levels.
- Mean reversion theory has given rise to numerous investment strategies, from stock trading techniques to options pricing models.
- Mean reversion trading attempts to capitalize on extreme changes in a security's price, based on the assumption that it will revert to its prior state.
- Common technical analysis tools for mean reversion include moving averages, the Relative Strength Index (RSI), Bollinger Bands, and the stochastic oscillator.

## Understanding Mean Reversion

Mean reversion is a financial theory positing that asset prices tend to return to their long-term mean or average level over time. This concept rests on the belief that asset prices and historical returns converge toward the long-term average. The greater the deviation from the mean, the higher the probability that the asset will revert in the future.

The theory has given rise to many investment strategies involving the purchase or sale of stocks or other securities whose recent performance has diverged significantly from their historical averages. However, a change in returns may also signal that a company no longer has the same prospects it once did, making mean reversion less likely.

Beyond percentage returns and prices, interest rates and even a company's price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio can be subject to mean reversion.

Investors deploy mean reversion strategies to profit from asset prices that have deviated significantly from their historical averages. The core assumption is that prices will eventually return to their long-term mean. Investors typically apply mean reversion in the following ways:

- **Statistical analysis:** Investors use statistical tools such as Z-scores to measure how far an asset's price has deviated from the mean. A Z-score above 1.5 or below -1.5 may signal a trading opportunity.
- **Pairs trading:** Investors and traders identify two correlated assets. When the price ratio between them deviates from the mean, they go long on the undervalued asset and short the overvalued one.
- **Volatility:** Some traders and investors apply mean reversion in the context of volatility, buying options when volatility is elevated, expecting it to revert toward its average.
- **Risk management:** Stop-loss and take-profit levels can be set around the mean to manage potential losses and secure gains.
- **Algorithmic trading:** Quantitative analysts use mean reversion in algorithmic trading strategies, often employing sophisticated mathematical models to predict price movements.

Key considerations for mean reversion include time frame and market conditions. The effectiveness of mean reversion strategies may vary depending on the time frame. Short-term traders may use intraday data, while long-term investors may use annual data.

Another consideration is that mean reversion works better in range-bound markets than in trending markets.

## Calculating Mean Reversion

Calculating mean reversion involves a series of statistical and quantitative steps to measure how far an asset's price has deviated from its historical mean.

First, gather historical price data for the relevant asset. The time frame may vary depending on the investor's or trader's horizon. Then, compute the average price over the chosen period.

$$ Mean = \text{Sum of Prices} / \text{Number of Observations} $$

Next, calculate the deviation of each price point.

$$ Deviation = Price - Mean $$

Then, compute the standard deviation of the price series to understand volatility.

$$ Standard\ Deviation = \sqrt{\text{Sum of Squared Deviations} / (\text{Number of Observations} - 1)} $$

Using these data, calculate the Z-score. The Z-score measures how many standard deviations an element is from the mean.

$$ Z\text{-}Score = Deviation / Standard\ Deviation $$

A Z-score above a particular threshold (typically 1.5 or 2) may indicate that the asset is overvalued, while a Z-score below another threshold (typically -1.5 or -2) may indicate that the asset is undervalued.

## Mean Reversion and Technical Analysis

Mean reversion is an important concept in technical analysis, serving as a foundational principle behind numerous indicators and trading strategies. It helps traders identify overbought or oversold conditions, thereby providing potential entry and exit points. The following are some technical analysis tools that incorporate mean reversion:

- **Moving averages:** Traders and investors commonly use moving averages to identify the mean price over a specific period. When price is above the moving average by more than a certain threshold, it may be considered overbought. Conversely, when price is below the moving average by a certain threshold, it may be considered oversold.
- **Bollinger Bands:** This technical indicator consists of a middle band (a simple moving average) and two outer bands calculated using standard deviation. Price is expected to revert to the middle band.
- **Relative Strength Index (RSI):** The RSI ranges from 0 to 100 and identifies overbought and oversold conditions. An RSI above 70 indicates overbought conditions, while a reading below 30 indicates oversold conditions, both suggesting potential mean reversion.
- **Stochastic oscillator:** This indicator compares a security's closing price to its price range over a specific period, typically 14 days. Stochastic values above 80 are considered overbought, while values below 20 are considered oversold.
- **Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD):** The MACD is used to identify changes in the strength, direction, momentum, and duration of a trend. When the MACD crosses its signal line, it may indicate that the asset is deviating from its mean.

Day trading involves buying and selling financial instruments within the same trading day, typically holding positions for only minutes or hours. Mean reversion plays a critical role in day trading strategies, as it helps traders profit from short-term price fluctuations.

Key strategies include intraday moving averages. Day traders typically use short-term moving averages to identify the intraday mean price. When an asset's price deviates significantly, a reversion is anticipated.

Additionally, day traders use the RSI and stochastic oscillator to identify intraday overbought or oversold conditions. Signals from these technical tools often prompt day traders to enter and exit positions. In Bollinger Band trading, day traders look for "squeezes" -- where the bands narrow, indicating low volatility and a potential large price move, with a reversion expected to the mean or middle band.

Some day traders employ algorithmic strategies based on mean reversion algorithms for high-frequency trading.

Swing trading is a trading style where positions are held for several days to several weeks, aiming to profit from short- to medium-term price movements. Mean reversion is a key concept in swing trading, helping traders identify potential reversals in price trends.

**Tip:** When it comes to moving averages, swing traders typically use longer periods than day traders to identify the mean price over a specific time frame.

A crossover or crossunder between price and the moving average, followed by a significant deviation, may signal a potential reversal. Additionally, tools like the RSI and MACD are used to identify overbought or oversold conditions that suggest potential mean reversion. Fibonacci retracements are also used to identify levels where price may revert to the mean, with the most common retracement levels being 38.2%, 50%, and 61.8%.

Finally, swing traders may also use candlestick patterns such as doji, hammer, bullish engulfing, and bearish engulfing to identify potential reversals, including mean reversion opportunities.

In forex trading, mean reversion strategies seek to capitalize on currency pairs reverting to their historical mean or average price. Mean reversion is particularly useful in forex for identifying short-term opportunities through technical analysis indicators.

Forex traders typically use moving averages to identify the mean exchange rate over a specific period. When a currency pair deviates significantly from this average, a reversion is generally anticipated.

Indicators such as the RSI and stochastic oscillator are commonly used to identify overbought or oversold conditions in currency pairs, suggesting potential mean reversion.

Forex traders and investors also use pivot points -- tools for identifying potential support and resistance levels where price may revert to the mean. Pivot points are calculated based on the high, low, and close of the previous trading session.

Finally, currency correlation is another tool. Some traders and investors apply mean reversion in the context of currency correlations. When two historically correlated currency pairs diverge, traders may go long the underperforming pair and short the outperforming pair.

## Hypothetical Example of Mean Reversion

Consider a mean reversion scenario involving XYZ Company.

Over the past 200 days, XYZ Company's stock has had an average closing price of $50. Following a positive earnings report, the stock jumps to $70.

Over the past 200 days, the stock's standard deviation is $5.

The Z-score is calculated as (70 - 50) / 5 = 4.

A Z-score of 4 indicates the stock is significantly overvalued relative to its historical mean. This could be a signal to short the stock, anticipating reversion to the mean.

Over the following weeks, the initial excitement fades, and the stock gradually drifts back to around $52, near its historical average.

## Pros and Cons of Mean Reversion

Mean reversion offers a structured and flexible approach to trading, but it also comes with its own challenges, including sensitivity to market conditions and higher trading costs. Traders and investors must be aware of these factors and employ effective risk management techniques.

Advantages of mean reversion include:

- **Systematic approach:** Mean reversion provides a structured methodology that makes identifying entry and exit points easier.
- **Flexibility:** It can be applied across multiple asset classes and time frames, from day trading to long-term investing.
- **Risk management:** Fixed stop-loss and take-profit levels can be set around the mean to help control risk.
- **Profit potential:** In range-bound markets, mean reversion strategies can generate profits when other trend-following strategies may be less effective.
- **Confirmation:** Multiple indicators can be used to confirm mean reversion signals, enhancing the strategy's reliability.

**Important:** Mean reversion theory focuses on the reversion of relatively extreme changes; normal growth or other fluctuations are considered part of the expected range.

Limitations of mean reversion include:

- **Market conditions:** Mean reversion is less effective in strong trending markets, where prices may not revert to the mean for extended periods.
- **Trading costs:** The strategy often involves frequent trading, which can result in higher transaction costs.
- **False signals:** Shorter time frames are particularly susceptible to market noise, which can produce false mean reversion signals.
- **Economic events:** Economic shocks or unexpected news can disrupt mean reversion patterns, leading to potential losses.
- **Lack of directionality:** Unlike trend-following strategies, mean reversion is non-directional and may not suit all trading styles.

## What Is a Mean Reversion Strategy?

A mean reversion strategy is a trading approach that exploits the tendency of financial assets to revert to their historical mean or average price. The strategy aims to identify assets that are significantly overvalued or undervalued and to trade based on the expectation that prices will return to the mean.

The optimal time frame for mean reversion depends on the trader's or investor's goals, risk tolerance, and the type of asset being traded.

## What Assets Are Best Suited for Mean Reversion Trading?

The choice of assets for a mean reversion strategy depends on several factors, including market conditions, the entity's trading and investment expertise, and risk tolerance.

Common assets well-suited to mean reversion strategies include equities, forex, commodities, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and fixed-income instruments.

## What Is the Difference Between Trend Following and Mean Reversion?

Trend following and mean reversion operate on different premises. Trend following aims to profit from a strong directional move in an asset. Mean reversion aims to capture opportunities when price deviates from an established mean or average.
