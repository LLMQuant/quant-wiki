# What Is a Moving Average (MA)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
In finance, a moving average (MA) is a widely used stock indicator in technical analysis. Calculating a moving average helps smooth out price data by creating a constantly updated average price.

By computing the moving average, the impact of random, short-term fluctuations on a stock's price over a specified time frame is mitigated. A simple moving average (SMA) uses an arithmetic average of prices over a given period, while an exponential moving average (EMA) places greater weight on more recent prices.

### Key Takeaways

- A moving average (MA) is a stock indicator commonly used in technical analysis.
- The moving average helps smooth price data over a specific period by creating a constantly updated average price.
- A simple moving average (SMA) is the arithmetic average of prices over a specified number of past days.
- An exponential moving average (EMA) is a weighted average that gives greater importance to recent prices, making it more responsive to new information.

## Understanding the Moving Average (MA)

Moving averages are calculated to identify a stock's trend direction or to determine its support and resistance levels. It is a trend-following, or lagging, indicator because it is based on past prices.

The longer the period of the moving average, the greater the lag. A 200-day moving average has significantly more lag than a 20-day moving average because it incorporates prices from the past 200 days. The 50-day and 200-day moving average figures are widely followed by investors and traders and are considered important trading signals.

Investors can choose different period lengths for calculating moving averages based on their trading objectives. Shorter moving averages are typically used for short-term trading, while longer-period moving averages are better suited for long-term investors.

While there is no way to predict the future movement of a specific stock, using technical analysis and research can help make better predictions. A rising moving average indicates the security is in an uptrend, while a declining moving average indicates a downtrend.

Similarly, upward momentum is confirmed with a bullish crossover, which occurs when a short-term moving average crosses above a longer-term moving average. Downward momentum is confirmed with a bearish crossover, which occurs when a short-term moving average crosses below a longer-term moving average.

## Types of Moving Averages

A simple moving average (SMA) is calculated by taking the arithmetic mean of a given set of values over a specified period. A set of numbers, or stock prices, are added together and then divided by the count of prices in the set. The formula for calculating the simple moving average of a security is as follows:

$$ \begin{aligned} &SMA = \frac{ A_1 + A_2 + \cdots + A_n }{ n } \\ &\textbf{where:} \\ &A = \text{Average in period } n \\ &n = \text{Number of time periods} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Plotting a simple moving average of 50 days of stock prices might appear as shown above.

An exponential moving average gives more weight to recent prices to make it more responsive to new information. To calculate an EMA, first compute the simple moving average (SMA) over a particular period.

Next, calculate the multiplier for weighting the EMA, known as the "smoothing factor," which typically follows the formula: [2 / (selected time period + 1)].

For a 20-day moving average, the multiplier would be [2 / (20 + 1)] = 0.0952. The smoothing factor is combined with the previous EMA to arrive at the current value. The EMA therefore gives a higher weight to recent prices, while the SMA assigns equal weight to all values.

$$ \begin{aligned} &EMA_t = \left [ V_t \times \left ( \frac{ s }{ 1 + d } \right ) \right ] + EMA_y \times \left [ 1 - \left ( \frac { s }{ 1 + d} \right ) \right ] \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &EMA_t = \text{EMA today} \\ &V_t = \text{Value today} \\ &EMA_y = \text{EMA yesterday} \\ &s = \text{Smoothing} \\ &d = \text{Number of days} \\ \end{aligned} $$

## Simple Moving Average (SMA) vs. Exponential Moving Average (EMA)

The EMA calculation places greater emphasis on recent data points. As a result, the EMA is considered a weighted average calculation.

When prices rise, the EMA has a higher value than the SMA, and when prices drop, the EMA falls faster than the SMA. This sensitivity to price changes is the main reason some traders prefer the EMA.

## Moving Average Examples

The calculation of a moving average differs by type: SMA or EMA. Below is an example of a simple moving average (SMA) for a security with the following closing prices over 15 days:

- Week 1 (5 days): 20, 22, 24, 25, 23
- Week 2 (5 days): 26, 28, 26, 29, 27
- Week 3 (5 days): 28, 30, 27, 29, 28

A 10-day moving average would average the closing prices for the first 10 days as the first data point. The next data point would drop the earliest price, add the 11th day's price, and take the average again.

The Bollinger Band technical indicator places bands at two standard deviations above and below a simple moving average. Generally, a move toward the upper band suggests the asset may be overbought, while a move close to the lower band suggests the asset may be oversold. Since standard deviation is a statistical measure of volatility, this indicator adjusts to market conditions.

## What Does a Moving Average Indicate?

A moving average is a statistic that captures the average change in a data series over time. In finance, moving averages are often used by technical analysts to track the price trends of specific securities. An upward trend in a moving average might signify an upturn in the price or momentum of a security, while a downward trend would be seen as a sign of decline.

## What Are Moving Averages Used For?

Moving averages are widely used in technical analysis, a discipline that seeks to understand and profit from the price movement patterns of securities and indices. Generally, technical analysts use moving averages to detect whether a change in momentum is occurring for a security, such as a sudden downward move in price. Other times, they use moving averages to confirm their suspicions that a change may be underway.

## What Are Examples of Moving Averages?

An exponential moving average (EMA) is a type of moving average that gives greater weight to more recent trading days. This type may be more useful for short-term traders, for whom longer-term historical data may be less relevant. A simple moving average is calculated by averaging a series of prices while giving equal weight to each price.

## What Is the MACD?

Traders use the moving average convergence divergence (MACD) to monitor the relationship between two moving averages, calculated by subtracting the 26-day EMA from the 12-day EMA. The MACD also uses a signal line, which helps identify crossovers -- a nine-day EMA plotted on the same chart as the MACD line.

The signal line is used to help identify trend changes in a security's price and to confirm the strength of a trend. When the MACD is positive, the short-term average is above the long-term average, signaling upward momentum. When the short-term average is below the long-term average, it signals that momentum is downward.

## What Is a Golden Cross?

A golden cross is a chart pattern in which a short-term moving average crosses above a long-term moving average. It is a bullish breakout pattern formed when a security's short-term moving average (such as the 15-day) breaks above its long-term moving average (such as the 50-day). Since longer-term indicators carry more weight, the golden cross indicates a bull market on the horizon and is reinforced by high trading volumes.

## Conclusion

A moving average (MA) is a stock indicator commonly used in technical analysis that smooths price data by creating a constantly updated average price. A rising moving average indicates the security is in an uptrend, while a declining moving average indicates a downtrend.

In general, the exponential moving average is preferred over the simple moving average because it gives a higher weight to recent prices and provides a clearer response to new information and trends.
