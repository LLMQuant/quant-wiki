# What Is a Long/Short Fund?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A long/short fund is a type of mutual fund, hedge fund, or exchange-traded fund (ETF) that takes both long and short investment positions. In simple terms, these funds establish long positions in stocks they expect to appreciate and short positions in stocks they expect to decline. These funds typically employ investment techniques pioneered by hedge funds -- leverage, derivatives, short positions -- that have since been adopted by mutual funds and ETFs. As the name implies, a majority of hedge funds fall into this category, along with 176 mutual funds and ETFs listed by the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA).[1] Long/short funds are also referred to as enhanced or 130/30 funds.

### Key Takeaways

- Long/short funds employ an investment strategy that takes long positions in undervalued stocks while short-selling overvalued ones.
- Long/short strategies go beyond traditional long-only investing by exploiting profit opportunities in securities perceived as both undervalued and overvalued.
- Long/short equity strategies are commonly used by hedge funds and often feature a 130/30 structure, where 130% of assets represent long exposure and 30% represent short exposure.
- Due to the significant active management, analysis, and trading these funds require, their expense ratios tend to be higher.

## Understanding Long/Short Funds

Long/short funds seek to enhance returns by investing in a specific market segment and simultaneously holding both long and short positions. Because they require active management, specialized expertise, and extensive analysis, these funds typically carry higher expense ratios. As of 2024, the average expense ratio for long/short funds listed in the FINRA database was 1.98%, compared with 0.42% for all equity mutual funds in 2023.[2]

Like their hedge fund counterparts, these mutual funds and ETFs employ similar strategies but with notable differences. They offer comparable portfolio construction, balancing higher risk with greater return potential, benchmarked against standard indices. Most long/short funds offer higher liquidity than long/short hedge funds, with no lock-up periods and relatively lower fees. However, their expense ratios remain higher than those of other public funds, and they tend to have lower liquidity. In addition, many require higher minimum investments and are among the 9% of mutual funds that charge front-end and back-end loads (commissions).[2]

Historically, mutual funds and ETFs -- particularly those employing long/short strategies -- have faced certain constraints on leverage and risk-taking. Dating back to the Great Depression era, these limitations were designed to protect ordinary investors who may not fully understand the complexities of these financial instruments. Although some rules have been relaxed in recent years, regulation remains stringent to shield public investors from undue risk.[4]

Long/short funds can be a good investment for those seeking targeted index exposure combined with some active management -- provided you understand the risks involved. These funds also offer the ability to hedge against market shifts and other trends, allowing skilled managers to make adjustments.

## The 130-30 Strategy

The most common long/short strategy involves 130% long and 30% short exposure relative to assets under management (130 - 30 = 100%).[5] For example, a fund manager might rank S&P 500 stocks by expected return, from best to worst.

The management team collects extensive data and applies quantitative rules to rank stocks. Selection criteria may include total return, risk-adjusted performance, or relative strength over a specific period -- six months, one year, or other time frames.

The manager then invests 100% in the top-ranked stocks and short-sells the bottom-ranked stocks up to 30% of portfolio value. The proceeds from short sales are reinvested in the top-ranked stocks, providing greater upside exposure.[5]

## Examples of Long/Short Funds

Let us examine two examples to illustrate how two funds in this category can allocate assets in very different ways.

The AQR Long-Short Equity Fund has been one of the better-performing long-term players in the long/short equity fund space. The fund invests across companies in multiple sectors globally. You can see the fund's percentage of long and short holdings in each sector.

As of Q1 2024, the fund had annualized total returns of 23.04%, 13.32%, and 10.6% over the past three, five, and ten years, respectively. Its annual expense ratio is 4.35%.[6]

The Invesco S&P 500 Downside Hedged ETF (PHDG) is an actively managed ETF designed to achieve positive returns in both bull and bear markets, regardless of stock and bond trends. This ETF contrasts sharply with QLEIX, demonstrating how long/short funds can take very different approaches to hedging downside risk.

While QLEIX generally shorts the same sectors in which it holds long positions, PHDG allocates its assets among the constituents of the S&P 500 Dynamic VEQTOR Index. This index includes equities representing the S&P 500 Index, volatility hedges representing the S&P 500 VIX Short-Term Futures Index, and cash.

Invesco states that its fund tracks the broader equity market's performance while providing a hedge against implied volatility. The fund adjusts its exposure based on S&P 500 equity and volatility levels. By April 2024, the fund's expense ratio was 0.39% -- relatively low for an actively managed fund, though it has index-fund characteristics, which typically carry even lower fees. Its three-, five-, and ten-year returns were 4.41%, 7.05%, and 4.69%, respectively.

## What Is the Difference Between Long and Short Investing?

Long investing involves purchasing a security with the expectation of selling it at a higher price in the future. Short investing involves borrowing shares from a broker, selling them, and then repurchasing them at a lower price to return to the broker -- the goal being to profit from a decline in the security's value.[7]

## What Other Investments Are Similar to Long/Short Funds?

In the broadest sense, options and other derivatives are commonly used to hedge the downside risk of equities. However, long/short funds incorporate such instruments into their performance optimization. Market-neutral funds seek to profit from price discrepancies between stocks while minimizing overall market exposure, carefully matching long and short positions to hedge broader market risk. Pairs trading is another related strategy that involves taking a long position in one stock while simultaneously shorting a closely related stock, aiming to profit from temporary pricing discrepancies between the two.[8]

## Why Is Short Selling Riskier Than Going Long?

Short selling is considered riskier primarily because there is no ceiling on how high a stock's price can rise. When you take a long position, the maximum loss is limited to 100%. With a short position, there is no upper bound.[5]

## Conclusion

Long/short funds do not simply invest in stocks deemed undervalued. What distinguishes these funds is that they also engage in short selling, profiting from price declines. The additional trading activity increases the potential for higher returns, but at a cost: long/short funds are generally riskier, more expensive, and less liquid than traditional mutual funds.
