# What Is Factor Investing?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Factor investing is a strategy that selects securities based on attributes associated with higher returns. Two main types of factors drive returns across stocks, bonds, and other assets: macroeconomic factors and style factors. Macroeconomic factors capture broad risks across asset classes, while style factors aim to explain returns and risk within an asset class.

Common macroeconomic factors include inflation rate, GDP growth, and unemployment rate. Microeconomic factors include a company's credit profile, share liquidity, and stock price volatility. Style factors encompass growth versus value, market capitalization, and industry sector.

### Key Takeaways

- Factor investing uses multiple factors -- including macroeconomic, fundamental, and statistical factors -- to analyze and explain asset prices and construct investment strategies.
- Factors identified by investors include growth vs. value, market capitalization, credit rating, and stock price volatility, among others.
- Smart beta is a common application of factor investing strategies.

## Understanding Factor Investing in Depth

From a theoretical standpoint, factor investing is designed to enhance portfolio diversification, generate above-market returns, and manage risk. Portfolio diversification has long been a popular tactic, but the benefits are diminished if the chosen securities move in lockstep with the broader market. For example, a stock-and-bond portfolio selected by an investor may both decline in value when certain market conditions arise. Factor investing can offset potential risks by targeting broad, persistent, and long-recognized drivers of returns.

Given that a traditional portfolio allocation such as 60% equities and 40% bonds is relatively easy to implement, factor investing can seem daunting due to the multitude of factors to choose from. Beginners can focus on simpler elements such as style (growth vs. value), size (large-cap vs. small-cap), and risk (beta) rather than complex attributes. These attributes are readily available for most securities and are detailed on popular stock research websites.

## Foundations of Factor Investing

The value factor aims to capture excess returns from stocks that are priced low relative to their fundamental value. This is typically tracked through metrics such as price-to-book, price-to-earnings, dividends, and free cash flow.[1]

Historically, portfolios of small-cap stocks have generated higher returns than portfolios containing only large-cap stocks. Investors can capture this size effect by observing a stock's market capitalization.[2]

Stocks that have performed well in the past tend to exhibit strong returns going forward. A momentum strategy is based on relative returns over a three-month to one-year time frame.[3]

The quality factor is primarily defined by low debt, stable earnings, and consistent asset growth. Investors can identify quality stocks using common financial metrics such as return on equity, debt-to-equity ratio, and earnings variability.[4]

Empirical research has shown that low-volatility stocks tend to earn greater risk-adjusted returns than highly volatile assets.[5] Measuring standard deviation over a one- to three-year period is a common method for capturing beta.

## Example: The Fama-French Three-Factor Model

A widely used multi-factor model is the Fama-French Three-Factor Model, which builds on the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM). Developed by economists Eugene Fama and Kenneth French, the model uses three factors: firm size, book-to-market value, and excess return on the market. In the model's terminology, the three factors are SMB (Small Minus Big), HML (High Minus Low), and the portfolio's return minus the risk-free rate. SMB accounts for small-capitalization companies that generate higher returns, while HML captures value stocks with high book-to-market ratios that tend to outperform the market.[6]
