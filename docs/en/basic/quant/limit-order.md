# What Is a Limit Order?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
In financial markets, a limit order is an instruction to buy or sell a stock or other security at a specified price or better. This mechanism gives traders greater control over the prices at which they execute trades. A limit order can be placed on either a buy or sell order:

- A buy limit order will only be executed at the limit price or lower.
- A sell limit order will only be executed at the limit price or higher.

While the price is guaranteed, execution is not. A limit order will only be filled when the market price meets the order's conditions.

The opposite of a limit order is a market order, which requests immediate execution at the current market price without any price restrictions.

### Key Takeaways

- A limit order ensures that an order is filled at or better than a specific price level.
- However, a limit order does not guarantee execution.
- Limit orders control the execution price but may result in missed opportunities in rapidly moving markets.
- Limit orders can be combined with stop orders to prevent large downside losses.
- Limit orders typically remain valid for a specified number of days (e.g., 30 days), until they are filled, or until the trader cancels them.

## How Limit Orders Work

A limit order uses a pre-set price to buy or sell a security. For example, if a trader wants to buy shares of XYZ but has a limit of $14.50, they will only purchase the stock at $14.50 or lower. If the trader wants to sell XYZ shares with a limit of $14.50, they will not sell until the price reaches $14.50 or higher.

By using a buy limit order, the investor is guaranteed to pay the limit price or better, though execution is not guaranteed. When a market is volatile, a limit order gives the trader more control over the execution price, particularly when they prefer not to use a market order.

Limit orders are especially useful when a stock is moving rapidly, as a trader may worry about receiving an unfavorable fill with a market order. They are also practical when a trader is not actively monitoring a stock, allowing them to set a specific buy or sell price. Limit orders can also be assigned an expiration date.

## Limit Order Example

A portfolio manager wants to buy shares of Tesla Inc. (TSLA) but believes the current valuation of approximately $750 is too high and wants to buy if the price falls to a certain level. The manager instructs a trader to buy 10,000 shares if the price drops below $650, good-till-cancelled (GTC). The trader then places a buy order with a $650 limit. If the stock falls below that threshold, the trader can begin purchasing. The order remains active until the stock reaches the manager's limit price or the manager cancels the order.

Additionally, the manager wants to sell shares of Amazon.com Inc. (AMZN) but believes the current price of approximately $2,300 is too low. The manager instructs the trader to sell 5,000 shares if the price rises above $2,750, GTC. The trader then places a sell order for 5,000 shares with a $2,750 limit.

**Note:** A brokerage may reject a limit order with illogical parameters (e.g., a buy limit set above the market price). Some brokerages offer this service at no additional charge.

## Limit Orders vs. Market Orders

When an investor places an order to buy or sell a stock, there are two primary execution options: "at market" or "at limit." A market order is designed to execute as quickly as possible at the current market price. A limit order, by contrast, sets the maximum or minimum price at which you are willing to buy or sell. Brokers typically charge higher commissions for more complex limit orders.

Buying stocks can be compared to buying a car. You can pay the dealer's sticker price and drive away, or you can negotiate and refuse the deal if the price is not met. The stock market works in a similar fashion.

A market order prioritizes execution speed; the security's price is secondary, and completing the trade quickly is what matters. A limit order prioritizes price; if the security's current value falls outside the limit order's parameters, the trade does not occur.

## What Is a Limit Order?

A limit order is an instruction to a broker to buy or sell a security at a specific price or better. It allows traders to execute trades at desired prices without constantly monitoring the market. It also serves as a risk management tool, helping to minimize losses by capturing sale prices at specific levels.

## How Does a Limit Order Work?

A limit order is submitted to a broker specifying the security, quantity, price, and whether you are buying or selling. The order is triggered only when the market price reaches the specified target. Even then, execution is not guaranteed in highly volatile markets, especially for illiquid securities.

## What Is the Difference Between a Limit Order and a Stop-Limit Order?

A limit order requests the purchase or sale of a security when a specific price is reached. A stop-limit order adds an additional layer by requiring a separate trigger price that differs from the sale price. For example, a limit order to sell a security at $15 would typically execute when the market price reaches $15. A stop-limit order, by contrast, could be set to sell only when the stock price drops from $20 to $16.

## How Long Does a Limit Order Last?

The duration of a limit order depends on the trader's specifications and the brokerage's policy. Many brokerages default limit orders to day orders: any unfilled orders at market close are cancelled without execution. Other brokerages offer custom durations, typically in 30-day intervals (e.g., 30, 60, or 90 days). Some brokerages offer good-till-cancelled (GTC) limit orders, which remain active until filled or explicitly cancelled by the trader.

## Why Was My Limit Order Not Filled?

A limit order may go unfilled for several reasons. First, the order triggers only when the market price reaches the buy or sell price you specified. If the security trades above your buy limit or below your sell limit, it will generally not be filled until the price moves in your direction.

A limit order can only be filled if there is sufficient liquidity. If there are not enough shares trading at your specified price, the order may not execute. This is most common with low-volume securities. Due to volatility, stocks on their IPO day may also face difficulty being filled because of rapid price swings.
