# What Is the Relative Strength Index (RSI)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a momentum indicator used in technical analysis. It measures the speed and magnitude of a security's recent price changes to evaluate whether the security is overvalued or undervalued.

The RSI is displayed as an oscillator (a line graph) with a range of 0 to 100. It was developed by J. Welles Wilder Jr. and first introduced in his seminal 1978 book, *New Concepts in Technical Trading Systems*.

The RSI can indicate securities that are overbought or oversold, and can also signal securities that may be primed for a trend reversal or corrective pullback. It can serve as a signal for buying and selling. Generally, an RSI reading of 70 or above indicates an overbought condition, while a reading of 30 or below indicates an oversold condition.

### Key Takeaways

- The Relative Strength Index (RSI) is a popular momentum oscillator introduced in 1978.
- The RSI provides technical traders with signals about bullish and bearish price momentum and is typically plotted beneath the chart of an asset's price.
- An asset is usually considered overbought when the RSI is above 70 and oversold when it is below 30.
- The RSI line crossing above or below the overbought or oversold line is often treated by traders as a signal to buy or sell.
- The RSI works best in trading ranges rather than trending markets.

## How the Relative Strength Index (RSI) Works

As a momentum indicator, the RSI compares a security's strength when prices are going up versus when prices are going down. Relating this comparison to price action can help traders anticipate how the security may perform. The RSI, used alongside other technical indicators, can help traders make more informed trading decisions.

The RSI is calculated in two steps, starting with the following formula:

$$ RSI_{\text{step one}} = 100- \left[ \frac{100}{ 1 + \frac{\text{Average Gain}}{\text{Average Loss} }} \right] $$

The average gain or loss used in this calculation is the average percentage gain or loss during the look-back period. The formula uses a positive value for average loss. Periods with price losses are counted as zero in the average gain calculation, and periods with price increases are counted as zero in the average loss calculation. The standard number of periods used to calculate the initial RSI value is 14. For example, imagine the market closed higher on 7 of the past 14 days with an initial average gain of 1%. The remaining 7 days all closed lower with an initial average loss of -0.8%.

The first calculation of the RSI would be as follows:

$$ 55.55 = 100 - \left [ \frac {100 }{ 1 + \frac{ \left ( \frac{ 1\% }{ 14 } \right) }{ \left( \frac{ 0.8\% }{ 14 } \right)} } \right ] $$

Once 14 periods of data are available, the second calculation can be performed. Its purpose is to smooth the results so that the RSI only approaches 100 or zero in a strongly trending market.

$$ RSI_{\text{step two}} = 100 - \left [ \frac{ 100 }{ 1 + \frac{ \left ( \text{Previous Average Gain} \times 13 \right ) \ + \ \text{Current Gain} }{ \left ( \left ( \text{Previous Average Loss} \times 13 \right ) \ + \ \text{Current Loss} \right ) } } \right ] $$

After calculating the RSI, the indicator can be plotted beneath the asset's price chart. The RSI will rise as the number and size of up days increase and will fall as the number and size of down days increase.

As the chart above illustrates, the RSI indicator can remain in the overbought territory for extended periods while the stock is in an uptrend. The indicator may also linger in oversold territory for a long time during a downtrend. This can confuse new analysts, but learning to use the indicator in the context of the prevailing trend will clarify these issues.

## Why Is the RSI Important?

- Traders can use the RSI to predict a security's price behavior.
- It can help traders validate trends and trend reversals.
- It can indicate overbought and oversold securities.
- It can provide short-term traders with buy and sell signals.
- It is a technical indicator that can be combined with other indicators to support trading strategies.

## Using RSI with Trends

To correctly interpret RSI readings, it is essential to understand the security's primary trend. For example, well-known market technician Constance Brown, CMT, has suggested that an oversold reading on the RSI during an uptrend is likely well above 30, while an overbought reading during a downtrend is likely much lower than 70.

In the following chart, notice that during a downtrend the RSI peaks near 50 rather than 70. Traders can view this as a more reliable indication of bearish conditions.

Many investors draw a horizontal trendline between 30 and 70 during strong trends to better identify the overall trend and extremes.

On the other hand, when a stock or asset price is in a long-term horizontal channel or trading range (rather than a strong up or downtrend), adjusting overbought or oversold RSI levels is generally unnecessary.

The RSI is less reliable in trending markets than in trading ranges. In fact, most traders recognize that the signals given by the RSI during strong uptrends or downtrends may often be false.

A related concept focuses on trading signals and techniques that conform to the trend. In other words, using bullish signals primarily in bullish trends and bearish signals primarily in bearish trends may help traders avoid the false alarms that the RSI can generate in trending markets.

## Overbought or Oversold

Generally, when the RSI crosses above 30 on the chart, it is a bullish signal, and when it crosses below 70, it is a bearish signal. Put another way, RSI values of 70 or above indicate that a security is becoming overbought or overvalued and may be primed for a trend reversal or corrective pullback. An RSI reading below 30 indicates an oversold or undervalued condition.

Overbought generally describes a security trading at a price above its true (or intrinsic) value. This means it is priced beyond what technical or fundamental analysis would consider reasonable. Traders who see signs that a security is overbought may expect a price correction or trend reversal and therefore sell the security.

The same logic applies in reverse for a security whose technical indicators such as the RSI highlight it as oversold. It can be seen as trading at a level lower than it should be. Traders watching for this signal may expect a price correction or trend reversal and buy the security.

## Interpreting RSI and RSI Ranges

During trends, RSI readings may fall into a band or range. During an uptrend, the RSI tends to stay above 30 and should frequently reach 70. In a downtrend, it is rare for the RSI to exceed 70; instead, the indicator frequently hits 30 or below.

These guidelines can help traders determine trend strength and spot potential reversals. For example, if the RSI fails to reach 70 over several consecutive price swings during an uptrend and then drops below 30, the trend has weakened and may be reversing to the downside.

The reverse is true for a downtrend. If a downtrend is unable to reach 30 or below and then rallies above 70, that downtrend has weakened and may be reversing to the upside. Trend lines and moving averages are helpful technical tools when using the RSI in this manner.

**Tip:** Be careful not to confuse RSI with relative strength. The former refers to a single security's momentum changes, while the latter compares the price performance of two or more securities.

## Example of RSI Divergence

An RSI divergence occurs when the price moves in the opposite direction of the RSI. In other words, a chart may display a change in momentum before a corresponding change in price occurs.

A bullish divergence occurs when the RSI displays an oversold reading followed by a higher low that corresponds with lower lows in the price. This may indicate rising bullish momentum, and a break above oversold territory could be used to trigger a new long position.

A bearish divergence occurs when the RSI creates an overbought reading followed by a lower high that corresponds with higher highs in the price.

This is a valid signal, but divergences can be rare when a stock is in a stable long-term trend. Using flexible overbought or oversold readings helps identify more potential signals.

## Example of Positive and Negative RSI Reversals

Another price-RSI relationship that traders look for is positive and negative RSI reversals. A positive RSI reversal may occur when the RSI reaches a lower low than its previous low at the same time that the security's price reaches a higher low. Traders would view this pattern as a bullish signal and a buy signal.

Conversely, a negative RSI reversal may occur when the RSI reaches a higher high than its previous high at the same time that the security's price reaches a lower high. This pattern would be viewed as a bearish signal and a sell signal.

## Difference Between RSI and MACD

The moving average convergence divergence (MACD) is another trend-following momentum indicator that shows the relationship between two moving averages of a security's price. The MACD is calculated by subtracting the 26-period EMA from the 12-period EMA. This result is the MACD line.

A nine-day EMA of the MACD, called the signal line, is then plotted on top of the MACD line. It can serve as a trigger for buy and sell signals. Traders may buy the security when the MACD crosses above its signal line and sell or short it when the MACD crosses below the signal line.

The RSI is designed to indicate whether a security is overbought or oversold relative to recent price levels. It is calculated using average price gains and losses over a given period of time, with a default period of 14 and values ranging from 0 to 100.

The MACD measures the relationship between two EMAs, while the RSI measures price change momentum relative to recent price highs and lows. These two indicators are often used together to give analysts a more complete technical picture of the market.

Both indicators measure momentum of an asset. However, they measure different factors, so they can sometimes give contradictory signals. For example, the RSI may show a reading above 70 for a sustained period, indicating the security is overextended on the buy side.

At the same time, the MACD could indicate that buying momentum is still increasing for the security. Either indicator may signal an upcoming trend change by showing divergence from price (price continues higher while the indicator turns lower, or vice versa).

## Limitations of the RSI

The RSI compares bullish and bearish price momentum and displays the result in an oscillator beneath the price chart. Like most technical indicators, its signals are most reliable when they conform to the long-term trend.

True reversal signals are rare and can be difficult to distinguish from false alarms. A false positive, for example, would be a bullish crossover followed by a sudden decline in a stock. A false negative would be a situation where there is a bearish crossover, yet the stock suddenly accelerates to the upside.

Since the indicator displays momentum, it can remain overbought or oversold for extended periods when an asset has significant momentum in either direction. Therefore, the RSI is most useful in an oscillating market (trading range) where the asset price alternates between bullish and bearish moves.

## What Does the RSI Represent?

The Relative Strength Index (RSI) measures the price momentum of a stock or other security. The basic idea behind the RSI is to measure how quickly traders are bidding the price of the security up or down. The RSI plots this result on a scale of 0 to 100.

Readings below 30 generally indicate that the stock is oversold, while readings above 70 indicate it is overbought. Traders often place the RSI chart beneath the security's price chart so they can compare its recent momentum against the market price.

## Should I Buy When the RSI Is Low?

Some traders consider it a buy signal if a security's RSI reading falls below 30. This is based on the idea that the security has been oversold and is therefore poised for a bounce. However, the reliability of this signal depends in part on the overall context. If the security is caught in a significant downtrend, it might continue trading at an oversold level for quite some time. In that situation, traders might delay buying until they see other technical indicators confirm the buy signal.

## What Happens When the RSI Is High?

Because the RSI is primarily used to determine whether a security is overbought or oversold, a high RSI reading can mean the security is overbought and the price may decline. Therefore, it could be a signal to sell the security.

## What Is the Difference Between RSI and Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)?

RSI and MACD are both momentum measurement tools that can help traders understand a security's recent trading activity. However, they achieve this goal in different ways.

In short, MACD works by smoothing the security's recent price movements and comparing that medium-term trend line to a shorter-term trend line showing its more recent price changes. Traders can then base their buy and sell decisions on whether the short-term trend line is rising or falling relative to the medium-term trend line.
