# What Is Beta?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Beta (β) is the second letter of the Greek alphabet and, in finance, measures the volatility or systematic risk of a security or portfolio relative to the broader market. The S&P 500 Index is commonly used as the benchmark, with a beta of 1.0. Stocks with a beta above 1.0 are considered more volatile than the S&P 500.

### Key Takeaways

- Beta (β) compares the volatility or systematic risk of a stock or portfolio to the overall market.
- Beta gives investors an approximation of how much risk a stock adds to a portfolio.
- The S&P 500 Index has a beta of 1.0.

## How Beta Works

The beta coefficient captures the volatility of an individual stock relative to the systematic risk of the entire market. Statistically, beta represents the slope of a regression line fitted through data points, where each point plots the return of an individual stock against the return of the market.

Beta effectively describes how a security's returns behave in response to market swings. It is used in the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), which establishes the relationship between systematic risk and expected return for an asset. CAPM is widely employed to price risky securities and to estimate expected returns given an asset's risk profile and cost of capital.

## Calculating Beta

A security's beta is calculated by dividing the covariance of the security's returns with market returns by the variance of market returns over a specified period. This calculation helps investors understand whether a stock tends to move in the same direction as the rest of the market, and provides insight into how volatile the stock is relative to the market.

For beta to yield useful insights, the market benchmark should be relevant to the stock in question. For example, computing the beta of a bond ETF against the S&P 500 is of little value because bonds and equities are fundamentally different asset classes.

$$ \begin{aligned}
&\text{Beta}(\beta) = \frac{\text{Covariance}(R_e, R_m)}{\text{Variance}(R_m)} \\
&\textbf{where:}\\
&R_e=\text{return on an individual stock}\\
&R_m=\text{return on the overall market}\\
&\text{Covariance}=\text{relationship between changes in a stock's returns and changes in market returns} \\
&\text{Variance}=\text{how far the market's data points spread from their average value} \\
\end{aligned} $$

## Interpreting Beta Values

- **Beta equal to 1:** A beta of 1.0 indicates that the stock's price activity is strongly correlated with the market. Adding a stock with a beta of 1.0 to a portfolio neither increases risk nor enhances the likelihood of excess returns.
- **Beta less than 1:** A beta below 1.0 means the security is theoretically less volatile than the market. Including such a stock makes the portfolio less risky than the same portfolio without it. Utility stocks typically carry lower betas because they tend to move more slowly than the market average.
- **Beta greater than 1:** A beta above 1.0 indicates the security's price is theoretically more volatile than the market. A stock with a beta of 1.2, for instance, is assumed to be 20% more volatile than the market. Technology stocks tend to have higher betas. Adding such a stock increases portfolio risk but may also boost returns.
- **Negative beta:** A beta of -1.0 means the stock is inversely correlated with the market benchmark on a 1:1 basis. Put options and inverse ETFs are designed to have negative betas. Certain sectors, such as gold mining, also commonly exhibit negative betas.

## How Investors Use Beta

Investors use beta to gauge how much risk a stock adds to their portfolio. A stock that deviates little from the market will not add much risk, but it also will not improve the portfolio's potential for higher returns.

Investors should compare a specific stock to an appropriate benchmark and examine the R-squared value against that benchmark. R-squared is a statistical measure that compares a security's historical price movements to the benchmark index. A security with a high R-squared value indicates a relevant benchmark. For example, a gold exchange-traded fund (ETF) such as SPDR Gold Shares (GLD) is tied to the performance of gold bullion. [1] Consequently, the beta and R-squared relationship between a gold ETF and the S&P 500 would be low.

Investors typically evaluate two categories of risk. Systematic risk is the risk of the entire market declining, also known as undiversifiable risk. Unsystematic risk, or diversifiable risk, is the uncertainty associated with an individual stock or sector. It can be mitigated through diversification.

**Tip:** A stock's beta changes over time because it links the stock's performance to overall market returns.

## Theory vs. Practice

Beta coefficient theory assumes that stock returns are normally distributed from a statistical perspective. In reality, returns are not always normally distributed. Therefore, what a stock's beta predicts about its future movements may not always hold true.

A stock with a very low beta could still have narrow price swings yet be locked in a long-term downtrend. Adding a downtrending low-beta stock only reduces portfolio risk if the investor defines risk strictly in terms of volatility rather than potential for loss.

Similarly, a high-beta stock trending primarily upward will increase portfolio risk but may also enhance gains. Investors should evaluate stocks from other perspectives, such as fundamental or technical factors, before assuming a stock will add or remove risk from a portfolio.

## Is Beta Useful for Long-Term Investing?

While beta provides valuable information when evaluating a stock, it has notable limitations. Beta can assess a security's short-term risk and analyze volatility. However, because beta is calculated using historical data points, it becomes less meaningful for investors seeking to predict a stock's long-term trajectory. A stock's volatility can change significantly over time depending on the company's growth stage and other factors.

## Is Beta a Good Measure of Risk?

Beta provides some risk information, but it is not a comprehensive risk measure. Beta only examines a stock's past performance relative to the S&P 500 and does not forecast future movements. It also does not account for a company's fundamentals or its earnings and growth potential.

## How Do Investors Interpret a Stock's Beta?

A stock with a beta of 1.0 is as volatile as the broader market. If the index rises or falls by 1%, the stock will, on average, do the same. A beta greater than 1.0 indicates higher volatility -- if the beta is 1.5 and the index moves 1%, the stock would move 1.5% on average. A beta below 1.0 signals lower volatility: a stock with a beta of 0.5 would rise or fall only half a percentage point when the index moves 1%.

## The Bottom Line

Beta (β) measures the volatility of a security or portfolio relative to the S&P 500, which carries a beta of 1.0. A beta of 1.0 means the stock is as volatile as the broader market. A beta above 1.0 signals greater volatility, while a beta below 1.0 indicates less volatility.
