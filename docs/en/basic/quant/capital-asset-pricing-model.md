# What Is the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) describes the relationship between systematic risk -- the general risk inherent in investing -- and the expected return of an asset, particularly equities. It is a financial model that establishes a linear relationship between the required return on an investment and its risk.

CAPM is built on the relationship among an asset's beta, the risk-free rate (typically the Treasury yield), and the equity risk premium (the expected market return minus the risk-free rate).

CAPM was developed to measure systematic risk. It is widely used in finance to price risky securities and to estimate expected returns for assets, taking into account both risk and cost of capital.

### Key Takeaways

- The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) is a financial model that calculates the expected rate of return for an asset or investment.
- CAPM achieves this by using the expected returns of both the market and a risk-free asset, along with the asset's correlation with the market (beta).
- CAPM has several limitations, including unrealistic assumptions and a reliance on a linear interpretation of risk and return.
- Despite these shortcomings, the CAPM formula remains widely used because it is simple and allows easy comparison of investment alternatives.
- For example, CAPM is often used alongside Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) to understand portfolio risk and expected return.

## The CAPM Formula

The formula for calculating the expected return of an asset given its risk is:

$$ \begin{aligned} &ER_i = R_f + \beta_i ( ER_m - R_f ) \\ &\textbf{where:} \\ &ER_i = \text{expected return of the investment} \\ &R_f = \text{risk-free rate} \\ &\beta_i = \text{beta of the investment} \\ &(ER_m - R_f) = \text{market risk premium} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Investors expect compensation for both risk and the time value of money. The risk-free rate in the CAPM formula accounts for the time value of money, while the remaining components compensate the investor for taking on additional risk.

The goal of the CAPM formula is to evaluate whether a stock is fairly valued when its risk and the time value of money are compared to its expected return. In other words, by understanding each component of CAPM, an investor can assess whether a stock's current price is consistent with its likely return.

**Important:** CAPM is designed to determine whether a stock's current price is consistent with its expected return.

## CAPM and Beta

Beta compares the volatility or systematic risk of a security or portfolio to the market as a whole. If a stock's risk exceeds that of the market, its beta will be greater than 1. If its beta is less than 1, the formula assumes the stock will reduce portfolio risk.

A stock's beta is then multiplied by the market risk premium -- the return expected from the market above the risk-free rate. The risk-free rate is subsequently added to the product of the stock's beta and the market risk premium. The result provides the investor with the required rate of return or discount rate needed to determine the asset's value.

## CAPM Example

Suppose an investor is evaluating a stock currently priced at $100 per share that pays a 3% annual dividend. The stock has a beta of 1.3 relative to the market, meaning it is more volatile than a broad market portfolio (e.g., the S&P 500). Assume the risk-free rate is 3% and the investor expects the market to appreciate by 8% annually.

Based on the CAPM formula, the expected return on the stock is 9.5%:

$$ \begin{aligned} &9.5\% = 3\% + 1.3 \times ( 8\% - 3\% ) \\ \end{aligned} $$

The CAPM-derived expected return is used to discount future dividends and capital appreciation over the expected holding period. If the discounted value of those future cash flows equals $100, the CAPM formula suggests the stock is fairly valued relative to its risk.

Consequently, whether CAPM truly works remains an open question. The central issue lies with beta. When professors Eugene Fama and Kenneth French examined stock returns on the NYSE, AMEX, and Nasdaq, they found that differences in beta over long periods failed to explain the performance of different stocks. The linear relationship between beta and individual stock returns also broke down over shorter periods. These findings suggest that CAPM may be flawed.

The assumption embedded in beta is that risk can be measured by a stock's price volatility. However, upward and downward price movements do not carry equivalent risk. The look-back period used to determine volatility is not standardized because stock returns -- and risk -- are not normally distributed.

CAPM also assumes the risk-free rate remains constant throughout the discounting period. If, in the example above, the Treasury yield rises to 5% or 6% during a 10-year holding period, the higher risk-free rate would increase the cost of capital and could make the stock appear overvalued.

**1960s:** The decade during which CAPM was conceptualized.

The market portfolio used to find the market risk premium is a theoretical value and cannot be purchased or invested in as an alternative to the stock. In most cases, investors use a major stock index such as the S&P 500 as a proxy for the market -- an imperfect comparison.

The most serious criticism of CAPM is the assumption that future cash flows can be estimated for the discounting process. If an investor could estimate a stock's future return with high accuracy, CAPM would be unnecessary.

## CAPM and the Efficient Frontier

Using CAPM to construct a portfolio is intended to help investors manage risk. If an investor could perfectly optimize a portfolio's return relative to risk using CAPM, the portfolio would sit on a curve known as the efficient frontier.

The graph illustrates that higher expected returns (vertical axis) require higher expected risk (horizontal axis). Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT) suggests that starting from the risk-free rate, a portfolio's expected return increases as risk rises. Any portfolio that falls on the Capital Market Line (CML) is superior to any portfolio to the right of that line, though in some cases a theoretical portfolio on the CML can be constructed to deliver the best return for the risk assumed.

The CML and efficient frontier may be difficult to define precisely, but they illustrate an important concept for investors: there is a trade-off between higher returns and higher risk. Because it is impossible to perfectly construct a CML portfolio, investors pursuing higher returns often take on disproportionate risk.

Portfolio B is expected to return 10% per year with a standard deviation of 16%. Portfolio B's risk rises faster than its expected return.

## CAPM and the Security Market Line (SML)

The efficient frontier shares the same assumptions as CAPM and can only be calculated theoretically. If a portfolio exists on the efficient frontier, it provides the maximum return for its level of risk. However, it is impossible to know whether a portfolio sits on the efficient frontier because future returns cannot be predicted.

The trade-off between risk and return applies to CAPM, and the efficient frontier graph can be rearranged to illustrate the trade-off for individual assets. In such a graph, the CML is referred to as the Security Market Line (SML). Instead of expected risk on the horizontal axis, a stock's beta is used. As shown, as beta increases from 1 to 2, expected return rises accordingly.

CAPM and the SML establish a link between a stock's beta and its expected risk. Beta is derived from statistical analysis comparing an individual stock's daily returns to the market's daily returns over the same period. A higher beta implies more risk, but a portfolio of high-beta stocks could theoretically exist on the CML where the trade-off is acceptable, even if not theoretically ideal.

The practical value of both models is diminished by assumptions about beta and market participants that diverge from real-world conditions. For instance, beta does not account for the relative riskiness of a stock that experiences frequent sharp declines compared to another stock with equally high beta but without the same pattern of downside moves.

## Practical Value of CAPM

Given the criticisms of CAPM and the assumptions behind its use in portfolio construction, it may be difficult to see how it can be useful. Nevertheless, CAPM remains a valuable tool for evaluating whether future expectations are reasonable or for making comparisons.

Suppose an advisor proposes adding a stock trading at $100 per share to a portfolio. The advisor uses CAPM with a 13% discount rate to justify the price. An investment manager can compare this assumption against the company's historical performance and its peers. If the peer group has returned about 10% and the stock has consistently delivered 9%, the manager should not accept the advisor's recommendation without a compelling justification.

**Tip:** CAPM can help generate ideas and reassess holdings, but it should not be the sole method for valuing a stock.

Investors can also use the concepts of CAPM and the efficient frontier to evaluate their portfolio's -- or individual stocks' -- performance relative to the rest of the market. For example, suppose a portfolio has returned 10% annually over the past three years with a standard deviation (risk) of 10%, while the market averaged 10% with a risk of 8%.

The investor could use this observation to reassess portfolio construction and identify which holdings may not lie on the SML. This could explain why the portfolio sits to the right of the CML. If holdings that drag on returns or disproportionately increase risk can be identified, the investor can take steps to improve performance.

Unsurprisingly, CAPM has fueled the adoption of index investing among risk-averse investors -- constructing a portfolio that mimics a particular market or asset class. This is largely because CAPM conveys the message that earning returns above the market requires taking on higher risk (beta).

## Who Developed CAPM?

The Capital Asset Pricing Model was developed by financial economists William Sharpe, Jack Treynor, John Lintner, and Jan Mossin in the early 1960s, building on the foundational work of Harry Markowitz in the 1950s.

## What Are Some Assumptions of CAPM?

CAPM's assumptions include:

- All investors are inherently risk-averse.
- Investors have the same time horizon for evaluating information.
- Capital can be borrowed without limit at the risk-free rate.
- Investments can be divided into unlimited fractional amounts.
- There are no taxes, inflation, or transaction costs.
- Risk and return are linearly related.

Many of these assumptions have been challenged as unrealistic or outright incorrect.

## What Are Some Alternatives to CAPM?

Several alternative models have been developed to address CAPM's shortcomings. One is the Arbitrage Pricing Theory (APT), which considers multiple factors categorized as macroeconomic or company-specific. Another is the Fama-French Three-Factor Model, which expands CAPM by adding company size risk and value risk factors to the market risk factor.

In 2015, Fama and French adapted their model to include five factors. In addition to the original three, the new model introduces "profitability" -- the concept that companies reporting higher future earnings tend to achieve higher stock market returns. The fifth factor, "investment," relates to internal investment and returns, suggesting that companies directing profits toward major growth projects may face underperformance in the stock market.

## What Is the International Capital Asset Pricing Model (ICAPM)?

The International Capital Asset Pricing Model (ICAPM) is a financial model that extends traditional CAPM principles to international investments. It broadens the CAPM framework by accounting for direct and indirect exposure to foreign currencies, in addition to the time value of money and market risk.

## Conclusion

CAPM uses the principles of Modern Portfolio Theory to determine whether a security is fairly valued. It relies on assumptions about investor behavior, risk and return distributions, and market fundamentals that do not perfectly align with reality.

Nevertheless, the core concepts behind CAPM and the associated efficient frontier can help investors understand the relationship between expected risk and return, enabling more informed decisions when adding securities to a portfolio.
