# What Is a Volatility Smile?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A volatility smile is a common graphical pattern that results from plotting the strike prices and implied volatility of a group of options with the same underlying asset and expiration date. The volatility smile gets its name because it resembles a smiling face. Implied volatility rises when the underlying asset of an option is further out-of-the-money (OTM) or in-the-money (ITM), compared to at-the-money (ATM) options, which tend to have the lowest implied volatility. Not all options exhibit a volatility smile.

### Key Takeaways

- When options with the same expiration date and underlying asset but different strike prices are graphed for implied volatility, the pattern tends to form a smile shape.
- The smile shows that ITM and OTM options have the highest implied volatility.
- Options with the lowest implied volatility typically have strike prices at or near the money.
- Not all options display an implied volatility smile. Near-term equity options and currency-related options are more likely to exhibit the pattern.
- A single option's implied volatility may also follow the volatility smile pattern as it moves deeper ITM or OTM.
- While implied volatility is a factor in option pricing, it is not the only one. Traders must also be aware of other factors that influence option prices and volatility.

## What Does the Volatility Smile Tell You?

The volatility smile is created by implied volatility changing as the underlying asset moves further ITM or OTM. The more an option is ITM or OTM, the greater its implied volatility tends to be, while ATM options tend to have the lowest implied volatility.

The volatility smile is not predicted by the Black-Scholes model, one of the principal formulas used for pricing options and other derivatives. The Black-Scholes model predicts that the implied volatility curve should be flat when plotted against varying strike prices. According to the model, implied volatility should be the same for all options expiring on the same date with the same underlying asset, regardless of strike price. However, in reality, this is not the case.

Volatility smiles began to appear in option pricing after the 1987 stock market crash. They were not present in U.S. markets prior to this event, which suggests the market structure was more consistent with the predictions of the Black-Scholes model. After 1987, traders realized that extreme events could occur and that markets exhibit significant skewness. Consequently, implied volatility in practice increases or decreases as options move further ITM or OTM.

Furthermore, the existence of the volatility smile indicates that ITM and OTM options tend to have greater demand than ATM options. Demand drives prices, which in turn affects implied volatility. This can be partly attributed to the reasons mentioned above. Extreme events can cause significant price swings in options, and the probability of large moves is factored into implied volatility calculations.

## Example of Using the Volatility Smile

A volatility smile can be observed when comparing various options with the same underlying asset and expiration date but different strike prices. If implied volatility is plotted for each strike price, a U-shape may emerge. The U-shape is not always as perfectly formed as in the textbook illustration.

For a rough estimate of whether an option displays a U-shaped curve, you can consult an options chain listing the implied volatilities for each strike price. If the option exhibits a U-shape, ITM and OTM options should have roughly equal implied volatilities. The further the strike price is from ATM, the higher the implied volatility, while ATM options should have the lowest implied volatility. If this pattern does not hold, the option does not conform to a volatility smile.

A single option's implied volatility can also be plotted over time as the underlying asset's price fluctuates. As the price moves further ITM or OTM, implied volatility may take on a U-shaped pattern.

This can be useful when seeking options with lower implied volatility. In that case, select an option near the money. For higher implied volatility, choose an option that is deeper ITM or OTM. Keep in mind, however, that as the underlying asset price approaches or moves away from the strike price, implied volatility will be affected. Maintaining a portfolio of options at a specific implied volatility level therefore requires continuous rebalancing.

Not all options exhibit a volatility smile. Before using the volatility smile to aid in trading decisions, always verify that the option's implied volatility actually follows this pattern.

## Difference Between Volatility Smile and Volatility Skew

While near-term equity options and forex options tend to align more closely with the volatility smile, index options and longer-term equity options tend to align more with a volatility skew. The skew indicates that implied volatility may be higher for either ITM or OTM options.

## Limitations of Using the Volatility Smile

First, it is critical to determine whether the options being traded actually conform to a volatility smile. The volatility smile is a model that some options may fit, but implied volatility may equally conform to a reverse or forward skew instead.

Moreover, due to other market factors such as supply and demand, the volatility smile (if applicable) may not be a clean U-shape. It may have a basic U-shape but could be quite irregular, with certain options showing higher or lower implied volatility than the model would predict.

The volatility smile highlights the general direction traders should look when seeking higher or lower implied volatility, but many additional factors must be considered when making option trading decisions.
