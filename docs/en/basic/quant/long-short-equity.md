# What Is Long/Short Equity?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Long/short equity is an investment strategy that takes long positions in stocks expected to appreciate and short positions in stocks expected to decline. The strategy seeks to capture gains from rising long positions while reducing market risk and profiting from price declines in short positions. Although this is not always the case, the strategy should generally produce a net profit.

Long/short equity is a popular liquid alternative strategy favored by hedge funds, many of which employ a market-neutral approach in which the dollar amounts of long and short positions are roughly equal.

### Key Takeaways

- Long/short equity is an investment strategy that takes long positions in undervalued stocks while short-selling overvalued ones.
- Long/short equity seeks to augment traditional long-only investing by exploiting profit opportunities from securities identified as both undervalued and overvalued.
- Long/short equity is commonly used by hedge funds, which often adopt a relative long bias -- for example, a 130/30 strategy with 130% long exposure and 30% short exposure.

## How Long/Short Equity Works

Long/short equity works by exploiting profit opportunities in both the upside and downside of anticipated price movements. The strategy identifies and takes long positions in stocks deemed relatively undervalued while shorting stocks deemed overvalued.

While many hedge funds employ long/short equity strategies with a long bias (e.g., 130/30, where long exposure is 130% and short exposure is 30%), relatively few adopt a short bias. Historically, generating profitable short ideas has been more challenging than finding long opportunities.

Long/short equity strategies can be differentiated along several dimensions -- by market geography (developed economies, emerging markets, Europe, etc.), sector (energy, technology, etc.), investment philosophy (value or growth), and more.

An example of a broad-mandate long/short equity strategy might be a global equity growth fund, while a narrower mandate could be an emerging-market healthcare fund.

## Long/Short Equity vs. Equity Market Neutral

The key difference between a long/short equity fund and an equity market neutral (EMN) fund is that the latter seeks to exploit stock price discrepancies by taking matched long and short positions in stocks with similar characteristics.

An EMN strategy attempts to keep the total value of its long and short holdings roughly equal, which helps reduce overall risk. To maintain this equivalence, equity market neutral funds must rebalance as market trends establish themselves and strengthen.

Consequently, while other long/short hedge funds let profits run with the trend and even leverage up, equity market neutral funds actively trim gains and increase the size of opposing positions. When the market inevitably reverses, equity market neutral funds again pare back profitable positions and shift toward the portfolio that had been losing value.

A hedge fund employing an equity market neutral strategy is typically aimed at institutional investors seeking a fund that can outperform bonds without carrying the high-risk, high-return profile of more aggressive funds.

## Long/Short Equity Example: Pairs Trading

A popular variation of the long/short model is "pairs trading," which involves offsetting a long position in one stock with a short position in another stock within the same sector.

For example, an investor in the technology sector might take a long position in Microsoft while offsetting it with a short position in Intel. If the investor purchases 1,000 shares of Microsoft at $33 per share and Intel is trading at $22, the short leg of this pairs trade would involve purchasing 1,500 shares of Intel so that the dollar amounts of the long and short positions are equal.

The ideal scenario for this long/short strategy would be for Microsoft to appreciate and Intel to decline. If Microsoft rises to $35 and Intel falls to $21, the overall profit on the strategy would be $3,500. Even if Intel rises to $23 -- since the factors that push stocks in a given sector up or down tend to be similar -- the strategy would still generate a $500 profit, albeit a smaller one.

To address the fact that stocks within the same sector tend to move in tandem, long/short strategies often use different sectors for their long and short legs. For example, if interest rates are rising, a hedge fund might short rate-sensitive sectors such as utilities while going long on defensive sectors such as healthcare.
