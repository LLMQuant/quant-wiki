# What Is a Straddle?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A straddle is a neutral options strategy that involves simultaneously purchasing a put option (the first leg) and a call option (the second leg) on the same underlying security, with the same strike price and expiration date.

A trader profits from a long straddle when the underlying security's price moves above or below the strike price by more than the total premium paid. On the call side, profit potential is virtually unlimited as long as the underlying moves sharply. On the put side, profit is capped at the difference between the strike price and zero, minus the premium paid.

### Key Takeaways

- A straddle involves simultaneously buying a put and a call option.
- Both options share the same expiration date and strike price on the same underlying security.
- The strategy is profitable only if the stock price rises or falls beyond the total premium paid.
- A straddle can indicate the market's expected volatility and trading range through expiration.
- The strategy is most effective when targeting high-volatility investments. Without significant price movement, the premiums paid on both options can easily exceed any potential profit.

## Understanding a Straddle

In finance, a straddle refers to two offsetting transactions on the same underlying security. Investors typically employ a straddle when they expect a stock's price to move significantly but are uncertain about the direction.

A straddle provides traders with two key insights: the level of volatility the market expects from the security, and the expected trading range through expiration.

## How to Create a Straddle

To determine the cost of a straddle, a trader must add the prices of the put and call options. Suppose a trader believes a stock may rise or fall from its current price of $55 following an earnings report on March 1. The trader can create a straddle by purchasing a put and a call at the $55 strike price, both expiring on March 15.

To determine the cost, the trader adds the price of the March 15 $55 call and the March 15 $55 put. If each contract trades at $2.50, the total premium is $5.00 x 100 = $500 (each contract covers 100 shares).

The premium implies that the stock must rise or fall 9% from the $55 strike price to be profitable by March 15. That breakeven percentage is calculated by dividing $5 by $55, yielding approximately 9%.

Options pricing also implies the expected trading range. A trader can add or subtract the straddle premium from the stock price to determine the range. In this case, the $5 premium added to $55 produces an expected range of $50 to $60.

If the stock trades between $50 and $60, the trader loses some but not all of the investment. If the stock drops to $48 at expiration, the call expires worthless but the put is worth $7, yielding a $2 profit. If the stock rises to $57, the call is worth $2 and the put is worthless, resulting in a $3 loss.

**Important:** The worst-case scenario is for the stock price to remain at or near the strike price.

## Pros and Cons of a Straddle

Straddles are designed to capture upside or downside gains. Consider a stock trading at $300. A trader pays a $10 premium to buy a call and a put, both at a $300 strike. If the stock moves up, the call generates profit. If it moves down, the put generates profit. Either way, the straddle can produce a gain regardless of direction.

Straddles are often deployed before major corporate events such as earnings reports. When the outcome is uncertain, investors may use a straddle as a hedging position to mitigate risk, allowing them to establish a position ahead of a potentially significant move.

The stock price must move by more than the premium paid for the straddle to be profitable. In the example above, if the stock only moves from $300 to $315, the net position results in a loss. Straddle profits typically require a substantial price swing.

Another drawback involves the guaranteed loss on one side. Depending on the direction of the move, one of the two options will inevitably expire worthless. This is particularly evident in low-volatility stocks where neither option may generate a profit. This "loss" may be compounded by higher transaction costs associated with opening multiple positions compared to a single directional trade.

Straddles are best suited for high-volatility periods and are not effective in all market environments. In stable conditions, they tend to underperform. The strategy works better for certain investments and is not ideal for low-beta securities.

### Pros

- The strategy can profit regardless of whether the underlying security rises or falls.
- It can be useful when significant news is expected but the market's reaction is uncertain.
- Investors can hedge their exposure rather than relying on a single directional bet.

### Cons

- The underlying security must exhibit volatility; without significant price movement, a straddle position is typically unprofitable.
- The investor will inevitably pay a premium for one option that never gets exercised.
- The strategy is not suitable for all market conditions or all types of securities, as it depends on volatility.

## Straddle Example

On June 18, options market activity implied that XYZ Company's stock could rise or fall 20% from its $26 strike price by the July 16 expiration, given a combined premium of $5.10 for a put and a call.

This placed the stock in a trading range of $20.90 to $31.15. A week later, on June 25, the company reported earnings and the stock plunged from $22.70 to $19.27.

In this case, the trader would have profited because the stock fell outside the range, exceeding the combined premium cost.

## What Is a Long Straddle?

A long straddle is an options strategy investors use when they anticipate volatility in a stock. The investor believes the stock will make a significant move beyond the trading range but is uncertain about the direction.

To execute a long straddle, the investor simultaneously buys an at-the-money call and an at-the-money put with the same expiration date and strike price. In many cases, the investor expects that an upcoming event -- such as an earnings release or acquisition announcement -- will shift the underlying stock from low to high volatility.

The goal is to profit from a large price movement. A small price shift is typically insufficient for a long straddle to be profitable.

## How Do You Profit from a Straddle?

To determine how much the underlying security must move for a straddle to be profitable, divide the total premium cost by the strike price. If the total premium is $10 and the strike price is $100, the calculation is $10 / $100 = 10%. The underlying security must rise or fall more than 10% from the $100 strike to generate a profit.

## Can You Lose Money on a Straddle?

Yes. If the stock price does not move significantly beyond the cost of the combined premiums, the trader faces a loss. This is precisely why straddle strategies are typically reserved for high-volatility investments.

## Conclusion

When an investor simultaneously buys a call and a put with the same expiration date and the same strike price, they have entered a straddle position. This strategy allows the investor to profit from a large price movement regardless of direction.

However, if the underlying security's price remains relatively stable, the investor stands to lose the premiums paid for the worthless options. On the other hand, if the stock makes a significant move in either direction, the investor can still profit.
