# What Is Market Neutral?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A market-neutral strategy is an investment approach in which an investor or investment manager seeks to profit from both rising and falling prices across one or more markets while attempting to completely avoid some specific form of market risk.

Market-neutral strategies are typically achieved by taking matching long and short positions in different stocks to increase returns from strong stock selection while reducing returns attributable to broad market movements.

### Key Takeaways

- Market neutral refers to an investment strategy employed by investment managers that seeks to profit from both rising and falling prices in financial markets.
- The investment selections in a market-neutral strategy are designed to avoid significant losses, as long and short positions serve as hedges against each other.
- Hedge funds commonly use market-neutral strategies because their investment objective is absolute return rather than relative return.
- The two main market-neutral strategies fund managers employ are fundamental arbitrage and statistical arbitrage.
- Market-neutral strategies have very low positive correlation with the market because they place specific bets on stock price convergence while hedging out general market risk.

## Understanding Market Neutral

There is no single universally accepted method for implementing a market-neutral strategy. Beyond the methods mentioned above, market-neutral strategists may also use other tools such as merger arbitrage, shorting specific sectors, and more.

Managers holding a market-neutral position are able to exploit momentum in any direction the market moves. Hedge funds commonly take market-neutral positions because they focus on absolute returns rather than relative returns. A market-neutral position might involve holding 50% long and 50% short in a particular sector such as oil and gas, or taking the same position across the broader market.

Market-neutral strategies are often likened to long/short equity funds, though they differ in important ways. Long/short funds aim to adjust their long and short stock exposures according to sector shifts, capitalizing on undervalued and overvalued opportunities.

A market-neutral strategy, by contrast, focuses on concentrated bets based on pricing discrepancies, with the primary goal of achieving zero beta relative to the appropriate market index in order to hedge out systematic risk. Although market-neutral funds use long and short positions, the objective of this fund category is distinctly different from that of ordinary long/short funds.

## Types of Market-Neutral Strategies

Fund managers primarily employ two market-neutral strategies: fundamental arbitrage and statistical arbitrage. Fundamental market-neutral investors use fundamental analysis -- rather than quantitative algorithms -- to project a company's future trajectory and trade based on expected stock price convergence.

Statistical arbitrage market-neutral funds use algorithms and quantitative methods to discover price discrepancies in stocks based on historical data. Then, based on these quantitative results, managers trade stocks that are likely to revert to their price means.

A major advantage of market-neutral funds is their strong emphasis on mitigating market risk when constructing portfolios. During periods of high market volatility, historical evidence shows that market-neutral funds tend to outperform funds employing certain other strategies.

Apart from pure short-selling strategies, market-neutral strategies historically have the lowest positive correlation with the market, primarily because they place specific bets on stock price convergence while hedging out general market risk.

## Example of a Market-Neutral Fund

Because it is a market-neutral strategy, the Vanguard Market Neutral Investor Shares Fund (VMNFX) is a liquid alternative fund that uses both long and short strategies, distinguishing it from the firm's other mutual funds, which only buy and sell long positions. The fund's strategy aims to minimize the stock market's impact on its returns, meaning the fund's returns may diverge significantly from those of the overall market.

Although most funds that short stocks -- such as hedge funds -- do not disclose their short holdings because SEC rules do not require it, Vanguard Market Neutral Investor Shares does publish its short positions.

The fund selects short positions by evaluating companies across five categories: growth, quality, management decisions, market sentiment, and valuation. It then creates a composite expected return for all stocks in its universe and shorts those with the lowest scores.

Investing in market-neutral funds is typically targeted at high-net-worth individuals. For example, VMNFX has a minimum investment of $50,000. Other funds may have extremely high expense ratios, far exceeding those of passively managed funds.
