# What Is Delta Hedging?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Delta hedging is an options trading strategy designed to reduce or hedge the directional risk associated with price movements in the underlying asset. It uses options to offset the risk of a single option holding or an entire portfolio of holdings. The investor aims to reach a delta neutral state to avoid directional bias in the hedge.

### Key Takeaways

- Delta hedging is an options strategy that seeks directional neutrality by establishing offsetting long and short positions on the same underlying asset.
- By reducing directional risk, delta hedging can isolate volatility changes for an options trader.
- Options positions can also be delta hedged using shares of the underlying stock.
- Delta hedging benefits traders who anticipate strong movement in the underlying stock, but it requires continuous monitoring and adjustment of positions.
- Options with a delta opposite to the current holding can be used to maintain a delta neutral position.

## How Delta Hedging Works

The most basic form of delta hedging involves an investor who buys or sells options and then offsets the delta risk by buying or selling a corresponding amount of stock or ETF shares. Investors may wish to offset the risk of an option or the underlying stock by using delta hedging strategies.

More advanced options strategies attempt to trade volatility through delta neutral trading strategies. Because delta hedging attempts to neutralize or reduce the magnitude of the move in an option's price relative to the asset's price, it requires constant rebalancing. Delta hedging is a complex strategy primarily used by institutional traders and investment banks.

Delta represents the change in the value of an option relative to a movement in the market price of the underlying asset. A hedge is an investment -- typically an option -- taken to offset the risk exposure of an asset.

**Important:** Delta is the ratio between the change in an option contract's price and the corresponding movement in the value of the underlying asset. Therefore, if the delta for an XYZ stock option is 0.45, a $1 increase in the underlying stock's market price would cause the option's value to rise by $0.45, all else being equal.

Assume the options under discussion have equities as their underlying security. Traders want to know an option's delta because it indicates how much the option's value, or premium, will rise or fall with a movement in the stock price. The theoretical change in the premium for each dollar move in the underlying price is the delta, and the relationship between these two movements is the hedge ratio.

The delta of a call option ranges between 0 and 1, while the delta of a put option ranges between -1 and 0. A put option with a delta of -0.50 is considered at-the-money, meaning the option's strike price equals the price of the underlying stock. Conversely, a call option with a delta of 0.50 has a strike price equal to the stock's price. [1]

Delta depends on whether the option is:

- In-the-money, or currently profitable
- At-the-money, where the strike price equals the market price
- Out-of-the-money, or currently not profitable

## Reaching Delta Neutral

An option position can be hedged with options that exhibit a delta opposite to the current option holding in order to maintain a delta neutral position. A delta neutral position is one where the overall delta is zero, which minimizes the movement of the option's price relative to the underlying asset.

For example, suppose an investor holds one call option with a delta of 0.50, indicating the option is at-the-money, and wishes to maintain a delta neutral position. The investor could purchase an at-the-money put option with a delta of -0.50 to offset the positive delta, bringing the position's delta to zero.

**Note:** Delta-gamma hedging is closely related to delta hedging. It is an options strategy that combines delta and gamma hedges to mitigate the risk of changes in the underlying asset and in the delta itself.

## A Brief Overview of Options

The value of an option is measured by its premium -- the fee paid to purchase the contract. By holding the option, the investor or trader can exercise the right to buy or sell 100 shares of the underlying asset, but is not required to do so if it is not profitable. The price at which they buy or sell is known as the strike price, set at the time of purchase along with the expiration date. Each contract equals 100 shares of the underlying stock or asset.

American-style option holders may exercise their rights at any time up to and including the expiration date. European-style options allow the holder to exercise only on the expiration date. Additionally, depending on the option's value, the holder may choose to sell the contract to another investor before expiration. [2]

For example, if a call option has a strike price of $30 and the underlying stock is trading at $40 at expiration, the option holder can convert 100 shares at the lower strike price of $30. If they choose, they can then sell those shares on the open market at $40, earning a profit. The profit would be $10 minus the call option's premium and any brokerage fees for placing the trades.

Put options are slightly more complex but work similarly to call options. Here, the holder expects the underlying asset's value to deteriorate before expiration. They may hold the asset in their portfolio or borrow shares from a broker. [3]

## Delta Hedging with Stocks

An option position can also be delta hedged using shares of the underlying stock. One share of the underlying stock has a delta of 1 since its value changes by $1 for each $1 move in the stock. For example, suppose an investor holds one call option with a delta of 0.75.

In this case, the investor could delta hedge the call option by shorting 75 shares of the underlying stock. When short selling, the investor borrows shares, sells those shares at market price to other investors, and later buys them back at a hopefully lower price to return to the lender. [4]

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Delta Hedging

Delta hedging can benefit traders who anticipate a strong move in the underlying stock, but it carries the risk of being over-hedged if the stock does not move as expected. If over-hedged positions must be unwound, trading costs increase.

A primary drawback of delta hedging is the necessity of constantly monitoring and adjusting positions. Depending on the stock's movement, the trader must frequently buy and sell securities to avoid being under-hedged or over-hedged.

The volume of transactions involved in delta hedging can become expensive, as trading fees are incurred when adjustments are made. It can be particularly costly when hedging is done with options, because options may lose time value, sometimes trading at prices lower than the underlying stock has increased.

Time value measures how much time remains before an option's expiration during which the trader can profit. As time passes and the expiration date approaches, the option loses time value because there is less time remaining to earn a profit. Consequently, the time value of an option affects its premium cost -- options with more time value typically have higher premiums, while those with less time value tend to have lower premiums. As time passes, the value of the option changes, which may necessitate increased delta hedging to maintain a delta neutral strategy. [5]

#### Advantages

- Allows traders to hedge the risk of adverse price changes in a portfolio
- Protects profits from an option or stock position in the short term without unwinding long-term holdings

#### Disadvantages

- Numerous transactions may be needed to constantly adjust the delta hedge, resulting in increased costs
- Over-hedging can occur if the delta is offset too aggressively or if the market changes unexpectedly

## Delta Hedging Example

Suppose a trader wants to maintain a delta neutral position for an investment in General Electric (GE) stock. The investor holds one put option on GE. One option represents 100 shares of GE stock.

The stock declines substantially, and the trader earns a profit on the put option. However, recent events then push the stock price higher. The trader believes this rally is a short-term event and expects the stock to fall again. Accordingly, the trader implements a delta hedge to protect the put option's gains.

GE's put option has a delta of -0.75, commonly referred to as -75. The investor establishes a delta neutral position by purchasing 75 shares of the underlying stock. At $10 per share, the investor spends a total of $750 to purchase 75 shares of GE. Once the stock's recent rally ends or events shift in favor of the trader's put option position, the trader can remove the delta hedge.

## How Does Delta Hedging Work?

Delta hedging is a trading strategy that involves options. Traders use it to hedge directional risk associated with price changes in the underlying asset, typically by buying and selling options and then offsetting the risk by buying or selling an equivalent number of shares of stock or ETF shares. The goal is to reach a delta neutral state without introducing directional bias into the hedge.

## Can You Use Delta to Decide How to Hedge Options?

Yes, you can use delta to hedge options. To do so, you must determine whether you should buy or sell the underlying asset. You can calculate the delta hedge quantity by multiplying the total value of the delta by the number of relevant option contracts. Multiply that number by 100 to arrive at the final result.

## What Is Delta-Gamma Hedging?

Delta-gamma hedging is an options strategy closely related to delta hedging. In delta-gamma hedging, delta and gamma hedges are combined to reduce the risk associated with changes in the underlying asset as well as changes in the delta itself. Remember that delta estimates the change in a derivative's price, while gamma describes the rate of change of an option's delta for each one-point move in price.

## Summary

Options traders have a variety of strategies to help mitigate the risks associated with these investments. One such strategy is delta hedging. When traders employ this strategy, they seek to reduce directional risk associated with price movements in the underlying asset. This is accomplished by buying or selling options and offsetting the risk with an equivalent number of shares of company stock or ETF shares. While this can be advantageous for traders who know how to use the strategy, they should be aware that it requires constant monitoring and can be quite costly.
