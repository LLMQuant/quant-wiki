# What Is a Simple Moving Average (SMA)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A simple moving average (SMA) calculates the average price of an asset, typically using closing prices, over a specified number of days.

### Key Takeaways

- A simple moving average calculates the average of a selected range of prices over a specified time period.
- As a technical indicator, the simple moving average can help determine whether an asset's price will continue rising or falling, or whether it will reverse its trend.
- A simple moving average can be enhanced with an exponential moving average (EMA), which places greater weight on more recent prices.

## Understanding the Simple Moving Average (SMA)

A simple moving average (SMA) is an arithmetic moving average calculated by adding the most recent prices and dividing by the number of time periods in the calculation. For example, you could add a security's closing price over a number of time periods and then divide this total by that same number of periods. Short-term averages respond quickly to changes in the price of the underlying security, while long-term averages are slower to react. Other types of moving averages include the exponential moving average (EMA) and the weighted moving average (WMA).

The SMA formula is:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{SMA}=\dfrac{A_1 + A_2 + ... + A_n}{n} \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &A_n=\text{the price of an asset at period } n\\ &n=\text{the number of total periods}\\ \end{aligned} $$

For example, if a security's closing prices over 15 days were as follows:

Week 1 (5 days): 20, 22, 24, 25, 23

Week 2 (5 days): 26, 28, 26, 29, 27

Week 3 (5 days): 28, 30, 27, 29, 28

392 / 15 = 26.13

Therefore, the security's 15-day simple moving average is $26.13.

A 10-day moving average would average only the most recent 10 closing prices rather than using all 15 days. Each time the market closes, a new closing price replaces the oldest price, and a new moving average is calculated. A 50-day moving average would use 50 days of data on a rolling basis.

A simple moving average is customizable and can be calculated for different time periods. It is computed by adding the closing price of a security over a number of periods and then dividing by the number of periods, yielding the security's average price over that time frame.

A simple moving average smooths out price volatility and makes it easier to view the price trend of a security. If the SMA points up, the price is rising; if it points down, the price is falling. The longer the time frame of the moving average, the smoother the SMA. Shorter-term moving averages are more volatile, but their readings are closer to the source data.

**Important:** One of the most commonly used simple moving averages is the 200-day SMA. However, there is a danger in following the crowd. As *The Wall Street Journal* has pointed out, thousands of traders base their strategies on the 200-day SMA, which means these predictions may become self-fulfilling and limit price movement. [1]

## Special Considerations

Moving averages are an important analytical tool for identifying current price trends and the potential for changes in established trends. The simplest use of an SMA in technical analysis is to quickly determine whether an asset is in an uptrend or downtrend.

A slightly more sophisticated analytical use is to compare a pair of simple moving averages, each covering a different time frame. If a shorter-term SMA is above a longer-term average, an uptrend is expected. Conversely, if the longer-term average is above the shorter-term SMA, a downtrend may be expected.

Two popular trading patterns that use simple moving averages include the death cross and the golden cross. A death cross occurs when the 50-day SMA crosses below the 200-day SMA. This is considered a bearish signal, indicating that further losses are in store. A golden cross occurs when a short-term SMA breaks above a long-term SMA, reinforced by high trading volumes, and may signal further gains ahead.

## Simple Moving Average vs. Exponential Moving Average

The key difference between a simple moving average and an exponential moving average is the sensitivity each exhibits to changes in the data used in its calculation. Specifically, the EMA assigns a higher weight to recent prices, while the SMA assigns equal weight to all values.

The two averages are interpreted in largely the same way and are both commonly used by technical traders to smooth out price fluctuations. Because the EMA places a higher weight on recent data, it responds more quickly to the latest price changes, making its results more timely. This is why many traders prefer the EMA over the SMA.

## Limitations of the Simple Moving Average

It is unclear whether more emphasis should be placed on the most recent days in the period or on more distant data. Many traders believe that new data better reflects the current trend of the security, while others feel that prioritizing certain dates will bias the trend. Therefore, the SMA may rely too heavily on outdated data, since it treats the influence of the 10th or 200th day the same as the 1st or 2nd day.

Similarly, the SMA relies entirely on historical data. Many people, including some economists, believe that markets are efficient -- a concept known as the efficient market hypothesis. This theory holds that current market prices already reflect all available information. If markets are truly efficient, using historical data to predict the future direction of asset prices is meaningless. Supporters and critics of this hypothesis are both plentiful among economists and financial thinkers.

## How Is the SMA Used in Technical Analysis?

Traders use the simple moving average (SMA) to chart the long-term trajectory of a stock or other security while filtering out the noise of day-to-day price movements. This allows traders to compare medium- and longer-term trends over a larger time horizon. For example, if a security's 50-day SMA falls below its 200-day SMA, this is typically interpreted as a bearish death cross pattern, signaling further declines. Conversely, a golden cross pattern indicates the potential for a market rally.

## How Do You Calculate a Simple Moving Average?

To calculate a simple moving average, the number of prices within a time period is divided by the number of total periods. For example, consider Tesla shares closing at $10, $11, $12, $11, and $14 over a five-day period. The simple moving average of Tesla shares would equal ($10 + $11 + $12 + $11 + $14) divided by 5, which is $11.60.

## What Is the Difference Between a Simple Moving Average and an Exponential Moving Average?

While a simple moving average gives equal weight to each value within a period, an exponential moving average places greater weight on recent prices. Exponential moving averages are typically seen as a more timely indicator of a price trend, which is why many traders prefer them over the simple moving average. Common short-term EMAs include the 12-day and 26-day, while the 50-day and 200-day EMAs are used to indicate long-term trends.
