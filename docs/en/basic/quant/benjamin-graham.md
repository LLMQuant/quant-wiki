# Who Was Benjamin Graham?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Benjamin Graham was an influential investor of the first half of the twentieth century. His work in securities research laid the groundwork for the deep fundamental valuation methods used by market participants in equity analysis today. His classic book, *The Intelligent Investor*, is widely recognized as a foundational text on value investing.

### Key Takeaways

- Benjamin Graham was a British-born investor and researcher whose work provided a framework for equity analysis.
- By age 25, Graham was earning approximately $500,000 per year, but the 1929 stock market crash wiped out nearly all of his income and investments.
- The 1929 crash inspired Graham to co-author a research book with David Dodd, titled *Security Analysis*.
- In 1949, Graham published *The Intelligent Investor: The Definitive Book on Value Investing*, which is considered the bible for investors.
- As a lecturer at Columbia University, Graham mentored the now-billionaire investor Warren Buffett.

## Early Life and Education

Benjamin Graham was born in London, England, in 1894. As a young child, his family emigrated to the United States, where they lost all of their savings in the Bank Panic of 1907. Graham attended Columbia University on a scholarship and, upon graduation, accepted a job offer from the Wall Street firm Newburger, Henderson, and Loeb.

By age 25, he was already earning approximately $500,000 per year. However, the 1929 stock market crash cost him nearly all of his investments, and the experience taught him invaluable lessons about the world of investing. His observations after the crash inspired him to write *Security Analysis* with David Dodd. The renowned American investor Irving Kahn also contributed to the book's research.

## Notable Achievements

Benjamin Graham is regarded as the founder of stock analysis, particularly value investing. According to Graham and Dodd, value investing involves deriving the intrinsic value of a common stock -- independent of its market price -- and comparing it with the stock's market value. Intrinsic value can be determined by examining a company's financial fundamentals, including:

- Assets
- Earnings
- Dividend payments

If the intrinsic value exceeds the current price, the investor should buy and hold until the market reverts to the mean. Mean reversion theory holds that over time, the market price and the intrinsic price will converge until the stock price reflects its true value. By purchasing undervalued stocks, investors buy at a price below true value and sell once the price reaches intrinsic value.

This price convergence effect occurs only in efficient markets. Graham was a firm proponent of efficient markets. If markets are not efficient, value investing loses its point: the fundamental premise of value investing is that the market will eventually correct to intrinsic value.

Despite irrational investor behavior, common stocks will not remain overvalued or undervalued indefinitely.

Graham noted that due to irrational behavior among investors and unpredictable factors such as stock market fluctuations, purchasing undervalued or out-of-favor stocks provides investors with a margin of safety -- room for human error.

Additionally, investors can achieve a margin of safety by buying stocks of companies with high dividend yields and low debt-to-equity ratios, and by diversifying their portfolios. If a company goes bankrupt, the margin of safety cushions the investor's losses. Graham typically bought stocks trading at two-thirds of their net asset value as his margin of safety buffer.

The original Graham formula for finding a stock's intrinsic value was:

$$ \begin{aligned}&V \ =\ EPS \ \times\ (8.5\ +\ 2g)\\&\textbf{where:}\\& V\ =\ \text{intrinsic value}\\&EPS\ =\ \text{trailing 12-month earnings per share}\\&8.5\ =\ \text{P/E ratio of a zero-growth stock}\\&g\ =\ \text{long-term growth rate of the company}\end{aligned} $$

In 1974, the formula was revised to include a risk-free rate of 4.4%, which was the average yield of high-grade corporate bonds in 1962, and the current yield on AAA corporate bonds, represented by the letter Y:

$$ V=\frac{EPS\ \times\ (8.5\ +\ 2g)\ \times\ 4.4}{Y} $$

*Security Analysis* was first published in 1934, at the onset of the Great Depression, while Graham was a lecturer at Columbia Business School. The book laid out the fundamental framework for value investing, which involves purchasing undervalued stocks with the potential for long-term growth. In an era when the stock market was widely regarded as a speculative vehicle, the concepts of intrinsic value and margin of safety -- first introduced in *Security Analysis* -- paved the way for fundamental stock analysis free from speculation.

In 1949, Graham wrote the acclaimed *The Intelligent Investor: The Definitive Book on Value Investing*. This book is widely considered the bible for value investors and introduced a character called "Mr. Market," a metaphor Graham used to illustrate the mechanics of market pricing.

Mr. Market is an imaginary business partner who attempts every day to sell or buy shares at different prices, often driven by his mood. Of course, the investor is under no obligation to accept any offer.

Graham argued that instead of relying on daily market sentiment driven by investor emotions (greed and fear), investors should analyze the value of stocks based on a company's operations and financial condition. This analysis should strengthen an investor's judgment when confronted with Mr. Market's quotes.

According to Graham, the intelligent investor sells to optimists and buys from pessimists. Investors should look for discrepancies between price and value caused by economic downturns, market crashes, one-time events, temporary negative publicity, and human error, seeking opportunities to buy low and sell high. If no such opportunities exist, the investor should ignore market noise.

While reaffirming the fundamentals from *Security Analysis*, *The Intelligent Investor* also advised investors to:

- Not follow the crowd
- Hold a portfolio of 50% stocks and 50% bonds or cash
- Be wary of day trading
- Take advantage of market fluctuations
- Not buy a stock merely because it is popular
- Understand that market volatility is inevitable and can be turned to the investor's advantage
- Watch out for creative accounting techniques that companies use to make their earnings per share appear more attractive

Notably, Warren Buffett was one of Benjamin Graham's most distinguished students. Buffett studied under Graham at Columbia University and, after graduation, worked at Graham's firm, Graham-Newman Corporation, until Graham retired. Under Graham's guidance and value investing principles, Buffett became one of the most successful investors in history, with a net worth of approximately $120.6 billion as of January 2024, ranking him eighth in the world. Other notable investors who studied and worked under Graham include Irving Kahn, Christopher Browne, and Walter Schloss.

Although Benjamin Graham passed away in 1976, his work endures and continues to be widely used by value investors and financial analysts who perform fundamental research on companies' value and growth prospects.

## What Is the Graham and Dodd Award?

The Graham and Dodd Award was established to honor former Columbia University finance professors Benjamin Graham and David Dodd. It recognizes outstanding research and financial writing published in the *Financial Analysts Journal*.

## What Is Benjamin Graham Known For?

Benjamin Graham is renowned as a value investor, lecturer, financial securities researcher, and mentor to billionaire investor Warren Buffett. Known as the "father of investing," Graham was a prolific author, and his book *The Intelligent Investor* is widely regarded as the bible of value investing.

## What Were Graham's Three Investing Principles?

Benjamin Graham's core investment principles include:

- Invest with a margin of safety
- Expect volatility and profit from it
- Know what type of investor you are, so you know what investment approaches suit you best

## Conclusion

Benjamin Graham is known as the "father of value investing," celebrated for his distinctive investment style, his literary contributions to the investment field, and his research. He taught at his alma mater, Columbia University, eventually becoming a finance professor there. His classic work, *The Intelligent Investor*, introduced the concept of value investing to the financial and investment world and defined many of the investment principles adopted by some of the world's most prominent investors.
