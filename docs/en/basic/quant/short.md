# What Is a Short Position?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A short, or short position, is created when a trader sells a security first with the intention of repurchasing it or covering it later at a lower price. A trader may decide to short a security when they believe the price of that security is likely to decrease in the near future. There are two types of short positions: naked and covered.

A naked short is when a trader sells a security without possessing it. However, this practice is illegal for equities in the United States and is entirely prohibited in India and certain other countries. A covered short is when a trader borrows shares from a stock loan desk; in return, the trader pays a borrow rate during the time the short position is in place.

In the futures or foreign exchange markets, short positions can be created at any time.

### Key Takeaways

- A short position is a trading technique in which an investor sells a security with plans to buy it back later.
- Shorting is a strategy used when an investor anticipates the security's price will fall in the short term.
- In common practice, short sellers borrow shares from an investment bank or other financial institution and pay a fee to borrow the shares while the short position is in place.

## Understanding Short Positions

When creating a short position, it is important to understand that the trader's profit potential is limited while the potential loss is theoretically unlimited. This is because the profit is limited to the stock's price declining toward zero. However, a stock's price could rise for years, making a series of higher highs. One of the most dangerous aspects of shorting is the possibility of a short squeeze.

A short squeeze occurs when a heavily shorted stock suddenly begins to increase in price, causing short sellers to scramble to cover their positions. A notable short squeeze took place in October 2008, when shares of Volkswagen surged as short sellers rushed to cover. During the squeeze, the stock price rose from approximately 200 euros to 1,000 euros in just over a month.

## How to Establish a Short Position

To place a short order, an investor must first have access to this type of order in their brokerage account. Since short trades incur margin and interest costs, you will need a margin account to establish a short position. Once you have the right type of account along with the necessary permissions, you can enter the order details on the order page just like any other trade.

Keep in mind that you are selling first to open a position, with the expectation of buying the asset back later at a lower price. In a short position, the entry price is the sell price and the exit price is the buy price. Also understand that margin trading involves interest, margin requirements, and possibly other brokerage fees.

## Short Position Example

Suppose a trader believes that Amazon's stock will decline after it reports quarterly results. To exploit this possibility, the trader borrows 1,000 shares from the stock loan desk with the intent to short the stock.

The trader then sells the 1,000 shares at $1,500. In the following weeks, the company reports weaker-than-expected revenue and guides for a disappointing quarter ahead. As a result, the stock plunges to $1,300; the trader buys to cover. The trade results in a gain of $200 per share, for a total profit of $200,000.

## What Is Margin?

In finance, margin is the collateral an investor must deposit with their broker or exchange to cover the credit risk the holder poses to the broker or exchange. For instance, a short position cannot be established without sufficient margin.

Under Regulation T, the Federal Reserve Board requires all short sale accounts to have 150% of the value of the short sale at the time the sale is initiated. The 150% consists of the full value of the short sale proceeds (100%) plus an additional margin requirement of 50% of the value of the short sale. [1]

## How Much Can You Lose on a Short Position?

Shorting means a trader borrows a security and sells it on the open market, planning to buy it back later at a lower price. In theory, the price of an asset has no upper limit -- it can climb indefinitely. This means that, in theory, the risk of loss on a short position is unlimited.

## What Is a Short Squeeze?

A short position represents shares that have been borrowed and sold, with the investor expecting to buy them back later. When the price of the underlying asset rises, the investor faces losses on the short position. In addition to mounting paper losses, maintaining a short position can become more difficult because if the underlying asset's price increases, the required margin collateral also increases to ensure the investor can repurchase the shares and return them to the broker.

When investors are forced to buy back shares to close their positions, it is called a short squeeze. If enough short sellers are forced to buy back shares simultaneously, it can cause a surge in demand for the stock, driving the underlying asset's price sharply higher.

## Summary

Although selling something you do not own may sound illegal, markets are tightly regulated. When traders believe a security's price will decline in the near term, they can enter a short position by selling the security first with plans to repurchase it later at a lower price.

To establish a short position, traders typically need to borrow shares of the security from their brokerage firm. This means shorting requires a margin account along with potential permissions and brokerage fees.

If the shorted security's price begins to rise rather than fall, losses can mount quickly. In fact, since a security's price has no ceiling, the losses on a short position are theoretically unlimited. Given this inherent risk and the complexity of the transactions, short selling securities is generally recommended only for more advanced traders and investors.
