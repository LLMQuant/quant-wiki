# What Is the Law of Large Numbers?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Law of Large Numbers is a fundamental principle in probability and statistics. It states that as a sample size grows, the sample mean converges toward the true population mean, because larger samples are more representative of the overall population.

In a financial context, the Law of Large Numbers suggests that a large, rapidly growing entity cannot sustain that growth rate indefinitely. Blue-chip companies with market capitalizations in the hundreds of billions are frequently cited as examples of this phenomenon.

### Key Takeaways

- The Law of Large Numbers states that the observed average from a large sample will be close to the true population average, and this proximity increases as the sample grows.
- It does not guarantee that any particular sample reflects the true population characteristics, especially when the sample is small.
- It also does not guarantee that a sample that fails to reflect the population will be balanced out by subsequent samples.
- The Law of Large Numbers indicates that larger samples will better represent the population average. The Central Limit Theorem, by contrast, states that larger samples will represent the population's distribution.
- In business, the term "Law of Large Numbers" is sometimes used informally to describe the relationship between scale and growth rate.

## Understanding the Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers can be understood from two perspectives.

In statistical analysis, it can be applied to a wide range of subjects. While it may not be practical to survey every individual in a given population, each additional data point collected increases the probability that the result is a true measure of the mean.

The Law of Large Numbers does not imply that a given sample, or a series of consecutive samples, will always reflect the true population characteristics -- especially when those samples deviate from the population mean. Nor does it guarantee that successive samples will push the observed average toward the population mean, as the gambler's fallacy would suggest.

In business, the term is sometimes used in connection with growth rates. Expressed as a percentage, it implies that as a company scales, maintaining the growth rate becomes increasingly difficult. Even if the percentage growth rate remains constant, the underlying dollar amounts keep increasing.

**Important:** The Law of Large Numbers should not be confused with the Law of Averages, which suggests that the distribution of outcomes in a large or small sample reflects the distribution of outcomes in the population.

## Statistical Examples of the Law of Large Numbers

If you want to determine the average value of a data set comprising 100 possible values, selecting 20 data points rather than relying on just two will more likely yield an accurate average. Two data points are highly likely to be outliers or non-representative, whereas it is far less probable that all 20 data points would be non-representative.

For example, if a data set includes all integers from 1 to 100 and you draw only the values 95 and 40, your estimate of the average would be approximately 67.5. As you continue to draw random samples up to 20 variables, the average should progressively approach the true mean as more data points are considered.

## The Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem

The Law of Large Numbers and the Central Limit Theorem are related concepts in statistical analysis. The Central Limit Theorem states that sample means will be evenly distributed as the sample size increases. This typically manifests as a bell-shaped curve, with the peak at the mean and the sample data uniformly distributed on either side.

The Law of Large Numbers also indicates that data becomes more precise as the sample grows, but it focuses on the center of the bell curve. It states that the sample mean will more closely approximate the population mean as the sample size increases. Thus, the Law of Large Numbers relates to the curve's peak, or mean, while the Central Limit Theorem relates to the curve's distribution.

## The Law of Large Numbers and Business Growth

In business and finance, the term "Law of Large Numbers" is sometimes used informally to describe how exponential growth rates tend to be unsustainable as scale increases. This is not directly related to the statistical law but may be a consequence of the law of diminishing marginal returns or diseconomies of scale.

The same principle applies to other metrics such as market capitalization or net profit. Investment decisions can be guided by the associated difficulties that companies with extremely high market capitalizations face in terms of stock appreciation. This concept is central to the distinction between growth stocks and value stocks, as companies may struggle to maintain rapid growth strategies after achieving market success.

## Business Example of the Law of Large Numbers

Tesla reported automotive sales of $24.604 billion in fiscal year 2020 and $44.125 billion the following year -- an increase of approximately 79%. [1] Electric vehicles represent an emerging market where Tesla began to experience the benefits of economies of scale.

The Law of Large Numbers suggests that as Tesla continues to grow, maintaining that level of production growth will become increasingly difficult. Assuming a few years of steady growth rates, Tesla clearly cannot sustain its current trajectory because the underlying dollar values become impractical.

## The Law of Large Numbers and Insurance

The Law of Large Numbers is also significant in the insurance industry, where it is used to calculate and refine expected risk.

Consider a scenario in which an insurance company evaluates how much to charge customers for automobile insurance. With a small data set, the company cannot adequately determine the appropriate risk profile.

As the company collects more data, it experiences the Law of Large Numbers in action. It may quickly discover, for instance, that young male drivers are most likely to be involved in accidents. This larger, more representative sample of driving events allows the insurer to make more accurate premium determinations.

The Law of Large Numbers also enables insurance companies to deepen their assessment criteria by analyzing which characteristics lead to higher risk.

## Why Is the Law of Large Numbers Important?

The Law of Large Numbers matters in statistical analysis because it establishes the validity of sample sizes. When you analyze with a small amount of data, the assumptions you make may not translate properly to the actual population. Ensuring that you capture enough data points to adequately represent the entire data set is therefore essential.

It is also important when setting company goals. A company might double its revenue in one year. If it achieves only 50% revenue growth the following year, it will have earned the same dollar amount over those two years. Percentages can be misleading because the underlying dollar values grow dramatically.

## How Do Companies Overcome the Law of Large Numbers?

Companies typically overcome the Law of Large Numbers by acquiring smaller growth companies that can deliver scalable growth. They also seek to improve efficiency and leverage their scale advantages in manufacturing, ordering, or distribution. Companies may focus more on dollar-value targets rather than percentage targets.

## What Is the Law of Small Numbers?

The Law of Small Numbers refers to people's tendency to underestimate the variability of small samples. When sample sizes are too small, individuals tend to overestimate the population's value based on an insufficient sample.

## What Does the Law of Small Numbers Mean in Psychology?

In psychology, the Law of Large Numbers manifests as a greater number of trials generally leading to more accurate expected values. As the number of trials increases, predictions converge more closely to the correct assessment.

## Summary

When analyzing data sets, it is essential to understand the Law of Large Numbers to determine whether your sample size is representative of the overall population. In a business context, however, pay attention to the implications of scale. The Law of Large Numbers indicates that as a company grows larger, maintaining percentage growth becomes increasingly challenging because the underlying dollar changes keep increasing.
