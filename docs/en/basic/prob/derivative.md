# What Is a Derivative?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The term "derivative" refers to a type of financial contract whose value depends on an underlying asset, group of assets, or benchmark. A derivative is set between two or more parties who can trade it on an exchange or over the counter (OTC).

These contracts can be used to trade any number of assets and carry their own risks. Derivative prices are derived from fluctuations in the underlying asset. These financial securities are commonly used to access specific markets and may be traded to hedge risk. Derivatives can be used either to mitigate risk (hedging) or to assume risk in expectation of a commensurate reward (speculation). Derivatives can transfer risk -- and the accompanying reward -- from the risk-averse to the risk-seeking.

### Key Takeaways

- Derivatives are financial contracts, set between two or more parties, whose value is derived from an underlying asset, group of assets, or benchmark.
- Derivatives can be traded on exchanges or over the counter.
- Derivative prices are derived from fluctuations in the underlying asset.
- Derivatives are typically leveraged instruments, which increases their potential risks and rewards.
- Common derivatives include futures contracts, forward contracts, options, and swaps.

## Understanding Derivatives

A derivative is a complex type of financial security set between two or more parties. Derivatives can take many forms, ranging from stock and bond derivatives to economic indicator derivatives.

Traders use derivatives to access specific markets and trade different assets. Typically, derivatives are considered a form of advanced investing. The most common underlying assets are stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies, interest rates, and market indices. Contract values depend on changes in the prices of the underlying asset (the primary instrument).

Derivatives can be used to hedge a position, speculate on the directional movement of an underlying asset, or give leverage to holdings. These assets are commonly traded on exchanges or OTC, and are purchased through brokerages. The Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) is one of the world's largest derivatives exchanges. [1]

It is important to remember that when companies hedge, they are not speculating on the commodity's price. Rather, hedging is simply a way for each party to manage risk. Each party has its profit or margin built into the price, and hedging helps protect those profits from being eliminated by market movements in the commodity's price.

OTC-traded derivatives generally carry greater counterparty risk -- the danger that one of the parties involved in the transaction might default. These contracts trade between two private parties and are unregulated. To hedge this risk, an investor can purchase a currency derivative to lock in a specific exchange rate. Derivatives that can hedge this type of risk include currency futures and currency swaps.

**Important:** Exchange-traded derivatives are more standardized and more heavily regulated than OTC derivatives.

## Special Considerations

Derivatives were originally used to ensure balanced exchange rates for internationally traded goods. International traders needed a system to account for the different values of national currencies.

Suppose a European investor has investment accounts denominated entirely in euros (EUR). They purchase shares of a U.S. company through a U.S. exchange using U.S. dollars (USD). This means they are now exposed to exchange rate risk while holding the stock -- the risk that the euro will appreciate against the dollar. If that happens, any profits the investor realizes upon selling the stock would be worth less when converted back to euros.

A speculator who expects the euro to appreciate against the dollar could profit by using a derivative that rises in value with the euro. When using derivatives to speculate on the price movement of an underlying asset, the investor does not need to hold or invest in the underlying asset directly.

**Note:** Many derivative instruments are leveraged, meaning a small amount of capital can control a large position in the underlying asset.

## Types of Derivatives

Today, derivatives are based on a wide variety of transactions and serve many more purposes. There are even derivatives based on weather data, such as the amount of rainfall or the number of sunny days in a region.

Many different types of derivatives are available for risk management, speculation, and leveraged positions. The derivatives market continues to grow, offering products to suit nearly any need or risk tolerance.

Derivative products are classified into two categories: "lock" and "option." Lock products (e.g., futures, forwards, or swaps) bind the respective parties to agreed-upon terms over the life of the contract. Option products (e.g., stock options) give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell the underlying asset or security at a specific price on or before the option's expiration date. The most common derivative types are futures, forwards, swaps, and options.

**Futures contracts**, or simply futures, are agreements between two parties to buy and deliver an asset at an agreed price on a future date. Futures are standardized contracts that trade on exchanges. Traders use futures contracts to hedge their risk or speculate on the price of the underlying asset. The parties involved are obligated to fulfill their commitment to buy or sell the underlying asset.

For example, suppose on November 6, 2021, Company A purchases an oil futures contract at $62.22 per barrel, expiring on December 19, 2021. The company does this because it needs oil in December and is concerned that the price will rise before then. Buying the futures contract hedges the company's risk because the seller is obligated to deliver oil at $62.22 per barrel once the contract expires. Suppose oil prices rise to $80 per barrel by December 19, 2021. Company A can accept delivery from the futures contract seller, but if it no longer needs the oil, it can sell the contract before expiration and keep the profit.

In this example, both the futures buyer and seller hedge their risk. Company A needed oil in the future and wanted to offset the risk of a December price increase by going long an oil futures contract. The seller could be an oil company concerned about falling prices that wanted to eliminate that risk by selling (shorting) a futures contract that fixed the price it would receive in December.

It is also possible that one or both parties are speculators with opposing views on the direction of December oil. In that case, one party would benefit from the contract while the other would not. Consider WTI crude oil futures traded on the CME, which represent 1,000 barrels of oil. If oil prices rose from $62.22 to $80 per barrel, the trader holding the long position (buyer) would profit by $17,780 [($80 - $62.22) x 1,000 = $17,780]. The trader holding the short position (seller) would lose $17,780.

Not all futures contracts are settled by delivery of the underlying asset at expiration. If both parties are speculative investors or traders, neither is likely to arrange delivery of large quantities of crude oil. Speculators can end their obligation to purchase or deliver the underlying commodity by closing out (unwinding) their contract with an offsetting contract before expiration.

In practice, many derivatives are cash-settled, meaning the gain or loss from the trade is simply an accounting cash flow in the trader's brokerage account. Cash-settled futures include many interest rate futures, stock index futures, and more unusual instruments such as volatility futures or weather futures.

**Forward contracts**, or simply forwards, are similar to futures but do not trade on exchanges. They trade only OTC. When a forward contract is created, the buyer and seller can customize the terms, size, and settlement process. As OTC products, forwards carry greater counterparty risk for both parties.

Counterparty risk is a type of credit risk -- the possibility that parties may be unable to fulfill the obligations outlined in the contract. If one party becomes insolvent, the other may have no recourse and could lose the value of its position.

Once created, forward contract parties can offset their positions with other counterparties, which can increase the potential for counterparty risk as more traders become involved in the same contract.

**Swaps** are another common type of derivative, often used to exchange one kind of cash flow for another. For example, a trader might use an interest rate swap to switch from a floating-rate loan to a fixed-rate loan, or vice versa.

Suppose Company XYZ borrows $1,000,000 and pays a variable interest rate on the loan that is currently 6%. XYZ may be concerned about rising interest rates that will increase the cost of this loan, or may encounter a lender reluctant to extend more credit while the company bears floating-rate risk.

Suppose XYZ creates a swap with Company QRS, which is willing to exchange the payments owed on the variable-rate loan for the payments owed on a fixed-rate loan at 7%. This means XYZ will pay QRS 7% on its $1,000,000 principal, and QRS will pay XYZ 6% interest on the same principal. At the swap's inception, XYZ only needs to pay QRS the 1-percentage-point difference between the two swap rates.

If interest rates fall so that the variable rate on the original loan is now 5%, XYZ will have to pay QRS the 2-percentage-point difference on the loan. If rates rise to 8%, then QRS will have to pay XYZ the 1-percentage-point difference between the two swap rates. Regardless of how interest rates change, the swap achieves XYZ's original objective of converting a variable-rate loan into a fixed-rate loan.

Swaps can also be constructed to exchange currency exchange rate risk or the risk of loan default or cash flows from other business activities. Swaps related to the cash flows and potential defaults of mortgage bonds were an extremely popular type of derivative -- in fact, somewhat too popular. The counterparty risk of this type of swap ultimately escalated into the 2008 credit crisis.

**Options contracts** are similar to futures in that they involve an agreement between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a future date. The key difference is that with options, the buyer is not obligated to exercise the agreement. It is an opportunity only, not an obligation as with futures. Like futures, options can be used to hedge or speculate on the underlying asset's price.

**Tip:** Regarding the timing of exercising your right to buy or sell, it depends on the "style" of the option. American options allow the holder to exercise the option at any time up to and including the expiration date. European options can only be exercised on the expiration date itself. Most stocks and ETFs have American-style options, while stock indices, including the S&P 500, have European-style options.

Suppose an investor owns 100 shares of a stock valued at $50 per share and believes its value will rise in the future. However, concerned about potential downside risk, the investor decides to hedge with options. The investor can purchase a put option, which grants the right to sell 100 shares of the underlying stock at a strike price of $50 per share until a specified future date (the expiration date).

If the stock drops to $40 per share at expiration and the put buyer decides to exercise, they would sell the stock at the original $50 strike price. If the put option cost $200, then the investor only loses the cost of the option, since the strike price equaled the stock price at the time of purchase. This strategy is called a protective put because it hedges the stock's downside risk.

Alternatively, suppose an investor does not currently own the $50 stock but believes its value will increase over the next month. The investor could purchase a call option granting the right to buy the stock at $50 before or at expiration. Suppose this call option costs $200 and the stock rises to $60 before expiration. The buyer can now exercise the option, purchasing stock worth $60 at the $50 strike price -- an initial profit of $10 per share. A call option represents 100 shares, so the actual profit is $1,000, minus the cost of the option (premium) and any brokerage commission fees.

In both examples, the seller is obligated to fulfill their side of the contract if the buyer chooses to exercise. However, if the stock price is above the strike price at expiration, the put expires worthless and the seller (option writer) keeps the premium. If the stock price is below the strike price at expiration, the call expires worthless and the call seller keeps the premium.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Derivatives

As the examples above illustrate, derivatives can be useful tools for both businesses and investors. They provide a way to:

- Lock in prices
- Hedge against unfavorable interest rate movements
- Mitigate risk

These advantages often come at a limited cost.

Derivatives can also be purchased on margin, meaning traders use borrowed funds to buy them, making them even more affordable.

However, derivatives are difficult to value because they are based on the price of another asset. The risks of OTC derivatives include counterparty risk that is difficult to predict or assess. Most derivatives are also sensitive to:

- Changes in time to expiration
- The cost of holding the underlying asset
- Interest rates

These variables make it difficult to perfectly match a derivative's value to the underlying asset.

Because derivatives have no intrinsic value (their value comes solely from the underlying asset), they are vulnerable to market sentiment and market risk. Supply and demand factors can cause a derivative's price and liquidity to rise and fall, regardless of what is happening to the underlying asset's price.

Finally, derivatives are typically leveraged instruments, and leverage cuts both ways. While it can amplify returns, it also accelerates losses.

#### Pros

- Lock in prices
- Hedge against risk
- Can be leveraged
- Diversify portfolios

#### Cons

- Difficult to value
- Subject to counterparty default (if OTC)
- Complex to understand
- Sensitive to supply and demand factors

## What Are Derivatives?

Derivatives are securities whose value depends on, or is derived from, an underlying asset. For example, an oil futures contract is a derivative whose value is based on the market price of oil. Derivatives have become increasingly popular in recent decades, with the total value of outstanding derivatives estimated at $610 trillion as of June 30, 2021. [3]

## What Are Some Examples of Derivatives?

Common examples of derivatives include futures contracts, options contracts, and credit default swaps. Beyond these, there is a vast quantity of derivative contracts tailored to meet the needs of diverse counterparties. In fact, since many derivatives are traded OTC, they can in principle be infinitely customized.

## What Are the Main Benefits and Risks of Derivatives?

Derivatives can be a very convenient way to achieve financial objectives. For example, a company wanting to hedge its commodity exposure can do so by buying or selling energy derivatives such as crude oil futures. Similarly, a company can hedge its currency risk by purchasing currency forward contracts. Derivatives can also help investors leverage their positions, such as purchasing equities through stock options rather than shares directly. The main drawbacks of derivatives include counterparty risk, the inherent risks of leverage, and the fact that complicated webs of derivative contracts can lead to systemic risk.
