# What Is Systematic Sampling?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Systematic sampling is a probability sampling method in which sample members are selected from a larger population according to a random starting point but with a fixed, periodic interval. This sampling interval is calculated by dividing the population size by the desired sample size.

### Key Takeaways

- Systematic sampling is a probability sampling method in which a random sample is selected from a larger population using a fixed, periodic interval.
- This fixed interval, called the sampling interval, is calculated by dividing the population size by the desired sample size.
- Advantages include the elimination of clustered selection and a low probability of data contamination.
- Disadvantages include overrepresentation or underrepresentation of particular patterns and a greater risk of data manipulation.
- There are three main types of systematic sampling: random systematic, linear systematic, and circular systematic.

## Understanding Systematic Sampling

When properly executed on a large, defined population, systematic sampling helps researchers -- including marketing and sales professionals -- obtain representative findings without contacting every individual.

Because simple random sampling of an entire population can be inefficient and time-consuming, statisticians turn to alternative methods such as systematic sampling. Selecting a sample size through a systematic approach can be done quickly. Once a fixed starting point has been identified, a constant interval is chosen to facilitate participant selection.

Systematic sampling is preferred over simple random sampling when the risk of data manipulation is low. If the risk of a researcher manipulating the interval length to achieve desired results is high, simple random sampling is more appropriate.

Systematic sampling is popular among researchers and analysts for its simplicity. They generally assume the results are representative of most normal populations unless a random characteristic is disproportionately present in every nth data sample -- which is unlikely. In other words, the population needs to exhibit a natural degree of randomness along the chosen metric. If the population has some standardized pattern, the risk of accidentally selecting very common cases becomes more apparent.

A target population must be selected before choosing participants. It can be identified based on any number of desired characteristics suitable for the research purpose. Selection criteria might include age, gender, location, education level, or occupation.

**Important:** There are several methods of sampling a population for statistical inference. Systematic sampling is one form of random sampling.

## When to Use Systematic Sampling

Systematic sampling is particularly well suited when the population under study exhibits a degree of order or regularity. For example, when surveying customers entering a store, systematic sampling allows you to select every nth customer, ensuring representation across different times of day or week. This approach helps avoid bias that might result from selecting only customers who arrive during specific time periods.

It is also appropriate when the population size is known and relatively large. Systematic sampling simplifies the process by eliminating the need to list and randomly select from the entire population, instead selecting samples at a set rhythm. This is especially useful in large-scale studies where time and resources are limited.

Systematic sampling works well when researchers want to ensure the sample is uniformly distributed across the population. For example, a company can select every nth person from a corporate directory filtered by surname. Other sampling forms might accidentally cluster similar groups (e.g., selecting too many people from the finance department based on how samples are aggregated).

Additionally, systematic sampling is straightforward to implement compared to other methods. It requires minimal computation and can be executed using a simple algorithm, especially when both the target sample size and total population size are known.

## Examples of Systematic Sampling

As a hypothetical example, suppose a statistician selects every 100th person from a population of 10,000. The sampling interval can also be systematic -- for instance, selecting a new sample every 12 hours.

As another example, to select a random group of 1,000 from a population of 50,000 using systematic sampling, all potential participants must be placed on a list, and a starting point must be selected. Once the list is formed, every 50th person (50,000 / 1,000 = 50) would be chosen as a participant, starting from the selected starting point.

For example, if the starting point is 20, the 70th person on the list would be selected, then the 120th, and so on. Once the end of the list is reached and additional participants are needed, the count wraps around to the beginning of the list to complete the selection.

**Note:** To conduct systematic sampling, the researcher must first know the size of the target population.

## Types of Systematic Sampling

There are generally three methods for generating a systematic sample:

**Random Systematic Sampling** is the classic form, where subjects are selected at a predetermined interval. For example, to select a sample of 100 students from a population of 1,000, a researcher could select every 10th student from a list arranged in random order. This ensures every member of the population has an equal chance of being selected while maintaining the systematic sampling pattern.

**Linear Systematic Sampling** creates a skip pattern along a linear path rather than randomly selecting the sampling interval. Instead of selecting every nth member from the population, the selection follows a predetermined sequence -- such as selecting every 5th member, then every 7th, then every 9th, and so on. This method can be useful where a specific sequence or order exists along a linear path, such as geographic locations along a route.

**Circular Systematic Sampling** involves the sample wrapping around to the same starting point once it reaches the end. Once the sampling interval reaches the last member of the population, it wraps around to the beginning and continues. This method is typically used when the population exhibits cyclical patterns or has no clear start or end point. For example, a researcher studying tree growth in a forest might use circular systematic sampling by selecting trees at regular intervals along a circular path, ensuring comprehensive coverage of the forest area.

## Systematic Sampling vs. Cluster Sampling

Systematic sampling and cluster sampling differ in how they draw sample points from the population. Cluster sampling divides the population into clusters and then draws a simple random sample from each cluster. Systematic sampling uses a fixed interval applied to the larger population to create the sample.

Systematic sampling selects a random starting point from the population and then draws a sample from regular, fixed intervals based on the population size. Cluster sampling divides the population into clusters and then draws a simple random sample from each one.

Cluster sampling is considered less precise than other sampling methods but can save costs in obtaining samples. It is a two-step procedure that can be used when compiling a complete population list is difficult. For example, constructing the entire population of grocery store customers for interviews would be impractical. However, one could create a random subset of stores (the first step) and then interview a random sample of those stores' customers (the second step).

## Mistakes to Avoid in Systematic Sampling

A common pitfall is selecting an inappropriate sampling interval. Choosing too small an interval can lead to oversampling and increased sampling error, while choosing too large an interval can result in undersampling and a less representative sample. This error can be avoided by thoroughly understanding the full scope of the population before beginning to sample.

Another mistake is failing to account for potential bias in the sampling frame. If the frame does not represent the population of interest, systematic sampling may produce biased results. For example, if the frame includes only individuals from certain demographic groups or geographic locations, the sample will not reflect the diversity of the entire population. This type of error exists across all forms of sampling.

An additional consideration is whether systematic patterns or cycles exist within the population. If a periodic pattern or trend aligns with the sampling interval, certain segments of the population may be systematically over- or underrepresented. For example, imagine selecting random players from baseball rosters. If the rosters are ordered by position, you might end up selecting the same position from different teams because the population has a cyclical pattern.

## Limitations of Systematic Sampling

One risk statisticians must consider involves how the list used with the sampling interval is organized. If the population is arranged in a cyclical pattern that matches the sampling interval, the selected sample may be biased.

For example, a company's HR department wants to select a sample of employees and ask them about company policies. Employees are organized into teams of 20, each led by a manager. If the list used to select the sample is clustered by team, the statistician risks selecting only managers (or no managers at all), depending on the sampling interval.

## How Do You Perform Systematic Sampling?

To perform systematic sampling, first determine the total population size. Then select a random starting point and choose every nth member from the population based on a predetermined sampling interval.

## When Should I Use Systematic Sampling?

Use systematic sampling when you need a simple and efficient method to select a representative sample from a large population with a known, uniformly distributed structure, and when randomization is not feasible or necessary for your research objectives.

## What Are the Advantages of Systematic Sampling?

Systematic sampling is easy to conduct and understand, which is why it is generally favored by researchers. The central assumption -- that results represent most normal populations -- ensures even sampling across the entire population.

Moreover, systematic sampling provides a higher degree of control compared to other methods. It also carries a low risk factor, as the probability of data contamination is minimal.

## What Are the Disadvantages of Systematic Sampling?

The main disadvantage is that the population size must be known. Without knowing the specific number of participants in the population, systematic sampling cannot function effectively. For example, a statistician examining the ages of homeless people in a specific region who cannot accurately determine how many there are will have neither a population size nor a starting point. Another disadvantage is that the population must exhibit some natural randomness; otherwise, the risk of selecting similar instances increases, defeating the purpose of the sample.

## How Do Cluster Sampling and Systematic Sampling Differ?

Cluster sampling and systematic sampling differ in how they extract sample points from the included population. Cluster sampling divides the population into clusters, then draws a simple random sample from each cluster. Systematic sampling selects a random starting point, then draws a sample at regular fixed intervals from the population based on its size. Cluster sampling is more prone to larger sampling errors than systematic sampling, though it may be a less expensive process.

## The Bottom Line

Sampling can be an effective way to draw conclusions about a large group of people, items, or other subjects of interest. Systematic sampling is one of the most popular methods because it is cheaper and less time-consuming than other options. While imperfect, if you have a large data set with no pattern between intervals, systematic sampling can provide a reliable sample at relatively low cost.
