# What Is a Z-Score?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A z-score is a statistical measurement that describes a value's relationship to the mean of a group of values. It is measured in terms of standard deviations from the mean. In investing and trading, z-scores serve as a measure of an instrument's volatility that traders can use to help assess price variability.

### Key Takeaways

- A z-score is a statistical measurement of a score's relationship to the mean in a group of scores.
- Z-scores can reveal to traders whether a value is typical or atypical for a specified data set.
- In general, a z-score between -3.0 and 3.0 indicates that a stock is trading within three standard deviations of its mean.
- Traders have developed numerous methods using z-scores to identify trades, assess correlations between trading positions, and evaluate trading strategies.

## Understanding Z-Scores

A z-score is a statistical measure that quantifies the distance between a data point and the mean of a data set, expressed in terms of standard deviations. It indicates how many standard deviations a data point is from the distribution's mean.

A z-score of 0 indicates that the data point's score is identical to the mean score. A z-score of 1.0 indicates a value that is one standard deviation from the mean. Z-scores can be positive or negative -- a positive value indicates the score is above the mean, while a negative value indicates it is below.

Z-scores are sometimes confused with the Altman Z-score, which is calculated using factors from a company's financial statements. The Altman Z-score estimates the likelihood of a business going bankrupt within the next two years, whereas a z-score can be used to determine how far a stock's return deviates from its average return -- and much more.

**Note:** A z-score is also known as a standard score.

## How to Calculate a Z-Score

Calculating a z-score requires first determining the mean and standard deviation of the data. Once you have these figures, you can compute the z-score. Suppose you have the following variables:

- x = 57
- $\mu$ = 52
- $\sigma$ = 4

You would apply the formula:

- z = (57 - 52) / 4
- z = 1.25

Thus, the z-score for the chosen value indicates it is 1.25 standard deviations from the mean.

## How Z-Scores Are Used

In its most basic form, the z-score allows you to determine how far a stock's return deviates from the mean of a sample of stocks, measured in standard deviations. The mean you use could be the average of a stock's annual returns, the average return of its listed index, or the average return of a selected group of stocks.

Some traders use z-scores in more advanced evaluation methods, such as weighting each stock's returns in factor investing, where z-scores and standard deviations are used to rank stocks based on specific attributes. In the foreign exchange market, traders use z-scores and confidence limits to test a trading system's ability to generate winning and losing streaks.

## Z-Scores vs. Standard Deviation

In most large data sets (assuming a normal distribution), 99.7% of values lie between -3 and 3 standard deviations, 95% lie between -2 and 2 standard deviations, and 68% lie between -1 and 1 standard deviation.

Standard deviation indicates the amount of variation (or dispersion) within a given data set. For example, if one normally distributed data sample has a standard deviation of 3.1 and another has a standard deviation of 6.3, the model with an SD of 6.3 is more dispersed and will plot a lower, flatter peak than the sample with an SD of 3.1.

Distribution curves have both negative and positive sides, resulting in positive and negative standard deviations and z-scores. However, this only indicates which side of the mean a value falls on -- negative values lie to the left of the mean, positive values to the right.

The z-score shows the number of standard deviations a given data point lies from the mean. Therefore, the standard deviation must be calculated first, as the z-score uses it to convey the data point's variability.

## What Is a Z-Score?

A z-score is a way of determining how far data is from the population mean, measured in standard deviations. It tells us whether a data point is typical or unusual compared to the rest of the population, which is useful for spotting unusual values and comparing data across different groups.

## How Is a Z-Score Calculated?

A z-score is calculated by finding the difference between a data point and the data set's mean, then dividing that difference by the standard deviation to determine how many standard deviations the data point is from the mean.

## How Are Z-Scores Used in Real Life?

Z-scores are used in many real-life applications, including medical assessments, test scoring, business decisions, and measuring investment and trading opportunities. Traders who use statistical measures like z-scores to evaluate trading opportunities are known as quantitative traders.

## What Is a Good Z-Score?

The higher (or lower) the z-score, the farther the point is from the mean. This is not inherently good or bad -- it simply shows where the data falls in a normally distributed sample. When evaluating investments or opportunities, interpretation depends on preference. For example, some investors use a z-score range of -3.0 to 3.0 because 99.7% of normally distributed data falls within this range, while others may prefer -1.5 to 1.5 because they want scores closer to the mean.

## Why Are Z-Scores Important?

Z-scores are important because they tell you where your data sits within a distribution. For example, a z-score of 1.5 means the data point is 1.5 standard deviations from the mean. Since 68% of data falls within one standard deviation (in a normal distribution), a z-score of 1.5 might be considered relatively far from the mean.

## The Bottom Line

A z-score is a statistical measurement that tells you how far your data is from the mean (average) in a normally distributed sample. At the most basic level, investors and traders use quantitative analysis methods such as z-scores to determine how a stock performs relative to other stocks or its own historical performance. In more advanced applications, traders use z-scores to weight investments against ideal criteria, develop other indicators, and even attempt to predict the outcomes of trading strategies.
