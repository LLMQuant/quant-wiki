# What Is a Probability Distribution?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A probability distribution is a statistical function that describes all the possible values a random variable can take within a given range and their associated likelihoods. This range is bounded by minimum and maximum values. The exact position of possible values along the distribution depends on several factors, including the distribution's mean (average), standard deviation, skewness, and kurtosis.

### Key Takeaways

- A probability distribution depicts the expected outcomes for a given data-generating process.
- Probability distributions come in many shapes and characteristics.
- They are defined by their mean, standard deviation, skewness, and kurtosis.
- Investors use probability distributions to forecast returns on assets such as stocks over time and to hedge risk.

## How Probability Distributions Work

Perhaps the most common probability distribution is the normal distribution, or bell curve, though many other distributions are widely used. The data-generating process underlying a phenomenon typically dictates its probability distribution. This process is described by the probability density function.

Probability distributions can also be used to generate cumulative distribution functions (CDFs), which accumulate the probability of events occurring. CDFs always start at zero and ultimately reach 100%. [1]

Academics, financial analysts, and fund managers may evaluate a particular stock's probability distribution to assess the expected returns it may yield in the future.

**Important:** A stock's historical returns can be measured from any time interval and may comprise only a fraction of the stock's actual returns. This subjects the analysis to sampling error, which can be significantly reduced by increasing the sample size.

## Discrete vs. Continuous Probability Distributions

Discrete and continuous probability distributions are the two fundamental types, each describing a different kind of random variable. Understanding the difference between them is essential for correctly applying statistical methods and interpreting data.

Discrete probability distributions describe scenarios where the set of possible outcomes is countable -- either finite or countably infinite. These distributions apply when a random variable can take specific, distinct values.

For example, the number of heads in 10 coin flips or the number of customers arriving at a store in one hour are discrete random variables. In these cases, you can enumerate all possible outcomes (zero, one, two, and so on). Discrete distributions tend to exhibit "jumpy" behavior because outcomes are limited.

Continuous probability distributions, by contrast, apply when a random variable can take any value within a given range. These values are uncountable because there are infinitely many possibilities within any interval.

For example, the exact height of individuals in a population or the precise time required to complete a task are continuous variables. Continuous probability distributions produce smoother curves because the set of possible outcomes is much larger.

## Types of Probability Distributions

Probability distributions fall into several categories, including the normal, chi-square, binomial, and Poisson distributions. Each serves different purposes and represents different data-generating processes. [1][2]

The binomial distribution evaluates the probability that an event occurs a specific number of times over a given number of trials, where the probability remains constant across each trial. It could be generated by recording a basketball player's free-throw results in a game, where 1 represents a basket and 0 represents a miss.

Another example is flipping a coin and calculating the probability of heads appearing in 10 consecutive flips. The binomial distribution is discrete because valid outcomes can only be 0 or 1. [3]

The most widely used distribution is the normal distribution, which appears frequently in finance, investing, science, and engineering. It is fully characterized by its mean and standard deviation. The distribution is not skewed and exhibits kurtosis.

This gives the distribution a symmetric shape that, when plotted, appears as a bell curve. A standard normal distribution has a mean of zero, a standard deviation of one, zero skewness, and a kurtosis of 3.

In a normal distribution, approximately 68% of the data falls within one standard deviation of the mean, approximately 95% within two standard deviations, and 99.7% within three standard deviations. Unlike the binomial distribution, the normal distribution is continuous -- all possible values are represented, not just 0 and 1. [4]

**Note:** Probability refers to the likelihood that an event will occur, expressed as the ratio of favorable outcomes to the total number of possible outcomes. Odds, on the other hand, express the probability of an event occurring relative to the probability of it not occurring. For example, if the probability of winning a game is 0.25, the odds are 1:3 (one win for every three losses).

## The Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers states that as the number of trials increases, the average of the results will converge toward the expected value, or true probability. This principle ensures that the sample mean converges to the population mean as the number of observations grows, providing stability for statistical inference.

## Validity of a Probability Distribution

Determining whether a probability distribution is valid requires two steps. First, verify that each probability is greater than or equal to zero and less than or equal to one. Second, confirm that the sum of all probabilities equals one. If both conditions hold, the probability distribution is valid.

Probability measures the likelihood of an event occurring, expressed as the ratio of favorable outcomes to total possible outcomes. Odds express the probability of an event occurring relative to the probability of it not occurring. For example, if the probability of winning a game is 0.25, the odds are 1:3 (one win for every three losses).

## Probability Distributions and the Central Limit Theorem

The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) is a statistical principle stating that the distribution of the sum of a large number of independent, identically distributed random variables approaches a normal distribution.

The significance of this theorem is that it allows statisticians to make inferences about population parameters even when the population distribution is unknown, provided the sample size is sufficiently large.

A key implication of the CLT is that the sampling distribution of the sample mean will be approximately normal for large sample sizes.

For example, imagine a class where students vary in height, but the average height is approximately 5 feet with some variation. According to the CLT, the distribution of sample mean heights will tend toward a normal (bell-shaped) curve.
