# What Is a Normal Distribution?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A normal distribution, also known as a Gaussian distribution, is a probability distribution that is symmetric about the mean. It indicates that data near the mean occur more frequently than data far from the mean. When plotted, the normal distribution appears as a "bell curve."

### Key Takeaways

- The normal distribution is the formal term for the probability bell curve.
- In a standard normal distribution, the mean is zero, the standard deviation is 1, the skewness is zero, and the kurtosis is 3.
- Normal distributions are symmetric, but not all symmetric distributions are normal.

## Properties of the Normal Distribution

The normal distribution is the most commonly assumed distribution type in technical stock market analysis. A standard normal distribution has two parameters: the mean and the standard deviation. In a normal distribution, the mean (average), median (midpoint), and mode (most frequent observation) are all equal. These values represent the peak of the distribution, which falls off symmetrically on either side, with its width defined by the standard deviation.

The normal distribution model is central to the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), which states that means computed from independent, identically distributed random variables are approximately normally distributed, regardless of the original distribution type of those variables.

A normal distribution is a symmetric distribution. Symmetric distributions occur when a dividing line produces two mirror images. However, not all symmetric distributions are normal, as some data may exhibit two peaks or a series of hills rather than the single bell curve that characterizes a normal distribution.

## Observations

For all normal distributions, 68.2% of observations fall within one standard deviation of the mean, 95.4% fall within two standard deviations, and 99.7% fall within three standard deviations.

This fact is sometimes called the "empirical rule" -- a heuristic that describes where most data falls in a normal distribution. Data falling beyond three standard deviations ("3-sigma") is considered rare.

Skewness measures the symmetry of a distribution. The normal distribution is symmetric, with a skewness of zero. A distribution with skewness less than zero (negative skewness, or left-skewed) has a longer left tail, while positive skewness (right-skewed) means the right tail is longer.

Kurtosis measures the thickness of a distribution's tails. The normal distribution has a kurtosis of 3.0. Distributions with kurtosis greater than 3.0 exhibit tail data that exceeds the tails of the normal distribution (e.g., values five or more standard deviations from the mean).

This excess kurtosis is known statistically as leptokurtic, but is more commonly called "fat-tailed." Fat tails in financial markets describe what is known as tail risk. Distributions with kurtosis less than 3.0 (platykurtic) exhibit tails that are generally thinner than those of the normal distribution.

## Applications of the Normal Distribution in Finance

The assumption of normality is applied to asset prices and price behavior. Traders may plot price points to fit recent price action into a normal distribution. The farther the price action moves from the mean, the more likely an asset is overvalued or undervalued. Traders can use standard deviations to suggest potential trades. This type of trading is typically done over very short time frames, as longer horizons make it more difficult to select entry and exit points.

Similarly, many statistical theories attempt to model asset prices under the assumption that they follow a normal distribution. In reality, price distributions tend to exhibit fat tails, meaning kurtosis is often greater than three. Such assets experience price movements exceeding three standard deviations beyond the mean more frequently than a normal distribution would predict. Even if an asset's price behavior has conformed to a normal distribution for some period, there is no guarantee that past performance will reflect future behavior.

Many natural phenomena appear to follow a normal distribution. For example, the average human height is approximately 175 cm (5 feet 9 inches), including both men and women.

## What Does Normal Distribution Mean?

A normal distribution describes data that is symmetrically distributed around a mean value, with the width of the curve defined by the standard deviation. Visually, it appears as a "bell curve."

## Why Is the Normal Distribution Called "Normal"?

The normal distribution is technically known as the Gaussian distribution, but it came to be called "normal" in 19th-century scientific publications because many natural phenomena appeared to "normally deviate" from the mean. This concept of "normal variation" was articulated by Sir Francis Galton in his 1889 work *Natural Inheritance*.

## What Are the Limitations of the Normal Distribution in Finance?

Although the normal distribution is a well-established statistical concept, its application in finance can be limited because financial phenomena -- such as expected stock market returns -- do not fall neatly into a normal distribution. Prices tend to follow a log-normal distribution, meaning they are right-skewed with fatter tails. Consequently, over-reliance on the bell curve for predictions can produce unreliable results. While most analysts are aware of this limitation, overcoming it remains challenging because it is often unclear which alternative statistical distribution to use.
