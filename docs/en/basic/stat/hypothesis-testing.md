# What Is Hypothesis Testing?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Hypothesis testing, sometimes called significance testing, is a procedure in statistics through which an analyst tests assumptions about a population parameter. The specific methodology depends on the nature of the data and the purpose of the analysis.

Hypothesis testing is used to assess the plausibility of a hypothesis using sample data. Such data may come from a larger population or a data-generating process. The term "population" is used for both cases in the following discussion.

### Key Takeaways

- Hypothesis testing is used to assess the plausibility of a hypothesis using sample data.
- The test provides evidence regarding the plausibility of the hypothesis.
- Statistical analysts test a hypothesis by measuring and examining a random sample drawn from the population under analysis.
- The four steps of hypothesis testing are: stating the hypotheses, formulating an analysis plan, analyzing the sample data, and interpreting the results.

## How Hypothesis Testing Works

In hypothesis testing, an analyst tests a statistical sample with the goal of providing evidence about the plausibility of the null hypothesis. Statistical analysts measure and examine a random sample of the population being analyzed. All analysts use a random population sample to test two distinct hypotheses: the null hypothesis and the alternative hypothesis.

The null hypothesis is typically an equality assumption about the population parameters. For example, a null hypothesis may state that the population mean return is equal to zero. The alternative hypothesis is effectively the opposite of the null hypothesis. As such, they are mutually exclusive, and only one can be true. However, one of the two hypotheses will always be correct.

**Important:** The null hypothesis is a statement about a population parameter, such as the population mean, that is assumed to be true.

## Example of Hypothesis Testing

Suppose someone wants to test whether a coin truly has a 50% probability of landing on heads. The null hypothesis would be that 50% is correct, and the alternative hypothesis would be that 50% is not correct. Mathematically, the null hypothesis is expressed as Ho: P = 0.5. The alternative hypothesis is denoted "Ha" and is identical to the null hypothesis except that the equality sign is crossed out, indicating it is not equal to 50%.

A random sample of 100 coin flips is taken. If the results show 40 heads and 60 tails, the analyst would assume that the coin does not have a fair 50% probability of landing heads and would reject the null hypothesis in favor of the alternative hypothesis.

If the result were 48 heads and 52 tails, it is plausible that the coin is fair and could still produce such a result. In this case, the null hypothesis is "accepted," and the analyst notes that the difference between the expected result (50 heads and 50 tails) and the observed result (48 heads and 52 tails) is "explainable by chance alone."

## When Did Hypothesis Testing Begin?

Some statisticians attribute the first hypothesis test to satirist John Arbuthnot in 1710, who studied male and female births in England after observing that slightly more males were born in nearly every year. Arbuthnot calculated that the probability of this occurring by chance was very small, and therefore attributed it to "divine providence."

## What Are the Benefits of Hypothesis Testing?

Hypothesis testing helps assess the accuracy of new ideas or theories by comparing them against data. This enables researchers to determine whether evidence supports their hypothesis, thereby avoiding false claims and conclusions. Hypothesis testing also provides a framework for decision-making based on data rather than personal opinion or bias. By relying on statistical analysis, hypothesis testing helps reduce the influence of random chance and confounding variables, providing a robust framework for drawing informed conclusions.

## What Are the Limitations of Hypothesis Testing?

Hypothesis testing relies entirely on data and does not provide a comprehensive understanding of the subject under study. Additionally, the accuracy of results depends on the quality of available data and the statistical methods employed. Inaccurate data or improperly formulated hypotheses can lead to erroneous conclusions or test failures. Hypothesis testing can also produce errors, such as when an analyst incorrectly accepts or rejects a null hypothesis. These errors can lead to false conclusions or a failure to identify significant patterns or relationships in the data.

## Conclusion

Hypothesis testing is a statistical process that helps researchers determine the reliability of a study. Using properly formulated hypotheses and a sequence of statistical tests, individuals or organizations can make inferences about the population under study and draw conclusions based on the data presented. All hypothesis testing methods follow the same four-step process: stating the hypotheses, formulating an analysis plan, analyzing the sample data, and interpreting the results.
