# What Is a Linear Relationship?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A linear relationship (or linear association) is a statistical term describing a straight-line relationship between two variables. A linear relationship can be expressed graphically, where the variable and constant are connected by a straight line, or mathematically, where the independent variable is multiplied by a slope coefficient and added to a constant to determine the dependent variable.

A linear relationship can be contrasted with a polynomial or nonlinear (curved) relationship.

### Key Takeaways

- A linear relationship (or linear association) is a statistical term describing a straight-line relationship between two variables.
- A linear relationship can be expressed graphically or as a mathematical equation of the form y = mx + b.
- Linear relationships are quite common in everyday life.

## Formula for a Linear Relationship

Mathematically, a linear relationship satisfies the following equation:

$$ \begin{aligned} &y = mx + b \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &m=\text{slope}\\ &b=\text{y-intercept}\\ \end{aligned} $$

In this equation, "x" and "y" are two variables related by the parameters "m" and "b." Graphically, y = mx + b plots as a straight line in the x-y plane with slope "m" and y-intercept "b." The y-intercept "b" is the value of "y" when x = 0. The slope "m" can be calculated from any two points (x1, y1) and (x2, y2):

$$ m = \frac{(y_2 - y_1)}{(x_2 - x_1)} $$

## What a Linear Relationship Tells You

To properly qualify as a linear relationship, an equation must satisfy three criteria: the equation expressing the linear relationship must contain no more than two variables, all variables in the equation must be of the first degree, and the equation must graph as a straight line.

A commonly used linear relationship is correlation, which describes how one variable changes linearly relative to another.

In econometrics, linear regression is a commonly used method for generating linear relationships to explain various phenomena. It is typically used to extrapolate future predictions from past events. However, not all relationships are linear. Some data describes curved relationships (such as polynomial relationships), and some data cannot be parameterized.

## Linear Functions

Mathematically, similar to a linear relationship, a linear function can be written in one variable as:

$$ \begin{aligned} &f(x) = mx + b \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &m=\text{slope}\\ &b=\text{y-intercept}\\ \end{aligned} $$

This is identical to the linear relationship formula, except that f(x) replaces y. This substitution highlights that x maps to f(x), whereas using y simply indicates that x and y are two quantities related through A and B.

$$ c \times f(A +B) = c \times f(A) + c \times f(B) $$

## Examples of Linear Relationships

Linear relationships are quite common in everyday life. Take the concept of speed: the formula for calculating speed is the ratio of distance to time. If a traveler on California Highway 99 drives 44.1 miles from Sacramento to Marysville in approximately 45 minutes, their travel speed is nearly 60 miles per hour.

Although this equation involves multiple variables, it remains a linear equation because one of the variables is always constant (distance).

A linear relationship can also be found in the equation: distance = speed x time. Since distance is typically a positive number, this linear relationship will be represented in the upper-right quadrant of a linear graph.

If a tandem bicycle travels at 30 miles per hour for 20 hours, the riders will cover 600 miles. Graphically, with distance on the Y-axis and time on the X-axis, the line tracking distance over 20 hours extends outward from the intersection of the X and Y axes.

Converting between Celsius and Fahrenheit (or vice versa) uses the following equations, which graph as linear relationships:

$$ \degree C = \frac{5}{9}(\degree F - 32) $$

$$ \degree F = \frac{9}{5}\degree C + 32 $$

Suppose the independent variable is the size of a house (measured in square feet) and the dependent variable is the house's market price. The relationship is: house size multiplied by the slope coefficient of 207.65, plus the constant $10,500. If a house is 1,250 square feet, its market value is (1,250 x 207.65) + $10,500 = $270,062.50. Graphically and mathematically, it looks as follows:

In this example, as the size of the house increases, the market value of the house increases linearly.

Some linear relationships between objects can be described as "proportional." This relationship takes the form:

$$ \begin{aligned} &Y = k \times X \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &k=\text{constant}\\ &Y, X=\text{proportional quantities}\\ \end{aligned} $$

When analyzing behavioral data, a perfect linear relationship between variables is rare. However, trend lines can often be found in the data that form a rough linear relationship. For example, one could observe daily ice cream sales and daily high temperatures as two variables interacting on a graph and find a rough linear relationship between them.

## What Is a Positive Linear Relationship?

A positive linear relationship appears as an upward-sloping straight line on a graph. This means that if one variable increases, the other also increases. Conversely, a negative linear relationship appears as a downward-sloping straight line; if one variable increases, the other decreases.

## What Is a Nonlinear Relationship?

A nonlinear relationship can be observed through a scatter plot on a graph that follows some pattern, but the pattern is not linear -- meaning the representation is not a straight line.

## Can You Give an Example of a Linear Relationship in Statistics?

An example of a linear relationship is a worker paid by the hour. The more hours they work, the more money they earn. This relationship is linear because each additional hour of work corresponds to an equal increase in earnings.

## Summary

A linear relationship in statistics shows a straight-line relationship between two variables. It typically demonstrates a correlation between two variables. Although no behavioral relationship perfectly produces a true linear relationship, trends in the data can often be found to suggest one.
