# Confidence Intervals: The Probability That a Population Parameter Falls Between a Set of Values
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Analysts typically use confidence intervals that contain 95% or 99% of the expected observations. Thus, if a statistical model produces a point estimate of 10.00 with a 95% confidence interval of 9.50 to 10.50, it means we are 95% confident that the true value falls within that range.

Statisticians and other analysts use confidence intervals to understand the statistical significance of their estimates, inferences, or predictions. If a confidence interval contains zero (or some other null hypothesis value), one cannot satisfactorily claim that the results generated by a test or experiment are attributable to a specific cause rather than to chance.

### Key Takeaways

- A confidence interval displays the probability that a parameter will fall between a pair of values around the mean.
- Confidence intervals measure the degree of uncertainty or certainty in a sampling method.
- They are also used in hypothesis testing and regression analysis.
- Statisticians often use p-values in conjunction with confidence intervals to gauge statistical significance.
- They are most commonly constructed using confidence levels of 95% or 99%.

## Understanding Confidence Intervals

Confidence intervals measure the degree of uncertainty or certainty in a sampling method. They can take any number of probability limits, with the most common being 95% or 99% confidence levels. Confidence intervals are computed using statistical methods such as the t-test.

Statisticians use confidence intervals to measure the uncertainty in a sample-based estimate of a population parameter. For example, a researcher randomly selects different samples from the same population and computes a confidence interval for each sample to see how well it might represent the true value of the population variable. The resulting datasets all differ; some intervals include the true population parameter and some do not.

A confidence interval is a range of values, bounded above and below by the statistic's mean, that likely contains an unknown population parameter. The confidence level refers to the percentage of probability, or certainty, that the confidence interval would contain the true population parameter when drawn at random many times.

In other words, "we are 99% confident (confidence level) that most of these samples (confidence interval) contain the true population parameter."

The biggest misconception about confidence intervals is that they represent the percentage of data from a given sample that falls between the upper and lower bounds. For example, one might incorrectly interpret the 99% confidence interval of 70 to 78 inches as meaning 99% of the random sample data falls between these two numbers.

This interpretation is incorrect, although a separate statistical analysis method does make such a determination. Doing so requires identifying the sample's mean and standard deviation and plotting these figures on a bell curve.

**Important:** Confidence interval and confidence level are related but not exactly the same.

## Calculating a Confidence Interval

Suppose a group of researchers is studying the heights of high school basketball players. The researchers take a random sample from the population and establish a mean height of 74 inches.

The mean of 74 inches is a point estimate of the population mean. A point estimate by itself has limited utility because it does not reveal the associated uncertainty; you cannot judge how far this 74-inch sample mean might be from the population mean. What is missing is the degree of uncertainty in a single sample.

Confidence intervals provide more information than point estimates. By using the sample's mean and standard deviation to establish a 95% confidence interval and assuming a bell-curve normal distribution, the researchers arrive at an upper and lower bound that contains the true mean 95% of the time.

Suppose this interval is between 72 inches and 76 inches. If the researchers take 100 random samples from the population of high school basketball players, the mean should fall between 72 and 76 inches in 95 of those samples.

If the researchers want greater confidence, they can expand the interval to 99%. Doing so invariably creates a broader range, as it makes room for a greater number of sample means. If they establish the 99% confidence interval as 70 inches to 78 inches, they can expect 99 out of 100 samples evaluated to contain a mean between those two numbers.

A 90% confidence level, on the other hand, means you can expect 90% of the interval estimates to include the population parameter, and so on.

## Why Are Confidence Intervals Used?

Statisticians use confidence intervals to measure the uncertainty in a sample variable. For example, a researcher randomly selects different samples from the same population and computes a confidence interval for each sample to see how it might represent the true value of the population variable. The resulting datasets all differ, with some intervals including the true population parameter and some not.

## What Is a Common Misconception About Confidence Intervals?

The biggest misconception about confidence intervals is that they represent the percentage of data from a given sample that falls between the upper and lower bounds. In other words, it would be incorrect to assume that a 99% confidence interval means 99% of the data in a random sample falls between these limits. What it actually means is that one can be 99% confident the range will contain the population mean.

## What Is a t-Test?

Confidence intervals are conducted using statistical methods such as the t-test. A t-test is an inferential statistic used to determine whether there is a significant difference between the means of two groups, which may be related to certain characteristics. Computing a t-test requires three key data values: the difference between the means of each dataset (called the mean difference), the standard deviation of each group, and the number of data values in each group.

## How Do You Interpret P-Values and Confidence Intervals?

A p-value is a statistical measure used to validate a hypothesis against observed data. It measures the probability of obtaining the observed results assuming the null hypothesis is true. Generally, a p-value less than 0.05 is considered statistically significant, indicating the null hypothesis should be rejected. This roughly corresponds to the probability that the null hypothesis value (typically zero) is contained within the 95% confidence interval.

## Conclusion

Confidence intervals allow analysts to understand whether the results of a statistical analysis are real or due to chance. When trying to make inferences or predictions based on sample data, some uncertainty exists about whether the analysis matches the true population under study. A confidence interval delineates the range within which the probable true value should fall.
