# What Is Statistical Significance?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Statistical significance is a determination made by an analyst that the results in a dataset are not explainable by chance alone. Statistical hypothesis testing is the method by which this determination is made. The test yields a p-value -- the probability of observing results as extreme as those in the data, assuming the results are truly due to chance.

Generally, a p-value of 5% or lower is considered statistically significant.

### Key Takeaways

- Statistical significance determines whether a relationship between two or more variables is caused by something other than chance.
- It is used to provide evidence regarding the plausibility of the null hypothesis, which posits that variation in the data is due solely to random chance.
- Statistical hypothesis testing is used to determine whether a dataset's results are statistically significant.
- A p-value of 5% or lower is generally regarded as statistically significant.

## Understanding Statistical Significance

Statistical significance is a judgment about the null hypothesis, which suggests that results are due to chance alone. A dataset is deemed statistically significant when the p-value is sufficiently small.

When the p-value is large, the results can be explained by chance, and the data is considered consistent with the null hypothesis -- though this does not prove the null hypothesis is true.

When the p-value is sufficiently small, typically 5% or lower, the results are not easily explained by chance, and the data is considered inconsistent with the null hypothesis. In this case, the null hypothesis is rejected, usually in favor of a more systematic explanation. [1]

**Important:** Statistical significance is frequently used in trials of new pharmaceutical drugs, vaccine testing, and pathology studies. This can provide investors with information about the likelihood of a company's new product launch being successful.

## Examples of Statistical Significance

Suppose financial analyst Alex is curious about whether some investors had advance knowledge of a company's sudden failure. Alex decides to compare the average daily market returns before and after the failure to determine whether there is a statistically significant difference between the two means.

The study's p-value was 28% (>5%), indicating that a difference as large as the observed one (-0.0033 to +0.0007) is not unusual under a chance-only explanation. Therefore, the data did not provide strong evidence that anyone had advance knowledge of the failure.

If the p-value had been 0.01%, far below 5%, then the observed difference would be highly unusual under a chance-only explanation. In that case, Alex might decide to reject the null hypothesis and investigate further whether some traders had prior knowledge.

Statistical significance is also used to test new medical products, including drugs, devices, and vaccines. Publicly released reports of statistical significance also provide investors with information about the success of a company's new product launches.

Suppose a leading diabetes drug company reports that a new insulin showed a statistically significant reduction in diabetes during testing. The test was conducted over 26 weeks with randomized treatment among diabetes patients, and the data showed a p-value of 4%. This signals to investors and regulators that the data indeed shows a statistically significant reduction in diabetes. [2]

Pharmaceutical company stock prices are frequently influenced by announcements of statistical significance for their new products. [3]

## How Is Statistical Significance Determined?

Statistical hypothesis testing is used to determine whether data is statistically significant and whether a phenomenon can be explained as a mere byproduct of chance. Statistical significance is a judgment about the null hypothesis, which posits that results exist only due to chance. Rejecting the null hypothesis is a necessary condition for the data to be deemed statistically significant.

## What Is a P-Value?

A p-value is a measure of the probability that observed differences occurred solely due to random chance. When the p-value is sufficiently small (5% or lower), the results are not easily explained by chance, and the null hypothesis can be rejected. When the p-value is large, the data can be considered consistent with the null hypothesis.

## Uses of Statistical Significance

Statistical significance is frequently used to test the effectiveness of new medical products, including drugs, devices, and vaccines. Publicly available statistical significance reports also provide investors with information about a company's new product launch success. Stock prices of pharmaceutical companies are often significantly affected by statistical significance announcements for new products. [4]

## Conclusion

Statistical significance is the outcome of hypothesis testing, yielding a p-value -- the probability that two or more variables are related for reasons other than chance. Generally, a p-value of 5% is considered the threshold. The lower the p-value, the greater the statistical significance of the dataset's results.

This form of testing is frequently used to evaluate pharmaceutical trials, and investors -- particularly those looking to assess companies launching new products -- also stand to benefit from it.
