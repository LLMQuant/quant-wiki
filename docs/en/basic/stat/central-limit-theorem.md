# What Is the Central Limit Theorem (CLT)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

In probability theory, the Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the distribution of sample means approximates a normal distribution (the "bell curve") as the sample size increases, regardless of the population's actual distribution shape.

In other words, the CLT is a statistical premise asserting that when sufficiently large samples are drawn from a population with finite variance, the mean of the sampled variable will approximate the overall population mean. Furthermore, as the sample size grows, these samples approximate a normal distribution, and their variance converges to the population variance, all consistent with the law of large numbers.

Although this concept was first introduced by Abraham de Moivre in 1733, it was not formally named the Central Limit Theorem until 1920, when Hungarian mathematician George Polya coined the term. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- The Central Limit Theorem (CLT) states that the distribution of sample means approximates a normal distribution as the sample size increases, regardless of the population's distribution.
- A sample size of 30 or more is generally considered sufficient for the CLT to hold.
- A key aspect of the CLT is that the average of the sample means and standard deviations will equal the population mean and standard deviation.
- A sufficiently large sample size enables more accurate predictions of population characteristics.
- In finance, the CLT is valuable when analyzing large numbers of securities to estimate portfolio distributions and characteristics of returns, risk, and correlation.

## Understanding the Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

According to the CLT, the mean of a data sample will be closer to the mean of the overall population as the sample size increases, regardless of the actual distribution of the data. In other words, the data is accurate whether the distribution is normal or aberrant.

As a general rule, sample sizes of approximately 30 are considered sufficient for the CLT to hold, meaning the distribution of sample means is fairly close to a normal distribution. The more samples drawn, the more the plotted results take on the shape of a normal distribution. [3]

The CLT is often used in conjunction with the law of large numbers, which states that the average of sample means will increasingly approach the population mean as the sample size grows -- an extremely useful property for accurately predicting population characteristics.

## Key Components of the Central Limit Theorem

The CLT comprises several key characteristics. These primarily revolve around samples, sample sizes, and the data population.

## Applications of the CLT in Finance

The CLT is particularly useful when examining the returns of individual stocks or broader indices, since the analysis is relatively straightforward and the requisite financial data is readily available. Consequently, investors of all types rely on the CLT to analyze stock returns, construct portfolios, and manage risk.

For example, if an investor wishes to analyze the overall return of a stock index comprising 1,000 stocks, they can randomly sample a subset of stocks to estimate the expected return of the entire index. To ensure reliability, at least 30 to 50 randomly selected stocks from different sectors should be sampled for the CLT to hold. Additionally, previously selected stocks should be replaced with different names to help eliminate bias.

## Why Is the Central Limit Theorem So Useful?

The CLT is invaluable when analyzing large datasets because it allows the assumption that the sampling distribution of the mean will be normally distributed in most cases. This greatly simplifies statistical analysis and inference. For instance, investors can use the CLT to aggregate individual security performance data and generate a distribution of sample means that represents the overall distribution of security returns.

## Why Is the Minimum Sample Size for the CLT 30?

A sample size of 30 is the commonly cited minimum for applying the Central Limit Theorem in statistics. [6] The larger the sample size, the more likely it is to be representative of the overall population dataset.

## What Is the Formula for the Central Limit Theorem?

The CLT does not have a specific formula in practice. Its principle applies straightforwardly: as long as the sample size is sufficiently large, the sampling distribution will approximate a normal distribution, and the sample mean will converge to the population mean. Therefore, with a sample size of at least 30, we can begin treating the data analysis as following a normal distribution.
