# What Are Treasury Notes?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A Treasury note (T-note) is a marketable U.S. government debt security with a fixed interest rate and a maturity between two and ten years.

Treasury notes are available through either competitive or non-competitive bids. With a competitive bid, investors specify the yield they desire, at the risk that their bid may not be approved. With a non-competitive bid, investors accept whatever yield is determined at auction.

### Key Takeaways

- A Treasury note is a U.S. government debt security with a fixed interest rate and a maturity of two to ten years.
- Treasury notes can be obtained through competitive bids (where investors specify the yield) or non-competitive bids (where investors accept the yield determined at auction).
- Treasury notes are similar to Treasury bonds, the only difference being their maturity -- Treasury bonds carry maturities of 20 to 30 years.

## Understanding Treasury Notes

Treasury notes are issued with maturities of two, three, five, seven, and ten years, making them a widely favored investment with a large and liquid secondary market.

Interest on Treasury notes is paid semiannually until maturity. Interest income is exempt from state and local taxes but remains subject to federal taxation, similar to Treasury bonds and Treasury bills.

Treasury notes, bonds, and bills are all debt instruments issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury. The primary difference among them is maturity. Treasury bonds, for example, carry maturities exceeding 10 years and extending up to 30 years, making them the longest sovereign fixed-income securities.

## Treasury Notes and Interest Rate Risk

The longer a Treasury note's maturity, the greater its exposure to interest rate risk. Beyond credit strength, the value of a note or bond depends on its sensitivity to changes in interest rates. Rate changes are typically driven by central bank policy or shifts in the shape of the yield curve.

These fixed-income instruments differ in their sensitivity to interest rate changes, meaning that price declines vary in magnitude. Sensitivity to rate changes is measured by duration, expressed in years. The factors used to calculate duration include the coupon rate, yield, present value, final maturity, and call features.

A clear example of an absolute interest rate change occurred in December 2015, when the Federal Reserve raised the federal funds rate by 25 basis points, moving the range from 0%--0.25% to 0.25%--0.50%. This increase in the benchmark rate caused prices of all outstanding U.S. Treasury notes and bonds to decline.

## Special Considerations

Beyond the benchmark rate, investor expectations can also drive fluctuations in the yield curve, known as yield curve risk. This risk relates to the steepness or flatness of the yield curve, reflecting changes in yields across similar securities with different maturities.

For example, a steepening yield curve indicates that the spread between short-term and long-term rates is widening, with long-term rates rising faster than short-term rates. If short-term rates exceed long-term rates, the result is an inverted yield curve.

Consequently, longer-term Treasury note prices decline relative to shorter-term notes during a steepening event. In a flattening yield curve, the spread narrows, and shorter-term Treasury note prices decline relative to longer-term notes.
