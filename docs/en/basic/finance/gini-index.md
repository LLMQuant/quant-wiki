# What Is the Gini Index?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Gini index measures the income or wealth distribution of a nation's population as an indicator of income inequality. Developed by Italian statistician Corrado Gini in 1912, [1] the coefficient ranges from 0 (or 0%) to 1 (or 100%), where 0 represents perfect equality and 1 represents perfect inequality. [2]

According to World Bank data, South Africa has the highest level of income inequality in the world, with a Gini index of 63.0%. Norway has the lowest, at 22.7%. The United States registers at 39.8%. [3]

### Key Takeaways

- The Gini index measures the distribution of income across a population.
- A higher Gini index indicates greater inequality, with high-income individuals receiving a larger share of total national income.
- Global inequality, as measured by the Gini index, has risen steadily over the past several centuries and peaked during the COVID-19 pandemic.
- Limitations of the Gini index may exaggerate income inequality and can obscure important information about income distribution.

## Understanding the Gini Index

If every resident of a country earned the same income, the income Gini coefficient would be 0. If a single resident earned all the income and everyone else earned nothing, the coefficient would be 1.

The same analysis applies to wealth distribution (the "wealth Gini coefficient"), but because wealth is harder to measure than income, Gini coefficients usually refer to income and are often reported simply as the "Gini coefficient" or "Gini index" without specifying that they refer to income. Wealth Gini coefficients tend to be much higher than income Gini coefficients.

Even in wealthy countries, the Gini index measures net income rather than net wealth, so a nation's wealth may be concentrated among a few individuals even if income distribution is relatively equal.

The Gini coefficient is an important tool for analyzing income or wealth distribution within a country or region, but it should not be mistaken for an absolute measure of income or wealth.

A high-income country and a low-income country can have the same Gini coefficient, as long as incomes are distributed similarly within each. For example, according to OECD data, Turkey and the United States have nearly identical Gini coefficients, despite Turkey having a far lower per capita GDP than the United States. [4][5]

## Graphical Representation of the Gini Index

The Gini index is frequently illustrated graphically through the Lorenz curve, which plots the cumulative share of income on the vertical axis against income-ranked percentiles of the population on the horizontal axis. The Gini coefficient equals the area between the line of perfect equality (which by definition has an area below it of 0.5) and the Lorenz curve, divided by the area below the line of perfect equality. In other words, it is twice the area between the Lorenz curve and the line of perfect equality. [2][6]

## The Global Gini Index

During the 19th and 20th centuries, the global Gini coefficient rose steadily.

In 1820, the global Gini coefficient stood at 0.50, while in both 1980 and 1992, the figure was 0.657.

The World Inequality Lab's most recent estimate for 2020 puts the global Gini coefficient at 0.67. [7][8]

Source: The World Bank

COVID-19 dealt a further blow to income equality. According to the World Bank, the Gini coefficient increased by approximately 1.5 percentage points in the five years following major epidemics such as Ebola and Zika. Economists believe COVID-19 drove the Gini coefficient up by 0.5 percentage points from 2019 to 2020, the largest single-year increase in global inequality since World War II. [8][9]

Some of the world's poorest countries have the highest Gini coefficients, while many of the lowest are found in wealthier European nations. However, the relationship between income inequality and per capita GDP is not perfectly negative, and the relationship has shifted over time. [10]

Michail Moatsos of Utrecht University and Joery Baten of the University of Tubingen showed that from 1820 to 1929, inequality rose slightly as per capita GDP increased, then gradually declined. From 1950 to 1970, inequality tended to fall once per capita GDP exceeded a certain threshold. From 1980 to 2000, inequality declined with higher per capita GDP, then curved sharply back upward. [11]

## Limitations of the Gini Index

Although the Gini coefficient is useful for analyzing economic inequality, it has several shortcomings.

The accuracy of the metric depends on reliable GDP and income data. Shadow economies and informal economic activity exist in every country.

In developing countries and at the lower end of the income distribution within countries, informal economic activity tends to represent a larger portion of true economic output. In both cases, this means that the measured Gini index will overstate true income inequality.

Accurate wealth data are even harder to obtain, given the prevalence of tax havens that make it difficult to determine the full extent of assets held by the wealthy.

Another flaw is that very different income distributions can result in identical Gini coefficients. Because the Gini coefficient distills a two-dimensional area (the gap between the Lorenz curve and the equality line) into a single number, it obscures information about the shape of inequality.

In everyday terms, this is similar to describing the content of a photograph solely by the length of one of its edges or the simple average brightness value of its pixels.

While supplementing with the Lorenz curve can provide more information in this regard, it also does not show demographic variation among subgroups in the distribution, such as income distribution across age, race, or social groups.

In this sense, understanding demographics can be important for interpreting what a given Gini coefficient represents. For example, a large retired population pushes the Gini coefficient higher.

## Which Country Has the Highest Gini Index?

South Africa, with a Gini coefficient of 63.0%, is currently recognized as the country with the highest level of income inequality.

The World Population Review attributes this extreme inequality to racial, gender, and geographical discrimination, with white males and urban workers in South Africa earning far more than everyone else. [8]

## What Does a Gini Index of 50 Mean?

The Gini index ranges from 0% to 100%, where 0 represents perfect equality and 100 represents perfect inequality. A Gini index of 50 marks the midpoint and can be considered indicative of a country with significant income inequality.

As of 2024, only 14 countries in the world have a Gini index of 50 or above. [12]

## Is the U.S. Gini Coefficient High or Low?

According to the World Bank, the U.S. has an estimated Gini coefficient of 39.8. For an advanced economy, this is a high reading.

Economists attribute rising income inequality in the United States to factors including technological change, globalization, the decline of labor unions, and the erosion of the minimum wage's real value. [12][13]

## The Bottom Line

If the gap between rich and poor continues to widen, assessing income disparities may become increasingly important. The Gini index offers a useful starting point for measuring income inequality.

Understanding the Gini index is not a panacea, but it can quantify and track the direction a society is heading, opening the door to dialogue and potential solutions.

Bear in mind, however, that the measure has its limitations. The coefficient is only as reliable as the data used to compute it, and a single numerical reading cannot tell the whole story.
