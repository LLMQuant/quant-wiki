# What Is a Leveraged Buyout?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A leveraged buyout (LBO) is the acquisition of one company by another using a significant amount of borrowed money to fund the purchase. The borrowing may take the form of bonds or loans. The assets of the target company are typically used as collateral for the loans, along with the assets of the acquiring company.

### Key Takeaways

- A leveraged buyout (LBO) occurs when the acquisition of a company is funded almost entirely with borrowed money.
- The popularity of LBOs declined after the 2008 financial crisis but has rebounded in recent years.
- In an LBO, the ratio of debt to equity used for the acquisition is typically as high as possible.
- LBOs are considered a ruthless and predatory business tactic because the target company's assets can be leveraged against it.

## Understanding Leveraged Buyouts (LBOs)

In an LBO, the ratio of debt to equity used for the acquisition is typically pushed as high as possible. The exact amount of debt depends on market lending conditions, investor appetite, and the cash flows the company is expected to generate after the acquisition. [1] Bonds issued in the acquisition are generally not investment-grade and are therefore classified as junk bonds, precisely because of this high debt-to-equity ratio.

**Important:** The purpose of an LBO is to enable companies to make large acquisitions without committing a significant amount of their own capital.

Returns on an LBO are typically realized through three channels:

- The company pays down debt, and this deleveraging process increases equity value.
- Investors improve margins by eliminating unnecessary expenses and boosting sales.
- The company is sold at exit at a higher valuation multiple than the one paid at entry -- a process known as multiple expansion.

Private equity groups that conduct LBOs have developed a reputation for needing to improve margins quickly, often perceived as ruthless and predatory. To achieve this, many investors implement aggressive cost-cutting measures, which may include workforce reductions.

To realize gains, private equity investors must sell or monetize their investments within a relatively short time frame. An LBO investment is typically held for five to seven years, though holding periods may be shorter or longer.

Exit strategies include:

- Taking a private company public through an IPO
- Selling to a competitor
- Conducting a secondary private equity transaction through another LBO round

Typically, an LBO is pursued because a private equity group has identified a company as a suitable target. A suitable target generally delivers annualized returns above 20%, meaning the company must generate sufficient cash to service debt ("deleverage") and should present opportunities to improve profitability and enterprise value.

## Examples of Leveraged Buyouts

One of the largest LBOs on record was the 2006 acquisition of HCA Healthcare by Kohlberg Kravis Roberts (KKR), Bain Capital, and Merrill Lynch, which valued HCA at approximately $33 billion. [2]

The volume of large-scale deals declined after the 2008 financial crisis, but as the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic receded, large LBOs returned. In 2021, a group of financiers led by Blackstone announced an LBO of medical equipment manufacturer Medline, valued at approximately $34 billion. [3][4]

Vista Equity Partners and Elliott Investment Management executed the first major LBO of 2022 in January, acquiring software maker Citrix Systems, Inc. for $13 billion. [5]

According to Yahoo Finance in December 2023, leveraged loans were expected to enter a new boom in 2024. [6]

## How Does a Leveraged Buyout Work?

An LBO takes place when a company seeks to acquire another company by borrowing a substantial amount of money. The acquirer typically issues bonds backed by the combined assets of both companies, allowing the target company's assets to serve as collateral.

Although large-scale LBOs experienced a resurgence in the early 2020s, they are generally viewed as a predatory or hostile tactic. [7]

## Why Do Leveraged Buyouts Happen?

LBOs are commonly used to take a public company private or to spin off a portion of a business. They can also be used to transfer private assets, such as in a change of small-business ownership. The primary advantage of an LBO is that the acquiring company can purchase a much larger target on a relatively small equity base.

## What Types of Companies Are Suitable for LBOs?

Private equity firms typically favor mature companies in stable industries for leveraged buyouts rather than startups or more speculative sectors. [8] The best LBO candidates generally have stable, reliable operating cash flows, established product lines, strong management teams, and viable exit strategies that allow the acquirer to realize returns.

## Conclusion

A leveraged buyout (LBO) is the acquisition of a company funded primarily through borrowed capital. Companies typically pursue LBOs to take a target private or to divest part of an existing business.

Because the target company has little control over the approval of the transaction and its own assets may be leveraged against it, LBOs are often regarded as a predatory business tactic. Activity declined after the 2008 financial crisis but has since recovered.
