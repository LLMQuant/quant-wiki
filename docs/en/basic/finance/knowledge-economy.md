# What Is the Knowledge Economy?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The knowledge economy is a system of consumption and production based on intellectual capital. In particular, it refers to the ability to capitalize on scientific discoveries and applied research.

The knowledge economy accounts for a large share of activity in most highly developed economies. In a knowledge economy, a significant component of value may consist of intangible assets such as the value of its workers' knowledge or intellectual property.

### Key Takeaways

- The knowledge economy describes the contemporary commercialization of science and academic research.
- In the knowledge economy, research-based innovation is commodified through patents and other forms of intellectual property.
- The knowledge economy sits at the intersection of private entrepreneurship, academia, and government-funded research.
- Knowledge-related industries represent a large share of activity in most highly developed countries.
- The knowledge economy relies on skilled labor and education, robust communication networks, and institutional structures that incentivize innovation.

## Understanding the Knowledge Economy

Developing economies tend to be heavily focused on agriculture and manufacturing, while highly developed countries have a larger share of service-related activity. This includes knowledge-based economic activities such as research, technical support, and consulting.

The knowledge economy is the marketplace for the production and sale of scientific and engineering discoveries. This knowledge can be commodified in the form of patents or other intellectual property protections. The producers of such information -- such as scientific experts and research laboratories -- are also considered part of the knowledge economy.

**Important:** The Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 was a major turning point in the treatment of intellectual property in the United States because it allowed universities to retain ownership of inventions or discoveries made with federal R&D funding and to negotiate exclusive licenses. [1]

Thanks to globalization, the world economy has become increasingly knowledge-based, bringing with it the best practices of each country's economy. Furthermore, knowledge-based factors have created an interconnected global economy in which human expertise and trade secrets are considered important economic resources.

It is important to note, however, that generally accepted accounting principles (GAAP) do not allow companies to include these assets on their balance sheets.

**Note:** The modern commercialization of academic research and basic science traces its roots to governments seeking military advantages.

## The Knowledge Economy and Human Capital

The knowledge economy addresses how education and knowledge -- often referred to as "human capital" -- can serve as a productive asset or business product to be sold and exported, yielding profits for individuals, businesses, and the economy.

This component of the economy relies heavily on intellectual capabilities rather than natural resources or physical contributions. In the knowledge economy, products and services grounded in intellectual expertise advance the technical and scientific fields, driving innovation across the broader economy.

The World Bank defines knowledge economies according to four pillars.

## Examples of the Knowledge Economy

Academic institutions, companies engaged in research and development (R&D), programmers developing new software and search engines for data, and health workers using digital data to improve treatments are all components of the knowledge economy.

These economic agents pass on the results of their research to workers in more traditional fields -- such as farmers who use software applications and digital solutions to better manage their crops, advanced technology-based medical procedures such as robot-assisted surgery, or schools that provide students with digital learning aids and online courses.

## How Large Is the Knowledge Economy?

Because it is not a clearly defined category like manufacturing, it is difficult to put an exact price tag on the global knowledge economy. However, a rough estimate can be obtained by measuring some of the major components. According to the U.S. Chamber of Commerce, the total U.S. intellectual property market is valued at $6.6 trillion, and IP-intensive industries account for more than one-third of GDP. The market size of the country's higher education institutions accounts for an additional $568 billion. [3][4]

## What Are the Most Valuable Skills in the Knowledge Economy?

The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) notes that while higher education and technical training are obvious assets, communication and teamwork are also fundamental skills in a knowledge-based economy. Since no single knowledge worker is likely to generate breakthrough innovations alone, these interpersonal and workplace competencies are essential for surviving in a knowledge-based workplace. [5]

## Which Country Has the Largest Knowledge Economy?

The factors of a knowledge economy are measured by the United Nations Development Programme's Global Knowledge Index, which replaced the World Bank Knowledge Economy Index after 2012. This metric scores each country on "enabling factors" for the knowledge economy, such as education levels, technical and vocational training, innovation, and communication technology. According to the most recent edition, Switzerland ranks as the top knowledge economy with a total score of 71.5%. Sweden and the United States follow closely, each scoring 70.0. [6]
