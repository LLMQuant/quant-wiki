# What Is the 80-20 Rule?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The 80-20 rule, also known as the Pareto Principle, is a widely cited aphorism asserting that, for any given event, roughly 80% of outcomes (or outputs) result from 20% of causes (or inputs).

In business, the goal of the 80-20 rule is to identify the inputs that are potentially the most productive and to prioritize them accordingly. For example, once managers identify the factors critical to their company's success, they should devote the greatest focus to those factors.

Although the 80-20 rule is frequently applied in business and economics, the concept can be extended to virtually any field. Wealth distribution, personal finance, spending habits, and even infidelity in personal relationships can all be examined through the lens of the 80-20 rule.

### Key Takeaways

- The 80-20 rule holds that 80% of outcomes result from 20% of causes.
- The 80-20 rule prioritizes the 20% of factors that produce the best results.
- A core principle of the 80-20 rule is to identify an entity's best assets and use them efficiently to create maximum value.
- The rule is a guideline, not a rigid mathematical law.
- People sometimes erroneously conclude that if 20% of factors should be prioritized, the other 80% can be ignored.

## How the 80-20 Rule Works

You can think of the 80-20 rule as a simple cause-and-effect relationship: 80% of outcomes (outputs) come from 20% of causes (inputs). The rule is often invoked to point out that 80% of a company's revenue is generated by 20% of its customers.

From this perspective, it may be advantageous for a company to focus on the 20% of clients who account for 80% of revenue and to tailor marketing efforts specifically to them. By doing so, the company can retain these customers and acquire new ones with similar characteristics. However, the 80-20 rule has a more fundamental implication.

The core of the 80-20 rule is to identify an entity's best assets and deploy them efficiently to create maximum value. For example, a student should determine which parts of a textbook will yield the greatest benefit for an upcoming exam and focus on those sections first. This does not mean the student should ignore the rest of the textbook.

Many people fail to recognize that the 80-20 rule is a guideline, not a hard mathematical law. Moreover, the percentages need not add up to 100%. Inputs and outputs represent different units, and the percentages of those units are not required to sum to 100%. What matters is the concept behind the rule.

There is another common misunderstanding of the 80-20 rule: the belief that if 20% of inputs are most important, the other 80% must be unimportant. This is a logical fallacy. Even when you prioritize the top 20% of inputs, the remaining 80% may still be significant.

**Important:** Business managers across all industries use the 80-20 rule to help narrow their focus and identify the issues causing the most problems within their departments and organizations.

## Background of the 80-20 Rule

The 80-20 rule is also called the Pareto Principle, and it is applied in Pareto analysis. It was originally used in macroeconomics to describe the distribution of wealth in early 20th-century Italy. It was introduced by the Italian economist Vilfredo Pareto in 1906, who is also known for the concept of Pareto efficiency. [1]

Pareto observed that 20% of the pea pods in his garden produced 80% of the peas. He extended this principle to macroeconomics, showing that 80% of Italy's wealth was held by 20% of the population.

In the 1940s, Dr. Joseph Juran, a prominent figure in the field of operations management, applied the 80-20 rule to quality control in business production.

He demonstrated that 80% of product defects were caused by 20% of the problems in production methods. By focusing on and reducing the critical 20% of production problems, a business could improve its overall product quality. Juran coined this phenomenon "the vital few and the trivial many." [2]

## Benefits of the 80-20 Rule

Although there is little rigorous scientific analysis that either proves or disproves the 80-20 rule, there is a substantial body of anecdotal evidence supporting it as essentially valid, if not numerically precise.

Performance results of salespeople across a wide range of industries have demonstrated success through the application of the 80-20 rule. Additionally, external consultants who use Six Sigma and other management strategies have incorporated the 80-20 principle into their practices with good results.

## Example of the 80-20 Rule

Carla, a graduate student at Harvard, was working on an assignment for her digital communications class. The project involved creating a blog and monitoring its success over the course of a semester.

Carla designed, created, and launched the site. At midterm, the professor evaluated the blogs. Carla's blog, despite gaining some visibility, generated the least traffic compared to her classmates' blogs.

Carla came across an article about the 80-20 rule. The article noted that the concept could be applied to any field. Carla began thinking about how she could apply the 80-20 rule to her blog project. She reflected: "I invested a huge amount of time, technical ability, and writing expertise into building this blog. Yet, despite all that effort, I am getting very little site traffic."

She realized that even spectacular content is nearly worthless if no one reads it. Carla deduced that perhaps her marketing of the blog was a bigger issue than the blog itself.

To apply the 80-20 rule, Carla assigned 80% to everything that went into creating the blog, including its content. The remaining 20% would be represented by the blog's visitors.

Using web analytics, Carla closely tracked the blog's traffic. She asked herself:

- Which sources constitute the top 20% of my blog traffic?
- Who are the top 20% of my target audience?
- What are the characteristics of this audience as a group?
- Can I afford to invest more money and effort to serve my top 20% of readers?
- In terms of content, which blog posts make up the top 20% of my best-performing topics?
- Can I improve on those topics and get even more traction from my content?

Carla analyzed the answers to these questions and edited her blog accordingly.

Notably, although her analysis confirmed that the blog's biggest problem was its marketing, Carla did not ignore the content. She remembered the common misconception cited in the article -- that if 20% of inputs are most important, the other 80% must be unimportant -- and chose not to make that mistake. She knew she needed to address all aspects of the content as well.

By applying the 80-20 rule to her blog project, Carla gained a better understanding of her audience and targeted her top 20% of readers more purposefully. She redesigned the blog's structure and content based on her findings, and traffic to her site increased by more than 220%.

## What Is the 80-20 Rule?

The 80-20 rule is a principle stating that 80% of outcomes result from 20% of causes. It is used to identify the factors most responsible for success -- typically in a business context -- and then to focus on those factors to improve results. The rule can also be applied beyond business to areas such as personal life, sports, music, and blogging.

## What Does the 80-20 Rule Mean?

At its core, the 80-20 rule simply emphasizes the importance of directing your energy toward the aspects of your business -- or life, athletic pursuits, musical performance, blog, and so on -- that yield the best results. It does not mean that less successful areas should be ignored. It is about prioritizing focus and tasks, and then addressing the issues that emerge from that prioritization.

## How Can I Use the 80-20 Rule for Investing?

When building a portfolio, you might consider investing in the 20% of stocks in the S&P 500 that have contributed 80% of the market's returns. Alternatively, you could create an 80-20 allocation: 80% of investments in lower-risk index funds and 20% in growth funds. Of course, past performance does not necessarily correlate with future results, so be sure to monitor your portfolio's performance to see whether results align with your intentions and goals.
