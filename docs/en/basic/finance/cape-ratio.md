# What Is the CAPE Ratio (Shiller P/E)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The CAPE ratio is a valuation metric that uses real (inflation-adjusted) earnings per share (EPS) over a 10-year period to smooth out fluctuations in corporate profits across different phases of the business cycle.

The CAPE ratio -- short for Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings ratio -- was popularized by Yale University professor Robert Shiller. It is also known as the Shiller P/E. The standard price-to-earnings ratio measures a stock's price relative to its company's earnings per share. EPS is calculated as a company's profit divided by outstanding shares of equity.

The CAPE ratio is generally applied to broad equity indices to assess whether the market is undervalued or overvalued. Although it is a widely followed measure, several industry practitioners have questioned its reliability as a predictor of future stock market returns.

### Key Takeaways

- The CAPE ratio is used to analyze a public company's long-term financial performance while accounting for the impact of different economic cycles on earnings.
- The CAPE ratio is similar to the standard P/E ratio but is primarily used to determine whether a stock or index is overvalued or undervalued.
- The ratio compares the share price to average earnings over the prior 10 years, adjusted for inflation, to incorporate the effects of economic conditions.

## CAPE Ratio Formula

$$ \text{CAPE Ratio} = \frac{\text{Share Price}}{\text{10-Year Average, Inflation-Adjusted Earnings}} $$

## What the CAPE Ratio Tells You

A company's profitability is significantly influenced by economic cycles. During expansions, profits rise substantially as consumer spending increases; during recessions, spending contracts and profits decline sharply or turn into losses. Although cyclical sectors -- such as commodities and financials -- experience far greater earnings volatility than defensive sectors like utilities and pharmaceuticals, few companies can maintain stable profitability through a deep recession.

The volatility in earnings per share also leads to wide swings in P/E ratios. For this reason, Benjamin Graham and David Dodd recommended in their seminal 1934 work *Security Analysis* that analysts use an average of seven to ten years of earnings when calculating valuation ratios. [1]

## Real-World Example of the CAPE Ratio

The CAPE ratio first drew public attention in December 1996, when Robert Shiller and John Campbell presented research to the Federal Reserve showing that stock prices were rising far faster than earnings. In the winter of 1998, Shiller and Campbell published their landmark paper "Valuation Ratios and the Long-Run Stock Market Outlook," in which they smoothed earnings for the S&P 500 by taking an average of real earnings over the preceding 10 years, going back to 1872. [2]

The ratio reached a then-record high of 28 in January 1997, with comparable peaks occurring in 1929 and 2000. Shiller and Campbell predicted that the market's real value would be 40% lower a decade hence. That forecast proved remarkably prescient: the 2008 market crash drove the S&P 500 down 60% from October 2007 to March 2009.

The CAPE ratio for the S&P 500 rose steadily during the second decade of the century as the U.S. economic recovery gathered pace and stock prices reached record levels. As of June 2024, the CAPE ratio stood at 35.49, well above its long-term average of 16.80. [3] Readings above 30 had previously occurred only in 1929 and 2000, sparking intense debate about whether such elevated levels portend a significant market correction.

## Limitations of the CAPE Ratio

Critics of the CAPE ratio argue that it is inherently backward-looking and therefore of limited practical utility. Another concern is the ratio's reliance on Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) earnings, the calculation of which has changed markedly in recent years.

In June 2016, Jeremy Siegel of the Wharton School published a paper arguing that using the CAPE ratio to forecast future equity returns may be overly pessimistic because of changes in the way GAAP earnings are computed. [4] Siegel suggested that using consistent earnings data -- such as operating earnings or National Income and Product Accounts (NIPA) after-tax corporate profits -- rather than GAAP earnings improves the forecasting ability of the CAPE model and points to higher expected U.S. equity returns.

## What Does CAPE Stand For?

CAPE stands for Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings. The ratio is commonly called the Shiller P/E, named after Yale University professor Robert Shiller, who popularized the metric.

## What Is the CAPE Ratio Typically Used For?

The CAPE ratio is most commonly applied to broad equity indices to assess whether the market appears undervalued or overvalued. Critics, however, contend that its backward-looking nature and reliance on GAAP earnings -- which have undergone significant methodological changes -- limit its usefulness.

## When Did the CAPE Ratio First Gain Public Attention?

The CAPE ratio first came to prominence in December 1996, when Robert Shiller and John Campbell presented research to the Federal Reserve showing that stock prices were rising faster than earnings. They published their findings in the winter 1998 paper "Valuation Ratios and the Long-Run Stock Market Outlook."

## Conclusion

The Cyclically Adjusted Price-to-Earnings (CAPE) ratio uses real earnings per share over the prior 10 years to smooth out fluctuations in corporate profits across different phases of the business cycle. This valuation metric analyzes long-term financial performance while accounting for the impact of varying economic conditions on company earnings.
