# What Is the Bond Market?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The bond market -- often called the debt market, fixed-income market, or credit market -- encompasses all trading and issuance of debt securities. Governments issue bonds to raise capital for debt repayment and infrastructure improvements. Public companies issue bonds to fund business expansion and ongoing operations.

### Key Takeaways

- Governments use bond proceeds to finance infrastructure improvements and repay existing debt.
- Companies issue bonds to raise capital for operations, product line expansion, or new facilities.
- Bonds may be issued in the primary market or traded in the secondary market, where investors can purchase existing debt through brokers or other intermediaries.

## History of the Bond Market

Transferable loans date back to ancient Mesopotamia, where debts denominated in units of grain weight could be exchanged between debtors. Recorded evidence of debt instruments stretches to approximately 2400 BCE, with a clay tablet discovered at Nippur (modern-day Iraq) referencing grain payment guarantees and consequences for non-repayment.

During the Middle Ages, sovereign governments issued debt to finance wars. The Bank of England, the world's oldest central bank, was established in the 17th century to fund the rebuilding of the British navy through bond issuance. The first U.S. Treasury bonds financed the War of Independence, and "Liberty Bonds" later funded World War I.

Early chartered companies such as the Dutch East India Company and the Mississippi Company issued debt instruments before issuing equity shares. These bonds were handwritten guarantees provided to creditors.

## Buying and Trading Bonds

Bonds trade in both the primary and secondary markets. The primary market is the "new issue" market, where transactions occur directly between bond issuers and initial purchasers. Primary market instruments are newly created debt securities not previously offered to the public.

In the secondary market, securities previously sold in the primary market are bought and sold. Investors can purchase these bonds through brokers who act as intermediaries. Secondary market bonds may be packaged into pension funds, mutual funds, and life insurance products.

## Types of Bonds

**Corporate bonds:** Companies issue corporate bonds to fund current operations, expand product lines, or build new facilities. These are generally long-term instruments with maturities of at least one year. Based on the credit rating of the bond and its issuer, corporate bonds fall into two broad categories.

Investment-grade bonds represent high-quality, relatively low-default-risk debt. Rating agencies such as Standard & Poor's and Moody's use letter-based systems to classify credit quality.

Junk bonds, also known as high-yield bonds, carry higher risk and are typically issued by companies with weaker financial profiles and elevated default probability. They offer higher interest rates to compensate for the additional risk.

**Government bonds (sovereign bonds):** Government bonds attract investors by paying face value at a specified maturity date along with periodic interest. This profile makes them appealing to conservative investors, and they are generally considered the lowest-risk investment available. In the United States, government bonds are called Treasuries and represent the most active and liquid segment of the bond market.

- **Treasury Bills (T-Bills):** Short-term government debt backed by the U.S. Treasury, with maturities of one year or less.
- **Treasury Notes (T-Notes):** Marketable government debt securities with fixed interest rates and maturities of one to ten years.
- **Treasury Bonds (T-Bonds):** Government debt securities issued by the federal government with maturities exceeding 20 years.

**Important:** In August 2023, Fitch Ratings downgraded the U.S. long-term credit rating from "AAA" to "AA+," citing expected fiscal deterioration over the next three years, a growing public debt burden, and an erosion of governance relative to "AA" and "AAA" peers over the past two decades.

**Municipal bonds ("munis"):** Issued by states, cities, special purpose districts, public utility districts, school districts, publicly owned airports, seaports, and other government entities to fund various projects. Municipal bonds are generally exempt from federal tax and may also be exempt at the state and local levels, making them attractive to tax-sensitive investors.

General obligation bonds (GO bonds) are issued by government entities without revenue from a specific project. Some GO bonds are backed by property taxes or paid from general funds. Revenue bonds are secured by specific revenue sources such as sales, fuel, hotel occupancy, or other taxes.

**Mortgage-backed securities (MBS):** Pooled securities backed by residential mortgages. Investors in MBS effectively provide funding to homebuyers through lenders. These securities typically pay monthly interest. MBS are a form of asset-backed security (ABS). During the 2007-2010 subprime mortgage crisis, these instruments suffered severe losses as their underlying mortgages collapsed in value.

**Emerging market bonds:** Governments and corporations in emerging economies issue bonds that offer growth opportunities but carry higher risk than domestic or developed-market debt. During the 1980s, U.S. Treasury Secretary Nicholas Brady initiated a program to restructure global debt through the issuance of dollar-denominated bonds. Over the following two decades, many Latin American countries issued Brady bonds, marking a rise in emerging market debt issuance. Bonds are also issued in developing countries across Asia, Latin America, Eastern Europe, Africa, and the Middle East.

Investing in emerging market bonds carries all standard debt risks -- including the issuer's economic and financial performance and ability to meet obligations -- amplified by political and economic volatility in developing countries. Additional risks include currency fluctuations and devaluation.

## Bond Indices

Just as the S&P 500 and Russell indices track equities, the Bloomberg Aggregate Bond Index, the Merrill Lynch Domestic Master, and the Citigroup U.S. Broad Investment-Grade Bond Index track and measure corporate bond portfolio performance.

The Bloomberg U.S. Aggregate Bond Index ("the Agg") is a market-weighted benchmark index that provides investors with a standard for evaluating fund or security performance. The index includes government and corporate bonds as well as investment-grade corporate debt instruments with issuance exceeding $300 million and maturities of one year or more. The Agg serves as a total return benchmark for many bond funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs).

## Bond Market vs. Stock Market

Bonds represent debt financing, while stocks represent equity financing. A bond is a form of credit: the issuer must repay the bondholder's principal plus interest. Stocks do not guarantee any return of capital to shareholders.

Due to legal protections and guarantees, bonds generally carry lower risk and lower expected returns than equities. Stocks are inherently riskier than bonds, with the potential for both greater gains and greater losses.

Both the stock and bond markets are typically highly active and liquid. Bond prices are sensitive to interest rate changes, generally moving inversely to rates. Stock prices are sensitive to changes in future earnings expectations and growth potential.

**Tip:** Even without direct access to the bond market, investors can gain bond exposure through bond-focused mutual funds and ETFs.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Bonds

Financial professionals generally recommend maintaining a diversified portfolio with some allocation to bonds. Bonds tend to be less volatile than equities and offer lower returns, but they carry credit risk and interest rate risk. Over longer time horizons, an excessive allocation to bonds is considered overly conservative.

#### Advantages

- Lower risk and volatility compared to equities.
- Wide selection of issuers and bond types.
- Creditors have priority over shareholders in bankruptcy proceedings.

#### Disadvantages

- Lower risk translates to lower returns.
- Primary market bond purchases may have high minimum investment thresholds for retail investors.
- Exposure to default risk and interest rate risk.

## Is the Bond Market a Good Investment?

As with any investment, the expected return on a bond must be weighed against its risk. Higher-risk issuers must offer higher yields. Junk bonds pay higher interest rates but carry greater default risk. U.S. Treasuries pay lower rates but carry minimal risk.

## Can Investors Lose Money in the Bond Market?

Yes. While bonds are generally less risky than stocks, bond prices can fluctuate and decline. If interest rates rise, even highly rated bond prices will fall. The sensitivity of a bond's price to rate changes is measured by duration. If an issuer defaults or enters bankruptcy, a bond may lose substantial value and may not fully repay the original investment or accrued interest.

## Conclusion

The bond market encompasses debt securities issued by domestic and foreign governments and corporations. Bonds can be structured with fixed or floating rates and may or may not be convertible into equity. Bonds are generally considered less volatile than stocks because they pay periodic interest and return principal at maturity.
