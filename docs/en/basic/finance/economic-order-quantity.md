# What Is Economic Order Quantity (EOQ)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Economic order quantity (EOQ) is the ideal number of units a company should purchase to meet demand while minimizing inventory-related costs such as holding costs, shortage costs, and ordering costs. This production scheduling model was developed by Ford W. Harris in 1913 and has been refined over time. The EOQ formula assumes that demand, ordering costs, and holding costs remain constant.

### Key Takeaways

- Economic order quantity (EOQ) is the optimal order size that meets demand while minimizing total costs associated with ordering, receiving, and holding inventory.
- The EOQ formula works best when demand, ordering costs, and holding costs remain stable over time.
- A significant limitation of EOQ is that it assumes constant demand for the company's products over time.

## What EOQ Can Tell You

The goal of the EOQ formula is to identify the optimal number of product units to order. When achieved, this helps a company minimize the costs of purchasing, transporting, and storing units. The EOQ formula can be modified to determine different production levels or ordering intervals. Large supply chain companies and those with high variable costs typically use algorithms within their software systems to calculate EOQ.

EOQ is an important cash flow tool. The formula can help a company control the amount of cash tied up in inventory balances.

For many companies, inventory represents the largest asset after human resources. These businesses must maintain sufficient inventory to satisfy customer demand. If EOQ can help reduce inventory levels, the cash savings can be redirected to other business purposes or investments.

The EOQ formula determines a company's inventory reorder point. When inventory falls to a certain level, the EOQ-driven business process triggers an order for additional units.

By establishing a reorder point, the business avoids stockouts and can continue fulfilling customer orders. If a company runs out of inventory, it incurs a shortage cost -- lost revenue from being unable to fill orders due to insufficient stock. Inventory shortages may also mean the company loses customers or that customers reduce future orders.

## Example of Using EOQ

EOQ takes into account the timing of reordering, the cost of placing an order, and the cost of storing merchandise. If a company is constantly placing small orders to maintain a specific inventory level, the ordering costs are higher and additional storage space is needed.

Consider, for example, a retail clothing store that carries a line of men's jeans and sells 1,000 pairs annually. The company's annual holding cost per pair is $5, and the fixed cost of placing an order is $2.

The EOQ formula yields the square root of (2 x 1,000 pairs x $2 order cost) / ($5 holding cost), which equals approximately 28.3 units. To minimize costs while meeting customer demand, the ideal order size is just over 28 pairs of jeans. A more complex component of the EOQ formula provides the reorder point.

## Limitations of EOQ

The EOQ formula assumes that consumer demand is constant. The calculation also assumes that ordering and holding costs remain unchanged.

These assumptions make it difficult or impossible to account for business events such as fluctuating consumer demand, seasonal changes in inventory costs, lost sales revenue due to inventory shortages, or purchase discounts a company might receive from bulk buying.

## What Is Economic Order Quantity?

Economic order quantity is an inventory management technique designed to support efficient inventory management decisions. It refers to the optimal amount of inventory a company should purchase to meet demand while minimizing holding and storage costs. An important limitation of EOQ is that it assumes constant demand for the company's products over time.

## When Is EOQ Higher or Lower?

EOQ is higher when a company's setup costs or product demand increases. Conversely, EOQ is lower when the company's holding costs rise.

## Why Is Economic Order Quantity Important?

Economic order quantity is important because it helps companies manage their inventory efficiently. Without inventory management techniques like EOQ, companies tend to hold too much inventory during periods of low demand and too little during periods of high demand. Both scenarios create missed opportunities.

## Conclusion

Inventory management is a critical component of business success. Excess inventory leads to higher costs and erodes profitability through maintenance expenses and the occasional need to dispose of obsolete stock. Insufficient inventory means a company loses sales by failing to meet demand. Economic order quantity helps ensure that businesses manage their inventory effectively.
