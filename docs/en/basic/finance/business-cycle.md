# What Is the Business Cycle?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The business cycle describes the fluctuation in aggregate economic activity within a nation. Each cycle consists of expansions occurring roughly simultaneously across many economic activities, followed by similarly broad contractions. This sequence of changes is cyclical but not periodic.

The business cycle is also referred to as the economic cycle.

### Key Takeaways

- The business cycle consists of coordinated cyclical upswings and downswings in broad economic indicators -- output, employment, income, and sales.
- The alternating phases of the business cycle are expansion and contraction.
- Contractions often lead to recessions, but the entire contraction phase is not necessarily a recession.
- Recessions typically begin at the business cycle peak -- when expansion ends -- and conclude at the trough, when the next expansion begins.
- The severity of a recession is measured by three Ds: depth, diffusion, and duration.

## Understanding the Business Cycle

At its core, the business cycle is defined by the alternation of expansion and contraction phases in aggregate economic activity and the co-movement of economic variables during each phase.

Aggregate economic activity is represented not only by real (inflation-adjusted) GDP but also by aggregate measures of industrial production, employment, income, and sales -- the key coincident economic indicators used to officially determine U.S. business cycle peaks and troughs.

A common misconception is that a contraction is synonymous with a recession, or that two consecutive quarters of declining real GDP constitute a recession. Importantly, recessions occur within contractions but do not always span the entire contraction phase. Two consecutive quarters of GDP decline is an informal rule of thumb -- not the definition used by the NBER. [1][2]

A business cycle recovery begins when the vicious cycle of recession reverses into a virtuous cycle: rising output triggers job gains, higher incomes, and increased sales, which in turn drive further output growth. The recovery becomes self-sustaining only when this domino effect spreads throughout the economy, leading to a sustained expansion.

**Important:** The stock market is not the economy. The business cycle should not be confused with market cycles, which are measured using broad stock price indices.

## Measuring and Dating the Business Cycle

The severity of a recession is measured by three Ds: depth, diffusion, and duration. Depth is determined by the peak-to-trough decline in broad measures of output, employment, income, and sales. Diffusion refers to how widely the downturn spreads across economic activities, industries, and geographic regions. Duration is the time interval between the peak and trough.

Expansions begin at the trough (or bottom) of the business cycle and continue until the next peak. Recessions begin at the peak and continue until the next trough.

The National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) determines the business cycle chronology -- the start and end dates of U.S. recessions and expansions. The NBER's Business Cycle Dating Committee defines a recession as "a significant decline in economic activity that is spread across the economy, lasting more than a few months, normally visible in real GDP, real income, employment, industrial production, and wholesale-retail sales." [3]

**Note:** The Great Depression included multiple recessions, one lasting 44 months. [4]

The Dating Committee typically determines recession start and end dates well after the fact. For example, after the 2007-09 recession ended, the committee "waited until the revisions to the national income and product accounts were released on July 30 and August 27, 2010" before announcing the June 2009 recession end date on September 20, 2010. [5]

On average, U.S. expansions have lasted much longer than contractions. Between 1945 and 2019, the average expansion lasted approximately 65 months, while the average recession lasted approximately 11 months. [6]

From the 1850s through World War II, the average expansion lasted about 26 months and the average recession about 21 months. The longest expansion on record ran from 2009 to 2020 -- 128 months. [6]

## Stock Prices and the Business Cycle

The largest stock price declines tend to occur around business cycle downturns (contractions and recessions), though not always. The Dow Jones Industrial Average fell 51.1% and the S&P 500 declined 56.8% between October 9, 2007 and March 9, 2009 during the Great Recession. [7]

This happens for several reasons, primarily because businesses adopt defensive postures during contractions and investor confidence erodes. Many of these shifts occur before the public recognizes that the economy is in contraction, but the stock market tracks underlying economic developments.

Therefore, if speculation or rumors about a recession emerge -- or if mass layoffs, rising unemployment, declining output, or other warning signs appear -- businesses and investors begin to react. Businesses adopt defensive strategies, reduce headcount, and budget for lower revenue. Investors flee to perceived safe havens, demand for risk assets declines, and stock prices fall.

It is important to remember that while stock prices tend to fall during economic contractions, the contraction itself does not cause the decline -- fear of recession does.

## What Are the Phases of the Business Cycle?

The business cycle generally consists of four distinct phases: expansion, peak, contraction, and trough.

## What Does the Business Cycle Describe?

The business cycle describes the economy's fluctuations over time, typically measured from the start of one recession to the start of the next. This includes periods of economic growth.

## Can the Business Cycle Be Predicted?

Business cycles are generally unpredictable. Economies are complex systems that operate and interact in myriad ways. While there may be indicators of change -- such as shifts in inflation and production -- predicting the precise timing of a business cycle turning point is extremely difficult, if not impossible.

## The Bottom Line

The business cycle is the time it takes for an economy to pass through all four phases: expansion, peak, contraction, and trough. Expansion -- characterized by rising business profits, increasing economic output, and falling unemployment -- is the phase in which the U.S. economy spends the most time. Contraction -- marked by declining profits and output -- is the shortest phase.
