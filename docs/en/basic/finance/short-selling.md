# What Is Short Selling?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Short selling is a trading strategy in which an investor speculates on a decline in a stock's price. Short sellers profit by betting against a security's price. Traders use short selling as a speculative tool, while investors and portfolio managers may use it to hedge downside risk in a long position.

### Key Takeaways

- Short selling involves borrowing a security and selling it on the open market, with the plan to repurchase it later at a lower price.
- Short sellers profit from a decline in a security's price.
- Short selling carries a high risk/reward ratio: it can generate substantial profits, but losses can escalate rapidly and may trigger margin calls.

## Timing and Conditions

Timing is critical for short selling. Stocks typically decline faster than they rise, and a substantial gain can be wiped out by an earnings miss or other bearish event. Conversely, entering a trade too early can make holding a short position costly, especially if the stock rises sharply. Short sellers typically look for opportunities in the following conditions:

- **Bear markets:** Traders who believe "the trend is your friend" have a greater probability of profitable short trades during entrenched bear markets than during strong bull phases. Short sellers thrive in environments where markets fall rapidly, broadly, and deeply.
- **Deteriorating fundamentals:** Slowing revenue or earnings growth, increasing business challenges, and rising input costs that squeeze margins can all signal deteriorating fundamentals. These may reflect an economic slowdown, adverse geopolitical developments, or bearish technical signals.
- **Bearish technical indicators:** Short selling may succeed when technical indicators confirm a bearish trend -- for example, a break below key long-term support or a bearish moving average crossover such as the death cross (the 50-day moving average crossing below the 200-day moving average).
- **Elevated valuations:** Valuations in certain sectors or the broader market can reach extreme levels during periods of widespread optimism. Market professionals call this phase of the investment cycle "priced for perfection" because investors are inevitably disappointed when lofty expectations go unmet. Experienced short sellers may wait until the market or sector rolls over and begins its downturn rather than rushing in.

## Short Selling Costs

Unlike buying and holding stocks, short selling involves substantial costs beyond standard trading commissions:

- **Margin interest:** Since short selling can only be conducted through a margin account, interest on short trades can accumulate, particularly when positions are held for extended periods.
- **Stock borrowing costs:** Hard-to-borrow stocks -- due to high short interest, limited float, or other factors -- carry "hard-to-borrow" fees that can be substantial. These fees, based on an annualized rate, can range from a fraction of a percent to over 100% of the trade value, prorated by the number of days the position is open.
- **Dividends and other payments:** Short sellers are responsible for paying dividends on the shorted stock to the lender. For shorted bonds, they must pay the coupon or interest due to the lender.

Short selling is a margin transaction. The trader uses existing investments as collateral to borrow from the brokerage. Investors must maintain a minimum 25% maintenance margin. If the account falls below this level, the trader faces a margin call and must deposit additional cash or liquidate positions. [1]

## Short Selling Example

Imagine a trader who believes XYZ stock, currently trading at $50, will decline over the next three months. The trader borrows 100 shares and sells them to another investor. The trader is now "short" 100 shares -- having sold something borrowed rather than owned.

A week later, the company reports poor quarterly results, and the stock falls to $40. The trader closes the short position by purchasing 100 shares at $40 on the open market. Profit on the short sale (excluding commissions and margin interest): $50 - $40 = $10 per share x 100 shares = $1,000.

Now suppose the trader does not close at $40 but holds on, expecting further decline. A competitor then announces an acquisition bid at $65 per share, and the stock surges. If the trader closes at $65, the loss is: $50 - $65 = -$15 per share x 100 shares = -$1,500. The trader must buy back shares at a higher price to cover the position.

Hedging aims to protect rather than profit. It is designed to preserve portfolio gains or limit losses. The cost of hedging is twofold: the direct cost (such as fees associated with short selling or premiums paid for protective options) and the opportunity cost of capping portfolio upside if the market continues to rise.

**Tip:** Investors can also short sell through exchange-traded funds (ETFs), a safer strategy with lower short squeeze risk. Put options offer an alternative way to profit from price declines without requiring a margin account.

## Advantages and Disadvantages

If the seller correctly predicts the price movement, short selling can deliver a positive return on investment, particularly when using margin for leverage. Short selling can also serve as an inexpensive hedge that offsets other portfolio holdings.

However, a short seller can lose more than 100% of the original investment. There is no ceiling on a stock's price. Additionally, the trader must fund the margin account while the position is open. When it is time to close, finding enough shares to buy back may be difficult if many other traders are also short or if the stock trades thinly.

A short squeeze occurs when a stock's price rises and short sellers rush to buy back shares to cover their positions, driving the price even higher.

#### Advantages

- Potential for high profits
- Low initial capital required
- Leveraged investment possible
- Hedges against other holdings

#### Disadvantages

- Potentially unlimited losses
- Margin account required
- Margin interest expense
- Short squeeze risk

## Regulation

Every country imposes restrictions and regulations on short selling within its markets. In the United States, short selling is regulated by the SEC under the Securities Exchange Act of 1934. Regulation SHO, implemented in 2005, is the primary rule governing short selling, requiring that short sales be executed only on an uptick or zero-plus-tick. [2]

Under Regulation SHO, brokers must locate a party willing to lend the shorted shares or have reasonable grounds to believe the shares can be borrowed. This prevents naked short selling -- selling shares that have not been borrowed. [2] In certain circumstances, such as extreme market volatility, the SEC may impose temporary short-selling bans on specific stocks. [3]

In October 2023, the SEC added rules requiring investors to report short positions to the SEC and requiring firms lending shares for short sales to report the activity to FINRA. [4] These new rules followed increased scrutiny of short selling, particularly after the 2021 GameStop (GME) meme-stock episode, when retail investors drove up the stock price and inflicted losses on hedge funds that had shorted the company.

**Important:** Regulations vary by jurisdiction. The European Securities and Markets Authority (ESMA) oversees short selling in the EU. Positions exceeding 0.2% of issued shares must be disclosed to regulators, and those exceeding 0.5% must be publicly disclosed. [5] In Hong Kong, the Securities and Futures Commission (SFC) regulates short selling, permitting it only for designated securities and requiring that shares be borrowed first. Naked short selling is illegal. [6]

## Notable Short Selling Example

Unexpected news events can trigger a short squeeze, forcing short sellers to cover at any price. In October 2008, Volkswagen briefly became the world's most valuable publicly traded company due to a short squeeze.

In 2008, investors knew Porsche was building a position in Volkswagen to gain majority control. Short sellers expected the stock to fall once Porsche took over, so they heavily shorted the shares. In a surprise announcement, Porsche revealed it had secretly acquired over 70% of the company through derivatives, triggering a massive feedback loop as short sellers scrambled to cover.

Short sellers were at a disadvantage because a government entity held 20% of Volkswagen's shares and was unwilling to sell, and Porsche controlled another 70%, leaving almost no shares available for repurchase. Both the short interest ratio and days-to-cover ratio exploded overnight, causing the stock to surge from around EUR 200 to over EUR 1,000. [7]

## Why Must Short Sellers Borrow Shares?

Because a company has a finite number of shares outstanding, short sellers must first locate shares to borrow. They borrow from existing long holders and pay the lender interest. This process is typically facilitated by the broker behind the scenes. When few shares are available for shorting, borrowing costs rise.

## What Are Short Selling Metrics?

Short selling metrics help investors gauge overall market sentiment. The short interest ratio (SIR), also called the short float, measures the number of shares currently sold short relative to the shares available ("floating") in the market. A very high SIR is associated with stocks that are declining or appear overvalued. The short interest to volume ratio (days-to-cover ratio) divides total short shares by average daily trading volume. A high days-to-cover value is also a bearish indicator.

## Why Does Short Selling Have a Negative Reputation?

Short selling has acquired a negative reputation partly because of unethical speculators who use short selling strategies and derivatives to artificially depress prices and conduct "bear raids" on vulnerable stocks. Most forms of such market manipulation are illegal in the United States but may still occur periodically.

## What Is a Short Squeeze?

Because shares in a short sale are sold on margin, relatively small price increases can magnify losses. The holder must buy back shares at the current market price to close the position and prevent further losses. If many traders do this simultaneously, the buying pressure can drive the price sharply higher -- creating a short squeeze.

## Conclusion

Short selling allows investors and traders to profit from declining markets. Bearish participants borrow shares on margin and sell them in the market, hoping to repurchase them later at a lower price. While critics view short selling as a bet against the market, many economists argue that the ability to sell short improves market efficiency and can serve as a stabilizing force. [8]
