# What Is the Discount Rate?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The discount rate is the interest rate charged by the Federal Reserve to commercial banks and other financial institutions for short-term loans. The discount rate applies to the Fed's lending facility, known as the discount window.

The discount rate can also refer to the interest rate used in discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis to determine the present value of future cash flows. In this context, investors and businesses can use the discount rate to evaluate potential investments.

### Key Takeaways

- In banking, the discount rate is the interest rate the Federal Reserve charges banks for short-term loans.
- Discount lending is a key monetary policy tool and part of the Fed's function as lender of last resort.
- In discounted cash flow analysis, the discount rate is the rate used to discount future cash flows.
- The discount rate in DCF expresses the time value of money and can determine whether an investment project is financially viable.
- As long as you know the future value, the present value, and the total number of years, you can calculate the discount rate in DCF.

## How the Federal Reserve Discount Rate Works

Commercial banks in the United States have two principal ways to borrow money for their short-term operational needs. They can borrow from and lend to other banks using the market-driven interbank rate, without any collateral, or they can borrow from the Federal Reserve Bank to meet their short-term operational needs.

Federal Reserve Bank loans are processed through the Fed's 12 regional branches. Financial institutions use these loans to cover any cash shortfalls, prevent any liquidity problems, or, in the worst-case scenario, prevent bank failure. This Fed-provided lending facility is known as the discount window.

These loans are extremely short-term -- 24 hours or less. The interest rate charged is the standard discount rate. The Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve sets this discount rate. [1]

The Fed's discount window program runs three tiers of loans, each using a separate but related rate. It also allows emergency credit approval for distressed banks.

The Fed independently determines the discount rates for the first two tiers. The rate for the third tier is based on prevailing rates in the market.

- First tier: Known as the primary credit program, this tier provides capital to financially sound banks with good credit records. The primary credit discount rate is typically set above existing market interest rates, which may be available from other banks or other sources of similar short-term debt.
- Second tier: Known as the secondary credit program, it offers similar loans to institutions that do not qualify for the primary rate. It is typically set 50 basis points above the primary rate (one percentage point = 100 basis points). Institutions at this tier are smaller and may not be as financially sound as those using the primary tier.
- Third tier: Known as the seasonal credit program, it serves smaller financial institutions with greater seasonal fluctuations in cash flows. Many are regional banks serving the agricultural and tourism sectors. Their business is considered relatively higher risk, so they pay a higher rate.
- Emergency credit: Banks applying for emergency credit must demonstrate that they cannot obtain a loan from another bank and require the support of at least five Federal Reserve Board governors for a vote. [1]

**Important:** All three types of Fed discount window loans are collateralized. Banks need to maintain a certain level of security or collateral to back the loan. Emergency credit may require collateral, but it is evaluated on a case-by-case basis subject to Fed vote.

Borrowing institutions rarely use this facility, mostly when they cannot find willing lenders on the market. The discount rate offered by the Fed is set relatively higher than interbank borrowing rates to discourage overly frequent use of the discount window.

The discount window primarily functions as an emergency option for distressed banks -- even borrowing from it may signal weakness to other market participants and investors.

## Federal Reserve Discount Rate Example

Use of the Fed's discount window spiked in late 2007 and 2008 as financial conditions deteriorated sharply and the central bank took steps to inject liquidity into the financial system.

In August 2007, the Board of Governors cut the primary discount rate from 6.25% to 5.75%, reducing the premium over the fed funds rate from 1% to 0.5%. In October 2008, the month after Lehman Brothers' collapse, discount window borrowing peaked at $403.5 billion, compared to a monthly average of $700 million from 1959 to 2006.

Due to the financial crisis, the Board of Governors also extended the loan period from overnight to 30 days, and then to 90 days in March 2008. [2] Once the economy recovered, those temporary measures were rolled back and the discount rate reverted to overnight lending only.

The Fed maintains its own discount rate under the discount window program in the United States. Most central banks around the world use similar measures, though they vary by region. For example, the European Central Bank (ECB) offers standing facilities that serve the same purpose. Financial institutions can obtain overnight liquidity from the central bank in exchange for providing sufficient eligible assets as collateral. [3]

**Note:** Most commonly, the Fed's discount window loans are overnight only, but in periods of extreme economic distress, such as the 2008-2009 credit crisis, the loan period can be extended.

## How the Discount Rate Works in Cash Flow Analysis

The same term, "discount rate," is also used in discounted cash flow analysis. DCF is used to estimate the value of an investment based on its expected future cash flows. Based on the concept of the time value of money, DCF analysis helps assess the viability of a project or investment by calculating the present value of expected future cash flows using a discount rate.

This analysis begins with estimating the investment required for the proposed project. It then considers the future returns it is expected to generate. Using a discount rate, the current value of any future cash flow can be calculated. If the net present value (PV) is positive, the project is considered viable. If it is negative, the project is not worth the investment.

In this context of DCF analysis, the discount rate refers to the interest rate used to determine present value. For example, $100 invested today in a savings plan with a 10% interest rate will grow to $110. In other words, $110 -- the future value (FV) -- when discounted at 10%, is worth $100 today (present value).

If one knows (or can reasonably forecast) all such future cash flows (like the $110 future value), then, using a particular discount rate, the present value of the investment can be obtained.

## What Discount Rate Should Be Used?

What is the appropriate discount rate for an investment or business project? When investing in standard assets such as Treasury bonds, the risk-free rate of return (often considered to be the three-month Treasury bill rate) is commonly used as the discount rate.

On the other hand, if a business is evaluating the viability of a potential project, it may use the weighted average cost of capital (WACC) as the discount rate. This is the average cost of capital the company pays to borrow or sell equity.

In either case, the net present value of all cash flows should be positive for the investment or project to be approved.

## Types of Discounted Cash Flows

Different types of discount rates apply to various business investments. The type required depends on the needs of the investor and the company itself. The most common include:

- Cost of debt: Companies must take on debt to fund operations and maintain business. The interest rate they pay on this debt is known as the cost of debt.
- Cost of equity: The cost of equity is the rate a company uses to compensate shareholders. Investors expect a specific rate of return in exchange for bearing the risk of investing in the company.
- Hurdle rate: The minimum rate of return required for a specific investment or venture is known as the hurdle rate. It enables decision-makers to assess whether the venture is worthwhile.
- Risk-free rate: The risk-free rate is the rate of return on an investment or business venture carrying no risk.
- Weighted average cost of capital: This is the rate of return investors expect to earn on their capital. Investors may include equity shareholders and bondholders.

## What Effect Does a Higher Discount Rate Have on the Time Value of Money?

Future cash flows are reduced by the discount rate, so the higher the discount rate, the lower the present value of future cash flows. A lower discount rate leads to a higher present value. As this implies, when the discount rate is higher, money in the future will be worth less than it is today -- meaning it will have less purchasing power.

## How Do You Calculate Discounted Cash Flow?

There are three steps to calculating the DCF of an investment:

- Forecast the expected cash flows from the investment.
- Select an appropriate discount rate.
- Discount the forecasted cash flows back to the present using a financial calculator, spreadsheet, or manual calculation.

## How Do You Choose the Appropriate Discount Rate?

The discount rate used depends on the type of analysis being undertaken. When considering an investment, the investor should use the opportunity cost of putting their money to work elsewhere as an appropriate discount rate. This is the rate of return the investor could earn in the marketplace on an investment of comparable size and risk.

A business can choose the most appropriate of several discount rates. This may be an opportunity cost-based discount rate, its weighted average cost of capital, or the historical average return of a similar project. In some cases, using the risk-free rate may be most appropriate.

## The Bottom Line

The discount rate is the lending rate at the Federal Reserve's discount window, where banks can obtain funds if they are unable to secure them from another bank on the market. The discount rate can also be calculated using a discounted cash flow model to inform business or investment decisions.
