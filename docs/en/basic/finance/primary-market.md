# What Is a Primary Market?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The primary market is where new securities originate. It is the venue -- often an exchange -- where companies, governments, and other entities raise capital through debt or equity securities, typically in an initial offering. Primary markets are facilitated by underwriting groups composed of investment banks, which set the initial price range for a given security and oversee its sale to investors.

Once the initial sale is complete, further trading takes place on the secondary market, which is where the bulk of daily trading activity occurs.

### Key Takeaways

- In the primary market, new stocks and bonds are sold to the public for the first time.
- In the primary market, investors can purchase securities directly from the issuer.
- Types of primary market issuances include initial public offerings (IPOs), private placements, rights issues, and preferred allotments.
- Stock exchanges represent the secondary market, where investors trade among themselves.
- Securities issued in the primary market can subsequently be traded on the secondary market -- essentially the familiar stock exchanges.

## Understanding the Primary Market

The primary market is where securities are created. It is in this market that companies first sell -- or float (in financial parlance) -- new stocks and bonds to the public in what is known as an initial distribution. These stocks and bonds, also called primary instruments, trade on mainstream exchanges at prices based on their market value.

Companies and government entities sell newly issued common stock, preferred stock, corporate bonds, and government bonds, notes, and bills on the primary market to fund business improvements or expand operations. Although an investment bank may set the initial price and receive a fee for facilitating the sale, the bulk of the proceeds goes to the issuer.

The primary market is not a physical place; rather, it reflects the nature of the goods. The defining characteristic of the primary market is that securities are purchased directly from the issuer, not "secondhand" from a prior buyer or investor.

**Important:** Investors in the primary market typically pay less than they would on the secondary market.

All primary market issuances are subject to strict regulation. Companies must file statements with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and other securities agencies, and must receive approval before their securities can be offered to investors.

Once the initial offering is complete -- that is, all stock shares or bonds have been sold -- the primary market closes. Those securities then begin trading on the secondary market.

## Types of Primary Market Issuances

An initial public offering (IPO) is one type of primary market issuance. An IPO occurs when a private company sells shares of stock to the public for the first time, commonly known as "going public." The process, including the initial pricing of new shares, is managed by a designated investment bank engaged by the company to underwrite the offering.

For example, company ABCWXYZ hires five underwriting firms to determine the financial details of its IPO. The underwriters specify that the stock's issue price will be $15. Investors can then purchase the IPO directly from the issuing company at that price. This is the first opportunity for investors to contribute capital to a company by purchasing its stock. A company's equity capital consists of the funds generated from the sale of stock on the primary market.

A rights offering (rights issue) allows companies to raise additional equity through the primary market after securities have already entered secondary market trading. Existing investors receive pro-rata rights based on the shares they currently own, while other investors may purchase newly minted shares.

## Private Placements and the Primary Market

Other types of primary market equity offerings include private placements and preferred allotments. A private placement allows a company to sell shares directly to significant investors -- such as hedge funds and banks -- without making them publicly available. A preferred allotment offers shares at a special price to select investors (typically hedge funds, banks, and mutual funds) that is not available to the general public.

Similarly, businesses and governments seeking to generate debt capital can issue new short-term and long-term bonds on the primary market. New bonds are issued with coupon rates corresponding to prevailing interest rates at the time of issuance, which may be higher or lower than the rates on existing bonds.

## Primary Market vs. Secondary Market

The primary market is where securities are created and first issued, while the secondary market is where those securities are subsequently traded among investors.

Consider U.S. Treasuries as an example. The bonds, notes, and bills issued by the U.S. government are auctioned at regularly scheduled intervals throughout the year. This is an instance of the primary market in action.

**Tip:** Individual investors can purchase newly issued U.S. Treasuries directly from the government through TreasuryDirect, an electronic marketplace and online account system. [1] This can save brokerage commissions and other intermediary fees.

After the auction, some investors who purchased government bonds or notes -- typically broker-dealers, banks, pension funds, or investment funds -- may wish to sell them. They offer these securities on stock exchanges or venues such as the NYSE, Nasdaq, or over-the-counter (OTC) markets, where other investors can buy them. These U.S. Treasuries are now trading on the secondary market.

For equities, the distinction between primary and secondary markets can seem somewhat blurred. In practice, the "stock market" as commonly understood is the secondary market where investors buy and sell shares among themselves. However, a stock exchange can serve as the venue for both primary and secondary market activity.

For instance, when a company goes public on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE), the first offering of its new shares constitutes a primary market transaction. The shares that trade thereafter, with prices quoted daily, are part of the secondary market.

## Types of Secondary Markets

Secondary markets are further divided into two types:

- **Auction markets** -- an open-outcry system where buyers and sellers congregate in one location and announce the prices at which they are willing to buy and sell securities.
- **Dealer markets** -- where market participants are connected through electronic networks and dealers maintain inventories of securities, standing ready to buy from or sell to market participants.

The key distinction between primary and secondary markets lies in the seller or source of the securities. In the primary market, the issuer of the shares or bonds is the seller. In the secondary market, the seller is another investor or owner. When you buy a security on the primary market, you are purchasing a new issuance directly from the issuer in a one-time transaction. When you buy a security on the secondary market, the original issuer -- whether a company or a government -- is not involved and does not benefit from the transaction.

In short: securities are purchased on the primary market and traded on the secondary market.

## Primary Market Examples

In June 2017, the Republic of Argentina announced a $2.75 billion debt sale in a two-part offering, with joint bookrunners including Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Merrill Lynch, Deutsche Bank, and Credit Suisse. It was the first time a sub-investment-grade sovereign -- Argentina had returned to debt markets the prior year after a major default -- offered century bonds. [2]

Facebook (now Meta) raised the largest IPO among internet companies -- and the largest in the U.S. tech sector -- when it went public in 2012. Expectations ran high: many investors believed the stock's value would appreciate rapidly on the secondary market given the company's popularity and rapid growth. Due to strong primary market demand, the underwriters set the share price at $38, at the top end of the targeted $35-$38 range, and increased the number of shares offered by 25% to 421 million. This resulted in a valuation of $104 billion, the largest for any newly public company at the time. [3][4]

Despite raising $16 billion through the primary market, Facebook's stock did not rise significantly on IPO day: it closed at $38.23 after 460 million shares changed hands, turnover exceeding 100%. The stock subsequently declined sharply and hit an all-time low of $17.73 on September 4, 2012. [5]

However, the company's pivot toward mobile platforms helped the stock recover.

An investor who put $10,000 into the IPO would have received approximately 263 shares of Facebook common stock. As of February 23, 2024, those shares were trading at $484 each, valuing the position at $127,292. [5] In retrospect, the $38 primary market purchase price proved to be quite an attractive entry point.

## The Relationship Between Primary and Secondary Markets

Both the primary and secondary markets are integral components of the capitalist financial system, in which capital is raised through the buying and selling of securities such as stocks and bonds. New securities are created and sold to investors in the primary market. Those securities are then traded among investors in the secondary market.

The primary market is also known as the new issues market. The secondary market is what we commonly refer to as the stock market or stock exchange.

## Types of Primary Markets

Almost every type of financial asset has a primary market. The largest include the primary stock market, the primary bond market, and the primary mortgage market.

The most common types of primary market issuances include:

- **Initial Public Offering (IPO):** A company issues stock to the public for the first time.
- **Rights Issue:** Existing shareholders are offered the opportunity to purchase additional new shares at a discounted price.
- **Private Placement:** Company stock is issued to individuals, corporate entities, or a small group of investors -- typically institutional or accredited investors -- rather than on the public market.
- **Preferred Allotment:** Shares are offered to a select group at a special or discounted price that differs from the publicly traded share price.

## The Role of the Primary Market

The primary market functions like a debut -- it marks the launch of a new security, whether a corporate stock or bond, into the financial marketplace. Primary markets enable companies and governments to attract investors and raise capital to pay down debt or expand operations. They also allow investors with capital to deploy it for income generation or to participate in the early stage of a promising new venture.

## Summary

The primary market is the symbolic venue where new securities make their debut -- where new bonds and corporate stocks are first issued and sold to investors. These securities are sold by the issuing companies, governments, or other entities, often with the assistance of investment banks that underwrite the new issues, set prices, and oversee the offering.

Nearly all types of assets have a primary market, with stocks (equity) and bonds being the most common. There are several types of primary market issuances, the most prominent being IPOs, followed by private placements and rights issues.

Most primary market buyers are institutional investors, although individual investors can readily participate in certain offerings, such as new U.S. Treasury issues.

After issuance in the primary market, existing stocks, bonds, and other securities trade on what is known as the secondary market -- essentially the familiar stock exchanges and equity markets.
