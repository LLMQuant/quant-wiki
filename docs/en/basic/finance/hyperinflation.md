# What Is Hyperinflation?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Hyperinflation refers to a rapid, excessive, and uncontrolled rise in the general price level across an economy. While inflation measures the pace at which prices for goods and services increase, hyperinflation represents an extreme acceleration of inflation -- typically defined as exceeding 50% per month.

Although hyperinflation is a rare event for developed economies, it has occurred historically in countries including China, Germany, Russia, Hungary, and Georgia.

### Key Takeaways

- Hyperinflation is a rapid and unconstrained increase in prices across an economy, typically exceeding 50% per month over a sustained period.
- Hyperinflation can occur when factors disrupt the underlying productive economy, compounded by excessive money printing by a central bank.
- Hyperinflation causes the prices of essential goods such as food and fuel to surge as demand outstrips supply.
- Hyperinflation episodes are typically rare, but once they begin, they can spiral out of control.

## Understanding Hyperinflation

Inflation in the United States is measured by the Bureau of Labor Statistics using the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which tracks approximately 94,000 goods and services; roughly 8,000 rental housing units; and prices for items including airfare, clothing, household goods, prescription drugs, used cars, and postage. [1]

Generally, the Federal Reserve targets what it considers a healthy long-run inflation rate of approximately 2%. [2] Hyperinflation represents an extreme form of inflation, not merely elevated inflation. Inflation above 5% is considered high; inflation of 50% or more per month qualifies as hyperinflation. [3]

**Note:** For comparison, U.S. inflation as measured by the CPI averaged approximately 2.5% annually from 2013 through 2022. The average from June 2022 through April 2023 was 5.55%. [4]

In a hyperinflationary environment, prices can rise daily or even weekly, creating an enormous impact on consumers' ability to afford basic necessities. For example, if you typically purchase the same items at the grocery store and the economy experiences a 5% daily inflation rate, your grocery bill could climb from $500 one week to $675 the next, and to approximately $911 the week after that.

## Causes of Hyperinflation

While several scenarios can trigger hyperinflation, the following are among the most common causes.

Central banks typically manage the money supply in circulation. In periods that historically demand monetary expansion -- such as recessions or depressions -- a central bank may increase the amount of currency in circulation. The intent is to encourage bank lending and spur consumer and business borrowing and spending.

However, if the increase in the money supply is not supported by economic growth (as measured by gross domestic product, or GDP), the result can be hyperinflation. If GDP is not growing, businesses raise prices to boost profits and remain viable.

Because consumers have more money, they pay higher prices, which fuels further inflation. If economic output continues to stagnate or contract while inflation keeps rising, businesses charge more, consumers pay more, and the central bank prints more money. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle of escalating inflation that eventually becomes hyperinflation.

Demand-pull inflation occurs when aggregate demand overwhelms aggregate supply. Prices rise rapidly because there are not enough goods and services to satisfy the combined demand of consumers and businesses.

**Important:** Hyperinflation is the product of multiple converging circumstances and misguided monetary policy decisions.

## Effects of Hyperinflation

Hyperinflation can produce a range of devastating consequences. People may begin hoarding goods -- particularly food -- leading to shortages in supply.

When prices rise excessively, the currency loses purchasing power, meaning consumers spend more to buy less. They have less money available to pay bills and purchase necessities.

People may also stop depositing money in financial institutions, causing banks and lenders to fail. Tax revenues may decline as consumers and businesses become unable to pay, leaving the government unable to provide basic services.

## How to Prepare for Hyperinflation

It is important to recognize that hyperinflation occurs infrequently, particularly in developed nations where central banks are focused on controlling inflation. Nevertheless, there are steps investors can take to reduce the impact of normal or elevated inflation on a portfolio.

A balanced and diversified portfolio can help limit losses during inflationary periods. Real assets such as commodities and real estate tend to appreciate during inflation. Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS) can hedge against rising inflation, as the principal invested in TIPS adjusts with changes in the inflation rate.

Mutual funds and exchange-traded funds that employ inflation swaps can also help counter the impact of inflation on a portfolio.

## Real-World Examples of Hyperinflation

One of the most destructive and prolonged episodes occurred in the former Yugoslavia during the 1990s. On the verge of national disintegration, the country was already experiencing annual inflation exceeding 76%. [5]

In 1991, it was discovered that the leader of the Serbian province, Slobodan Milosevic, had looted the national treasury by directing the Serbian central bank to issue $1.4 billion in loans to his associates. [5]

The theft forced the government's central bank to print excessive amounts of money to meet its fiscal obligations. Hyperinflation quickly engulfed the economy, eroding the country's remaining wealth and forcing its citizens to resort to bartering. The daily inflation rate nearly doubled each day until it reached a staggering 313,000,000% per month. [6]

The government seized control of production and wages, leading to food shortages. Incomes fell by more than 50%, and production ground to a halt. Eventually, the government replaced its currency with the German mark, which helped stabilize the economy. [7]

Following World War II, Hungary experienced hyperinflation with prices rising at 207% per day at their peak. [6]

In March 2007, Zimbabwe entered a period of hyperinflation that lasted until early 2009, with a daily inflation rate reaching 98%. [6] The country's hyperinflationary period had its roots in 1999, following multiple droughts and subsequent declines in GDP.

As a result, the country was forced to borrow beyond its productive capacity. The government increased spending to pay bonuses to war veterans, became involved in the Congo War, and borrowed from the International Monetary Fund to improve development and living standards.

The government resorted to printing money to finance these expenditures, driving inflation higher and prompting residents to emigrate to escape the economic collapse. By 2010, millions had left the country, and the economy was in ruins. [9]

## What Happens If Hyperinflation Occurs?

Hyperinflation does not emerge without warning. In the United States, if economists detect signs of hyperinflation, the Federal Reserve would deploy every monetary policy tool at its disposal to prevent it. This intervention would occur long before inflation approached 50%. In the past, Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker raised interest rates above 21% to combat inflation that had exceeded 14% -- triggering two recessions before inflation was brought under control. [10]

## Could the United States Experience Hyperinflation?

Barring an extremely severe deterioration in economic conditions, hyperinflation is unlikely in the United States. The Federal Reserve and the federal government possess numerous tools to prevent it.

## What Was the Worst Hyperinflation in History?

Hungary experienced hyperinflation from August 1945 through July 1946, with a daily inflation rate of 207%. [6]

## The Bottom Line

Hyperinflation occurs when a country's inflation rate rises by 50% or more in a single month. It is distinct from merely high inflation; a rate of 5% is considered high inflation.

Hyperinflation drives up consumer prices and can make it difficult or impossible for a nation to meet its fiscal obligations or produce goods and services. It causes the prices of everyday necessities to rise rapidly, making them unaffordable for many consumers. However, hyperinflation does not occur frequently and is typically precipitated by identifiable causes such as war, natural disasters, or political corruption.
