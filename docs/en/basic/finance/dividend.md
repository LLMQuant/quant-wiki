# What Is a Dividend?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A dividend is a distribution of a company's earnings to its shareholders, as determined by the company's board of directors. Dividends are typically paid on a quarterly basis and may be disbursed in cash or reinvested in additional shares of stock.

The dividend yield is the per-share dividend expressed as a percentage of the company's stock price -- for example, 2.5%. [1]

Common shareholders of a dividend-paying company are eligible to receive a distribution as long as they own the stock before the ex-dividend date.

### Key Takeaways

- A dividend is a distribution of corporate earnings to eligible shareholders.
- Dividend payments and amounts are determined by the company's board of directors.
- The dividend yield is the per-share dividend expressed as a percentage of the company's stock price.
- Many companies do not pay dividends, opting instead to retain earnings for reinvestment in the business.

## Understanding Dividends

Dividends must be approved by shareholder voting rights. [2] While cash dividends are the most common form, dividends can also be issued as shares of stock. Various mutual funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) also pay dividends.

A dividend is a reward paid to shareholders for investing in a company's equity. It typically originates from the company's net profits. For investors, dividends represent income; for the company, they are recorded as a liability. While profits can be retained within the company as retained earnings for ongoing and future operations, the remainder may be distributed to shareholders as dividends.

Companies may continue paying dividends even when they do not generate sufficient profits to maintain their established distribution track record.

The board of directors can choose to issue dividends over various timeframes and at different payout rates. Dividends may be paid on a scheduled frequency -- monthly, quarterly, or annually. For example, Walmart Inc. (WMT) and Unilever (UL) make regular quarterly dividend payments. [3] [4]

Companies can also issue non-recurring special dividends, either independently or in addition to a scheduled dividend. United Bancorp Inc. declared a special dividend of $0.15 per share on February 23, 2023. [5]

## Dividend-Paying Companies

Large, established companies with predictable earnings tend to be the best dividend payers. The following sectors maintain strong records of regular dividend payments:

- Basic materials
- Oil and gas
- Banking and financial services
- Healthcare and pharmaceuticals
- Utilities

Companies structured as master limited partnerships (MLPs) and real estate investment trusts (REITs) are required to make specified distributions to shareholders. [6] [7] Funds may also pay dividends on a regular basis as stipulated in their investment objectives.

Startups -- particularly in the technology or biotechnology sectors -- may not offer regular dividends, as these companies are often in the early stages of development and retain earnings for research and development, business expansion, and operations.

## Important Dividend Dates

Dividend payments follow a chronological sequence of events, and the associated dates are critical for determining which shareholders are eligible to receive payment.

- **Announcement date:** The dividend is announced by company management on the announcement (or declaration) date and must be approved by shareholders before it can be paid.
- **Ex-dividend date:** The date on which dividend eligibility expires is called the ex-dividend date. For example, if a stock has an ex-dividend date of Monday, May 5, shareholders who purchase the stock on or after that date will not receive the dividend. Shareholders who own the stock one business day before the ex-dividend date -- Friday, May 2, or earlier -- are eligible for the distribution.
- **Record date:** The record date is the cutoff established by the company to determine which shareholders are eligible for the dividend or distribution.
- **Payment date:** The company issues the dividend on the payment date, at which point the funds are credited to investors' accounts. [8] [2]

## How Dividends Affect a Stock's Price

For example, a company trading at $60 per share announces a $2 dividend on the announcement date. As the news becomes public, the stock price may rise by $2, reaching $62.

If the stock trades at $63 one business day before the ex-dividend date, it is adjusted downward by $2 on the ex-dividend date and begins trading at $61 at the open, because anyone buying on the ex-dividend date will not receive the dividend.

## Why Do Companies Pay Dividends?

Shareholders generally expect dividends as a return on their investment. Dividend payments reflect positively on a company and help maintain investor confidence.

A generous dividend announcement can signal that a company is performing well and generating solid profits. However, it may also indicate that the company lacks suitable projects to generate better future returns and is therefore distributing cash to shareholders rather than reinvesting in growth.

A company with a long history of dividend payments that announces a reduction or elimination of its dividend may signal to investors that the company is in trouble. AT&T Inc. cut its annual dividend in half to $1.11 on February 1, 2022, and its stock price fell 4% that day. [9] [10]

However, a dividend reduction or elimination is not necessarily bad news. Management may have a plan to deploy the capital elsewhere -- for example, into a high-return project that has the potential to magnify shareholder returns over the long run.

## Fund Dividends

Dividends paid by funds -- such as bond funds or mutual funds -- differ from those paid by corporations. Funds employ the net asset value (NAV) principle, which reflects the valuation of their holdings or the prices of assets in the fund's portfolio. [11]

Regular dividend payments from a fund should not be mistaken for exceptional performance. For example, a bond fund may pay monthly dividends because it receives monthly interest from its interest-bearing holdings and simply passes that income -- in whole or in part -- through to fund investors.

An equity fund pays dividends from earnings generated by the many stocks in its portfolio or by selling a portion of its holdings and distributing the resulting capital gains.

## Are Dividends Irrelevant?

Economists Merton Miller and Franco Modigliani argued that a company's dividend policy is irrelevant and has no effect on its stock price or cost of capital. A shareholder can remain indifferent to a company's dividend policy because, in the case of a high dividend payment, the investor can simply use the cash received to buy more shares.

If dividend payments are low, the investor can sell shares to generate the needed cash. In either case, the combined value of the investment in the company and the cash held remains the same. Miller and Modigliani therefore concluded that dividends are irrelevant and that investors should not be concerned with a company's dividend policy, since they can synthetically create their own. [12] That said, dividends remain an attractive investment incentive, providing shareholders with additional income.

## How to Buy Dividend-Paying Investments

Investors seeking dividend income have several options, including stocks, mutual funds, and ETFs. The dividend discount model and the Gordon Growth Model can help in selecting stock investments. These techniques rely on anticipated future dividend streams to value shares.

To compare multiple stocks based on their dividend-paying performance, investors can use the dividend yield factor, which measures the dividend as a percentage of the stock's current market price.

The dividend rate can also be expressed as the dollar amount of the dividend per share (DPS). In addition to dividend yield, another important performance measure for evaluating the returns generated by a specific investment is the total return factor, which accounts for interest, dividends, stock price appreciation, and other capital gains.

Taxes are another important consideration when investing for dividend income. Investors in high tax brackets in jurisdictions that offer zero or relatively low tax rates on dividends generally prefer dividend-paying stocks. For example, Greece and Slovakia impose lower taxes on dividend income, while dividend earnings in Hong Kong are tax-exempt. [13] [14]

## How Often Are Dividends Distributed to Shareholders?

Dividends are typically distributed quarterly, although some companies pay semi-annually. Payments can be received as cash or reinvested in additional shares of the company's stock.

## What Is an Example of a Dividend?

If a company's board of directors decides to issue an annual dividend of 5% per share and the company's stock is valued at $100, the dividend is $5. If dividends are issued quarterly, each distribution would be $1.25.

## Why Do Dividends Matter?

While dividends can signal that a company has stable cash flow and is generating profits, they also provide investors with recurring income. Dividend payments can also offer insight into a company's intrinsic value. Many jurisdictions offer preferential tax treatment for dividends, treating them as tax-exempt income. [15] [16]
