# What Is a Put Option?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A put option is a contract that gives the option buyer the right -- but not the obligation -- to sell (or go short) a specified quantity of an underlying security at a predetermined price within a specified time frame. This predetermined price is known as the strike price.

Put options can be traded on a wide range of underlying assets, including stocks, currencies, bonds, commodities, futures, and indices. A put option contrasts with a call option, which gives the holder the right to buy the underlying security at or before the option's expiration date at a specified price.

### Key Takeaways

- A put option gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell a specified quantity of an underlying security at a specified price within a specified time frame.
- Put options are available across a broad range of asset types, including stocks, indices, commodities, and currencies.
- Put option prices are influenced by changes in the underlying asset's price, the option's strike price, time decay, interest rates, and volatility.
- Put options increase in value as the underlying asset price falls, as volatility of the underlying increases, and as interest rates decline.
- Put options decrease in value as the underlying asset price rises, as volatility falls, as interest rates rise, and as expiration approaches.

## How Put Options Work

A put option increases in value when the underlying stock or security declines in price, and loses value when the underlying price rises. Put options are therefore commonly used for hedging or for speculating on downside price movements.

Investors frequently use puts in a risk management strategy known as a protective put -- a form of investment insurance or hedge designed to ensure that losses on the underlying asset do not exceed a certain amount. In this strategy, the investor purchases a put option to hedge against downside risk in a portfolio holding. If the option is exercised, the investor sells the stock at the put's strike price. If the investor does not own the underlying stock and exercises the put, a short position is created.

Generally, a put option's value declines as expiration approaches, primarily due to time decay. Time decay accelerates as the option nears expiration because the window for a profitable trade shrinks. When an option loses its time value, the remaining value is intrinsic value. Intrinsic value equals the difference between the strike price and the underlying stock price. An option with intrinsic value is said to be in the money (ITM).

**Important:** An option's intrinsic value = the difference between the market price of the underlying security and the option's strike price. For a put: intrinsic value = strike price minus market price. For a call: intrinsic value = market price minus strike price.

Out-of-the-money (OTM) and at-the-money (ATM) puts have no intrinsic value because exercising them would offer no benefit. Investors could instead short sell at the higher prevailing market price rather than exercise an OTM put at an unfavorable strike price. However, in a non-bearish market, short selling is generally riskier than buying puts.

Time value, or extrinsic value, is reflected in the option's premium. If a put has a $20 strike price and the underlying stock trades at $19, the intrinsic value is $1. But the put might trade at $1.35 -- the additional $0.35 is time value, reflecting the possibility that the underlying price could change before expiration. Different put options can be combined to form put spreads.

When selling put options, several considerations apply. Understanding the contract's value and profitability is essential before executing a trade; otherwise, the stock could fall below the break-even point.

## Where to Trade Options

Put options and many other types of options are traded through brokerage firms. Some brokers offer specialized features for options traders. Identifying a broker that matches your investment needs is important.

## Alternatives to Exercising a Put Option

The buyer of a put option does not need to hold the option until expiration. As the underlying stock price fluctuates, the option's premium changes to reflect the latest price movements. The option buyer can sell the option to minimize losses or realize a profit based on price changes since purchase.

Similarly, the option writer can close the position. If the underlying price is above the strike price, the option may expire worthless, allowing the writer to retain the full premium. But if the underlying price approaches or falls below the strike, the writer may buy back the option to exit the position. The gain or loss equals the difference between the premium collected and the premium paid to close.

## Put Option Example

Suppose an investor purchases one put option on the SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY), trading at $445, with a strike price of $425 expiring in one month. The premium is $2.80, or $280 ($2.80 x 100 shares).

If SPY falls to $415 before expiration, the $425 put is in the money and trades at a minimum of $10 (its intrinsic value: $425 - $415). Assuming the put trades at $10.50:

The investor must decide whether to (a) exercise the option, selling 100 shares of SPY at the $425 strike price, or (b) sell the put for a profit. Consider two scenarios: (i) the investor already owns 100 shares of SPY, and (ii) the investor does not.

**If the investor owns SPY (protective put):** The broker sells 100 shares at the $425 strike price.

Net profit = [($425 - $400 purchase price) - $2.80 premium] x 100 = $2,220

**If the investor does not own SPY (speculative trade):** Exercising the put creates a short position at $425. The investor then buys back 100 shares at $415 to close.

Net profit = [($425 - $415) - $2.80] x 100 = $720

However, a simpler approach is to sell the put at its current price:

Profit = [$10.50 - $2.80] x 100 = $770

A key point: selling the option rather than exercising it produces $770 versus $720 -- $50 more -- because selling captures the remaining $0.50 per share in time value ($0.50 x 100 = $50). This is why most long option positions with value before expiration are sold rather than exercised.

For the put buyer, the maximum loss is limited to the premium paid. The maximum gain occurs if the underlying stock price falls to zero.

**Selling vs. Exercising:** Most in-the-money long options are closed by selling rather than exercising before expiration, because exercise forfeits time value and incurs higher transaction costs and margin requirements.

## Writing Put Options

The previous section addressed puts from the buyer's perspective. Now consider the other side of the trade: the put seller, or writer, who holds a short put position.

Unlike a long put, a short (written) put obligates the investor to take delivery of -- or purchase -- the underlying stock at the strike price specified in the option contract.

Suppose an investor is bullish on SPY at $445 and does not believe it will fall below $430 in the next month. The investor writes a put with a $430 strike and collects a premium of $3.45 per share (x 100 shares = $345).

If SPY remains above $430 through expiration, the option expires worthless and the investor retains the $345 premium -- the maximum profit on the trade.

Conversely, if SPY falls below $430 before expiration, the investor must purchase 100 shares at $430, even if SPY has dropped to $400, $350, or lower. The theoretical maximum risk is $430 per share, or $43,000 per contract ($430 x 100 shares), which would occur if the underlying fell to zero.

The put writer's maximum gain is the premium collected, and the maximum loss occurs when the underlying falls to zero. The payoff profiles for put buyers and put writers are therefore mirror images.

## Is Buying a Put Similar to Short Selling?

Buying puts and short selling are both bearish strategies, but they differ in important ways. A put buyer's maximum loss is limited to the premium paid; purchasing puts does not require a margin account and can be accomplished with limited capital. Short selling, by contrast, carries theoretically unlimited risk and is significantly more expensive due to stock borrowing fees and margin interest. Short selling is considered riskier than buying puts.

## Should I Buy ITM or OTM Puts?

The answer depends on your trading objectives, risk tolerance, and available capital. In-the-money (ITM) puts cost more because they confer the right to sell at a higher price. OTM puts are cheaper but have a lower probability of profit. If you want to spend less on portfolio protection and are willing to accept moderate downside risk, OTM puts may be appropriate.

## Can I Lose My Entire Put Premium?

Yes. If the underlying security's price does not fall below the strike price by expiration, the put option expires worthless, and you lose the entire premium paid.

## Should Beginners with Limited Capital Write Puts?

Writing puts is an advanced options strategy suited to experienced traders and investors. Strategies such as cash-secured puts require significant capital. If you are new to options and have limited funds, writing puts is a risky endeavor and is not recommended.

## Summary

A put option enables the holder to sell a security at a guaranteed price, even if the market price has fallen. This makes it a powerful tool for both hedging strategies and speculative trades. Along with call options, puts are among the most fundamental derivative contracts.
