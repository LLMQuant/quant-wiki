# What Is a Convertible Bond?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A convertible bond is a fixed-income corporate debt security that pays interest but can be converted into a predetermined number of common shares or equity. Conversion from bond to stock may occur at specific times during the bond's life and is typically at the bondholder's discretion.

As a hybrid security, a convertible bond's price is particularly sensitive to changes in interest rates, the underlying stock price, and the issuer's credit rating.

### Key Takeaways

- Convertible bonds pay fixed-income interest but can be converted into a predetermined number of common shares.
- Conversion occurs at specific times during the bond's life and is generally at the bondholder's discretion.
- Convertible bonds offer investors a hybrid security that provides the interest payments of a bond along with the option to own the underlying stock.

## Understanding Convertible Bonds

Convertible bonds provide companies with a flexible financing option. Beyond standard bond interest payments, they offer investors the opportunity to participate in equity upside. The conversion ratio determines how many shares a bondholder receives upon converting one bond. For example, a 5:1 ratio means one bond converts into five shares of common stock.

The conversion price is the per-share price at which the convertible security can be exchanged for common stock. It is established when the conversion ratio is set and is typically found in the bond indenture or the securities prospectus.

If interest rates are favorable, the bond issuer may also force conversion by exercising a call provision. Forced conversion removes the investor's choice of whether to hold or convert.

One variant is known as "death spiral debt." In this structure, the bond is repaid in stock at a fixed dollar value. If the stock price drops sharply, holders of such convertible bonds will convert, diluting equity and driving the stock price further down.

**Tip:** Investors are not required to convert a convertible bond. They can hold it to maturity, at which point it behaves like a regular bond.

## Convertible Bonds vs. Regular Bonds

Convertible and regular bonds share many features but have key differences beyond the conversion option.

First, convertible bondholders can participate in the company's equity appreciation. Regular bondholders hold a pure debt instrument, while convertible bondholders have the option to benefit from stock price gains.

Second, because the embedded conversion option has value, convertible bonds typically offer lower coupon rates than comparable regular bonds. Regular bonds compensate investors with higher rates for the absence of equity upside.

Both types of bonds can trade on the secondary market prior to maturity. However, liquidity may vary depending on the issuer's credit rating, market conditions, and investor demand.

Finally, convertible bonds can theoretically serve as a portfolio rebalancing tool. If an investor recognizes an overweight in debt instruments, they can convert bonds into equity rather than selling outright.

## Characteristics of Convertible Bonds

- **Conversion option:** The bondholder can convert the bond into a specified number of shares of the issuing company's stock.
- **Interest payments:** As a debt instrument, convertible bonds make regular coupon payments. The coupon rate may be lower than that of non-convertible bonds due to the added value of the conversion option.
- **Maturity date:** Like traditional bonds, convertible bonds have a fixed maturity date. If the bondholder chooses not to convert, they receive the bond's face value at maturity.
- **Conversion ratio:** Specifies the number of shares received upon conversion. For example, a 20:1 ratio means one bond converts into 20 shares.
- **Conversion price:** The price at which the bond can be converted into stock, typically set above the stock's market price at the time of issuance.
- **Call and put provisions:** Some convertible bonds include call provisions (allowing the issuer to force conversion or redeem the bond before maturity, usually at a premium) and put provisions (allowing the bondholder to sell the bond back to the issuer at a specified price before maturity).

**Tip:** Convertible bond arbitrage is a trading strategy that seeks to exploit pricing discrepancies between a convertible bond and its underlying stock.

## Types of Convertible Bonds

**Vanilla convertible bonds** give investors the choice of holding to maturity or converting to stock. If the stock price declines after issuance, the investor can hold to maturity and receive face value. If the stock appreciates significantly, the investor can convert and either hold or sell the shares. Ideally, investors convert when the expected stock sale proceeds exceed the bond's face value plus remaining interest.

**Mandatory convertible bonds** require investors to convert at a specific conversion ratio and price level.

**Reverse convertible bonds** grant the company -- not the investor -- the right to convert the bond into stock or keep it as a fixed-income investment until maturity. Conversion occurs at a preset price and ratio.

## Calculating the Conversion Price

The conversion price is typically set at a premium to the stock's market price at issuance, incentivizing future conversion.

The calculation is straightforward: divide the bond's face value by the conversion ratio. For example, a $1,000 face value bond with a conversion ratio of 20 has a conversion price of $50 per share ($1,000 / 20). The bondholder can convert each bond into 20 shares at $50 per share.

In most cases, the conversion ratio remains fixed throughout the bond's life and is documented in the bond's terms at issuance.

However, if the conversion price were set at or below the current market price, bondholders could immediately convert and sell shares at a profit, effectively rendering the debt component meaningless. Therefore, conversion prices are almost always set above the market price at the time of issuance.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Convertible Bonds

Issuing convertible bonds can help companies avoid the negative investor sentiment associated with equity issuance. Each new share issuance dilutes existing shareholders' ownership. Convertible bonds defer potential dilution, and bondholders only convert when the company performs well.

Convertible bonds also offer a degree of downside protection in default scenarios. The bond protects the investor's principal while still allowing participation in the company's upside.

For example, a startup may have a capital-intensive project that generates near-term losses but is expected to become profitable. Convertible bond investors can recover some principal if the company fails, while sharing in the gains if it succeeds.

Investors benefit from the built-in equity kicker -- essentially a bond with an embedded call option on the stock. However, convertible bonds typically offer a lower coupon rate in exchange for the conversion feature. This lower value is known as the bond floor.

Companies benefit because they issue debt at a lower interest rate than traditional bonds. However, most convertible bonds are considered riskier and more volatile than typical fixed-income instruments.

#### Advantages

- Investors receive fixed-rate interest payments while retaining the option to convert to stock and participate in equity appreciation.
- Bondholders gain a measure of default protection, as they receive payment before common shareholders.
- Companies raise capital while avoiding immediate share dilution.
- Companies may pay lower interest rates than on conventional bonds.

#### Disadvantages

- Lower coupon rates due to the conversion feature.
- Issuers without established earnings (such as startups) add extra risk for convertible bond investors.
- If bonds are converted to stock, equity dilution may depress the share price and earnings per share.

## Convertible Bonds and Taxes

Several tax considerations apply to convertible bonds (these may differ for bonds held in tax-advantaged accounts):

- **Interest income:** Periodic coupon payments are generally taxed as ordinary income in the year received.
- **Conversion:** Converting a bond into stock is generally not a taxable event. However, any subsequent capital gains or losses realized from selling the stock are taxable.
- **Original issue discount (OID):** Some convertible bonds are issued below face value, creating an OID. OID is generally taxed as ordinary income over the bond's life.
- **Dividends:** If the issuer pays dividends on the stock received through conversion, those dividends may qualify for preferential tax treatment as qualified dividends, taxed at rates lower than ordinary income for eligible investors.

## Convertible Bond Example

Suppose ExxonMobil (XOM) issues a convertible bond with a $1,000 face value, a 4% coupon, and a 10-year maturity. The conversion ratio is 100 shares per bond.

If held to maturity, the investor receives $1,000 in principal plus $40 in annual interest. However, if the company's stock surges to $11 per share, the 100 shares are worth $1,100 -- exceeding the bond's value. The investor can convert and sell 100 shares for $1,100.

## What Is the Conversion Ratio?

The conversion ratio specifies the number of shares a bondholder receives upon conversion. For example, a 10:1 ratio means one convertible bond converts into 10 shares of the issuer's stock.

## How Do Interest Payments Work?

Convertible bond interest payments function like those of regular bonds. Bondholders receive periodic coupon payments -- typically semi-annually or annually -- based on the coupon rate and face value. These payments continue until the bond matures or is converted to equity.

## What Happens If a Convertible Bond Is Not Converted?

If a convertible bond is not converted by maturity, the issuer is generally obligated to repay the bond's face value plus any unpaid interest.

## When Can a Bondholder Convert?

Bondholders can typically convert at any time before maturity, provided the conditions specified in the bond's terms are met. Some issuers may stipulate a minimum holding period before conversion is permitted (e.g., not before three months).

## Conclusion

A convertible bond is a hybrid security that allows investors to convert their bonds into shares of the issuing company's stock at a predetermined conversion price. It offers the potential for capital appreciation through equity participation while providing downside protection through fixed interest payments.
