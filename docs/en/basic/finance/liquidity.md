# What Is Liquidity?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Liquidity refers to the efficiency or ease with which an asset or security can be converted into cash without affecting its market price. Cash itself is the most liquid of all assets. As a result, the availability of cash is the single greatest factor in determining whether a market can operate efficiently.

The more liquid an asset, the easier and more efficient the conversion to cash. Conversely, less liquid assets take more time to convert and may carry higher costs.

### Key Takeaways

- Liquidity refers to the ease with which an asset or security can be converted into cash without affecting its market price.
- Cash is the most liquid asset, while tangible items are less liquid.
- There are two main types of liquidity: market liquidity and accounting liquidity.
- The current ratio, quick ratio, and cash ratio are the most commonly used measures of liquidity.

## Understanding Liquidity

In other words, liquidity describes the degree to which an asset can be quickly bought or sold at a price that reflects its intrinsic value. Cash is universally considered the most liquid asset because it can be most quickly and easily converted into other assets. Tangible assets such as real estate, fine art, and collectibles are all relatively illiquid. Other financial assets, ranging from stocks to partnership units, fall at various positions along the liquidity spectrum.

For example, if a person needs a $1,000 refrigerator, cash is the asset most easily used to obtain it. If that person has no cash but possesses a rare book collection appraised at $1,000, they are unlikely to find someone willing to trade the refrigerator for the collection. Instead, they would have to sell the collection and use the proceeds to buy the refrigerator.

If the person can wait months or years before making the purchase, that may be acceptable. But if they have only a few days, they may need to sell the books at a discount rather than waiting for a buyer willing to pay full value. Rare books are an example of an illiquid asset.

**Tip:** There are two main measures of liquidity: market liquidity and accounting liquidity.

Market liquidity refers to the extent to which a market -- such as a country's stock market or a city's real estate market -- allows assets to be bought and sold at stable, transparent prices. In the example above, the market for exchanging rare books for a refrigerator is so illiquid that it essentially does not exist.

By contrast, the stock market is characterized by higher market liquidity. If an exchange has a high trading volume that is not dominated by selling, the price a buyer offers (the bid price) and the price a seller is willing to accept (the ask price) will be close together.

Investors therefore do not need to give up unrealized gains for a quick sale. When the spread between bid and ask narrows, the market becomes more liquid; when it widens, the market becomes more illiquid. Real estate markets are typically far less liquid than stock markets. The liquidity of other asset markets -- including derivatives, contracts, currencies, and commodities -- generally depends on their size and the number of open exchanges on which they can be traded.

Accounting liquidity measures an individual's or company's ability to meet financial obligations using available liquid assets -- that is, the ability to pay debts as they come due.

In the example above, the rare book collector's assets are relatively illiquid and would likely not be worth their full $1,000 in a pinch. In investment terms, assessing accounting liquidity means comparing liquid assets to current liabilities -- financial obligations due within one year.

A number of ratios measure accounting liquidity, and they differ in how strictly they define liquid assets. Analysts and investors use these figures to identify companies with strong liquidity. This is also considered a measure of market depth.

## Liquidity Example

In terms of investments, equities as a class are among the most liquid assets. However, not all equities or other fungible securities are equal when it comes to liquidity. Some options and stocks trade more actively on exchanges than others. More active trading indicates that market interest in those stocks is stronger, attracting a larger and more consistent pool of traders and investors.

**Tip:** In addition to trading volume, factors such as the width of bid-ask spreads, market depth, and order book data can provide further insight into a stock's liquidity. Therefore, while volume is an important factor in evaluating liquidity, it should not be relied upon exclusively.

These more liquid stocks are typically characterized by their daily trading volume, which can run into the millions or even hundreds of millions of shares. When a stock has high volume, it means there are many buyers and sellers in the market, making it easy for investors to buy or sell without significantly affecting the price. Conversely, low-volume stocks may be harder to buy or sell because there are fewer market participants, resulting in lower liquidity.

For example, on March 13, 2023, Amazon.com Inc. (AMZN) traded a volume of 69.6 million shares on exchanges. By comparison, Intel Corp. (INTC) traded only 48.1 million shares, suggesting relatively lower liquidity. However, Ford Motor Co. (F) traded 118.5 million shares, making it the most actively traded -- and most liquid -- of the three that day.

## Why Liquidity Matters

If markets are not liquid, it becomes difficult to sell or convert assets or securities into cash. You may own a very rare and valuable family heirloom appraised at $150,000. However, if there is no market (i.e., no buyers) for your object, then the appraisal is irrelevant since no one will pay anywhere near its appraised value -- it is deeply illiquid. You may even need to hire an auction house to act as a broker to find potentially interested parties, which will take time and incur costs.

Liquid assets, however, can be easily and quickly sold at full value with minimal cost. Companies must also hold sufficient liquid assets to cover short-term obligations such as bills and payroll, or they may face a liquidity crisis that could lead to bankruptcy.

## What Are the Most Liquid Assets or Securities?

Cash is the most liquid asset, followed by cash equivalents such as money market accounts, certificates of deposit (CDs), or time deposits. Marketable securities such as stocks and bonds listed on exchanges are also generally very liquid and can be sold quickly through a broker. Gold coins and certain collectibles may also be readily sold for cash.

## What Are Some Illiquid Assets or Securities?

Securities that are traded over the counter (OTC), such as certain complex derivatives, are often quite illiquid. For individuals, a home, a timeshare, or a car are all relatively illiquid since finding a buyer may take weeks to months, and completing a transaction and receiving payment can take several more weeks. Furthermore, broker fees tend to be quite high (for example, the average real estate agent commission is 5% to 7%).

## Why Are Some Stocks More Liquid Than Others?

The most liquid stocks tend to be those with heavy interest from various market participants and high daily transaction volumes. Such stocks attract a greater number of market makers, maintaining tighter two-sided markets.

Illiquid stocks tend to have wider bid-ask spreads and less market depth. These stocks tend to be less well-known, have lower trading volume, and often have lower market values and volatility. As a result, the stock of a large multinational bank tends to be more liquid than that of a small regional bank.

## Conclusion

Liquidity is the ease with which an asset or security can be converted into cash, and cash itself is the most liquid of all assets. Other liquid assets include stocks, bonds, and other exchange-traded securities. Tangible assets tend to be less liquid, meaning that selling them -- a home, for example -- may require more time, effort, and cost.

Market liquidity and accounting liquidity are the two main classifications, and financial analysts use various ratios -- including the current ratio, quick ratio, acid-test ratio, and cash ratio -- to measure them. Maintaining liquidity is important for individuals and companies alike, enabling them to pay off short-term debts and obligations and avoid a liquidity crisis.
