## What Is Volatility?

Volatility is a statistical measure of the dispersion of returns for a given security or market index. It is typically measured by the standard deviation or variance of returns. In most cases, the higher the volatility, the riskier the security.

In securities markets, volatility is often associated with large price swings in either direction. For example, when the stock market rises or falls by more than 1% over a sustained period, it is called a volatile market.

An asset's volatility is a key factor in pricing options contracts.

### Key Takeaways

- Volatility represents how much an asset's price fluctuates around its average price.
- There are several ways to measure volatility, including beta coefficients, option pricing models, and the standard deviation of returns.
- Volatile assets are generally considered riskier than less volatile assets because price movements are harder to predict.
- Implied volatility measures the market's expected volatility, while historical volatility measures price movements over past periods.
- Volatility is an important variable in options pricing calculations.

## Understanding Volatility

Volatility generally refers to the degree of risk or uncertainty associated with the magnitude of changes in a security's value.

High volatility means that the value of a security can be spread over a wider range. This implies that the price of the security can change dramatically over a short period in either direction. Low volatility means that the value of a security does not fluctuate significantly and tends to be more stable.

One way to measure an asset's variation is to quantify the daily returns (the percentage change in price on a daily basis). Historical volatility is based on historical prices and represents the degree of variability in an asset's returns. This number is unitless and is expressed as a percentage.

While variance captures the dispersion of returns around the mean for an asset, volatility is the variance bounded by a specific time period. Accordingly, we can report daily, weekly, monthly, or annualized volatility. It is useful to think of volatility as the annualized standard deviation.

## Types of Volatility

Implied volatility (IV), also known as projected volatility, is one of the most important metrics for options traders. As the name suggests, it allows traders to gauge how volatile the market is likely to be going forward.

The concept also provides traders with a way to calculate probability. An important point is that it should not be considered science and therefore does not predict the future direction of the market.

Unlike historical volatility, implied volatility is derived from the price of an option itself and represents expectations of future volatility. Because it is implied, traders cannot use past performance as an indicator of future performance. Instead, they must estimate the potential of the option in the market.

**Note:** Implied volatility is a key feature of options trading.

Historical volatility (HV), also known as statistical volatility, gauges the fluctuations of underlying securities by measuring price changes over a predetermined period. When historical volatility rises, the security's price is moving more than normal. At that point, there is typically an expectation that something will change. If historical volatility is declining, it means that uncertainty has been eliminated and the market is returning to normal.

This calculation can be based on intraday changes but is typically measured from one closing price to the next. Depending on the intended duration of the options trade, historical volatility can be measured over periods ranging from 10 to 180 trading days.

## Volatility and Options Pricing

Volatility is a key variable in options pricing models, estimating the extent to which the return of the underlying asset will fluctuate between now and the option's expiration. Volatility, expressed as a percentage coefficient within the pricing formula, arises from daily trading activities, and the method used to measure volatility affects the value of the coefficient.

Volatility is also used to price options contracts using models such as the Black-Scholes model or binomial tree models. More volatile underlying assets translate into higher options premiums because, with greater volatility, there is a higher probability that the options will expire in the money.

Options traders attempt to predict an asset's future volatility, so the price of an option in the market reflects its implied volatility.

**Important:** The greater the volatility, the higher the market price of options contracts tends to be.

## Other Measures of Volatility

One measure of a specific stock's relative volatility compared to the market is its beta. Beta approximates the overall volatility of a security's returns against the returns of a relevant benchmark (typically the S&P 500). For example, a stock with a beta value of 1.1 would be expected to move 110% for every 100% move in the benchmark.

Conversely, a stock with a beta of 0.9 would be expected to move 90% for every 100% move in the benchmark.

Market volatility can also be observed through the Volatility Index (VIX), a quantitative measure of stock market volatility.

The Chicago Board Options Exchange created the VIX as a gauge of the expected 30-day volatility of the U.S. stock market, derived from real-time quote prices of S&P 500 call and put options. It effectively serves as a measure of the bets investors and traders are placing on the future direction of the markets or individual securities. A high VIX reading implies a risky market.

Traders can also trade the VIX using a variety of options and exchange-traded products, or use VIX values to price certain derivative products.

## Tips for Managing Volatility

Investors may find periods of high volatility unsettling as prices can swing wildly or drop suddenly. Long-term investors are well advised to ignore short-term volatility and stay the course. Over the long run, stock markets tend to rise. Meanwhile, emotions like fear and greed -- which can be amplified in volatile markets -- can undermine a long-term investment strategy.

Some investors can use volatility as an opportunity to add to their portfolios by buying when prices are relatively cheap.

Hedging strategies can also be used to navigate volatility, such as buying protective put options to limit downside losses without selling any shares. However, note that put options become more expensive when volatility is elevated.

## What Is Volatility Mathematically?

Volatility is a statistical measure of the dispersion of data around its mean over a given period. It is calculated as the standard deviation multiplied by the square root of the number of time periods, T. In finance, it represents this dispersion of market prices, presented on an annualized basis.

## Is Volatility the Same as Risk?

Volatility is commonly used to describe risk, but this is not always precise. Risk involves the chance of suffering a loss, while volatility describes the magnitude and speed of price movements. If increased price swings also increase the chance of losses, then risk rises accordingly.

## Is Volatility a Good Thing?

Whether volatility is good or bad depends on the type of trader you are and your risk tolerance. For long-term investors, volatility can mean turbulence, but for day traders and options traders, volatility often equates to trading opportunities.

## What Does High Volatility Mean?

If volatility is high, it means prices are moving rapidly and dramatically -- either up or down.

## What Is the VIX?

The VIX is the Chicago Board Options Exchange Volatility Index, which measures the broader market's short-term volatility as implied by the 30-day S&P 500 options contracts. The VIX generally rises when stocks decline and falls when stocks advance. Also known as the "fear index," the VIX can serve as a gauge of market sentiment, with higher values indicating greater volatility and investor anxiety.

## Conclusion

Volatility is the magnitude and speed at which prices move over a given period. In the stock market, increased volatility is often a sign of investor fear and uncertainty. This is why the VIX is sometimes called the "fear index."

At the same time, volatility presents opportunities for day traders to enter and exit positions. Volatility is also an essential component of options pricing and trading.
