# What Is a Leverage Ratio?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A leverage ratio is a financial metric that measures how much capital takes the form of debt (loans), or that assesses a company's ability to meet its financial obligations. The leverage ratio category is important because companies rely on a mix of equity and debt to finance operations, and knowing the amount of debt a company carries helps evaluate whether it can repay its obligations as they come due.

Several common leverage ratios are discussed below.

### Key Takeaways

- A leverage ratio is a financial metric that assesses a company's ability to meet its financial obligations.
- Leverage ratios can also be used to measure a company's operating expense mix to understand how changes in output affect operating income.
- Common leverage ratios include the debt-to-equity ratio, equity multiplier, degree of financial leverage, and consumer leverage ratio.
- Banks are subject to regulatory oversight regarding the amount of leverage they can hold.

## What Leverage Ratios Tell You

In most cases, a leverage ratio evaluates a company's ability to meet its financial obligations. Too much debt can be dangerous for a company and its investors. However, if a company's operations can generate a rate of return above the interest rate on its loans, then debt can help fuel growth.

Uncontrolled debt levels can lead to credit downgrades or worse. On the other hand, too little debt can also raise questions. A reluctance or inability to borrow may signal thin operating margins.

**Important:** Several different ratios may be classified as leverage ratios. The main factors considered are debt, equity, assets, and interest expenses.

Leverage ratios can also be used to measure a company's operating expense mix to understand how changes in output affect operating income. Fixed and variable costs are the two types of operating costs; the mix varies by company and industry.

Another leverage ratio is the consumer leverage ratio. This ratio looks at consumer debt levels relative to disposable income and is used in economic analysis and by policymakers.

**Tip:** Understanding how debt amplifies returns is the key to understanding leverage. Debt is not inherently bad, especially when it is used to invest in projects that generate positive returns. Leverage can therefore amplify returns, but it can also amplify losses if returns turn negative.

## Banks and Leverage Ratios

Banks are among the most leveraged institutions in the United States. The combination of fractional reserve banking and Federal Deposit Insurance Corporation (FDIC) protection creates a banking environment with limited lending risk.

To compensate for this, three separate regulatory bodies -- the FDIC, the Federal Reserve, and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency -- review and restrict leverage ratios for American banks. [1] These agencies limit the amount banks can lend relative to the capital they devote to their own assets. Capital levels are critical because banks can "write down" the capital portion of their assets if total asset values decline. Assets funded by debt cannot be written down because the bank's bondholders and depositors are entitled to those funds.

Banking regulations around leverage ratios are highly complex. The Federal Reserve created guidelines for bank holding companies, though the limits vary depending on the rating assigned to the bank. In general, banks experiencing rapid growth or facing operational or financial difficulties are required to maintain higher leverage ratios. [2]

**Note:** Several forms of capital requirements and minimum reserves imposed on American banks through the FDIC and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency indirectly affect leverage ratios.

Scrutiny of leverage ratios has increased since the Great Recession of 2007-2009, when "too big to fail" banks became the rallying cry for making banks more solvent. This pressure has not dissipated, and restrictions have continued to tighten. [3]

In 2023, following several bank failures, regulators proposed that banks with $100 billion or more in assets substantially increase their capital buffers. These limits naturally constrain lending because raising capital is more difficult and expensive than borrowing. Higher capital requirements may reduce dividends or dilute share value if more equity is issued. [4]

For banks, the Tier 1 leverage ratio is the measure most commonly used by regulators. [5][6]

## Types of Leverage Ratios

There are many different leverage ratios. Below are some of the most common.

Perhaps the best-known financial leverage ratio is the debt-to-equity ratio (D/E). It is expressed as:

$$ \text{Debt-to-Equity Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Liabilities}}{\text{Total Shareholders' Equity}} $$

For example, in the quarter ending June 30, 2023, United Parcel Service's long-term debt was $19.35 billion and its total shareholders' equity was $20 billion. The company's D/E for that quarter was 0.97. [7]

A high D/E ratio generally indicates that a company has been aggressive in financing growth with debt. This can lead to volatile earnings due to additional interest expense. If interest expenses grow too high, the company's probability of default or bankruptcy increases.

Typically, a D/E ratio above 2.0 indicates a risky scenario for investors; however, this benchmark varies by industry. Businesses that require substantial capital expenditures (CapEx), such as utilities and manufacturing companies, may need to secure more loans than companies in other sectors.

**Tip:** It is best to compare a company's leverage ratios against its own historical performance and against peers in the same industry for more meaningful analysis.

The equity multiplier is similar but replaces debt with assets in the numerator:

$$ \text{Equity Multiplier} = \frac{\text{Total Assets}}{\text{Total Equity}} $$

For example, assume Macy's has assets valued at $19.85 billion and shareholders' equity of $4.32 billion. The equity multiplier would be:

$$ \$19.85 \text{ billion} \div \$4.32 \text{ billion} = 4.59 $$

Although debt is not explicitly referenced in the formula, it is an underlying factor since total assets include debt.

Recall that Total Assets = Total Debt + Total Shareholders' Equity. The company's high ratio of 4.59 indicates that assets are primarily funded by debt rather than equity. From the equity multiplier calculation, Macy's assets are financed by $15.53 billion in liabilities.

The equity multiplier is a component of DuPont analysis used to calculate return on equity (ROE):

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{DuPont Analysis} = NPM \times AT \times EM\\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &NPM=\text{net profit margin}\\ &AT=\text{asset turnover}\\ &EM=\text{equity multiplier}\\ \end{aligned} $$

**Tip:** Generally, a lower equity multiplier is preferable because it indicates the company is not generating excessive debt to finance its assets.

The debt-to-capitalization ratio measures the amount of debt in a company's capital structure. It is calculated as:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{Total Debt to Capitalization} = \frac{(SD + LD)}{(SD + LD + SE)}\\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &SD=\text{short-term debt}\\ &LD=\text{long-term debt}\\ &SE=\text{shareholders' equity}\\ \end{aligned} $$

In this ratio, operating leases are capitalized and equity includes both common and preferred shares. An analyst may choose to use total debt to measure the debt used in a company's capital structure. In that case, the formula would include minority interests and preferred shares in the denominator.

The degree of financial leverage (DFL) measures the sensitivity of a company's earnings per share (EPS) to fluctuations in its operating income, as a result of changes in its capital structure. It measures the percentage change in EPS for a unit change in earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) and is expressed as:

$$ \begin{aligned} &DFL = \frac{\% \text{ change in EPS}}{\% \text{ change in EBIT}} \\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &EPS=\text{earnings per share}\\ &EBIT=\text{earnings before interest and taxes}\\ \end{aligned} $$

DFL can alternatively be represented as:

$$ DFL = \frac{EBIT}{EBIT - \text{Interest}} $$

This ratio indicates that the higher the degree of financial leverage, the more volatile earnings will be. Since interest is typically a fixed expense, leverage magnifies returns and EPS. This is favorable when operating income is rising but problematic when operating income is under pressure.

The consumer leverage ratio quantifies the amount of debt held by the average American consumer relative to disposable income.

Some economists have argued that the rapid increase in consumer debt levels over recent decades has been a contributing factor to corporate earnings growth. Others have blamed high levels of consumer debt as a primary cause of the Great Recession.

$$ \text{Consumer Leverage Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Household Debt}}{\text{Personal Disposable Income}} $$

The debt-to-capital ratio is one of the most meaningful debt ratios because it focuses on the relationship of debt liabilities as a component of a company's total capital base. It is calculated by dividing total debt by total capital, which equals total debt plus total shareholders' equity. Debt includes all short-term and long-term obligations.

This ratio is used to evaluate a company's financial structure and how it finances operations. Generally, the higher the debt-to-capital ratio, the higher the default risk. If the ratio is very high, earnings may not be sufficient to cover the cost of debt and liabilities.

As with other metrics, what constitutes a reasonable debt-to-capital ratio depends on the industry. Some industries use more leverage than others.

The debt-to-EBITDA leverage ratio measures the amount of income generated and available to pay down debt before covering interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization expenses. This ratio is commonly used by credit rating agencies and is calculated by dividing short-term and long-term debt by EBITDA to determine the probability of default on issued debt.

The ratio helps determine how many years of EBITDA would be needed to repay all debt. Typically, a ratio above 3 may be cause for concern, though this varies by industry.

The debt-to-EBITDAX ratio is similar to the debt-to-EBITDA ratio. It simply measures debt against EBITDAX rather than EBITDA. EBITDAX stands for earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation (or depletion), amortization, and exploration expense. It extends EBITDA by excluding exploration costs -- a common expense for oil and gas companies.

This ratio is commonly used in the United States to normalize different accounting treatments for exploration costs (the full-cost method versus the successful-efforts method). Exploration costs are typically found on financial statements as exploration, abandonment, and dry hole costs. Other noncash expenses that should be added back include impairments, accretion of asset retirement obligations, and deferred taxes.

Another leverage ratio related to interest payments is the interest coverage ratio. One problem with reviewing only a company's total debt liabilities is that they reveal nothing about the company's ability to service that debt. This is exactly what the interest coverage ratio aims to address.

This ratio equals operating income divided by interest expense and shows the company's ability to make interest payments. A ratio of 3.0 or higher is generally considered desirable, though the threshold varies by industry.

The times interest earned (TIE) ratio, also known as the fixed-charge coverage ratio, is a variation of the interest coverage ratio. This leverage ratio highlights cash flow relative to interest owed on long-term debt.

To calculate it, find the company's earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) and divide by the interest expense on long-term debt. Pre-tax earnings are used because interest is tax-deductible; ultimately, all of the earnings can be used to pay interest. As with other ratios, higher numbers are more favorable.

## What Does Leverage Mean in Finance?

Leverage is the use of debt to make investments. The goal is to generate returns that exceed the cost of borrowing. If a company fails to do this, it is neither doing a good job nor creating value for shareholders.

## How Is a Leverage Ratio Calculated?

There are various leverage ratios, each calculated differently. In many cases, it involves dividing a company's debt by something else, such as shareholders' equity, total capital, or EBITDA.

## What Is a Good Leverage Ratio?

It depends on the specific leverage ratio being used and the type of company. For example, capital-intensive industries rely more on debt than service-oriented businesses, so they would be expected to carry higher leverage. To gauge what constitutes an acceptable level, look at leverage ratios for a specific industry. It is also worth noting that having very little debt is not necessarily a positive sign. Companies can use debt to generate greater returns for shareholders.

## The Bottom Line

Leverage ratios are useful tools. They provide a straightforward way to understand the extent to which a company relies on debt to fund its operations and expansion.

Debt matters. When used wisely, it can generate a rate of return above its cost. But too much is dangerous and can lead to default and financial ruin.

Leverage varies by industry. Certain types of companies rely on debt more than others, and even banks are told how much leverage they may hold. Leverage ratios are most useful when compared against historical data or industry peers.
