# What Is Dilution?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Dilution occurs when a company issues new shares, resulting in a decrease in existing shareholders' ownership percentage of the company. Dilution can also occur when holders of stock options -- such as company employees -- or holders of other optionable securities exercise their rights. When the number of shares outstanding increases, each existing shareholder owns a smaller proportionate piece of the company -- they are diluted -- making each share less valuable.

A share of stock represents equity ownership in a company. When a company's board of directors decides to take the company public -- typically through an initial public offering (IPO) -- it authorizes the number of shares to be initially offered. This outstanding share count is commonly referred to as the "float." If the company subsequently issues additional shares (often called a secondary offering), it increases the float and thereby dilutes the stock: shareholders who purchased the original IPO now own a smaller stake in the company than they did before the new shares were issued.

### Key Takeaways

- Dilution is the reduction in shareholders' equity resulting from the issuance or creation of new shares.
- Dilution also reduces a company's earnings per share (EPS), which can have a negative impact on share price.
- Dilution can occur when a company raises additional equity capital, although existing shareholders are typically at a disadvantage.

## Understanding Dilution

Dilution is essentially the act of cutting the equity "pie" into more slices. There will be more slices, but each one will be smaller. You still get your slice, but it represents a smaller share of the total than you expected -- which is generally unwelcome.

While dilution primarily affects equity ownership, it also reduces a company's earnings per share (EPS = net income divided by the float), which often depresses the stock price. For this reason, many public companies publish estimates of both basic and diluted EPS -- effectively a "what-if" scenario for investors in the event new shares are issued. Diluted EPS assumes that potentially dilutive securities have already been converted into outstanding shares.

Dilution can occur when a company raises additional equity capital, because newly created shares are issued to new investors. The potential upside of raising capital in this way is that the funds received from selling additional shares can improve the company's profitability and growth prospects, ultimately increasing the value of its stock.

Understandably, dilution is generally not viewed favorably by existing shareholders. Companies sometimes initiate share buyback programs to help offset the effects of dilution. Note that stock splits do not cause dilution. When a company splits its stock, existing investors receive additional shares while the share price is adjusted proportionally, keeping their percentage ownership in the company unchanged.

## General Example of Dilution

Suppose a company has issued 100 shares to 100 individual shareholders. Each shareholder owns 1% of the company. If the company then conducts a secondary offering and issues 100 new shares to an additional 100 shareholders, each shareholder now owns only 0.5% of the company. The smaller ownership percentage also diminishes each investor's voting power.

## Real-World Example of Dilution

Typically, a public company announces its intention to issue new shares well in advance, thereby diluting its current equity pool. This allows both new and existing investors to plan accordingly. For example, MGT Capital filed a proxy statement on July 8, 2016, outlining a stock option plan for newly appointed CEO John McAfee. The statement also disclosed the structure of recent company acquisitions, which were funded through a combination of cash and stock.

Both the executive stock option plan and the acquisitions were expected to dilute the existing pool of outstanding shares. In addition, the proxy statement included a proposal to authorize the issuance of newly authorized shares, suggesting that the company anticipated further dilution in the near term.

## Dilution Protection

Shareholders typically resist dilution because it devalues their existing equity. Dilution protection refers to contractual provisions that restrict or entirely prevent an investor's stake in a company from being reduced in later funding rounds. The dilution protection feature is triggered if the company's actions would reduce the investor's percentage claim on company assets.

For example, if an investor holds a 20% stake and the company is about to conduct an additional funding round, the company must offer the investor discounted shares to at least partially compensate for the dilution of the overall ownership stake. Dilution protection provisions are commonly found in venture capital financing agreements. Dilution protection is sometimes referred to as "anti-dilution protection."

Similarly, an anti-dilution clause is a provision in an option or convertible security -- also known as an "anti-dilution provision" -- that protects investors from equity dilution caused by the later issuance of shares at a price lower than what the investor originally paid. These clauses are common in convertible preferred stock, a popular form of venture capital investment.
