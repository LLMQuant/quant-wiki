# What Is Keynesian Economics?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Keynesian economics is a macroeconomic theory that examines aggregate spending in the economy and its effects on output, employment, and inflation. Developed by British economist John Maynard Keynes during the 1930s in response to the Great Depression, Keynesian economics holds that government intervention can stabilize the economy.

Keynes's theory was the first to clearly separate the study of individual economic behavior and incentives from the study of broad aggregate variables and macroeconomic structures. Based on his framework, Keynes advocated increased government spending and lower taxes to stimulate demand and pull the global economy out of depression.

Keynesian economics subsequently came to denote the idea that optimal economic performance can be achieved -- and recessions prevented -- through active government management of aggregate demand via fiscal and monetary policy.

### Key Takeaways

- Keynesian economics advocates active government policy to manage aggregate demand in order to address or prevent recessions.
- The core principle is that government intervention can support and strengthen the economy.
- John Maynard Keynes developed his theory in response to the Great Depression.
- He was highly critical of prior economic thought, which he characterized as classical economics.
- Active fiscal and monetary policies are the primary tools Keynesian economists recommend for managing the economy and combating unemployment.

## Understanding Keynesian Economics

Keynesian economics represented a fundamentally new way of thinking about spending, output, and inflation. Previously, classical economic thought held that cyclical swings in employment and output created profit opportunities that individuals and entrepreneurs would naturally pursue, thereby correcting imbalances in the economy.

According to Keynes's interpretation of classical theory, if aggregate demand declined, the resulting weakness in production and employment would cause prices and wages to fall. Lower inflation and wages would induce employers to make capital investments and hire more workers, stimulating employment and restoring growth. [1]

Keynes argued that the depth and persistence of the Great Depression severely tested this assumption. [1]

In *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* and other works, Keynes challenged the classical framework. He contended that during downturns, business pessimism and certain characteristics of market economies compound economic weakness, driving aggregate demand even lower than it has already fallen. [1]

For example, Keynesian economics disputes the notion that lower wages alone can restore full employment, since labor demand curves slope downward like any other demand curve. [1]

Similarly, poor business conditions may cause firms to cut capital investment rather than take advantage of lower prices to invest in new plant and equipment -- further reducing aggregate spending and employment. [2]

## Keynesian Economics and the Great Depression

Keynesian economics is sometimes called "depression economics" because Keynes wrote *The General Theory* during a period of deep global depression.

The influential 1936 book was shaped by Keynes's understanding of events during the Great Depression -- events he believed classical economic theory could not adequately explain.

Other economists had argued that in the wake of a broad economic downturn, businesses and investors pursuing their own interests at lower input prices would restore output and prices to equilibrium. Keynes believed the Great Depression contradicted this view.

During that era, output was low and unemployment remained persistently high. The Depression inspired Keynes to reconceptualize the nature of the economy. From these theories, he developed real-world applications with implications for societies in economic crisis.

Keynes rejected the notion that the economy would naturally return to equilibrium. Instead, he argued that once a recession takes hold, the fear and pessimism it generates among businesses and investors tend to become self-fulfilling, potentially leading to sustained periods of depressed economic activity and elevated unemployment.

To combat this, Keynes advocated countercyclical fiscal policy: during economic hardship, the government should engage in deficit spending to offset declines in investment and stimulate consumer spending, thereby stabilizing aggregate demand. [2]

Keynes was sharply critical of the British government of his time. [3] The government had substantially increased welfare spending and raised taxes to balance the national accounts. Keynes argued this would not encourage people to spend, leaving the economy unable to recover.

Keynes also criticized excessive saving -- except for specific purposes like retirement or education -- viewing it as dangerous for the economy because idle money meant less stimulus for growth. [4]

Many economists criticized Keynes's approach, arguing that businesses responding to economic incentives would naturally restore equilibrium unless the government prevented them from doing so through price and wage interventions. Keynes, writing as the world was mired in a prolonged depression, was less optimistic about the economy's self-correcting capabilities and believed government was better positioned than market forces to foster a strong economy.

**Note:** Keynes recommended that governments increase spending and cut taxes, which would boost consumer demand. This would, in turn, lead to increased overall economic activity and lower unemployment. [2]

## Keynesian Economics and Fiscal Policy

The multiplier effect, formulated by Keynes's student Richard Kahn, is a central component of Keynesian countercyclical fiscal policy.

According to Keynes's fiscal stimulus theory, an injection of government spending ultimately generates more business activity and even more spending. The theory holds that spending boosts aggregate output and generates additional income. If workers are willing to spend their extra income, the resulting GDP growth can be even larger than the initial stimulus amount. [5]

The magnitude of the Keynesian multiplier is directly related to the marginal propensity to consume. Consumer spending becomes business revenue, which firms then deploy for equipment, wages, energy, materials, purchased services, taxes, and investor returns. Workers' income is then spent, and the cycle continues.

Keynes and his followers argued that individuals should save less and spend more, raising their marginal propensity to consume to achieve full employment and economic growth.

Keynes's fiscal stimulus theory dominated academic economics for decades. Eventually, however, economists including Milton Friedman and Murray Rothbard demonstrated that the Keynesian model misrepresented the relationship between saving, investment, and economic growth. [6] Many economists continue to rely on multiplier-based models, though most acknowledge that fiscal stimulus is far less effective than the original multiplier theory suggested.

The fiscal multiplier commonly associated with Keynesian theory is one of two broad multipliers in economics. The other is the money multiplier, which describes the money-creation process arising from fractional-reserve banking. [7] The money multiplier is less controversial than its Keynesian fiscal counterpart.

**Note:** According to the multiplier effect, every dollar spent in fiscal stimulus ultimately creates more than one dollar of growth. This appeared to offer government economists a justification for politically popular spending programs nationwide.

## Keynesian Economics and Monetary Policy

Keynesian economics focuses on demand-side solutions during recessionary periods. Government intervention in economic processes is a key element of the Keynesian toolkit against unemployment, underemployment, and low demand. This emphasis on direct government involvement often puts Keynesian theorists at odds with those who advocate limited government participation in markets.

Keynesians argue that wages and employment respond sluggishly to market conditions and require government intervention to stay on track. Furthermore, they contend that prices do not adjust quickly, changing only gradually in response to monetary policy interventions -- giving rise to the branch of Keynesian economics known as monetarism. [8]

If prices are slow to change, it becomes possible to use the money supply as a policy tool and to manipulate interest rates to encourage borrowing and lending. Lowering interest rates is one way governments can meaningfully intervene in the economic system, encouraging consumption and investment spending. [8]

Short-term demand increases triggered by rate cuts can reinvigorate the economic system and restore employment and demand for services. New economic activity then feeds continued growth and employment. [2]

Keynesian theorists argue that without intervention, this cycle would be disrupted, market growth would become more unstable, and the economy would be prone to excessive fluctuation. Keeping interest rates low is an attempt to stimulate the economic cycle by encouraging businesses and individuals to borrow more. However, lowering rates does not always directly lead to economic improvement.

Monetarist economists focus on managing the money supply and lowering interest rates as a solution to economic distress, but they generally try to avoid the zero lower bound problem. As interest rates approach zero, stimulating the economy through further rate cuts becomes less effective, reducing the incentive to invest rather than hold cash or near-cash equivalents such as short-term Treasuries. [9]

When interest rate manipulation fails to stimulate investment, it may no longer be sufficient to generate new economic activity, and recovery efforts can stall entirely. This is known as a liquidity trap.

When rate cuts fail to produce results, Keynesian economists argue that other strategies must be employed -- primarily fiscal policy. Other interventionist measures include direct control of the labor supply, changing tax rates to indirectly increase or decrease the money supply, altering monetary policy, or controlling the supply of goods and services until employment and demand recover. [2]

**Important:** Keynesian theorists believe that economies do not stabilize quickly on their own and require active intervention to boost short-term demand. [8]

## Keynesian Economics and the 2007-08 Financial Crisis

In response to the Great Recession and the 2007-2008 financial crisis, Congress and the executive branch adopted several measures drawing on Keynesian economic theory. The federal government bailed out heavily indebted firms across multiple industries, including banks, insurance companies, and automakers.

It also placed Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac -- two major market makers and guarantors of mortgages and housing loans -- into conservatorship.

In 2009, President Obama signed the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act, an $831 billion government stimulus package designed to save existing jobs and create new ones. It included tax cuts, credits, and unemployment benefits for families, along with designated spending for healthcare, infrastructure, and education.

These stimulus measures and federal interventions helped the U.S. economy recover and prevented the Great Recession from becoming a full-scale depression.

## Who Was John Maynard Keynes?

John Maynard Keynes (1883-1946) was a British economist best known as the founder of Keynesian economics and the father of modern macroeconomics. Keynes studied at King's College, Cambridge -- one of Britain's most elite institutions -- and earned a bachelor's degree in mathematics in 1905. [3] He excelled in mathematics but had almost no formal training in economics.

## How Does Keynesian Economics Differ from Classical Economics?

According to Keynes, classical economics held that fluctuations in employment and output created profit opportunities that individuals and entrepreneurs would naturally pursue, eventually correcting imbalances. [4]

In contrast, Keynes argued that during recessions, business pessimism and certain features of market economies compound weakness, driving aggregate demand further downward. Keynesian economics holds that during such difficult times, governments should engage in deficit spending to compensate for declining investment and stimulate consumer spending, stabilizing aggregate demand. [2]

## What Is Monetarism?

Monetarism is a macroeconomic theory holding that governments can promote economic stability by targeting the growth rate of the money supply. Closely associated with economist Milton Friedman, monetarism is a branch of Keynesian economics that emphasizes the use of monetary policy over fiscal policy to manage aggregate demand -- in contrast to most Keynesian economists. [8]

## The Bottom Line

John Maynard Keynes and Keynesian economics were revolutionary in the 1930s and largely shaped mid-20th century postwar economic policy. His theories came under attack in the 1970s, made a comeback in the 2000s, and remain debated to this day.

Keynesian economics recognizes a role for government in stimulating aggregate demand. Federal spending and tax cuts put more money in people's pockets, which can stimulate demand and investment.

Unlike free-market economists, Keynesians welcome limited government intervention and stimulus during recessionary periods.
