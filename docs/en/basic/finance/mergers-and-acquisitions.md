# What Are Mergers and Acquisitions (M&A)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) refer to the consolidation of companies or their major business assets through financial transactions between two or more entities. A company may:

- Fully acquire and absorb another company
- Merge with it to create a new entity
- Acquire some or all of its key assets
- Make a tender offer for its shares
- Execute a hostile takeover

All of these fall under the umbrella of M&A activity. The term is also used to describe the departments within financial institutions that facilitate or manage such transactions.

### Key Takeaways

- Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) describe the consolidation or combination of companies or their assets.
- In an acquisition, one company fully purchases another.
- A merger occurs when two companies combine to form a new legal entity operating under a single corporate name.
- M&A requires the valuation of a company or its assets to determine the appropriate purchase price.
- M&A can be financed through a combination of debt, cash, and stock.

## Understanding the Difference Between Mergers and Acquisitions

The terms "merger" and "acquisition" are often used interchangeably, but they carry different meanings.

When one company takes over another and establishes itself as the new owner, the transaction is an acquisition. Hostile takeovers -- where the target company does not wish to be acquired -- are always classified as acquisitions. However, acquisitions can also be conducted on friendly terms.

A merger occurs when two companies join forces to move forward as a single new entity, rather than remaining separately owned and operated. This is generally described as a "merger of equals" when the two companies are of comparable size.

For example, when Daimler-Benz merged with Chrysler, both legacy companies ceased to exist and a new entity -- DaimlerChrysler -- was formed. The shares of both companies were surrendered, and new company shares were issued. The company was rebranded as Mercedes-Benz Group AG (MBG) in February 2022.

**Note:** A purchase deal may also be called a merger when both CEOs agree that combining is in the best interest of both companies.

**Important:** M&A transactions generate substantial profits for the investment banking industry, but not all merger or acquisition deals close successfully.

## Types of Mergers and Acquisitions

The following are common transaction types within the M&A framework:

**Mergers:** Both companies' boards approve the combination and seek shareholder approval. The goal is to enhance both brands, combine strengths in a new entity, and create greater market share. For example, in 2024, HBC announced it would acquire Neiman Marcus Group and merge it with its Saks Fifth Avenue brand. The combined entity, Saks Global, aims to compete more effectively with online retail giants.

**Acquisitions:** The acquiring company obtains a controlling interest in the target without changing its name or organizational structure. Amazon's 2017 acquisition of Whole Foods is an example -- Amazon expanded into grocery delivery and the health-conscious consumer segment while Whole Foods gained access to Amazon's vast customer base.

**Consolidations:** Two or more companies merge to increase market share and eliminate competition. Facebook (now Meta) consolidated its position in social media by acquiring competitors such as Instagram for $1 billion in 2012. Instagram continued operating independently under Meta, while other acquisitions (like Beluga, which became Facebook Messenger) were fully integrated.

**Tender offers:** A company offers to buy the outstanding shares of another at a specific price rather than the market price, communicating directly with shareholders and bypassing management and the board. Johnson & Johnson's 2008 tender offer to acquire Omrix Biopharmaceuticals for $438 million is an example.

**Asset acquisitions:** One company directly acquires the assets of another, subject to shareholder approval. Asset acquisitions are particularly common in bankruptcy proceedings, where multiple bidders compete for the bankrupt company's assets.

**Management buyouts (MBOs):** Company executives purchase a controlling stake to take the company private. These transactions are typically financed with disproportionate levels of debt and require majority shareholder approval. Elon Musk's 2022 acquisition of Twitter for $44 billion -- which included $25.5 billion in margin loans and debt financing -- is a notable recent example.

## How Mergers Are Structured

Mergers can be structured in several ways based on the relationship between the two companies:

- **Horizontal merger:** Two direct competitors sharing the same product lines and markets.
- **Vertical merger:** A customer-company or supplier-company relationship (e.g., an ice cream maker and a cone supplier).
- **Congeneric merger:** Two businesses serving the same consumer base in different ways (e.g., a TV manufacturer and a cable company).
- **Market-extension merger:** Two companies selling the same products in different markets.
- **Product-extension merger:** Two companies selling different but related products in the same market.
- **Conglomerate merger:** Two companies with no common business areas.

Mergers can also be distinguished by two financing methods:

**Purchase mergers:** One company acquires another through cash payment or debt issuance. The sale is taxable, which benefits the acquirer through tax advantages. Acquired assets can be written up to actual purchase price, and the difference between book value and purchase price can be depreciated annually, reducing the acquiring company's tax liability.

**Consolidation mergers:** An entirely new company is formed, and both companies are acquired and combined under the new entity. Tax treatment is the same as in a purchase merger.

## Horizontal vs. Vertical Acquisitions

**Horizontal integration** involves acquiring a related business operating at the same level of the value chain -- for example, Marriott International's acquisition of Starwood Hotels & Resorts.

**Vertical integration** involves acquiring a business at a different stage of the production process. For example, Apple acquired AuthenTec, which made the Touch ID fingerprint sensor technology used in iPhones.

## How Acquisitions Are Financed

A company can acquire another through cash, stock, debt assumption, or a combination of all three. In some cases, the investment bank facilitating the sale may also provide financing to the acquirer -- known as staple financing.

In smaller deals, one company may acquire all of another's assets outright. In a reverse merger, a private company with strong prospects acquires a listed shell company and becomes publicly traded.

## How Mergers and Acquisitions Are Valued

Both sides of an M&A deal typically disagree on the target company's valuation. The seller aims for the highest possible price; the buyer seeks the lowest. Several objective valuation methods are available:

- **Price-to-earnings ratio (P/E):** The acquirer makes an offer as a multiple of the target's earnings. Examining P/E ratios across the same industry provides guidance.
- **Enterprise value-to-sales (EV/Sales):** The acquirer offers a multiple of the target's revenue, benchmarked against industry peers.
- **Discounted cash flow (DCF):** A key valuation tool that estimates present value based on projected future free cash flows, discounted at the company's weighted average cost of cost of capital (WACC).
- **Replacement cost:** In rare cases, an acquisition is valued at the cost of recreating the target company from scratch -- the sum of all equipment and staffing costs. This method is less applicable in service industries where key assets are people and intellectual property.

## Impact on Shareholders

In the days leading up to a merger or acquisition, the acquiring company's stock typically experiences a temporary decline, while the target company's stock usually rises. This reflects the premium the acquirer must pay above the target's pre-deal share price.

After the deal closes, the combined stock price generally exceeds each company's pre-acquisition value. Shareholders of the merged entity typically experience solid long-term performance and dividends, absent adverse economic conditions.

However, shareholders of both companies may experience dilution of voting power, particularly in stock-for-stock mergers where the new company exchanges its shares for the target company's shares at an agreed conversion ratio.

## What Is the Difference Between a Merger and an Acquisition?

"Acquisition" generally describes a transaction in which one company absorbs another. "Merger" describes a transaction in which the acquiring and target companies combine to form an entirely new entity. Because each combination has unique characteristics and motivations, the terms often overlap in practice.

## Why Do Companies Acquire Other Companies?

Competition and growth are two primary drivers of capitalism. When facing competition, a company must simultaneously cut costs and innovate. One solution is to acquire competitors, eliminating them as threats. Companies also pursue acquisitions to gain access to new product lines, intellectual property, human capital, and customer bases. Through consolidation, overall operational efficiency improves and costs decline as each company leverages the other's strengths.

## What Is a Hostile Takeover?

Friendly acquisitions are most common and occur when the target company agrees to be acquired, with board and shareholder approval. Hostile takeovers occur when the target does not consent. The acquiring company must actively purchase a majority of shares to gain control and force the acquisition.

## Summary

Mergers and acquisitions (M&A) encompass the many ways companies and their assets are purchased, consolidated, or combined. An acquisition is typically a full purchase of one company by another; in a merger, two companies combine to form a new entity.

M&A can be financed through stock, debt, cash, or a combination thereof. Transactions may be friendly or hostile -- hostile takeovers proceed without the target company's consent.
