# What Is an Interest Rate?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

An interest rate is the charge a lender imposes on a borrower, typically expressed as a percentage of the principal (the loan amount). The interest rate on a loan is generally quoted on an annual basis, known as the annual percentage rate (APR).

Interest rates also apply to savings accounts and certificates of deposit (CDs). In this context, the bank or credit union pays the account holder a percentage return on deposited funds. The annual percentage yield (APY) refers to the interest earned on these deposit accounts.

### Key Takeaways

- An interest rate is the charge levied by a lender on a borrower, over and above the principal, for the use of an asset.
- Interest rates also apply to the amount earned by a bank or credit union on deposit accounts.
- Most mortgages use simple interest, but some loans use compound interest, which accrues on both the principal and previously accumulated interest.
- Borrowers deemed low risk by lenders receive lower interest rates; those deemed high risk are charged higher rates.
- APY is the rate earned on a savings account or CD, calculated using compound interest.

## Understanding Interest Rates

Interest is essentially the cost of using an asset. Borrowed assets can include cash, consumer goods, vehicles, and real property. An interest rate can therefore be thought of as the "cost of money" -- the higher the rate, the more expensive it is to borrow the same amount.

Interest rates apply to most lending and borrowing transactions. Individuals borrow to purchase homes, fund projects, start or grow businesses, or pay for education. Businesses use loans to finance capital projects and expand operations by acquiring fixed assets such as land, buildings, and machinery. Borrowed money is typically repaid in a lump sum by an agreed date or through periodic installments.

For loans, the interest rate is applied to the principal. It represents both the borrower's cost of debt and the lender's rate of return. The total amount repaid generally exceeds the amount borrowed because the lender must be compensated for the loss of use of the funds during the loan period. The difference between the total repayment and the original loan amount is the interest charged.

Borrowers perceived as low risk are typically charged lower rates; those perceived as high risk face higher rates, resulting in more expensive loans.

**Important:** Risk is typically assessed by reviewing the borrower's credit score. Maintaining an excellent credit score is essential for securing the most favorable loan terms.

## Simple Interest

Suppose you borrow $300,000 from a bank at a 4% simple interest rate. You would need to repay $300,000 + (4% x $300,000) = $300,000 + $12,000 = $312,000.

If the borrower needs the loan for only one year, the interest payment at year-end is $12,000. For a 30-year mortgage:

Simple interest = $300,000 x 4% x 30 = $360,000

A 4% annual rate translates to $12,000 per year in interest. Over 30 years, the borrower pays $12,000 x 30 = $360,000 in interest -- illustrating how banks profit from loans and mortgages.

## Compound Interest

Some lenders prefer compound interest, which means the borrower pays more over time. Compound interest -- also known as "interest on interest" -- is applied to both the principal and previously accumulated interest. The bank assumes that at the end of the first year the borrower owes the principal plus that year's interest; at the end of the second year, the borrower owes the principal, plus the first year's interest, plus compound interest on the first year's total.

Interest owed under compounding is higher than under simple interest because interest is charged monthly on the principal, including accrued interest from prior months. For shorter time frames, the two methods yield similar results. As the lending period lengthens, the difference between the two approaches grows substantially.

Using the example above, after 30 years the total interest on a $300,000 loan at 4% approaches $673,019 under compound interest.

Another example: a $10,000 loan at 5% for three years generates $1,576.25 in compound interest.

## Compound Interest and Savings Accounts

When depositing money into a savings account, compound interest works in the depositor's favor. The interest earned on the account compounds, compensating the account holder for allowing the bank to use the deposited funds.

For example, if you deposit $500,000 into a high-yield savings account, the bank may use $300,000 of those funds for mortgage lending. To compensate you, the bank pays 5% annual interest. The bank charges borrowers 8% and pays depositors 5%, earning a net spread of 3%. In effect, savers lend money to the bank, which then lends it to borrowers in exchange for interest.

## The Borrower's Cost of Debt

While interest rates represent income for lenders, they constitute a cost of debt for borrowers. Companies weigh borrowing costs against equity costs (such as dividend payments) to determine which source of financing is least expensive. Since most firms fund their capital needs through either debt or equity issuance, the goal is to achieve an optimal capital structure.

## APR vs. APY

Consumer loan interest rates are typically quoted as the annual percentage rate (APR) -- the rate of return lenders require for the use of their money. For example, credit card rates are expressed as APR. In the examples above, 4% is the mortgage APR. APR does not account for intra-year compounding.

The annual percentage yield (APY) is the rate earned on a savings account or CD at a bank or credit union and does account for compounding.

## How Are Interest Rates Determined?

The interest rates banks charge are influenced by several factors, including prevailing economic conditions. A country's central bank -- such as the Federal Reserve in the United States -- sets benchmark rates that serve as a reference for the APR ranges offered by commercial banks. When the central bank sets higher rates, the cost of debt rises, discouraging borrowing and dampening consumer demand. Interest rates tend to rise alongside inflation.

To combat inflation, banks may raise reserve requirements, producing a tighter money supply, or credit demand may increase. In a high-rate environment, people tend to save more because they earn higher returns. The stock market may suffer as investors prefer higher-yielding savings over equities with lower potential returns. Businesses also face higher costs for capital funding, leading to economic contraction.

In a low-rate environment, the economy tends to be stimulated because borrowers can access credit cheaply. Since savings rates are low, businesses and individuals are more likely to spend and invest in riskier assets such as equities. This spending stimulates the economy and injects capital into markets, promoting expansion.

Although governments prefer lower rates, persistently low rates eventually create market imbalances -- demand exceeds supply, fueling inflation. When inflation rises, interest rates increase, a dynamic related to Walras's Law.

## Interest Rates and Discrimination

Despite laws such as the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA) that prohibit discriminatory lending practices, systemic racial disparities in lending persist in the United States.

Research shows that white applicants are more likely to be approved for mortgages. According to data reported under the Home Mortgage Disclosure Act, in 2022, Black, Hispanic white, and Asian applicants were denied conventional mortgages at rates of 16.4%, 11.1%, and 9.2%, respectively. The denial rate for white applicants was far lower at 5.8%. Additional data show that race can influence interest rates. Analysis by Realtor.com of 2018-2019 mortgage data found that homebuyers in predominantly Black neighborhoods received mortgage rates 13 basis points higher than those in predominantly white neighborhoods.

Research from Harvard University and the Urban Institute has also confirmed mortgage rate discrimination. In 2022, these institutions reported that the average Black homeowner paid a rate 33 basis points higher than the average white homeowner, amounting to roughly $250 more in annual interest.

Not all research reaches the same conclusion. A study by Federal Reserve economists found no evidence that any race received preferential treatment, suggesting that disparities reported elsewhere may reflect Black and Hispanic borrowers choosing slightly higher rates in exchange for lower upfront costs.

The Federal Reserve acknowledges that discrimination has improved, attributing progress in part to automated underwriting and stricter enforcement of the Fair Housing Act and the Equal Credit Opportunity Act.

## Why Are 30-Year Loan Rates Higher Than 15-Year Rates?

Interest rates reflect default risk and opportunity cost. Longer-term loans and debt are inherently riskier because the borrower has more time to default. The opportunity cost is also greater over longer periods because the principal is locked up and unavailable for other uses.

## How Does the Fed Use Interest Rates in the Economy?

The Federal Reserve, like other central banks worldwide, uses interest rates as a monetary policy tool. By raising borrowing costs for commercial banks, the central bank influences numerous other rates -- including those on personal loans, business loans, and mortgages. Higher rates generally make borrowing more expensive, dampening demand and cooling an overheated economy. Conversely, lowering rates makes borrowing more affordable, stimulating consumption and investment.

## Why Do Bond Prices React Inversely to Interest Rate Changes?

A bond is a debt instrument that typically pays a fixed coupon rate over its life. If prevailing rates are 5%, a $1,000 par bond with a 5% coupon pays $50 annually. If rates rise to 10%, new bonds pay $100 per $1,000 of face value. The existing bond paying only $50 must sell at a substantial discount to attract buyers. Conversely, if rates drop to 1%, new bonds pay only $10 per $1,000 par. The bond paying $50 becomes highly sought after, and its price is bid upward.

## Summary

An interest rate is the borrower's cost of debt and the lender's rate of return. When you take out a loan, you pay the lending entity an additional charge as compensation. Similarly, when you deposit funds in a savings account, the financial institution may reward you because it can use those funds to make additional loans.

These charges or payments are called interest and are calculated at a predetermined rate.
