# What Is a Housing Bubble?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A housing bubble is a run-up in home prices driven by demand, speculation, and excessive spending. Housing bubbles typically begin with an increase in demand at a time when supply is limited. Speculators pour money into the market, further inflating demand. When demand eventually weakens or stagnates while supply increases, prices fall sharply and the bubble bursts.

### Key Takeaways

- A housing bubble is a sustained but temporary condition characterized by inflated valuations and rampant speculation in real estate markets.
- The United States experienced a major housing bubble in the 2000s, fueled by capital inflows into real estate and loose lending standards.
- Once home values collapse and mortgages exceed equity, homeowners may be forced into foreclosure.

## What Causes a Housing Bubble?

Housing bubbles can be driven by a number of atypical factors, including artificially stimulated demand, speculation, unusually high levels of investment, excess liquidity, deregulated real estate financing markets, and extreme forms of mortgage-backed derivative products.

These factors can push housing prices to unsustainable levels, causing demand to outpace supply. Because of the substantial transaction and holding costs associated with homeownership, real estate markets are not as susceptible to bubbles as other financial markets.

However, a rapid expansion of credit supply -- resulting in low interest rates and relaxed lending standards -- can draw borrowers into the market. Rising interest rates and tightening credit standards reduce demand and can trigger the collapse of a housing bubble.

## Effects of a Housing Bubble

Housing bubbles can affect entire communities and the broader economy. They may force homeowners to find ways to repay their mortgages through various relief programs, or they may need to tap retirement accounts just to remain in their homes. Housing bubbles can dramatically erode home equity, and homeowners often discover that their mortgage balances exceed their homes' market values.

Once home values plummet and mortgages exceed equity, homeowners may face foreclosure. Foreclosure is the process by which a lender attempts to recover the outstanding loan balance by seizing the collateral property and selling it. It is typically triggered when a borrower misses monthly payments or fails to meet other terms of the mortgage agreement.

"Negative equity" describes a situation in which a homeowner owes more on the mortgage than the home is worth. These mortgages are often referred to as "underwater" or "upside-down," eroding personal net worth and preventing homeowners from relocating until market conditions improve. [1]

## Example of a Housing Bubble

The U.S. housing bubble unfolded in the aftermath of the 2007-2008 financial crisis. Following the collapse of the dot-com bubble in the late 1990s, investors redirected capital from technology stocks into real estate. The Federal Reserve cut interest rates to combat the mild recession that followed the tech bust and to alleviate uncertainty after the September 11, 2001 attacks. [2]

Government policies encouraged homeownership, and financial market innovation increased the liquidity of real estate-related assets. As interest rates plummeted, home prices soared. An estimated 20% of mortgages originated in 2005 and 2006 went to subprime borrowers -- individuals who would not have qualified under normal lending criteria. [3] More than 75% of these subprime loans were adjustable-rate mortgages with low initial rates that were scheduled to reset after two to three years. [4]

Government encouragement of broad homeownership prompted banks to lower rates and lending requirements. [5] This spurred a home-buying frenzy that drove the median sale price of homes up 55% from 2000 to 2007. [6] As signs of an economic slowdown emerged, adjustable-rate mortgages began resetting at higher rates in 2007. From 2007 to 2009, home prices declined 19%, triggering a massive sell-off in mortgage-backed securities. [6]

**Important:** In 2023, there were 357,062 foreclosure filings in the United States. During the housing bubble years of 2009 and 2010, annual foreclosure totals exceeded 2.8 million. [7]

## What Is a Speculator in Real Estate?

Speculators purchase properties because they believe certain market or economic factors will drive an increase in value, sometimes over a short time horizon. The goal is to "flip" the property and sell it promptly once prices rise, capturing a profit. Unlike speculators, investors typically anticipate longer-term returns driven by factors beyond or in addition to short-term market fluctuations. [8]

## What Is an Adjustable-Rate Mortgage?

An adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) has an interest rate that fluctuates over time, affecting the borrower's monthly payment. Most ARMs include rate caps and other controls to prevent frequent, drastic swings. The advantage of an ARM is that the initial interest rate is typically lower than that of a fixed-rate mortgage. [9]

## What Is the Foreclosure Process?

Foreclosure rules vary by state, but the process is generally initiated when a homeowner stops making mortgage payments. The mortgage contract grants the lender a security interest in the property, which gives the lender the legal right to seize the home after providing proper notice and allowing the homeowner an opportunity to cure the default. The lender then sells the property to recover part -- or all -- of the outstanding loan balance. [10]

## Conclusion

Housing bubbles can severely impair home values and homeowner equity. As prices rise, investors may flood into the market, and buyers may take on risky loans. When the bubble bursts and prices collapse, some borrowers face financial distress or foreclosure.
