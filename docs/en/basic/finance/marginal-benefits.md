# What Are Marginal Benefits?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A marginal benefit is the maximum amount a consumer is willing to pay for an additional good or service. It also represents the additional satisfaction or utility a consumer receives when the additional good or service is purchased. The marginal benefit for a consumer tends to decrease as consumption of the good or service increases.

In the business world, the marginal benefit for producers is often referred to as marginal revenue.

### Key Takeaways

- A marginal benefit represents the maximum cost a consumer will pay for an additional good or service.
- A marginal benefit is also the incremental satisfaction a consumer receives from purchasing an additional good or service.
- The marginal benefit generally decreases as consumption increases.
- The marginal benefit of some necessities, such as medication, does not decrease over time.
- Companies can use their research on marginal benefits to find the optimal price point for any transaction.

## Understanding Marginal Benefits

Similar to marginal utility, a marginal benefit applies to any additional unit purchased by the consumer after the first unit has been acquired. The term "utility" describes the level of satisfaction a consumer assigns to the unit being consumed.

Marginal benefit can be expressed as the dollar amount the consumer is willing to spend on an additional unit, or it can be expressed in hypothetical units such as "utils." This assumes that the consumer considers the price paid to equal the minimum intrinsic value of the item.

For example, if a person purchases a burger for $10, it can be assumed that the consumer derives at least $10 worth of perceived value from that item. If they would buy a second burger for $5 but would not pay $6, the marginal benefit of the second burger must fall between $5 and $6.

Not all products experience a change in perceived value. For example, prescription medication can retain its utility over the long term as long as it continues to perform as needed. In addition, the marginal benefits of certain staple goods such as bread or milk also remain relatively consistent over time.

## The Law of Diminishing Marginal Benefits

As more units are consumed, the consumer tends to receive less utility or satisfaction from consumption.

To illustrate this, consider the example above. Assume a consumer wants to purchase an additional burger. If they are willing to pay $10 for this additional burger, the marginal benefit of consuming that burger is equal to the initial $10 purchase.

However, if the consumer decides they are only willing to pay $9 for the second burger, the marginal benefit is $9. The more burgers the consumer has, the less they want to pay for the next one. This is because the benefit decreases as the quantity consumed increases.

## Formula for Marginal Benefit

The marginal benefit can be determined from the slope of the demand curve or using the following formula:

$$ \begin{aligned}\text{Marginal Benefit}=\frac{\text{Total Additional Benefit}}{\text{Total Additional Units Consumed}}\end{aligned} $$

## Types of Marginal Benefits

There are three possible value ranges for marginal benefits.

Most goods have a positive marginal benefit, meaning the consumer derives a net benefit from consuming each additional unit. Although the marginal benefit may decline over time, the consumer still experiences increased satisfaction with additional consumption.

A negative marginal benefit means the consumer feels worse after consuming an additional unit. This can occur with activities like excessive drinking or eating unhealthy snacks, where additional consumption leads to declining satisfaction.

A good with zero marginal benefit is one where the consumer experiences no change in satisfaction from additional consumption. This can occur when a product has qualities that both increase and decrease satisfaction simultaneously, resulting in a net benefit of zero.

## Marginal Benefits and Unit Pricing

Even though a consumer may be willing to pay $10 for a burger, $10 is not necessarily the market price. Price is determined by market forces. When consumers perceive an item's value to be lower than the market price, they may ultimately choose not to make the transaction.

## Marginal Benefits for Businesses

Marginal benefits have important applications for businesses, especially in the areas of marketing and research. Companies need to consider that a customer may compare the marginal cost of an additional purchase with the marginal benefit. Marginal cost is the additional cost incurred when producing a subsequent unit.

Returning to the example above, if a customer buys the first burger for $10 and a second for $9, the customer may place a marginal benefit of $9 on the second burger and decide to buy it at that marginal cost. But if the customer is full after only one burger, the $9 marginal cost will outweigh the benefit, and they may decide against buying it.

Companies can use their research on marginal benefits to identify the optimal price point for any transaction. Companies can also use this research to understand the additional costs of selling a second item relative to the first.

## Marginal Benefits vs. Marginal Costs

The concept of marginal benefits is similar to marginal costs, which represent the incremental cost to a producer of creating an additional unit of a good. Like marginal benefits, marginal costs typically decline as the total number of units increases.

For example, imagine a hypothetical factory that produces paper cups. The production process requires $1 million in equipment capable of producing up to 100,000 cups per day. Additionally, the production process requires $1 worth of paper and glue materials per cup.

If the factory is currently producing fewer than 100,000 cups, the marginal cost of each additional cup is only $1. Fixed costs (such as equipment and facilities) are not included. However, if the factory is already running at full capacity, the marginal cost of producing additional cups could be far more than $1, reflecting the cost of additional machinery or changes to the production process.

## How Do You Calculate Marginal Benefits?

Marginal benefits can be calculated from the slope of the demand curve. For example, to find the marginal benefit of the nth unit of a product, take the slope of the demand curve at the point where current consumption equals n.

## What Are Marginal Benefits to Producers?

For manufacturers and other suppliers, the marginal benefit of a good represents the incremental profit they can earn by selling an additional unit. This does not necessarily equal the expected per-unit profit. For example, if a company decides to sell an additional 1,000 bottles of soda but expects half of them to go unsold, the marginal benefit would be half the per-unit profit.

## What Is the Principle of Diminishing Marginal Benefits?

The principle of diminishing marginal benefits, also known as the law of diminishing utility, holds that the benefit of consuming a good decreases as consumption increases. While each additional unit represents a net gain for the consumer, that gain is less than the satisfaction obtained from previous consumption.

## Summary

Marginal benefit represents one of the most fundamental concepts in microeconomics. It is the value or satisfaction an economic agent derives from consuming an additional unit of a particular good. Marginal benefit also determines how much a consumer is willing to pay to obtain more of a specific good.
