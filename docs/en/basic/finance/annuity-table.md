# What Is an Annuity Table?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

An annuity table is a tool used to determine the present value of an annuity or other series of structured payments. Commonly used by accountants, actuaries, and other insurance professionals, it considers both the amount invested in the annuity and the length of time it has been in place to determine the amount owed to the annuity purchaser or annuitant.

The present value of any future annuity amount can also be calculated using a financial calculator or purpose-built software.

### Key Takeaways

- An annuity table is a tool for determining the present value of an annuity.
- An annuity table calculates the present value of an annuity by applying a discount rate to future payments.
- Annuity tables use the discount rate and number of payment periods to produce an appropriate factor.
- Using the annuity table, you multiply the periodic payment amount by the given factor.

## How an Annuity Table Works

An annuity table provides a factor, based on time and a discount rate (interest rate), that when multiplied by the annuity payment yields its present value. For instance, an annuity table can be used to calculate the present value of an annuity that pays $10,000 per year for 15 years at an assumed interest rate of 3%.

**Important:** According to the time value of money, a lump sum received today is worth more than the same amount received in the future.

Having $10,000 today is more valuable than receiving $1,000 per year for 10 years, because the lump sum can be invested to earn interest. At the end of the 10-year period, the $10,000 lump sum would be worth more than the cumulative annual payments, even if those payments were invested at the same interest rate.

## Annuity Tables and the Present Value of an Annuity

The present value formula for an ordinary annuity (as distinct from an annuity due) is as follows:

$$ \begin{aligned}&\text{P} =\text{PMT}\times\frac{ 1 - (1 + r) ^ {-n}}{r}\\&\textbf{where:}\\&\text{P} = \text{Present value of the annuity stream}\\&\text{PMT} =\text{Dollar amount of each annuity payment}\\&r = \text{Interest rate (also known as the discount rate)}\\&n = \text{Number of periods}\end{aligned} $$

Suppose an individual has the opportunity to receive an annuity that pays $50,000 per year for 25 years at a discount rate of 6%, or alternatively to take a lump-sum payment of $650,000. The individual needs to determine the more advantageous option. Using the formula above, the present value of the annuity is:

$$ \begin{aligned}&\text{PVA} = \$50,000 \times \frac{1 - (1 + 0.06) ^ {-25}}{0.06} = \$639,168\\&\textbf{where:}\\&\text{PVA}=\text{Present value of the annuity}\end{aligned} $$

Based on this calculation, the annuity is worth $10,832 less than the lump sum on a time-adjusted basis, so the individual should choose the lump-sum payment.

Note that this formula applies to an ordinary annuity, where payments are made at the end of each period. In the example above, each $50,000 payment would occur at year-end for 25 years. For an annuity due -- where payments are made at the beginning of each period -- the formula is adjusted by multiplying by $(1 + r)$:

$$ \begin{aligned}&\text{P} = \text{PMT} \times\left(\frac{1 - (1 + r) ^ {-n}}{r}\right) \times (1 + r)\end{aligned} $$

If the above example were an annuity due, its value would be:

$$ \begin{aligned}&\text{P}= \$50,000\\&\quad \times\left( \frac{1 - (1 + 0.06) ^ {-25}}{0.06}\right)\times (1 + 0.06) = \$677,518\end{aligned} $$

In this case, the individual should choose the annuity due, as it is worth $27,518 more than the lump-sum payment.

Rather than computing these values from the formula, you can use an annuity table. The table simplifies the calculation by automatically providing the factor corresponding to the latter portion of the formula. For an ordinary annuity, the table supplies a number (called the factor) that is precomputed for the expression $(1 - (1 + r)^{-n}) / r$.

The factor is determined by the interest rate (r) and the number of periods (n). In the annuity table, periods are typically listed in the left column and interest rates along the top row. Locate the correct interest rate and period number to find the factor at their intersection. Multiply that factor by the dollar amount of the annuity payment to obtain the present value of the ordinary annuity.

## What Is an Annuity?

An annuity is an insurance contract that provides an income stream, typically during retirement. Annuities may be fixed, variable, or indexed. They have two phases: first, the accumulation (savings) phase, and then the payout (income) phase. Payments may begin immediately or on a deferred basis.

## What Is the Difference Between an Ordinary Annuity and an Annuity Due?

An ordinary annuity generates payments at the end of each period, while an annuity due requires or generates payments at the beginning of each period.

## Can Lottery Winners Use an Annuity Table?

Lottery winners can use an annuity table to determine whether taking a lump-sum payout today or receiving structured payments over many years is more economically advantageous. However, lottery winnings are a relatively rare form of annuity. More commonly, annuities are investment products designed to provide individuals with a steady stream of retirement income.

## Summary

Annuity tables are primarily used by accounting, insurance, and other financial professionals to determine the present value of an annuity. By considering both the amount invested and the time horizon, the table facilitates decisions about how much should be paid to or by an annuity purchaser or annuitant.
