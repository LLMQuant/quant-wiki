# What Is a Balance Sheet?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A balance sheet is a financial statement that reports a company's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity at a specific point in time. The balance sheet provides the foundation for investors to calculate rates of return and evaluate a company's capital structure.

In short, the balance sheet is a financial statement that provides a snapshot of what a company owns and owes, as well as the amount invested by shareholders. Balance sheets can be used in conjunction with other important financial statements for fundamental analysis or for calculating financial ratios.

### Key Takeaways

- A balance sheet is a financial statement that reports a company's assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity.
- It is one of three core financial statements used to evaluate a business.
- It provides a snapshot of a company's financial position -- what it owns and what it owes -- as of the date of publication.
- The balance sheet follows an equation in which assets equal the sum of liabilities and shareholders' equity.
- Fundamental analysts use balance sheets to calculate financial ratios.

## How a Balance Sheet Works

The balance sheet provides an overview of a company's financial position at a single moment in time. It cannot, on its own, reveal trends over time. For this reason, the balance sheet should be compared with statements from prior periods.

Investors can assess a company's financial health using several ratios derived from the balance sheet, including the debt-to-equity ratio and the acid-test ratio. The income statement and cash flow statement also provide valuable context for evaluating a company's finances, along with any notes or addenda in an earnings report that may reference the balance sheet.

For example, if a company takes a five-year, $4,000 loan from a bank, its assets (specifically, the cash account) will increase by $4,000. Its liabilities (specifically, the long-term debt account) will also increase by $4,000, balancing the two sides. If the company receives $8,000 from investors, its assets will increase by that amount, as will its shareholders' equity. All revenues the company generates in excess of its expenses will flow into the shareholders' equity account. These revenues will appear on the asset side as cash, investments, inventory, or other assets.

**Important:** The balance sheet should also be compared with those of other companies in the same industry, as different industries have unique approaches to financing.

## Special Considerations

As noted above, the balance sheet contains information on assets, liabilities, and shareholders' equity. Assets should always equal the sum of liabilities and shareholders' equity. This means the balance sheet should always balance, hence its name. If it does not balance, there may be issues including inaccurate or misplaced data, inventory or exchange rate errors, or miscalculations.

Each category consists of several smaller accounts that break down the specifics of a company's finances. These accounts vary widely by industry, and the same terms can carry different meanings depending on the nature of the business. Companies may choose to use a format called a common-size balance sheet, which shows percentage and numerical values. This reporting format facilitates quick comparison of items.

Investors may encounter several common components.

## Components of a Balance Sheet

Accounts in this section are listed from top to bottom in order of their liquidity -- that is, the ease with which they can be converted into cash. They are divided into current assets, which can be converted to cash within one year, and non-current or long-term assets, which generally cannot.

The general order of current asset accounts is as follows:

- Cash and cash equivalents are the most liquid assets and may include Treasury bills and short-term certificates of deposit, as well as hard currency.
- Marketable securities are equity and debt securities for which there is a liquid market.
- Accounts receivable (AR) refers to money owed to the company by customers. This may include an allowance for doubtful accounts, since some customers may not pay what they owe.
- Inventory refers to any goods available for sale, valued at the lower of cost or market price.
- Prepaid expenses represent the value of expenses already paid, such as insurance, advertising contracts, or rent.

Long-term assets include:

- Long-term investments are securities that will not or cannot be liquidated in the coming year.
- Fixed assets include land, machinery, equipment, buildings, and other durable, typically capital-intensive assets.
- Intangible assets include non-physical (but still valuable) assets such as intellectual property and goodwill. These assets are generally only listed on the balance sheet if they are acquired rather than developed internally. Their value may therefore be vastly understated (by not including a globally recognized logo, for example) or overstated.

Liabilities are any amounts the company owes to outside parties, including bills owed to suppliers, interest owed to creditors, rent, utilities, and payroll. Current liabilities are due within one year and are listed in order of their due date. Long-term liabilities are due at any point after one year.

Current liability accounts may include:

- Current portion of long-term debt is the portion of long-term debt due within the next 12 months. For example, if a company has a 10-year warehouse loan, 1 year is a current liability and 9 years is a long-term liability.
- Interest payable is accrued interest owed, often arising as part of past-due obligations such as late property tax payments.
- Wages payable are salaries, wages, and benefits owed to employees, typically for the most recent pay period.
- Customer prepayments are money received from a customer before a service is rendered or a product is delivered. The company is obligated to either (a) provide the good or service or (b) refund the customer's money.
- Dividends payable are dividends that have been authorized but not yet disbursed.
- Earned and unearned premiums are similar to prepayments in which the company receives payment upfront but has not yet fulfilled its part of the agreement and must refund unearned amounts if it fails to perform.
- Accounts payable is typically the most common current liability. Accounts payable are debt obligations on invoices processed as part of business operations, usually payable within 30 days of receipt.

Long-term liabilities may include:

- Long-term debt includes any interest and principal on bonds issued.
- Pension fund liabilities are the amounts a company is required to pay into its employees' retirement accounts.
- Deferred tax liabilities are taxes that have accrued but will not be paid for another year. Beyond timing, this figure reconciles differences between financial reporting requirements and the way taxes are assessed, such as depreciation calculations.

Some liabilities are considered off-balance-sheet, meaning they do not appear on the balance sheet.

Shareholders' equity is the money attributable to the owners or shareholders of a business, also known as net assets, as it equals total assets minus liabilities -- the company's debts to non-shareholders.

Retained earnings are the net earnings a company reinvests in the business or uses to pay off debt. The remaining amount is distributed to shareholders in the form of dividends.

Treasury stock is shares the company has repurchased. These may be resold in the future to raise cash or reserved to repel a hostile takeover.

Some companies issue preferred stock, which will be listed separately in this section. Preferred stock is assigned an arbitrary par value (as is common stock in some cases) that bears no relation to the market value of the shares. The common stock and preferred stock accounts are calculated by multiplying the par value by the number of shares issued.

Additional paid-in capital, or capital surplus, represents the amount shareholders have invested in excess of the common or preferred stock par value accounts, rather than market price. Shareholders' equity is not directly related to a company's market capitalization. The latter is based on the current price of the stock, while paid-in capital is the sum of equity purchased at any price.

**Note:** Par value is typically just a very small amount, such as $0.01.

## Importance of the Balance Sheet

Regardless of a company's size or the industry in which it operates, there are many benefits to reading, analyzing, and understanding its balance sheet.

First, the balance sheet helps determine risk. This financial statement lists everything a company owns and all of its debts. A company will be able to quickly assess whether it has borrowed too much, whether its assets are insufficiently liquid, or whether it has enough cash on hand to meet current demands.

The balance sheet is also used to secure capital. A company usually must provide a balance sheet to a lender when applying for a business loan. A company typically must also provide a balance sheet to private investors when seeking private equity financing. In both cases, the external party wants to assess the financial health of the company, its creditworthiness, and whether the company will be able to repay its short-term debts.

Managers can use financial ratios to measure a company's liquidity, profitability, solvency, and turnover (cadence), some of which require figures drawn from the balance sheet. When analyzed over time or compared against competitors, managers can better understand ways to improve a company's financial health.

Finally, balance sheets can help attract and retain talent. Employees generally prefer to know that their jobs are secure and that the company they work for is in good financial health. For public companies that are required to disclose their balance sheets, this requirement enables employees to review how much cash the company has on hand, whether the company is making sound decisions in managing its debt, and whether the company's financial health meets their expectations.

## Limitations of the Balance Sheet

Although the balance sheet is an invaluable piece of information for investors and analysts, it does have drawbacks. Because the balance sheet is static, many financial ratios draw on data from both the balance sheet and the more dynamic income statement and cash flow statement to paint a fuller picture of business operations. Therefore, the balance sheet alone may not provide a complete picture of a company's financial health.

A limitation of the balance sheet is its narrow time scope. This financial statement captures a company's financial position on a single day only. Looking at a single balance sheet can make it difficult to determine whether a company is performing well. For example, imagine a company reports $1 million in cash at month-end. Without context, a comparison point, knowledge of its prior cash balances, and an understanding of industry operating requirements, knowing how much cash the company has is of limited value.

Different accounting systems and methods of handling depreciation and inventory will also change the figures reported on the balance sheet. As such, managers have some ability to manipulate the numbers to appear more favorable. Pay attention to the balance sheet's footnotes to determine which systems are used in its accounting, and watch for potential red flags.

Finally, the balance sheet is subject to several areas of professional judgment that may materially affect the report. For example, accounts receivable must be continually assessed for impairment and adjusted to reflect potential bad debts. Without knowing which receivables a company is likely to actually collect, the company must make estimates and reflect its best guess as part of the balance sheet.

## Why Is the Balance Sheet Important?

The balance sheet is an essential tool used by executives, investors, analysts, and regulators to understand the current financial health of a business. It is generally used alongside the other two types of financial statements: the income statement and the cash flow statement.

Balance sheets allow users to get an at-a-glance view of a company's assets and liabilities. The balance sheet can help answer questions such as whether the company has a positive net worth, whether it has enough cash and short-term assets to cover its obligations, and whether the company is heavily indebted relative to its peers.

## What Does the Balance Sheet Contain?

The balance sheet contains information about a company's assets and liabilities. Depending on the company, this might include short-term assets such as cash and accounts receivable, or long-term assets such as property, plant, and equipment (PP&E). Similarly, liabilities may include short-term obligations such as accounts payable and wages payable, or long-term liabilities such as bank loans and other debt obligations.

## Who Prepares the Balance Sheet?

Depending on the company, different parties may be responsible for preparing the balance sheet. For small private businesses, the balance sheet might be prepared by the owner or a company bookkeeper. For mid-size private firms, balance sheets may be prepared internally and then reviewed by an external accountant.

Public companies, on the other hand, are required to obtain external audits by public accountants and must also ensure that their books are kept to a much higher standard. The balance sheets and other financial statements of these companies must be prepared in accordance with Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) and must be filed regularly with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). [2]

## What Is the Balance Sheet Used For?

The balance sheet explains a company's financial status at a specific point in time. Unlike an income statement, which reports financial information over a period, the balance sheet is used to assess the health of a company on a given date.

Bank statements are often used by parties external to a company to gauge its health. Banks, lenders, and other institutions may calculate financial ratios from balance sheet balances to assess how much risk a company carries, how liquid its assets are, and how likely the company is to remain solvent.

A company can use its balance sheet to make internal decisions, though the information provided is generally less useful than an income statement. A company may look at its balance sheet to gauge risk, ensure it has sufficient cash on hand, and evaluate how it might raise additional capital (through debt or equity).

## What Is the Balance Sheet Formula?

In accounting, the ending balance is the final balance obtained by totaling all debits and credits. The balance sheet, as a critical financial tool, calculates a company's assets, liabilities, and equity. Total assets is the sum of all short-term, long-term, and other assets. Total liabilities is the sum of all short-term, long-term, and other liabilities. Total equity is the sum of net income, retained earnings, owner contributions, and shares issued. The formula is: Total Assets = Total Liabilities + Total Equity.
