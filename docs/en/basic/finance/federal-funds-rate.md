# What Is the Federal Funds Rate?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The federal funds rate refers to the target interest rate range set by the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC). This target is the rate at which commercial banks borrow and lend their excess reserves to each other on an overnight basis. The FOMC, the policy-making body of the Federal Reserve System, meets eight times a year to set the target federal funds rate as part of its monetary policy aimed at promoting economic growth.

### Key Takeaways

- The federal funds rate is the target interest rate range set by the FOMC.
- It is the rate at which commercial banks borrow and lend excess reserves to each other overnight.
- The FOMC sets the target federal funds rate eight times a year based on prevailing economic conditions.
- The federal funds rate influences short-term interest rates on consumer loans and credit cards.
- Investors closely monitor the federal funds rate because of its impact on the stock market.

## Understanding the Federal Funds Rate

The federal funds rate is the rate banks charge other institutions for lending excess cash from their reserve balances on an overnight basis.

By law, banks must maintain reserves in a Federal Reserve Bank account equal to a certain percentage of their deposits. The amount a bank must hold at the Fed is known as the reserve requirement and is based on a percentage of the bank's total deposits.

Financial institutions are required to maintain interest-bearing accounts at Federal Reserve Banks to ensure they have sufficient funds to cover depositor withdrawals and other obligations. Any reserves above the required level may be lent to other banks that may be running short.

**Note:** In March 2020, the Federal Reserve reduced reserve requirements to 0%, effectively eliminating them. The Fed indicated that this step would free up liquidity in the banking system and that there were no near-term plans to reinstate the requirement.

The average of a bank's end-of-day account balances over a two-week reserve maintenance period determines whether it meets its reserve requirement. If a bank anticipates that its end-of-day balance will exceed the required level, it can lend the surplus to an institution that expects a shortfall. The interest rate the lending bank charges is the federal funds rate.

The FOMC makes rate-adjustment decisions based on key economic indicators that may signal inflation, recession, or other issues affecting sustainable economic growth. These indicators include measures such as the core inflation rate and durable goods orders reports.

The target for the federal funds rate has varied widely as economic conditions have changed. In the early 1980s, it reached as high as 20% in response to inflation. With the onset of the Great Recession, the rate was slashed to a historic low of 0% to 0.25% to encourage economic growth.

As of March 2020, the target rate was maintained at 0% to 0.25%. By 2022, in response to rising inflation, the Fed began gradually raising rates -- a process that continued into 2023.

The FOMC last raised the federal funds rate target to the 5.25% to 5.50% range in July 2023. It maintained this range at subsequent meetings and reaffirmed it at its last meeting in June 2024, consistent with its inflation target and full employment objective. Although the economy was expanding and unemployment remained low, the FOMC remained cautious about persistently elevated inflation.

## Special Considerations

The FOMC cannot force banks to charge the exact federal funds rate. Instead, the target rate serves as a guiding framework. The actual rate charged by lending banks is determined through negotiation between the two parties. The weighted average of rates across all such transactions is known as the effective federal funds rate.

While the FOMC cannot mandate a specific federal funds rate, the Federal Reserve can adjust the money supply to move rates toward the target. By increasing the amount of money in the system, rates can be pushed lower; by reducing the money supply, rates can be driven higher.

In addition to the federal funds rate, the Fed also sets the discount rate -- the rate at which the Fed directly charges banks for borrowing. This rate is typically higher than the target federal funds rate, in part to encourage banks to borrow from each other at the lower federal funds rate.

## Impact of the Federal Funds Rate

The federal funds rate is one of the most important interest rates in the U.S. economy. It influences monetary and financial conditions, which in turn affect key aspects of the broader economy such as employment, growth, and inflation.

The rate indirectly affects short-term interest rates on everything from mortgages and auto loans to credit cards, as lenders typically set their rates based on the prime lending rate. The prime rate is the rate banks charge their most creditworthy customers, and it is heavily influenced by the federal funds rate.

Investors pay close attention to the federal funds rate. The stock market typically reacts strongly to changes in the target rate.

For instance, a small rate decrease can prompt a significant market rally, as borrowing costs for companies decline. Many equity analysts pay particular attention to statements from FOMC members to gauge the likely future direction of the target rate.

## How Does the Federal Funds Rate Work?

The federal funds rate is the rate banks charge each other for borrowing or lending excess reserves on an overnight basis. By law, banks must maintain a minimum level of reserves proportional to their deposits. This reserve requirement is held at a Federal Reserve Bank. When a bank has excess reserves, it can lend those funds overnight to another bank that is running short.

## How Is the Federal Funds Rate Determined?

The FOMC typically meets eight times a year to set the federal funds rate. These rates are influenced by economic indicators such as the core inflation rate and the durable goods orders report, which provide signals about the nation's economic health.

## What Is the Difference Between the Federal Funds Rate and Regular Interest Rates?

Both the federal funds rate and interest rates are among the most important financial indicators in the United States. The key distinction is that the federal funds rate establishes the range at which banks lend to each other overnight. Because this affects borrowing costs and financial conditions, the stock market tends to be highly sensitive to changes in these rates. The federal funds rate also indirectly influences short-term interest rates. By contrast, the discount rate -- set by the Federal Reserve -- determines the cost for banks to borrow directly from the Fed.

## Summary

The federal funds rate is used by the Federal Reserve to manage monetary policy and is closely watched by investors as a gauge of where markets may be headed. It is one of the most important financial data points in the United States.
