# What Is Gross Domestic Product (GDP)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Gross domestic product (GDP) is the total monetary or market value of all finished goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific period. As a broad measure of overall domestic production, it serves as a comprehensive scorecard of a country's economic health.

Although GDP is typically calculated on an annual basis, it is sometimes reported quarterly as well. In the United States, for example, the government releases annualized GDP estimates for each fiscal quarter and for the calendar year. The individual data sets in these reports are presented in real terms, meaning the figures are adjusted for price changes and are therefore net of inflation.

### Key Takeaways

- GDP is the monetary value of all finished goods and services produced within a country during a specific period.
- GDP provides an economic snapshot of a country, used to estimate the size of an economy and its growth rate.
- GDP can be calculated using three approaches -- expenditure, production, or income -- and can be adjusted for inflation and population to yield deeper insights.
- Real GDP accounts for the effects of inflation, while nominal GDP does not.
- Despite its limitations, GDP is a critical tool guiding policymakers, investors, and businesses in strategic decision-making.

## Understanding GDP

The calculation of a country's GDP encompasses all private and public consumption, government expenditures, investments, additions to private inventories, paid-in construction costs, and the foreign balance of trade. Exports add to the figure, while imports are subtracted. [1]

Among all the components that make up a country's GDP, the foreign balance of trade is especially significant. A country's GDP tends to increase when the total value of goods and services that domestic producers sell to foreign countries exceeds the total value of foreign goods and services that domestic consumers purchase. When this occurs, a country is said to have a trade surplus.

If the opposite situation occurs -- that is, if domestic consumers spend more on foreign products than domestic producers sell to foreign consumers -- it is called a trade deficit. In this case, GDP tends to decline.

GDP can be computed on a nominal or real basis, the latter accounting for inflation. Overall, real GDP is a better method for expressing long-term national economic performance because it uses constant dollars. [2]

Consider a country with a nominal GDP of $100 billion in 2012. By 2022, its nominal GDP had grown to $150 billion. Over the same period, prices rose by 100%. Looking solely at nominal GDP, the economy appears to have performed well. However, real GDP (expressed in 2012 dollars) would be only $75 billion, revealing that an overall decline in real economic performance actually occurred during this period.

## What GDP Tells You

A country's GDP represents the total final market value of all the products and services it produces in a year. Another way to measure GDP is to sum four factors: consumer spending, government spending, net exports, and gross investment.

In the United States, GDP is calculated quarterly by the Bureau of Economic Analysis (BEA). The BEA bases its estimates on price data, survey results, and other information collected by agencies such as the Census Bureau, the Federal Reserve, the Department of the Treasury, and the Bureau of Labor Statistics. [3]

## Types of GDP

GDP can be reported in several ways, each providing slightly different information.

**Nominal GDP** evaluates economic production using current prices. In other words, it does not strip out inflation or the pace of rising prices, which can inflate the growth figure. All goods and services counted in nominal GDP are valued at the prices at which they actually sold that year. Nominal GDP is evaluated in either local currency or U.S. dollars at market exchange rates to compare GDP across countries in purely financial terms.

Nominal GDP is used to compare output across different quarters within the same year. When comparing GDP over two or more years, real GDP is used. By removing the effects of inflation, the comparison between different years can focus entirely on volume.

**Real GDP** is an inflation-adjusted measure that reflects the quantity of goods and services produced by an economy in a given year, with prices held constant from year to year to separate the effects of inflation or deflation from the trend in output over time. Since GDP is based on the monetary value of goods and services, it is subject to inflation.

Rising prices tend to increase a country's GDP, but this does not necessarily reflect any change in the quantity or quality of goods and services produced. By looking at nominal GDP alone, it can be difficult to tell whether the figure is rising because of a real expansion in production or merely because of price increases.

Economists use a process that adjusts for inflation to arrive at an economy's real GDP. By adjusting output in any given year against the price levels of a reference year (called the base year), economists can account for inflation's impact. This enables year-over-year comparisons of a country's GDP to determine whether real growth has occurred.

Real GDP is calculated using a GDP price deflator, which is the price difference between the current year and the base year. For example, if prices rose 5% since the base year, the deflator would be 1.05. Nominal GDP is divided by this deflator, yielding real GDP. Nominal GDP is usually higher than real GDP because inflation is typically positive. [2]

Real GDP accounts for changes in market value and thus narrows the gap between output figures from year to year. If there is a large discrepancy between a country's real and nominal GDP, this may be a sign of significant inflation or deflation in its economy.

**Per capita GDP** measures GDP per person in a country's population. It indicates that the amount of output or income per person in an economy can serve as an indicator of average productivity or average living standards. Per capita GDP can be stated in nominal, real (inflation-adjusted), or purchasing power parity (PPP) terms. [4]

At a basic level, per capita GDP shows the economic production value attributable to each citizen. This also translates into a measure of overall national wealth, since GDP market value per person readily serves as a prosperity indicator.

Per capita GDP is often analyzed alongside more traditional measures of GDP. Economists use this metric to gain insight into the domestic productivity of their own country relative to others. Per capita GDP considers both a country's GDP and its population, so understanding how each factor contributes to the overall result and influences per capita GDP growth is important.

For example, if a country's per capita GDP is growing while population levels remain stable, it may be the result of technological advances producing more with the same population level. Some countries may have high per capita GDP but a small population, which usually means they have built a self-sufficient economy based on an abundance of special resources.

## GDP Growth Rate

The GDP growth rate compares the year-over-year (or quarterly) change in a country's economic output to measure how fast an economy is growing. Usually expressed as a percentage, this measure is popular among economic policymakers because GDP growth is closely linked to key policy targets such as inflation and unemployment rates. [5]

If GDP growth accelerates, it may be a sign that the economy is overheating, and the central bank may seek to raise interest rates. Conversely, central banks view a shrinking (or negative) GDP growth rate -- that is, a recession -- as a signal that rates should be lowered and stimulus may be necessary.

While not a direct measure of GDP, economists look at PPP to understand how a country's GDP measures up in "international dollars," using a method that adjusts for local price and cost-of-living differences to make cross-country comparisons of real output, real income, and living standards. [6]

**1.3%:** The annualized growth rate of U.S. GDP in the first quarter of 2024. U.S. GDP posted 3.4% growth in Q4 2023. [7]

## GDP Formula

GDP can be determined through three primary methods. When calculated correctly, all three should yield the same figure. These three approaches are commonly known as the expenditure approach, the output (or production) approach, and the income approach.

The **expenditure approach**, also called the spending approach, calculates spending by different groups participating in the economy. U.S. GDP is primarily measured using the expenditure approach. It can be calculated with the following formula:

$$ \begin{aligned}&\text{GDP} = \text{C} + \text{G} + \text{I} + \text{NX} \\&\textbf{where:} \\&\text{C} = \text{Consumption} \\&\text{G} = \text{Government spending} \\&\text{I} = \text{Investment} \\&\text{NX} = \text{Net exports} \\\end{aligned} $$

All of these activities contribute to a country's GDP. Consumption refers to private consumption expenditures, or consumer spending. Consumers spend money to acquire goods and services such as groceries and haircuts. [8] Consumer spending is the largest component of GDP, accounting for more than two-thirds of U.S. GDP. [9]

Consumer confidence therefore has a significant bearing on economic growth. A high confidence level indicates consumers are willing to spend, while a low confidence level reflects uncertainty about the future and an unwillingness to spend.

Government spending represents government consumption expenditure and gross investment. Government spends money on equipment, infrastructure, and payroll. Government spending may become relatively more important as a component of GDP when consumer spending and business investment both decline sharply -- for example, in the aftermath of a recession.

Investment refers to private domestic investment, or capital expenditures. Businesses spend money to invest in their activities -- for example, purchasing machinery. Business investment is a critical component of GDP because it increases the productive capacity of an economy and boosts employment levels.

The net exports formula subtracts total imports from total exports (NX = Exports - Imports). The goods and services an economy produces for export, less the imports purchased by domestic consumers, represent a country's net exports. All expenditures by companies located in a given country, even if they are foreign companies, are included in this calculation. [8]

The **production approach** is essentially the reverse of the expenditure approach. Instead of measuring input costs that contribute to economic activity, the production approach estimates the total value of economic output and deducts the cost of intermediate goods consumed in the process (such as materials and services). While the expenditure approach projects forward from costs, the production approach looks backward from the vantage point of a state of completed economic activity. [10]

The **income approach** represents a middle ground between the other two methods. It calculates the income earned by all factors of production in an economy, including wages paid to labor, rent earned by land, return on capital in the form of interest, and corporate profits.

The income approach factors in some adjustments for items that are not considered payments to factors of production. First, there are taxes -- such as sales taxes and property taxes -- classified as indirect business taxes. In addition, depreciation -- a reserve fund set aside by businesses to cover the replacement of equipment worn through use -- is also added to national income. All of this together constitutes national income. [11]

## GDP vs. GNP vs. GNI

Although GDP is a widely used measure, there are other ways to gauge a country's economic growth. While GDP measures economic activity within a country's physical borders (regardless of whether producers are local or foreign-owned entities), gross national product (GNP) measures the overall production by a country's nationals or companies, including those based abroad. GNP excludes domestic production by foreigners. [12]

Gross national income (GNI) is yet another measure of economic growth. It is the sum of all income earned by a country's citizens or nationals (regardless of whether the underlying economic activity is conducted domestically or abroad). [13] The relationship between GNP and GNI is similar to that between the production (output) and income approaches used to calculate GDP.

GNP uses the production approach, while GNI uses the income approach. With GNI, the income of a country is calculated as its domestic income plus its indirect business taxes and depreciation (as well as its net foreign factor income). The figure for net foreign factor income is calculated by subtracting all payments made to foreign companies and individuals from all payments made to domestic businesses.

In an increasingly global economy, GNI has been put forward as a potentially better metric for overall economic health than GDP. Because certain countries have most of their income withdrawn abroad by foreign corporations and individuals, their GDP figures are much higher than the figures representative of their GNI.

For example, in 2019, there was a significant difference between Luxembourg's GDP and GNI, primarily due to large payments made to the rest of the world via foreign corporations doing business in Luxembourg, attracted by the small nation's favorable tax laws. [14] In contrast, the United States' GNI and GDP do not differ substantially. As of Q1 2024, U.S. GDP was $28.25 trillion, while its GNI was approximately $25.98 trillion as of the end of 2022. [15][16]

## Adjustments to GDP

A number of adjustments can be made to a country's GDP to improve its usefulness. For economists, a country's GDP reveals the size of the economy but provides little information about the standard of living in that country. Part of the reason is that population size and cost of living are not consistent around the world. Economists can use the tax-to-GDP ratio to better understand how a nation's tax revenue impacts its economy and its people.

Comparing the nominal GDP of China with Ireland's would not provide much meaningful information about the living realities of those countries, since China has approximately 300 times the population of Ireland. [17]

To help address this problem, statisticians sometimes compare per capita GDP across countries. Per capita GDP is calculated by dividing a country's total GDP by its population and is frequently cited to assess a nation's standard of living. Even so, the measure remains imperfect.

Suppose China has a per capita GDP of $1,500 while Ireland has a per capita GDP of $15,000. This does not necessarily mean that the average Irish person is 10 times better off than the average Chinese person. Per capita GDP does not account for how expensive it is to live in a country.

PPP attempts to address this by comparing how many goods and services an exchange-rate-adjusted unit of money can purchase in different countries -- essentially comparing the price of an item or basket of items in two countries after adjusting for the exchange rate between them.

Real per capita GDP, adjusted for purchasing power parity, is a heavily refined statistic for measuring true income, which is an important element of well-being. A person in Ireland might earn $100,000 a year, while a person in China might earn $50,000 a year. In nominal terms, the Irish worker is better off. But if a year's worth of food, clothing, and other items costs three times as much in Ireland as it does in China, the Chinese worker has a higher real income.

## How GDP Data Is Used

Most nations release GDP data on a monthly and quarterly basis. In the United States, the BEA publishes an advance release of quarterly GDP four weeks after the quarter ends and a final release three months after the quarter ends. BEA releases are exhaustive and contain a wealth of detail, enabling economists and investors to obtain information and insights on various aspects of the economy. [18]

The market impact of GDP is generally limited because it is backward-looking, and a substantial amount of time has already elapsed between the quarter's end and the data release. However, GDP data can have an impact on markets if the actual figures deviate significantly from expectations.

Because GDP provides a direct indication of the health and growth of the economy, businesses can use GDP as a guide for their business strategies. Government entities such as the Federal Reserve use the growth rate and other GDP statistics as part of their decision-making process to determine what type of monetary policy to implement.

If the growth rate is slowing, they may implement expansionary monetary policy to try to boost the economy. If the growth rate is robust, they may use monetary policy to slow things down in an effort to ward off inflation.

Real GDP is the indicator that says the most about the health of the economy. It is widely followed and discussed by economists, analysts, investors, and policymakers. The advance release of the latest data will almost always move markets, although that impact can be limited, as noted above.

## GDP and Investing

Investors watch GDP because it provides a framework for decision-making. The corporate profits and inventory data in the GDP report are a great resource for equity investors, as both categories show total growth during the period; corporate profits data also displays pre-tax profits, operating cash flows, and breakdowns for all major sectors of the economy.

Comparing the GDP growth rates of different countries can play a role in asset allocation, aiding decisions about whether to invest in fast-growing economies abroad -- and if so, which ones.

One useful metric investors can use to gauge stock market valuation is the ratio of total market capitalization to GDP, expressed as a percentage. The closest equivalent in terms of stock valuation is a company's market cap to total sales (or revenues), which in per-share terms is the well-known price-to-sales ratio.

Just as stocks in different sectors trade at widely diverging price-to-sales ratios, different nations trade at market-cap-to-GDP ratios that are literally all over the map. For example, according to the World Bank, the U.S. had a market-cap-to-GDP ratio of 197.4% in 2020, while China's ratio was just over 83.6% and Hong Kong's ratio was 1,777.2%. [19]

However, the utility of this ratio lies in comparing it to historical norms for a particular nation. For example, the U.S. had a market-cap-to-GDP ratio of 141.6% at the end of 2006, which dropped to 78.5% by the end of 2008. [19] In hindsight, these represented zones of significant overvaluation and undervaluation, respectively, for U.S. equities.

The biggest drawback of this data is its lack of timeliness; investors only get one update per quarter, and revisions can be large enough to significantly alter the percentage change in GDP.

## History of GDP

The concept of GDP was first proposed in a 1937 report to the U.S. Congress in response to the Great Depression. It was conceived and presented by economist Simon Kuznets of the National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER). [20]

At the time, the preeminent measurement system was GNP. After the Bretton Woods conference in 1944, GDP was widely adopted as the standard means for measuring national economies. However, the United States continued to use GNP as its official measure of economic welfare until 1991, when it switched to GDP. [21][22]

Beginning in the 1950s, however, some economists and policymakers began to question GDP. Some observed a tendency to accept GDP as an absolute indicator of a nation's failure or success, despite its failure to account for health, happiness, (in)equality, and other constituent factors of public welfare. In other words, these critics drew attention to the distinction between economic progress and social progress.

Most authorities, such as Arthur Okun, an economist on President John F. Kennedy's Council of Economic Advisers, held firm to the belief that GDP is an absolute indicator of economic success, claiming that for every increase in GDP there would be a corresponding drop in unemployment. [23]

## Criticisms of GDP

There are, of course, drawbacks to using GDP as an indicator. Beyond the lack of timeliness, several other criticisms of GDP as a measure include:

- **It ignores the value of informal or unrecorded economic activity.** GDP relies on recorded transactions and official data, so it does not account for the extent of informal economic activity. GDP fails to account for the value of under-the-table employment, black market activity, or unpaid volunteer work, all of which can be significant in certain countries. It also cannot account for the value of leisure time or household production, which are universal conditions of human life in every society.
- **It is geographically limited in a globally open economy.** GDP does not account for profits earned by foreign companies in a country that are remitted back to foreign investors. This can overstate a country's actual economic output. For example, Ireland had a GDP of $533.14 billion and a GNI of $382.87 billion in 2022, a difference of about $150.27 billion (or more than 28% of GDP), largely due to profit repatriation by foreign companies headquartered in Ireland. [24][25]
- **It emphasizes material output without considering overall well-being.** GDP growth alone cannot measure a nation's development or its citizens' well-being. A country may be experiencing rapid GDP growth, but this may impose a significant cost on society in terms of environmental impact and widening income disparities.
- **It ignores business-to-business activity.** GDP considers only final goods production and new capital investment and intentionally nets out intermediate spending and transactions between businesses. By doing so, GDP overstates the importance of consumption relative to production in the economy and is less sensitive as an indicator of economic fluctuations compared to metrics that include business-to-business activity.
- **It counts costs and waste as economic benefits.** GDP counts all final private and government spending as an addition to income and output, regardless of whether it is productive or profitable. This means that obviously unproductive or even destructive activities are routinely counted as economic output and contribute to growth in GDP. This includes spending directed toward extracting or transferring wealth among members of society rather than producing it (such as the administrative costs of taxation or money spent on lobbying and rent-seeking); spending on investment projects for which the necessary complementary goods and labor do not exist, or for which no real consumer demand exists (such as construction of empty ghost cities or bridges to nowhere connected to no road network); and spending on goods and services that are themselves destructive, or only necessary to offset other destructive activities, rather than to create new wealth (such as the production of weapons of war or spending on policing and anti-crime measures).

## Global Sources for National GDP Data

The World Bank hosts one of the most reliable web-based databases. It has one of the best and most comprehensive lists of countries for which it tracks GDP data. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) also provides GDP data through its multiple databases, such as the World Economic Outlook and International Financial Statistics. [26][27]

Another highly reliable source of GDP data is the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD). The OECD provides not only historical data but also forecasts for GDP growth. The drawback of using the OECD database is that it tracks only OECD member countries and a few nonmember countries. [28]

In the United States, the Fed collects data from multiple sources, including a country's statistical agencies and the World Bank. The drawback of using a Fed database is a lack of updating in GDP data and an absence of data for certain countries. [29]

The BEA, a division of the U.S. Department of Commerce, issues its own analysis document with each GDP release, which is a great investor tool for analyzing figures and trends and reading highlights of the very lengthy full release. [18]

## What Is a Simple Definition of GDP?

Gross domestic product is a measurement that seeks to capture a country's economic output. Countries with larger GDPs will have a greater amount of goods and services generated within them and will generally have a higher standard of living. For this reason, many citizens and political leaders see GDP growth as an important measure of national success, often referring to GDP growth and economic growth interchangeably. Due to various limitations, however, many economists have argued that GDP should not be used as a proxy for overall economic success, much less the success of a society.

## Which Country Has the Highest GDP?

The two countries with the highest GDP in the world are the United States and China. However, their ranking depends on how you measure GDP. Using nominal GDP, the United States comes in first with $25.44 trillion as of 2022, compared to $17.96 trillion for China. [30]

Many economists argue that it is more accurate to use purchasing power parity GDP as a measure of national wealth. By this metric, China is the world leader with a 2022 PPP GDP of $31.77 trillion, followed by the United States at $25.44 trillion. [31]

## Is a High GDP Good?

Most people perceive a higher GDP as a good thing because it is associated with greater economic opportunity and a higher standard of material well-being. It is possible, however, for a country to have a high GDP and still be an unattractive place to live, so it is important to consider other measures as well.

For example, a country could have a high GDP and a low per capita GDP, suggesting that significant wealth exists but is concentrated in the hands of very few people. One way to address this is to look at GDP alongside another measure of economic development, such as the Human Development Index (HDI). [6]

## The Bottom Line

In their seminal textbook *Economics*, Paul Samuelson and William Nordhaus neatly summarized the importance of national accounts and GDP. They likened GDP's ability to provide an overall picture of the state of the economy to that of a satellite in space surveying the weather across an entire continent. [32]

GDP enables policymakers and central banks to judge whether the economy is contracting or expanding, whether it needs a boost or restraint, and whether a threat such as recession or inflation looms on the horizon. Like any measure, GDP has its imperfections. In recent decades, governments have created various nuanced modifications in an attempt to increase GDP accuracy and specificity. Means of calculating GDP have also evolved continually since its conception to keep up with evolving measurements of industry activity and the generation and consumption of new forms of intangible assets.
