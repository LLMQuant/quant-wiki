# What Is Macroeconomics?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that studies the behavior of an economy as a whole -- encompassing markets, businesses, consumers, and governments. It examines economy-wide phenomena such as inflation, price levels, the rate of economic growth, national income, gross domestic product (GDP), and changes in unemployment.

Some of the key questions macroeconomics addresses include: What causes unemployment? What causes inflation? What creates or stimulates economic growth? Macroeconomics seeks to measure how well an economy is performing, to understand the forces that drive it, and to project how performance can be improved.

### Key Takeaways

- Macroeconomics is the branch of economics that deals with the structure, performance, behavior, and decision-making of the whole, or aggregate, economy.
- The two main areas of macroeconomic research are long-term economic growth and short-term business cycles.
- Macroeconomics in its modern form is often defined as starting with John Maynard Keynes and his theories about market behavior and government policy in the 1930s; several schools of thought have developed since.
- In contrast to macroeconomics, microeconomics focuses more on the influences on and choices made by individual actors in the economy (e.g., people, companies, and industries).

## Understanding Macroeconomics

As its name suggests, macroeconomics is a field that analyzes the economy through a wide lens. This includes examining variables such as unemployment, GDP, and inflation. In addition, macroeconomists develop models to explain the relationships among these factors.

These models and the forecasts they produce are used by government entities to help construct and evaluate economic, monetary, and fiscal policy. Businesses use the models to set strategy in domestic and global markets, and investors use them to predict and plan for movements across various asset classes.

Properly applied, economic theories can illuminate how economies function and the long-term consequences of particular policies and decisions. Macroeconomic theory can also help individual businesses and investors make better decisions by providing a more thorough understanding of how broad economic trends and policies affect their own industries.

## History of Macroeconomics

While the term "macroeconomics" dates back to the 1940s, many of the discipline's core concepts have been studied for far longer. Topics such as unemployment, prices, growth, and trade have concerned economists since the field's inception in the 18th century. Elements of earlier works by Adam Smith and John Stuart Mill addressed issues that would now be recognized as belonging to the domain of macroeconomics.

Macroeconomics in its modern form is typically defined as beginning with John Maynard Keynes and his 1936 publication of *The General Theory of Employment, Interest, and Money*. In it, Keynes explained the fallout from the Great Depression, when goods went unsold and workers remained unemployed.

**Note:** Throughout the 20th century, Keynesian economics (as Keynes's theories came to be known) diverged into several other schools of thought.

Before Keynes's theories became widely accepted, economists did not generally differentiate between microeconomics and macroeconomics. The same microeconomic laws of supply and demand operating in individual goods markets were understood to interact among individual markets to bring the economy into general equilibrium, as described by Leon Walras.

The link between goods markets and large-scale financial variables such as price levels and interest rates was explained through the unique role that money plays in the economy as a medium of exchange, by economists including Knut Wicksell, Irving Fisher, and Ludwig von Mises.

## Macroeconomics vs. Microeconomics

Macroeconomics differs from microeconomics, which focuses on smaller factors that influence choices made by individuals. Individuals are typically divided into subgroups such as buyers, sellers, and business owners. These actors interact with one another according to the laws of supply and demand for resources, using money and interest rates as pricing mechanisms for coordination. The factors studied in microeconomics and macroeconomics often influence each other.

A key distinction between the two is that macroeconomic aggregates can sometimes behave in ways that are very different from -- or even opposite to -- analogous microeconomic variables. For example, Keynes invoked the so-called paradox of thrift, which argues that while individuals save money to build wealth at the microeconomic level, when everyone tries to increase savings simultaneously, it can contribute to a slowdown in the economy and less aggregate wealth at the macroeconomic level. This is because spending would decline, affecting business revenues and lowering worker compensation.

## Limitations of Macroeconomics

It is equally important to understand the limitations of economic theory. Theories are often created in a vacuum, lacking specific real-world details such as taxation, regulation, and transaction costs. The real world is also decidedly complex, including matters of social preference and conscience that do not lend themselves to mathematical analysis.

**Important:** The phrase "ceteris paribus" is frequently encountered in economic theory and discussion, roughly translating to "all other things being equal." Economists use this phrase to focus on specific relationships between the variables under discussion, while assuming all other variables remain constant.

Even with the limitations of economic theory, it is important and valuable to track major macroeconomic indicators such as GDP, inflation, and unemployment. This is because the performance of companies, and by extension their stocks, is significantly influenced by the economic conditions in which they operate.

Similarly, understanding which theories are currently in favor and how they may influence a particular government administration can be highly valuable. These economic theories can indicate how the government will approach taxation, regulation, government spending, and similar policies. By better understanding economics and the ramifications of economic decisions, investors can get at least a glimpse of the probable future and act accordingly with confidence.

## Schools of Macroeconomic Thought

The field of macroeconomics is organized into many different schools of thought, with differing views on how markets and their participants operate.

**Classical** economists held that prices, wages, and rates are flexible and markets tend to clear unless prevented from doing so by government policy, building on Adam Smith's original theories. The label "classical economists" is not actually a school of macroeconomic thought per se, but rather a label applied first by Karl Marx and later by Keynes to denote previous economic thinkers with whom they disagreed.

**Keynesian economics** was largely founded on the work of John Maynard Keynes and marked the beginning of macroeconomics as a study area separate from microeconomics. Keynesians focus on aggregate demand as the principal factor in issues such as unemployment and the business cycle.

Keynesian economists believe that the business cycle can be managed through active government intervention -- fiscal policy, in which the government spends more during recessions to stimulate demand, or cuts spending during expansions to cool demand. They also believe in monetary policy, in which a central bank stimulates lending by lowering interest rates, or restricts it by raising rates.

Keynesian economists also believe that there are certain rigidities in the system, particularly sticky prices, that prevent the proper clearing of supply and demand.

**Monetarism** is a branch of Keynesian economics credited largely to the works of Milton Friedman. Working within and extending Keynesian models, monetarists argue that monetary policy is generally a more effective and more desirable policy tool than fiscal policy for managing aggregate demand. Monetarists also acknowledge limits to monetary policy that make fine-tuning the economy ill-advised, tending instead to prefer adherence to policy rules that promote stable inflation rates.

**New classical** economists, along with New Keynesians, are largely built on the goal of integrating microeconomic foundations into macroeconomics to resolve the apparent theoretical contradictions between the two subjects.

The new classical school emphasizes the importance of microeconomics and models based on that behavior. New classical economists assume that all agents try to maximize their utility and have rational expectations, which they incorporate into macroeconomic models. New classical economists believe that unemployment is largely voluntary, that discretionary fiscal policy is destabilizing, and that inflation can be controlled with monetary policy.

**New Keynesian** economists also seek to add microeconomic foundations to traditional Keynesian economic theory. While New Keynesians accept that households and firms operate on the basis of rational expectations, they still maintain that there are a variety of market failures, including sticky prices and wages. Because of this "stickiness," the government can improve macroeconomic conditions through fiscal and monetary policy.

**The Austrian School** is an older school of economics that is enjoying something of a resurgence in popularity. Austrian economic theories mainly apply to microeconomic phenomena. However, like the so-called classical economists, they never strictly separated micro- and macroeconomics.

Austrian theories also have important implications for what are otherwise considered macroeconomic subjects. In particular, Austrian business cycle theory explains broadly synchronized (macroeconomic) swings in economic activity across markets as a consequence of monetary policy and the role that money and banking play in linking (microeconomic) markets to each other and across time.

## Macroeconomic Indicators

Macroeconomics is a fairly broad field, but two specific areas of research dominate the discipline. The first looks at the factors that determine long-term economic growth. The other examines the causes and consequences of short-term fluctuations in national income and employment, also known as the business cycle.

**Economic growth** refers to an increase in aggregate production in an economy. Macroeconomists try to understand the factors that either promote or retard economic growth in order to support economic policies that will foster development, progress, and rising living standards.

Economists can use many indicators to measure economic performance. These indicators fall into 10 categories: [1]

- **GDP indicators:** Measure the amount of output an economy produces
- **Consumer spending indicators:** Measure how much capital consumers feed back into the economy
- **Income and savings indicators:** Measure consumer earnings and savings
- **Industry performance indicators:** Measure GDP by industry
- **International trade and investment indicators:** Show the balance of payments, trade volume, and international investment between trading partners
- **Price and inflation indicators:** Show fluctuations in prices paid for goods and services and changes in currency purchasing power
- **Fixed asset investment indicators:** Show how much capital is tied up in fixed assets
- **Employment indicators:** Display employment by industry, state, county, and other areas
- **Government indicators:** Show government spending and revenue
- **Special indicators:** Include all other economic indicators, such as personal income distribution, global value chains, healthcare spending, and small business well-being

Superimposed on long-term macroeconomic growth trends, the levels and rates of change of important macroeconomic variables such as employment and national output undergo fluctuations. These fluctuations are called expansions, peaks, recessions, and troughs -- and they occur in that order. When plotted on a graph, these fluctuations show that businesses perform in a cyclical pattern; hence, it is called the business cycle.

The National Bureau of Economic Research (NBER) measures the business cycle, which uses GDP and gross national income to date the cycle. [2] The NBER is also the body that declares the beginning and end of recessions and expansions.

## How to Influence Macroeconomics

Because macroeconomics is such a broad field, positively influencing the economy is challenging and takes considerably longer than changing individual behavior in microeconomics. As a result, economies need a dedicated entity that studies and identifies the techniques capable of effecting large-scale change.

In the United States, the Federal Reserve is the central bank, with a mandate to promote maximum employment and price stability. These two factors have been identified as critical to positively influencing change at the macroeconomic level.

To influence change, the Fed implements monetary policy through the tools it has developed over the years, designed to affect its dual mandate. These tools include: [3]

- **Federal funds rate range:** A target range set by the Fed that guides the interest rate for overnight lending between depository institutions, to facilitate short-term borrowing
- **Open market operations:** The purchase and sale of securities in the open market to change the supply of reserves
- **Discount window and rate:** Lending to depository institutions to help banks manage liquidity
- **Reserve requirements:** Maintaining reserves to help banks maintain liquidity
- **Interest on reserve balances:** Encouraging banks to hold reserves for liquidity and paying them interest for doing so
- **Overnight reverse repurchase agreement facility:** A supplementary tool used to help control the federal funds rate through selling securities and repurchasing them the next day at a more favorable price
- **Term deposit facility:** Term deposits of reserves, used to drain reserves from the banking system
- **Central bank liquidity swaps:** Establishing swap lines with central banks of selected countries to improve liquidity conditions in both U.S. and participating foreign central banks
- **Foreign and International Monetary Authorities Repo Facility:** A facility for institutions to enter into repurchase agreements with the Fed as a backstop source of liquidity
- **Standing Overnight Repo Facility:** A tool that encourages or discourages borrowing above a set rate, helping to control the effective federal funds rate

The Fed continuously updates the tools it uses to influence the economy, so it also maintains a list of many additional tools previously employed that could be implemented again if needed. [4]

## What Is the Most Important Concept in Macroeconomics?

Output -- referring to a nation's total production of goods and services -- is said to be the most important concept in macroeconomics. Output is often considered a snapshot of the economy at a given moment in time.

## What Are the Three Main Concerns of Macroeconomics?

The three main macroeconomic concerns are the unemployment level, inflation, and economic growth.

## Why Is Macroeconomics Important?

Macroeconomics helps governments evaluate how an economy is performing and decide what actions can be taken to accelerate or slow growth.

## The Bottom Line

Macroeconomics is a field of study used to evaluate the overall performance of an economy and develop actions that can positively influence it. Economists work to understand how specific factors and actions affect output, input, spending, consumption, inflation, and employment.

The study of economics began long ago, but the field did not begin evolving into its current form until the 1700s. Macroeconomics now plays a significant role in government and business decision-making.
