# What Is a Treasury Bond (T-Bond)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A Treasury bond (T-bond) is a government debt security issued by the U.S. federal government with a maturity of 20 or 30 years. T-bonds pay periodic interest before maturity, at which point the holder also receives the face value equal to the principal.

Treasury bonds are a key component of U.S. sovereign debt, collectively known as Treasuries. They are considered virtually risk-free because they are backed by the U.S. government's taxing authority. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- Treasury bonds (T-bonds) are fixed-rate U.S. government debt securities with maturities of 20 or 30 years.
- T-bonds pay semi-annual interest and, at maturity, the face value is returned to the bondholder. [1]
- Along with Treasury bills, Treasury notes, and Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), T-bonds are one of four virtually risk-free government-issued securities. [2]

## Understanding Treasury Bonds

Treasury bonds are one of four types of debt instruments issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury to finance government spending. The four types are Treasury bills, Treasury notes, Treasury bonds, and Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS). Each differs in maturity and interest payment structure.

They are considered benchmarks within their respective fixed-income categories because they carry virtually no risk. T-bonds are backed by the U.S. government, which can raise taxes and revenue to ensure full payment. [2]

These investments also serve as benchmark rates within their fixed-income categories, offering a base interest rate derived from a low-risk investment. T-bonds are issued with maturities of 20 and 30 years. [1]

Like other government bonds, T-bonds pay semi-annual interest, and the income is taxed only at the federal level. Treasury bonds are issued through monthly online auctions conducted directly by the U.S. Treasury. Bond prices and yields are determined at auction. After issuance, T-bonds trade actively on the secondary market and can be purchased through banks or brokers.

Individual investors frequently use Treasury bonds to keep a portion of their retirement savings in risk-free assets, generate steady retirement income, or save for a child's education or other major expenses. Investors must hold T-bonds for at least 45 days before they can be sold on the secondary market. [1]

## Treasury Bond Considerations

T-bonds are issued with maturities of 20 or 30 years and a minimum denomination of $100. Interest is paid semi-annually. Bonds are initially sold at auction; the maximum non-competitive purchase is $5 million, or 35% of the offering if the bid is competitive. [3]

Competitive bids state the rate the bidder is willing to accept; they are accepted or rejected based on comparison with the bond's set rate. Non-competitive bids guarantee allocation but require acceptance of the set rate. After auction, bonds can be sold on the secondary market. [4]

T-bonds trade actively on the secondary market, making them highly liquid. However, this active trading also means prices fluctuate. Current auction rates and yields determine secondary market pricing.

Similar to other bond types, when auction rates rise, T-bond prices on the secondary market fall because the future cash flows of existing bonds are discounted at higher rates. Conversely, when prices rise, auction yield declines. [4]

In the fixed-income market, Treasury yields help form the yield curve, which charts yields across all government maturities. The yield curve normally slopes upward (shorter maturities carry lower yields). However, when long-term rates fall below short-term rates, the curve inverts. An inverted yield curve can signal an impending recession. [5]

## What Types of Treasury Securities Exist?

There are three primary types of U.S. Treasury securities: bonds, notes, and bills. Treasury bills mature in less than one year, notes mature in 2 to 5 years, and bonds mature in 20 or 30 years. All are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government. [2]

## How to Buy Treasury Bonds

To purchase Treasury bonds, visit TreasuryDirect.gov, create an account, and buy bonds directly from the government. [6]

## Are Treasury Bonds a Good Investment?

Whether T-bonds are a good investment depends on the individual investor. T-bonds are considered risk-free assets and are well suited for investors with low risk tolerance. However, their safety comes with lower interest rates, which limit returns. During stock market downturns, T-bonds can be an attractive option. Despite being risk-free, they are subject to inflation risk and interest rate risk, which can erode investor returns. [1]

## Conclusion

Treasury bonds are a component of the broader U.S. Treasury securities market. They are low-risk assets suitable for investors seeking safety over high returns. Investors can also gain exposure to T-bonds through exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and mutual funds. The UK equivalent is the gilt, which also trades through gilt funds.

T-bonds are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government -- which oversees the world's largest and most stable economy -- making them considered risk-free investments. [1]
