# What Is a Hedge?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A hedge is an investment strategy designed to reduce the potential for losses in other investments, typically by taking a position that moves in the opposite direction. In essence, hedging acts as an insurance policy that offsets large losses in other parts of a portfolio.

The term "hedging" is commonly used to describe the practice of diversifying a portfolio -- for example, by purchasing shares in a conservative bond fund to offset potential losses from more volatile equity holdings.

In financial markets, where traders constantly buy and sell assets -- some of which carry significant risk -- hedging often involves derivative instruments. Because the relationship between a derivative and its underlying asset is well-defined, derivatives serve as effective hedging tools.

### Key Takeaways

- A hedge is a strategy aimed at limiting or offsetting risk in an investment or portfolio.
- One widely used hedging technique involves purchasing derivative contracts.
- Portfolio diversification can also serve as a form of hedging. For example, holding both cyclical and counter-cyclical stocks is one such approach.

## How Hedging Works

Hedging is analogous to purchasing insurance. If you own a property in a flood-prone area, you can protect against flood risk by buying flood insurance -- in other words, by hedging. You cannot eliminate the risk of flooding, but you can mitigate the financial damage it might cause.

Similarly, if you invest in shares of a high-growth technology company and are confident its business will thrive over the coming quarters, you might also invest in a stable consumer staples company in case your assessment proves wrong.

## Drawbacks of Hedging

Hedging involves a trade-off between risk and return: while it reduces potential downside, it also limits potential gains.

Hedging is not free. In the flood insurance analogy, monthly premiums accumulate, and if a flood never occurs, the policyholder receives nothing in return. Still, most people would choose to accept that cost rather than face an uncapped loss.

In professional investing, hedging operates the same way. Investors and fund managers employ hedging strategies to reduce and control their risk exposure using a variety of tools, many of which are based on derivatives.

**Note:** A perfect hedge eliminates all risk from an investment position or portfolio. In other words, the hedge is 100% inversely correlated with the vulnerable asset. This is more of an ideal than a reality, and even a theoretically perfect hedge carries costs.

## Hedging with Derivatives

Derivatives are financial contracts whose value depends on the price of an underlying security. Futures, forwards, and options are among the most common types of derivative contracts.

The effectiveness of a derivative hedge is expressed through its delta, sometimes referred to as the hedge ratio. Delta measures the change in the derivative's price for every $1 movement in the price of the underlying asset.

The specific hedging strategy and the pricing of hedging instruments depend largely on the downside risk of the underlying security. Generally, greater downside risk translates to higher hedging costs.

Downside risk tends to increase with volatility. An option linked to a more volatile security with a longer time to expiration will be more expensive as a hedging instrument.

As a general rule, the higher the strike price of a put option, the more expensive it is -- but the greater the price protection it provides. These variables can be adjusted to create a less expensive option with less protection or a more expensive option with greater protection.

**Note:** The name "hedge fund" derives from the broad discretion hedge fund managers enjoy in alternative investments and risk strategies such as leveraged investing.

## Example of Hedging with Put Options

In the investment world, hedging with put options is a common practice. A put option gives the holder the right, but not the obligation, to sell the underlying security at a predetermined price on or before the expiration date.

For example, suppose Morty purchases 100 shares of Stock PLC at $10 per share. He might hedge his investment by buying a one-year put option with an $8 strike price. This option gives Morty the right to sell 100 shares at $8 at any time during the next year.

Assume he pays $1 per share for the option, or $100 in total premium. If the stock is trading at $12 one year later, Morty will choose not to exercise the option and will be out the $100 premium. However, he is unlikely to be concerned, since his unrealized gain is $100 (after accounting for the premium cost).

On the other hand, if the stock falls to $0, Morty will exercise the option and sell his shares at $8, resulting in a loss of $300 (including the premium). Without the option, he would have faced a total loss of his entire investment.

## Hedging Through Diversification

Strategically diversifying a portfolio to reduce specific risks can also be considered a form of hedging. For example, Rachel might invest in a luxury goods company with rising profits. However, she may worry that a recession could devastate the luxury consumer market. One way to address this concern is to buy tobacco or utility stocks, which typically hold steady during recessions and pay substantial dividends.

This strategy involves trade-offs: if wages are high and jobs are plentiful, the luxury goods manufacturer may flourish, but few investors would be drawn to defensive counter-cyclical stocks, which may decline as capital flows toward more exciting opportunities.

There are also risks: luxury stocks and the hedge may not always move in opposite directions. Both could decline simultaneously due to a catastrophic event, as occurred during the financial crisis.

## Spread Hedging

For investors in index funds, moderate price declines are fairly common and highly unpredictable. Investors focused on this area may be more concerned about moderate drops than severe ones. In such cases, a bear put spread is a common hedging strategy.

In this type of spread, the index investor purchases a put option with a higher strike price and then sells a put option with a lower strike price but the same expiration date.

Based on the index's performance, the investor receives price protection equal to the difference between the two strike prices, minus the cost. While this may provide only moderate protection, it is often sufficient to cover a brief decline in the index.

## Hedging and the Everyday Investor

Most individual investors do not trade derivative contracts. Investors with a long-term strategy, such as those saving for retirement, can generally afford to ignore day-to-day market fluctuations.

For buy-and-hold investors, there may seem to be little reason to learn about hedging. However, because large companies and investment funds regularly hedge their positions -- and because individual investors may follow or participate in these financial entities -- understanding hedging provides valuable insight into the behavior of major market participants.

## What Is Risk Hedging?

Hedging is a strategy for limiting investment risk. An investor hedges by taking a position in another investment that is likely to move in the opposite direction.

## What Is an Example of Hedging?

Hedging is commonly used to offset potential losses in foreign exchange trading. A forex trader who anticipates currency movements may execute a directly opposing trade to limit losses from price fluctuations. This way, regardless of what happens, the trader preserves some potential gains.

## Is Hedging an Imperfect Science?

Yes. A perfect hedge would eliminate all risk from an investment position or portfolio. In other words, the hedge would be 100% inversely correlated with the vulnerable asset. This is purely hypothetical, and even a theoretically perfect hedge carries costs that reduce its returns.

## Conclusion

Hedging is a fundamental financial concept that enables investors and traders to minimize a range of risk exposures. A hedge is essentially an offsetting or opposing position that gains (or loses) value as the primary position loses (or gains) value.

A hedge can be thought of as an insurance policy on an investment or portfolio. These offsetting positions can be achieved through closely correlated assets or through diversification.

Among professional traders, the most common and effective hedges involve derivatives such as futures, forwards, or options contracts.
