# What Are Economies of Scale?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Economies of scale are the cost advantages a company obtains as production becomes more efficient. Companies can achieve economies of scale by increasing output and lowering costs, because costs are spread over a larger number of goods. Costs can be either fixed or variable.

### Key Takeaways

- Economies of scale are cost advantages companies gain as production becomes more efficient, because costs are spread over a greater number of goods.
- A company's size is related to whether it can achieve economies of scale -- larger companies enjoy greater cost savings and higher production levels.
- Economies of scale can be internal or external. Internal economies are driven by factors within an individual company, while external factors affect an entire industry.

## Understanding Economies of Scale

In general, a company's size matters for economies of scale. The larger the business, the greater the cost savings. Economies of scale can be either internal or external. Internal economies are based on management decisions, while external economies are driven by factors outside the firm.

Internal functions such as accounting, information technology, and marketing are also considered sources of operational efficiency and synergy.

Economies of scale are an important concept for any business in any industry. They represent the cost savings and competitive advantages that larger companies have over smaller ones.

Most consumers do not understand why a small business charges more for a similar product than its larger competitors. That is because the cost per unit depends on the scale of the company's production. Large companies can produce more by spreading production costs over a greater number of goods. An industry can also influence product costs when several different companies produce similar goods within the same sector.

Economies of scale reduce per-unit costs for several reasons. First, labor specialization and more integrated technology boost production volumes. Second, lower per-unit costs can come from bulk orders from suppliers, larger advertising purchases, or lower costs of capital. Third, spreading internal function costs across more units produced and sold helps reduce costs.

**Important:** When a company grows too large and pursues economies of scale, diseconomies of scale can occur. [1]

## Internal vs. External Economies of Scale

As noted above, there are two different types of economies of scale:

- **Internal economies of scale:** Arise from within the company itself, due to changes in how it operates or produces goods.
- **External economies of scale:** Based on factors affecting an entire industry rather than any single company.

Internal economies of scale occur when a company cuts costs internally and are therefore unique to that particular firm. This may result from the company's sheer size or from decisions made by management. Types of internal economies of scale include:

- **Technical:** Large-scale machinery or production processes that increase productivity.
- **Purchasing:** Lower costs through bulk procurement.
- **Managerial:** Hiring specialists to oversee and improve different parts of the production process.
- **Risk-bearing:** Spreading risk across multiple investors.
- **Financial:** Better creditworthiness, which improves access to capital and more favorable interest rates.
- **Marketing:** Greater advertising power spread over a larger market, plus enhanced negotiating position within the market.

Larger companies can typically achieve internal economies of scale -- lowering costs and increasing output -- because, for example, they can purchase resources in bulk, hold patents or special technologies, or access more capital.

External economies of scale, on the other hand, are achieved through factors outside the firm that affect an entire industry. No single company can control these costs. External economies arise when there is a highly skilled labor pool, subsidies and/or tax breaks, and partnerships or joint ventures -- anything that can lower costs for many companies within a specific industry.

## Limits of Economies of Scale

For decades, management techniques and technology have focused on the limits of economies of scale.

More flexible technology has lowered setup costs. Equipment is priced more in line with production capacity, enabling smaller producers such as steel mini-mills and craft breweries to compete more effectively.

Outsourcing of functional services has made costs more similar for businesses of various sizes. These services include accounting, human resources, marketing, finance, legal, and information technology.

Micro-manufacturing, hyper-local manufacturing, and additive manufacturing (3D printing) can lower both setup and production costs. Global trade and logistics help reduce costs regardless of the size of an individual plant.

According to the International Monetary Fund, the prices of capital goods -- as well as the cost of machinery and equipment -- have declined over the past three decades across emerging, developed, and industrialized nations. [2]

## Examples of Economies of Scale

In a hospital, a doctor's consultation still takes about 20 minutes, but all of the hospital system's business overhead costs are spread over more consultations, and the people assisting physicians are no longer degree-holding nurses but technicians or nursing assistants.

A job shop produces goods in batches -- for example, shirts with a company logo. A significant cost driver is setup. In a job shop, larger production runs lower the per-unit cost because the setup costs of designing a logo and creating a screen print are spread over more shirts. In an assembly plant, per-unit costs are reduced through more seamless technology using robotics.

A restaurant kitchen is often used to illustrate how economies of scale reach their limit: too many chefs in a small space get in each other's way. In economics charts, this has been depicted using a U-shaped curve, where average cost per unit first falls and then rises. Costs that rise as output grows are known as "diseconomies of scale."

## What Are Economies of Scale?

Economies of scale are advantages that can sometimes arise from scaling up a business. For example, a company may enjoy an economy of scale in bulk purchasing. By buying a large quantity of a product at once, it can negotiate a lower per-unit price than its competitors.

## What Causes Economies of Scale?

Generally, economies of scale can be achieved in two ways. First, a company can realize internal economies of scale by reorganizing how its resources -- such as equipment and personnel -- are distributed and used within the firm. Second, a company can realize external economies of scale by increasing its size relative to competitors and leveraging that larger scale to engage in competitive practices such as negotiating bulk discounts.

## Why Are Economies of Scale Important?

Economies of scale are important because they can help provide businesses with a competitive advantage in their industry. Companies will therefore try to realize economies of scale wherever possible, just as investors will seek to identify economies of scale when selecting investments. A particularly well-known example of an economy of scale is known as the network effect.
