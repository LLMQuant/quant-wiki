# What Is the Velocity of Money?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The velocity of money measures the rate at which money is exchanged in an economy. It is the number of times a unit of currency moves from one entity to another. The velocity of money also refers to how much a unit of currency is used over a given period. Simply put, it is the rate at which consumers and businesses in an economy collectively spend money.

The velocity of money is typically measured as the ratio of gross domestic product (GDP) to a country's M1 or M2 money supply. The word "velocity" here refers to the speed at which money changes hands.

### Key Takeaways

- The velocity of money measures the rate at which money is exchanged in an economy.
- The velocity of money is calculated by dividing GDP by the money supply.
- The velocity of money formula shows how quickly one unit of money supply is transacted for goods and services in an economy.
- The velocity of money tends to be higher in expanding economies and lower in contracting economies.

## Understanding the Velocity of Money

The velocity of money is important for gauging how quickly money in circulation is being used to purchase goods and services. It is used to help economists and investors assess the health and vitality of an economy. A high velocity of money is generally associated with a healthy, expanding economy. A low velocity of money is usually associated with recessions and contractions.

Economists use the velocity of money to measure the rate at which money is used for goods and services in an economy. While it is not necessarily a key economic indicator on its own, it can be tracked alongside other key indicators that help determine economic health, such as GDP, unemployment, and inflation. GDP and the money supply are the two components of the velocity of money formula.

Economies that exhibit a higher velocity of money relative to others tend to be more developed. The velocity of money is also known to fluctuate with business cycles. When an economy is in an expansion, consumers and businesses tend to spend money more readily, causing the velocity of money to increase. When an economy is contracting, consumers and businesses are typically more reluctant to spend, and the velocity of money is lower.

Because the velocity of money is typically correlated with business cycles, it can also be correlated with key indicators. Therefore, the velocity of money usually rises alongside GDP and inflation. Alternatively, it is generally expected to fall when key economic indicators such as GDP and inflation decline in a contracting economy.

## Example of the Velocity of Money

Consider an economy consisting of two individuals, A and B, who each have $100 in cash. Individual A buys a car from B for $100. Now B has $200 in cash. Then B purchases a house from A for $100, and B enlists A's help with new construction on the house, paying A another $100 for the effort. A now has $200 in cash. B then sells a car to A for $100, and both A and B end up with $100 in cash. Thus, even though each party in the economy had only $100, they conducted $400 worth of transactions.

In this economy, the velocity of money would be 2, resulting from $400 in transactions divided by $200 in money supply. This multiplication in the value of goods and services exchanged is made possible through the velocity of money.

## The Velocity of Money Formula

While the above provides a simplified example, the velocity of money is used on a much larger scale as a measure of transactional activity for an entire country's population. In general, this measure can be thought of as the turnover rate of the entire money supply in an economy.

For this application, economists typically use GDP and either M1 or M2 as the money supply. Therefore, the velocity of money equation is written as GDP divided by money supply.

$$ \text{Velocity of Money} = \frac{ \text{GDP} }{ \text{Money Supply} } $$

GDP is typically used as the numerator in the velocity of money formula, though gross national product (GNP) may also be used. GDP represents the total amount of goods and services available for purchase in an economy. In the denominator, economists typically identify the velocity of money for both M1 and M2.

The Federal Reserve defines M1 as the sum of all currency held by the public and transaction deposits at depository institutions. M2 is a broader measure of money supply, adding savings deposits, time deposits, and real money market mutual funds. [1] Furthermore, the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis tracks the quarterly velocity of money using both M1 and M2. [2]

## Velocity of Money and the Economy

Economists disagree about whether the velocity of money is a useful indicator of the health of an economy or, more specifically, of inflationary pressures. The "monetarists" who subscribe to the quantity theory of money argue that money velocity should be stable absent changing expectations, but a change in money supply can alter expectations and therefore money velocity and inflation.

For example, in theory, an increase in the money supply should lead to a commensurate increase in prices because there is more money chasing the same level of goods and services in the economy. The opposite should occur with a decrease in money supply. Critics, on the other hand, argue that in the short term, the velocity of money is highly variable and prices are resistant to change, resulting in a weak and indirect link between money supply and inflation.

Empirically, data suggest that the velocity of money is indeed variable. Moreover, the relationship between money velocity and inflation is also variable. For example, from 1959 through the end of 2007, the velocity of the M2 money stock averaged approximately 1.9x, with a maximum of 2.198x in 1997 and a minimum of 1.653x in 1964. [3]

Since 2007, the velocity of money has fallen dramatically as the Federal Reserve greatly expanded its balance sheet in an effort to combat the global financial crisis and deflationary pressures.

Money velocity appeared to have bottomed at 1.435 in the second quarter of 2017 and was gradually rising until the global recession triggered by the COVID-19 pandemic prompted massive U.S. federal economic stimulus. At the end of the second quarter of 2020, M2V stood at 1.100 -- the lowest reading of M2 money velocity in history. [3]

## Factors That Influence the Velocity of Money

Several factors influence the velocity of money in an economy, including:

- Money supply: The velocity of money is inversely related to the money supply. When a central bank increases the money supply, the rate of economic transactions also accelerates. This can lead to inflation.
- Consumer behavior: Velocity is also influenced by the behavior of economic agents. When consumers prioritize saving over spending, the rate of transactions slows and the velocity of money declines. When consumers prioritize spending, the velocity of money accelerates.
- Payment systems: The velocity of money is also influenced by characteristics of the monetary system, such as the availability of credit or electronic banking. When there are fewer barriers to transactions, the velocity of money tends to accelerate; when spending is difficult, it declines.

## Why Is the Velocity of Money Slowing?

According to the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, the velocity of the M1 money supply has been steadily declining since the 2008 recession. [4]

Much of this decline is attributable to demographic changes and the lingering effects of the Great Recession. As baby boomers approach retirement and household wealth has declined significantly, many consumers are more motivated to save than ever before.

Federal regulations may also have played a role, as the Dodd-Frank Act raised reserve requirements and leverage ratios for banks. Because these institutions are required to hold more assets rather than lend or spend them, there is less money moving through the economy.

One factor behind the slowing velocity of money is straightforward: the COVID-19 pandemic. The velocity of money fell sharply in 2020, likely due to heightened economic uncertainty and the influx of stimulus payments.

## What Does the Velocity of Money Measure?

The velocity of money estimates the movement of money in an economy -- in other words, the number of times the average dollar changes hands in a single year. A high velocity of money indicates a bustling economy with strong economic activity, while a low velocity indicates a general reluctance to spend money.

## How Is the Velocity of Money Calculated?

The velocity of money is calculated by dividing a country's gross domestic product by the total supply of money. This calculation can use either the M1 money supply, which includes physical currency, checkable deposits, and certain other figures, or the M2 supply, which also includes savings deposits and money market funds.

## Why Is the Velocity of Money So Low?

According to the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, the velocity of money in the United States fell sharply during the first and second quarters of 2020. While there is no definitive explanation, the decline is likely due to reduced activity during the COVID-19 pandemic and an increase in consumer savings driven by economic uncertainty.

## Conclusion

The velocity of money represents the heartbeat of an economy. It measures how quickly money changes hands from one transaction to the next. During boom times, the velocity of money tends to be high, indicating brisk activity and frequent transactions. During downturns, velocity slows, indicating that consumers are less willing to spend or make transactions.
