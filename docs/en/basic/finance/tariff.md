# What Is a Tariff?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Most countries are constrained by their natural resources and capacity to produce specific goods and services. They trade with other nations to obtain the products their populations need and demand. However, trade between partners does not always proceed amicably. Policy disagreements, geopolitical tensions, competition, and many other factors can create friction between trading partners.

One tool governments use to address disputes with trading partners is the tariff. A tariff is a tax imposed by one country on goods and services imported from another, designed to influence the exporting nation, generate revenue, or protect competitive advantages.

### Key Takeaways

- Governments impose tariffs to generate revenue, protect domestic industries, or exert political leverage on another country.
- Tariffs often produce unintended side effects, including higher consumer prices.
- Tariffs have a long and contentious history, and the debate over whether they constitute sound policy continues.

## Understanding Tariffs

Tariffs are used to restrict imports. In simple terms, they raise the price of goods and services purchased from another country, making those imports less attractive to domestic consumers.

A key point is that tariffs affect the exporting country because consumers in the tariff-imposing country may shy away from the now-more-expensive imported goods. However, if consumers still choose the imported product, the tariff effectively raises costs for those consumers.

There are two types of tariffs:

- **Specific tariffs:** A fixed fee levied based on the type of good -- for example, a $500 tariff on each imported automobile.
- **Ad valorem tariffs:** A percentage levied based on the value of the good -- for example, 5% of the import's declared value.

## Why Governments Impose Tariffs

Governments may impose tariffs for several reasons:

- **Revenue generation:** Tariffs can be used to increase government revenue. These are known as revenue tariffs and are not primarily intended to restrict imports. For example, during 2018 and 2019, the Trump administration imposed tariffs on many goods to rebalance trade deficits. Tariff revenues totaled $41.6 billion in FY2018 and $71.9 billion in FY2019. [1][2]
- **Protecting domestic industries:** Tariffs can shield specific industries, companies, and jobs. In April 2018, President Trump imposed a 25% ad valorem tariff on steel products from all countries except Canada and Mexico. [3] In March 2022, President Biden replaced the steel tariff on UK imports with a 500,000-ton tariff-rate quota and reached quota agreements with several other countries. [4][5]
- **Protecting consumers:** By making foreign-produced goods more expensive, tariffs can make domestically produced alternatives more attractive. Products manufactured in countries with less stringent regulations -- such as goods coated with lead-based paint -- can be priced out of the market through tariffs.
- **Foreign policy tool:** Tariffs can function as an extension of diplomacy and economic leverage. For example, after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, President Biden suspended normal trade relations with Russia in April 2022. In June, he raised tariffs on Russian imports not already covered by the suspension to 35%. [6]

## Unintended Side Effects of Tariffs

Tariffs can produce unintended consequences:

- They may reduce domestic industry efficiency and innovation by diminishing competition.
- They can harm domestic consumers, as reduced competition tends to push prices higher.
- They may create tensions by favoring certain industries or geographic regions. Tariffs designed to help urban manufacturers, for example, may hurt rural consumers who pay higher prices for finished goods without benefiting from the policy.
- Attempts to pressure a rival country through tariffs can escalate into a retaliatory cycle -- commonly known as a trade war.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Tariffs

#### Advantages

- **Generate revenue:** Tariffs provide governments with an additional revenue stream, potentially easing the tax burden on citizens and helping reduce fiscal deficits.
- **Open negotiations:** Countries can use tariffs to initiate trade or policy discussions. Both parties can leverage tariffs as a starting point for economic dialogue.
- **Support national objectives:** One of the most popular uses of tariffs is to ensure domestic products receive preferential treatment, supporting local businesses and the economy.
- **Stabilize markets:** Tariffs can help stabilize markets and make prices more predictable.

#### Disadvantages

- **Create intergovernmental friction:** Using tariffs to punish or discourage disapproved behavior can strain relations between nations and generate broader problems.
- **Trigger trade wars:** The typical response from a country targeted by tariffs is to retaliate in kind, setting off a trade war from which neither side benefits.

## History of Tariffs

In pre-modern Europe, a nation's wealth was believed to consist of fixed, tangible assets such as gold, silver, land, and other physical resources. Trade was viewed as a zero-sum game. If a country imported more than it exported, resources -- primarily gold -- would flow abroad, draining national wealth. Cross-border trade was viewed with suspicion, and countries preferred to establish colonies with exclusive trading arrangements.

This system, known as mercantilism, relied heavily on tariffs and even outright trade bans. Colonial powers imported raw materials from their colonies (which were typically prohibited from selling those materials elsewhere), transformed them into finished goods, and sold them back to the colonies. High tariffs ensured colonies purchased manufactured goods only from the mother country. [7][8]

Scottish economist Adam Smith was among the first to challenge this arrangement. His *Wealth of Nations*, published in 1776 -- the same year the American colonies declared independence in part over high taxes and restrictive trade -- laid the intellectual groundwork for free trade. [9]

David Ricardo later developed Smith's ideas into the theory of comparative advantage. This theory holds that if one country is better at producing a certain product and another is better at producing a different one, each should devote resources to its strength. Countries should trade with one another rather than erect barriers that force them into less productive activities. Under this framework, tariffs drag on economic growth, even if they benefit narrow sectors in specific circumstances. [10]

**Note:** These two approaches -- free trade based on comparative advantage, and protectionist trade based on a zero-sum worldview -- have fluctuated in popularity over the centuries.

Relatively free trade flourished in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. World War I shattered the optimistic notion that international commerce had made large-scale war obsolete. Nationalist trade policies, including high tariffs, dominated until after World War II. [11]

From the postwar period onward, free trade enjoyed a 50-year resurgence, culminating in the creation of the World Trade Organization (WTO) in 1995. [12] Free trade agreements such as NAFTA (now the USMCA) and the European Union proliferated. [13]

However, skepticism of this model -- sometimes labeled neoliberalism by critics -- has grown. The United Kingdom voted to leave the EU in 2016. [14] That same year, Donald Trump won the U.S. presidential election on a platform that included tariffs on Chinese and Mexican imports. He subsequently imposed tariffs on China but suspended proposed tariffs on Mexico. [15][16]

Critics of tariff-free multilateral agreements span the political spectrum, arguing that such deals erode national sovereignty and encourage a race to the bottom on wages, worker protections, and product quality. Proponents counter that tariffs lead to trade wars, harm consumers, and stifle innovation.

## What Is a Simple Definition of a Tariff?

A tariff is an additional charge that one country imposes on imported goods.

## What Is an Example of a Tariff?

One of the most famous examples in American history is the British tax on tea imported to the American colonies, which precipitated the Boston Tea Party. [17]

## How Do Tariffs Work?

As a surcharge on imported goods, tariffs redirect buyer intent and spending away from the exporting country.

## The Bottom Line

Tariffs have existed in some form for centuries. Trading partners impose tariffs to exert political influence, protect domestic industries and consumers, and advance national objectives.

Regardless of how they are portrayed in the news, tariffs are not inherently negative. They can serve as a means to reopen negotiations between trading partners, provide both sides with an opportunity to express concerns, and even help stabilize a country's markets.
