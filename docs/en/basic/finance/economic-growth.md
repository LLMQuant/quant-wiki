# What Is Economic Growth?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Economic growth refers to an increase in the production of economic goods and services from one period to another. It can be measured in nominal or real terms. Traditionally, aggregate economic growth is measured using gross national product (GNP) or gross domestic product (GDP), although alternative metrics are sometimes employed.

### Key Takeaways

- Economic growth is an increase in the production of goods and services within an economy.
- Increases in capital goods, labor force, technology, and human capital all contribute to economic growth.
- Tax cuts are generally less effective at stimulating economic growth than increases in government spending.
- Economic growth is commonly measured by the increase in the aggregate market value of additional goods and services produced, using estimates such as GDP.

## Understanding Economic Growth

In simple terms, economic growth refers to an increase in aggregate output within an economy, typically manifested as a rise in national income.

In most cases -- though not always -- aggregate increases in output correlate with higher average marginal productivity. This leads to rising incomes, incentivizing consumers to spend more, which in turn drives higher material living standards and quality of life.

In economics, growth is commonly modeled as a function of physical capital, human capital, labor, and technology. Increasing the quantity or quality of the working-age population, the tools available to them, and the methods by which they combine labor, capital, and raw materials all lead to higher economic output.

## Phases of Economic Growth

Economic activity moves through distinct phases. This movement is known as the "business cycle" and consists of four stages:

- **Expansion:** During this phase, employment, income, industrial production, and sales all increase, accompanied by rising real GDP.
- **Peak:** This is the point at which economic expansion reaches its zenith -- a turning point.
- **Contraction:** In this phase, the elements of expansion begin to decline. When a significant drop in economic activity spreads across the economy, it becomes a recession.
- **Trough:** This is the point at which economic contraction reaches its lowest level.

A complete business cycle is measured from peak to peak or trough to trough. These cycles are typically irregular in length, and periods of contraction can occur within expansions, and vice versa.

Since World War II, the U.S. economy has experienced more expansions than contractions. Between 1945 and 2019, the average expansion lasted approximately 65 months, while the average contraction lasted only 11 months. However, the Great Recession from December 2007 to June 2009 lasted 18 months. It was followed by the longest expansion on record, lasting 128 months, until the COVID-19 pandemic struck in 2020. [1]

**Note:** Governments typically attempt to stimulate economic growth by lowering interest rates, which reduces borrowing costs. However, this approach can only be sustained for a limited time. Eventually, as occurred in 2022 and 2023, rates must be raised to combat rising prices and prevent the economy from overheating. [2]

## How to Measure Economic Growth

The most widely used measure of economic growth is real GDP -- the total value of all goods and services produced in an economy, adjusted for inflation. There are three different ways to examine real GDP:

- **Quarterly growth rate (annualized):** This measures the change in GDP from one quarter to the next, then compounds it into an annualized rate. For example, if the quarterly change is 0.3%, the annualized rate would be extrapolated to 1.2%.
- **Four-quarter or year-over-year rate:** This compares GDP in a single quarter with the same quarter in the prior year, expressed as a percentage. Businesses frequently use this approach to offset the effects of seasonal variation.
- **Annual average growth rate:** This is the average of each quarter's change. For instance, if the four quarters of a given year show growth rates of 2%, 3%, 1.5%, and 1%, the annual average growth rate would be 7.5% / 4 = 1.875%.

**Note:** GDP is the most popular measure of economic growth and is calculated by summing all spending by consumers, businesses, and the government over a given period. The formula is: GDP = Consumer Spending + Business Investment + Government Spending + Net Exports.

Measuring the value of goods is inherently complex. Some goods and services are considered more valuable than others -- a smartphone, for example, is worth more than a pair of socks. Growth must be measured by the value of goods and services, not merely their quantity.

Another challenge is that different people assign different values to the same goods and services. A heater is more valuable to a resident of Alaska, while an air conditioner is more valuable to someone in Florida. Some people value steak more than fish, and vice versa.

A common approximation is current market value. In the United States, this is measured in dollars and aggregated to produce overall measures of output, including GDP.

**Important:** There are alternatives to GDP. For example, the World Bank uses gross national income per capita -- which includes income remitted by citizens abroad -- to measure economic growth, classify countries for analytical purposes, and determine borrowing eligibility. [3]

## What Drives Economic Growth

Economic growth depends on four contributing areas:

First is an increase in the quantity of physical capital goods in the economy. Adding capital tends to raise labor productivity. Newer, better, and more numerous tools mean workers can produce more output per unit of time.

As a simple example, a fisherman with a net catches more fish per hour than one with a rod. However, two critical points apply to this process.

Someone in the economy must first engage in some form of saving to free up resources for creating new capital. Furthermore, the new capital must be of the right type, in the right place, and activated at the right time for workers to use it productively.

The second driver of economic growth is technological improvement. The invention of gasoline fuel is one example. Before the discovery of its energy-generating capacity, petroleum had relatively low economic value. That changed when gasoline proved to be a more efficient method of transporting goods.

Improved technology allows workers to produce more output with the same stock of capital goods by combining them in more effective ways. As with capital growth, the rate of technological progress depends heavily on rates of saving and investment, as these are necessary for engaging in research and development.

**Note:** The four factors of production are land and natural resources, labor, capital equipment, and entrepreneurship.

A third driver of economic growth is expanding the labor force. All else being equal, more workers generate more economic goods and services.

During the 19th century, robust U.S. economic growth was partly attributable to the large influx of cheap, productive immigrant labor. However, as with capital-driven growth, there are important caveats. [4]

An increase in the labor force necessarily increases the amount of output that must be consumed to provide for new workers' basic subsistence. New workers must therefore be productive enough to at least offset this requirement rather than being net consumers.

Moreover, as with capital additions, it is essential that the right types of workers flow into the right jobs, in the right locations, and combine with the right complementary capital goods to realize their productive potential.

The final driver is increases in human capital. This means workers become more skilled at their craft, raising their productivity through skills training, trial and error, or simply more practice. Saving, investment, and specialization are the most consistent and controllable methods.

In this context, human capital can also refer to social and institutional capital. Behavioral tendencies toward greater social trust and reciprocity, as well as political or economic innovations such as improved protection of property rights, are all types of human capital that can raise economic productivity.

## Why Economic Growth Matters

In short, economic growth means more people will have access to more goods and services -- which is why governments strive to create it. However, it is not solely about money, goods, and services. Politics also enters the equation, and how economic growth is harnessed to advance social progress is crucial.

According to research conducted by the United Nations University World Institute for Development Economics Research, "most countries that have succeeded in reducing poverty and increasing access to public goods have built this progress on the foundation of strong economic growth." However, the institute notes that growth will not be sustainable if its benefits flow only to an elite group. [5]

## How Taxes Affect Economic Growth

Taxes influence economic growth through their impact on demand, at least in the short term. Tax cuts increase demand by boosting disposable income and encouraging businesses to hire and invest. However, the magnitude of the effect depends on the state of the economy. If the economy is operating near full capacity, the impact may be modest. If the economy is running well below its potential, the effect will be more pronounced. The Congressional Budget Office (CBO) estimates that the impact is roughly three times greater in the latter scenario. [6]

The CBO has also found that tax cuts are generally less effective at stimulating economic growth than increases in government spending. This is because most spending directly stimulates demand, whereas tax cuts stimulate both saving and demand. One way to mitigate this effect is to target tax cuts at low- and middle-income households, which are less likely to channel the money into savings. [6]

## Alternative Terms for Economic Growth

Other words and terms for economic growth include "boom," "economic development," "economic upturn," "economic surge," "industrial development," and "economic vitality."

## Conclusion

Economic growth occurs when the production of goods and services increases in a given period compared with a prior period. It is typically measured by GDP and serves as an indicator of a nation's economic health. However, the extent to which the fruits of growth are broadly shared is a critical factor in its sustainability -- not to mention social health and progress.
