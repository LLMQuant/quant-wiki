# What Is an Annual Return?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

An annual return is the return that an investment generates over a period, expressed as a time-weighted annual percentage. Sources of return can include dividends, capital distributions, and capital appreciation. The annual return is measured relative to the initial investment amount and represents a geometric mean rather than a simple arithmetic average.

### Key Takeaways

- The annual return, or annualized return, measures how much an investment has grown on average per year over a given period.
- The annualized return is computed using a geometric mean, which captures the compounding effect of yearly returns.
- Annual returns are more useful than simple returns when comparing the performance of investments over time or evaluating one investment against another.
- Annual returns can be applied to a wide range of assets, including stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs, commodities, and certain derivatives.

## Understanding Annual Returns

The annual return is applicable to a broad array of assets, including stocks, bonds, funds, commodities, and certain types of derivatives. It is the de facto standard for comparing the performance of liquid investments and is considered more accurate because it adjusts for compounding. Different asset classes typically exhibit different annual return profiles.

## Annual Returns for Stocks

The annual return, also called the annualized return, expresses the increase in a stock's value over a specified time period. The calculation requires the stock's current price and its purchase price. If any stock splits have occurred, the purchase price must be adjusted accordingly. Once prices are established, the simple return percentage is computed first and then annualized.

The simple return is calculated as the current price minus the purchase price, divided by the purchase price.

## Annual Return Calculation Example

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{CAGR} = \left ( \left ( \frac{ \text{Ending Value} }{ \text{Beginning Value} } \right ) ^ \frac 1 { \text{Years} } \right ) - 1 \\ &\textbf{where:} \\ &\text{CAGR} = \text{Compound Annual Growth Rate} \\ &\text{Years} = \text{Holding period in years} \\ \end{aligned} $$

Suppose an investor purchases a stock on January 1, 2024, at $20 per share and sells it on January 1, 2029, at $35 per share, realizing a $15 gain. Over the five-year holding period, the investor also receives a total of $2 in dividends. The total return over five years is $17, equivalent to 85% of the initial investment ($17 / $20).

The annual return required to achieve an 85% total return over five years can be calculated using the compound annual growth rate (CAGR) formula:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\left ( \left ( \frac { 37 }{ 20 } \right ) ^ \frac 1 { 5 } \right ) - 1 = 13.1\% \text{ annual return} \\ \end{aligned} $$

The annualized return differs from a typical average in that it reflects the true gains or losses of an investment while also highlighting the difficulty of recovering from losses. If an initial investment loses 50%, a 100% gain is required in the following year just to break even. The annualized return smooths outcomes to facilitate more meaningful comparisons, since the range of possible gains and losses can vary enormously.

**Note:** Annual return statistics are commonly cited in marketing materials for mutual funds, ETFs, and other individual securities.

## Annual Returns for 401(k) Accounts

Calculating the annual return on a 401(k) account for a specific year requires a different approach. First, compute the total return by identifying the beginning and ending values for the observation period. Any contributions made during the period must be subtracted from the ending value before proceeding.

After determining the adjusted ending value, divide it by the beginning balance. Then subtract 1 from the result and multiply by 100 to express the total return as a percentage.

## What Is the Modified Dietz Formula?

The Modified Dietz formula is a method of calculating annual returns that accounts for cash flows. It compounds returns within each period.

## Are There Other Methods for Calculating Annual Returns?

Returns can be calculated on a monthly basis and then multiplied by 12 to obtain an annualized figure. Numerous online calculators are available to perform these computations.

## How Do You Calculate the Overall Return on an Investment?

To calculate the return on investment (ROI), subtract the initial cost of the investment from its final value. Then divide the result by the total cost of the investment, including fees, commissions, and markups. Multiply by 100 to express it as a percentage.

## Conclusion

Calculating the annual return reveals how much an investor has gained or lost on a particular investment. For investors seeking growth in stocks, bonds, or mutual funds, this metric is essential for comparing the performance of liquid investments over time.

If you are uncertain whether you are calculating annual returns correctly, consulting a financial professional can help ensure you are well-positioned for your next investment decision.
