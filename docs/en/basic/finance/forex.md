# What Is Forex (FX)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Forex (FX) refers to the global electronic marketplace for trading international currencies and currency derivatives. Although it has no central physical location, the forex market is the largest and most liquid market in the world, with trillions of dollars changing hands each day. Most of the trading is conducted through banks, brokers, and financial institutions.

The forex market is open 24 hours a day, five days a week, excluding holidays. Although many equity markets close on holidays, the forex market remains open, though trading volumes may be lower.

The name "forex" is a portmanteau of "foreign" and "exchange," commonly abbreviated as "FX."

### Key Takeaways

- The forex market is a global electronic network for currency trading.
- Formerly limited to governments and financial institutions, individuals can now buy and sell currencies directly on the forex market.
- In the forex market, profits or losses result from the difference between the prices at which a trader buys and sells a currency pair.
- Forex traders do not deal in cash. Brokers generally roll over their positions at the end of each trading day.

## Understanding Forex (FX)

Forex traders use various analysis techniques to find the best entry and exit points. Forex exists so that large quantities of one currency can be exchanged for the equivalent value in another currency at the current market rate.

Some of these transactions occur because financial institutions, companies, or individuals have a business need to exchange one currency for another. For example, a U.S. company might exchange dollars for yen to pay for goods ordered from Japan and invoiced in yen.

A great deal of forex trading is speculative, aimed at profiting from movements in the value of currencies. Traders profit from price movements in specific currency pairs.

Currencies are listed in pairs, such as USD/CAD, EUR/USD, or USD/JPY. These represent the U.S. dollar (USD) versus the Canadian dollar (CAD), the euro (EUR) versus the USD, and the USD versus the Japanese yen (JPY).

Each pair has an associated price, for example 1.2569. If this price is associated with the USD/CAD pair, it means it costs 1.2569 CAD to buy one USD. If the price rises to 1.3336, it now costs 1.3336 CAD to buy one USD. The USD has strengthened (the CAD has weakened) because it now costs more CAD to buy one USD.

In the forex market, currencies are traded in lot sizes known as micro, mini, and standard lots. A micro lot is 1,000 units of a given currency, a mini lot is 10,000 units, and a standard lot is 100,000 units.

These are clearly much larger trades than going to a bank to exchange $500 for a trip. In the electronic forex market, trades are set up in blocks of currency that can be traded in any volume, up to what the individual trading account allows. For example, you can trade seven micro lots (7,000), three mini lots (30,000), or 75 standard lots (7,500,000).

The forex market has several unique characteristics, chief among them its sheer size. Daily forex trading volume exceeds $5 trillion, compared with approximately $200 billion in daily equity trading volume.

The largest forex trading centers are located in the world's major financial hubs, including London, New York, Singapore, Tokyo, Frankfurt, Hong Kong, and Sydney.

The forex market is open 24 hours a day, five days a week, across major global financial centers, meaning you can buy and sell currencies at virtually any time.

Historically, forex trading was largely limited to governments, large corporations, and hedge funds. Today, anyone can trade forex. Many investment companies, banks, and retail brokers allow individuals to open accounts and trade currencies.

When you trade in the forex market, you are buying or selling the currency of a particular country relative to another currency. There is no physical exchange of money from one party to another as there would be at a foreign exchange kiosk.

In the electronic marketplace, traders typically hold a position in a specific currency, hoping that the currency they bought will appreciate (or that the currency they sold will depreciate), in order to make a profit.

A currency is always traded relative to another currency. If you sell one currency, you are buying another; if you buy one currency, you are selling another. The profit is made from the difference between the transaction prices.

Spot market trades are for immediate delivery, with delivery defined as two business days for most currency pairs. The major exception is the purchase or sale of USD/CAD, which settles in one business day.

The business day calculation excludes Saturdays, Sundays, and legal holidays in either currency of the traded pair. During the Christmas and Easter seasons, some spot trades can take up to six days to settle. Funds are exchanged on the settlement date, not the transaction date.

The U.S. dollar is the most actively traded currency. The euro is the most active counterpart currency, followed by the Japanese yen, British pound, and Chinese renminbi.

Market moves are driven by a combination of speculation, economic strength and growth, and interest rate differentials.

Retail traders typically do not want to take delivery of the currencies they buy. They are only interested in profiting from the difference in trading prices. Because of this, most retail brokers automatically "roll over" their currency positions at 5:00 p.m. EST each day.

The broker essentially resets the positions and provides a credit or debit for the interest rate differential between the two currencies in the held pair. The trade continues and the trader does not need to deliver or settle the transaction.

When the trade is closed, the trader realizes a profit or loss based on the original transaction price and the price at which the trade was closed. The rollover credit or debit may add to or detract from this gain.

Since the forex market is closed on Saturday and Sunday, the interest rate credit or debit from those days is applied on Wednesday. Therefore, holding a position at 5:00 p.m. on Wednesday will result in being credited or debited triple the usual amount.

Any forex transaction that settles after the spot date is considered a forward. The price is calculated by adjusting the spot rate to account for the difference in interest rates between the two currencies. The adjustment amount is called "forward points."

Forward points reflect only the interest rate differential between two markets. They are not a forecast of how the spot market will trade at a future date.

A forward contract is a tailor-made agreement that can be for any amount and can settle on any date that is not a weekend or holiday. As with spot transactions, funds are exchanged on the settlement date.

A forex or currency futures contract is an agreement between two parties to deliver a set amount of currency at a future date, known as the expiry date. Futures contracts are traded on exchanges at standardized currency values and settlement dates.

Unlike forwards, the terms of a futures contract are non-negotiable. Profit is made from the difference between the prices at which the contract is bought and sold.

Most speculators do not hold futures contracts until expiry, as that would require them to deliver or settle the currency the contract represents. Instead, they buy and sell contracts before expiry, realizing their profits or losses.

## How Forex Differs from Other Markets

There are several fundamental differences between the forex market and other markets such as the U.S. stock market.

This means that investors are not held to the same strict standards or regulations as those in the stock, futures, or options markets. There are no clearinghouses and no central bodies overseeing the entire forex market. You can short-sell at any time because in forex you are never truly short -- if you sell one currency, you are buying another.

Since the market is unregulated, fees and commissions vary widely across brokers. Most forex brokers make money by marking up the spread on currency pairs, while others charge commissions that fluctuate based on the amount of currency traded. Some brokers use both approaches.

There is no cutoff limiting when you can and cannot trade. Because the market is open 24 hours a day, five days a week, you can trade at any time. The exception is weekends or when no global financial center is open due to a holiday.

The forex market allows leverage of up to 50:1 in the United States and even higher in some parts of the world. This means that a trader can open an account with $1,000 and buy or sell up to $50,000 worth of currency. Leverage is a double-edged sword; it magnifies both profits and losses.

## Forex Trading Example

Suppose a trader believes the euro will appreciate against the U.S. dollar. In other words, the USD will weaken against the EUR.

The trader buys EUR/USD at a price of 1.2500 for $5,000 worth of currency. Later that day, the price increases to 1.2550. The trader is up $25 (5,000 * 0.0050). If the price dropped to 1.2430, the trader would be down $35 (5,000 * 0.0070).

Currency prices are constantly fluctuating, so the trader might decide to hold the position overnight. The broker will roll over the position, resulting in a credit or debit based on the interest rate differential between the eurozone and the United States.

If the eurozone has an interest rate of 4% and the U.S. has a rate of 3%, the trader holds the higher-interest-rate currency in this example. Therefore, at rollover, the trader should receive a small credit. If the euro interest rate were lower than the U.S. dollar rate, the trader would be debited at rollover.

Rollover can affect a trading decision, especially if the trade could be held for the long term. Large differences in interest rates can result in significant daily credits or debits, which can greatly enhance or erode the profits (or increase or reduce the losses) of the trade.

Most brokers provide leverage. Many U.S. brokers offer leverage up to 50:1. Suppose our trader uses 10:1 leverage for this transaction. If using 10:1 leverage, the trader does not need to have $5,000 in the account despite trading $5,000 in currency. Only $500 is required.

In this example, given that the trader needs only $500 or $250 of trading capital (or even less with higher leverage), the $25 profit can be realized quickly. This shows the power of leverage. The flip side is that the trader could just as quickly lose capital.

## Is Forex Trading Suitable for Beginners?

Forex trading can be risky and complex, involving rapid decision-making driven by fast-moving exchange rates. It may therefore not be suitable for beginners; however, traders can learn forex trading through practice accounts or by starting with a small amount of capital.

## How Much Money Do You Need to Start Forex Trading?

You can start forex trading with as little as $100. This is enough to begin buying and selling currencies. It is also a reasonable amount for beginners since it does not represent a large sum at risk.

## What Are the Risks of Forex Trading?

Forex trading carries many risks. Exchange rates are highly volatile and change frequently, which can quickly impact trades. Additionally, the heavy use of leverage in forex means that even small movements can lead to substantial losses. There is also transaction risk, interest rate risk, and global or country risk.

## Conclusion

Forex is shorthand for foreign exchange, referring to the global trading of currencies and currency derivatives. It is the world's largest financial market, involving the buying and selling of currency pairs to capitalize on movements in exchange rates.
