# What Is a Bond?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
## What Is a Bond?

A bond is a fixed-income instrument in which an investor lends money to a government or corporation at a specified interest rate for a defined period. At maturity, the borrower repays the principal along with accrued interest to the bondholder.

Bonds are widely used by corporations, municipalities, states, and sovereign governments to finance projects and operations. Bondholders are creditors of the issuer. Key terms include the maturity date -- the date on which the principal must be repaid -- and the coupon terms, which may specify a fixed or variable interest rate.

### Key Takeaways

- Bonds are called fixed-income instruments because they traditionally pay a fixed interest rate (coupon) to the holder.
- Bond prices move inversely to interest rates: when rates rise, bond prices fall, and vice versa.
- Bonds have maturity dates at which the full principal must be repaid; failure to do so constitutes default.

## How Bonds Work

A bond is a debt instrument that represents a loan made by an investor to the issuer. Bonds allow individual investors to act as lenders. Governments commonly issue bonds to fund infrastructure such as roads, schools, and dams. Corporations borrow to expand operations, acquire property and equipment, pursue profitable ventures, fund research and development, or hire employees.

Bonds are fixed-income securities and one of the three main asset classes available to individual investors, alongside equities and cash equivalents. The borrower issues a bond that specifies the loan terms, interest payment schedule, and the maturity date at which the principal must be repaid. The interest payment -- part of the return earned by the bondholder -- is determined by the coupon rate.

Most bonds are initially priced at par value, typically $1,000 per bond. The actual market price depends on the issuer's credit quality, time to maturity, and how the coupon rate compares to the prevailing interest rate environment. The face value is the amount repaid to the lender at maturity.

**Note:** The secondary market allows bondholders to sell bonds to other investors or purchase bonds from others -- often long after the original issuance. Bond investors are not required to hold a bond until maturity.

## Characteristics of Bonds

- **Face value (par value):** The bond's value at maturity and the reference amount used by the issuer to calculate interest payments.
- **Coupon rate:** The interest rate paid on the bond's face value, expressed as a percentage.
- **Coupon dates:** The scheduled dates on which interest payments are made.
- **Maturity date:** The date on which the bond matures and the issuer repays the face value.
- **Issue price:** The price at which the bond is originally sold. In many cases, bonds are issued at par.

## Types of Bonds

There are four primary categories of bonds in the market. Investors may also encounter foreign bonds issued by international corporations and governments on certain platforms.

- **Corporate bonds:** Companies issue corporate bonds rather than seeking bank loans because bond markets often offer more favorable terms and lower interest rates.
- **Municipal bonds:** Issued by states and municipalities. Some municipal bonds offer tax-exempt coupon income to investors.
- **Government bonds:** Securities issued by the U.S. Treasury. Those maturing in one year or less are called "bills," one to ten years are "notes," and maturities beyond ten years are "bonds." The entire category is commonly referred to as "Treasuries."
- **Agency bonds:** Bonds issued by government-affiliated organizations such as Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac.

## Bond Prices and Interest Rates

Bond prices fluctuate daily based on supply and demand. An investor who holds a bond to maturity receives the principal plus interest. However, bonds can be sold on the open market at varying prices. Bond prices move inversely to interest rates: when rates rise, bond prices fall to equalize the bond's yield with prevailing market rates, and vice versa.

A fixed-rate bond issuer promises to pay a coupon based on the bond's face value. For example, a $1,000 bond with a 10% annual coupon pays the holder $100 per year. If market rates are also 10% at issuance, investors are indifferent between corporate and government bonds, as both yield $100. However, if rates fall to 5%, a government bond only pays $50, while the corporate bond still pays $100.

Investors will bid the corporate bond's price up until it trades at a premium -- approximately $2,000 -- at which point the $100 coupon equates to a 5% yield. Conversely, if rates spike to 15%, the government bond pays $150, and no one would pay $1,000 for only $100 in annual income. The bond would sell at a discount until the yield equalizes, around $666.67.

## Yield to Maturity (YTM)

Yield to maturity (YTM) represents the total expected return if the bond is held until maturity. It is considered a long-term bond yield expressed as an annual rate.

YTM is the internal rate of return earned by an investor who holds the bond to maturity with all payments made on schedule. It is used to compare bonds with different coupons and maturities. The YTM calculation involves solving for the discount rate that equates the present value of future cash flows to the bond's current price.

Investors can measure the sensitivity of a bond's price to interest rate changes using duration. Duration estimates the percentage change in a bond's price for a 1% change in interest rates. This measure is formally known as modified duration. Bonds with longer maturities or lower coupon rates are most sensitive to interest rate changes.

## Varieties of Bonds

A wide range of bond types is available to investors, depending on the interest rate structure, callability, and other features.

- **Zero-coupon bonds (Z-bonds):** Do not pay periodic interest. Instead, they are issued at a discount to face value and redeemed at par upon maturity. U.S. Treasury bills are an example.
- **Convertible bonds:** Debt instruments with an embedded option allowing the bondholder to convert the bond into equity (stock) under specified conditions.
- **Callable bonds:** Feature an embedded call option that allows the issuer to redeem the bond before maturity. Callable bonds carry higher risk for investors because the issuer is more likely to call the bond when its value rises.
- **Puttable bonds:** Allow the bondholder to sell the bond back to the issuer before maturity. This feature is valuable to investors concerned about declining bond values or rising interest rates. Puttable bonds typically trade at a premium relative to otherwise identical bonds without the put feature.

## Determinants of a Bond's Coupon Rate

Two primary factors determine a bond's coupon rate: credit quality and time to maturity. If the issuer has a lower credit rating, the risk of default is higher, and these bonds compensate investors with higher interest rates. Longer-maturity bonds also typically pay higher coupons because the bondholder faces greater interest rate and inflation risk over the extended holding period.

## How Are Bonds Rated?

Bond credit ratings are produced by agencies such as Standard & Poor's (S&P), Moody's, and Fitch Ratings. The highest-quality bonds are classified as "investment grade," including debt issued by the U.S. government and highly stable corporations such as major utilities. Bonds that are not investment grade but have not defaulted are called "high-yield" or "junk" bonds. These carry a higher risk of future default, and investors demand higher coupon payments to compensate for that risk.

## What Is Duration?

As interest rates change, bond and portfolio values rise or fall. Sensitivity to changes in the interest rate environment is called "duration." The term can be confusing for new bond investors because it does not refer to the time remaining until maturity. Rather, duration describes how much a bond's price will rise or fall in response to a change in interest rates.

## The Bottom Line

Bonds are instruments issued by corporations and governments to finance projects and operations. Because bonds traditionally pay a fixed interest rate to creditors, they are classified as fixed-income instruments. Investors can purchase corporate bonds through financial institutions or online brokers, and government bonds through TreasuryDirect.gov.
