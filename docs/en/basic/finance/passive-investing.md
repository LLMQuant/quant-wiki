# Passive Investing: A Strategy to Maximize Returns by Minimizing Trading
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Passive investing is an investment strategy that seeks to maximize returns by minimizing buying and selling activity. Index investing is a common passive strategy in which investors purchase a representative benchmark -- such as the S&P 500 -- and hold it over a long time horizon. Passive investing stands in contrast to active investing.

### Key Takeaways

- Passive investing broadly refers to a buy-and-hold portfolio strategy designed for long-term investment horizons with minimal market trading.
- Index investing is perhaps the most common form of passive investing, in which investors seek to replicate and hold a broad market index.
- Compared with actively managed portfolios, passive investing tends to be lower in cost, less complex, and often produces superior after-tax returns over medium- to long-term periods.

## Understanding Passive Investing

Passive investing methods aim to avoid the fees and limited performance that can result from frequent trading. The goal of passive investing is to build wealth gradually. Also known as a buy-and-hold strategy, passive investing means purchasing securities and holding them for extended periods. Unlike active traders, passive investors do not seek to profit from short-term price fluctuations or market timing. The underlying assumption of a passive investment strategy is that the market will produce positive returns over the long term.

Passive managers generally believe that outperforming the market is difficult, so they aim to match overall market or sector performance. Passive investing attempts to replicate market performance by constructing well-diversified portfolios of individual stocks -- which, if researched individually, would require extensive analysis. The introduction of index funds in the 1970s made it much easier to achieve market-matching returns. In the 1990s, exchange-traded funds (ETFs) that track major indices -- such as the SPDR S&P 500 ETF (SPY) -- further simplified the process by allowing investors to trade index funds as easily as stocks.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Passive Investing

Maintaining a well-diversified portfolio is essential to successful investing, and diversification through index investing is an accessible approach. Index funds spread risk broadly by holding representative securities from their target benchmark. Because index funds track a target benchmark or index rather than seeking winners, they avoid constant buying and selling of securities. As a result, their fees and operating costs are typically lower than those of actively managed funds.

As an investment approach, index funds are straightforward because they simply seek to track an index. There is no need to select and monitor individual fund managers or choose among investment themes.

However, passive investing is subject to total market risk. Index funds track the entire market, so when overall stock or bond prices fall, index funds follow suit. Another risk is the lack of flexibility. Index fund managers are generally unable to employ defensive measures such as reducing equity positions, even when they believe stock prices will decline. Passively managed index funds face performance constraints because they are designed to deliver returns closely aligned with their benchmark index rather than seeking outperformance. They rarely beat the index and typically return slightly less due to operating costs.

Some key advantages of passive investing include:

- **Ultra-low fees:** No one is picking stocks, so oversight costs are significantly reduced. Passive funds simply follow the index that serves as their benchmark.
- **Transparency:** The assets held in an index fund are always clearly visible.
- **Tax efficiency:** The buy-and-hold strategy typically does not trigger large annual capital gains taxes.
- **Simplicity:** Holding an index or a set of indices is far easier to implement and understand than a dynamic strategy that requires constant research and adjustment.

Proponents of active investing would argue that passive strategies have the following weaknesses:

- **Too restrictive:** Passive funds are locked into a specific index or predetermined set of investments with little variation. Investors are committed to these holdings regardless of market conditions.
- **Smaller potential returns:** By definition, passive funds will almost never beat the market, even during turbulent times, because their core holdings are designed to track it. A passive fund may occasionally edge ahead of the market slightly, but it will never deliver the outsized returns that active managers seek -- unless the market itself is booming. Active managers, however, can potentially deliver larger returns (see below), though these come with commensurately higher risk.

**Tip:** Fund fees vary widely. Actively managed funds typically have higher operating costs than passively managed funds, but it is always important to examine fees before selecting an investment fund.

For comparison, active investing has its own pros and cons:

- **Flexibility:** Active managers are not required to follow a specific index. They can buy stocks they view as exceptional opportunities.
- **Hedging:** Active managers can use techniques such as short selling or put options to hedge risk, and they can exit specific stocks or sectors when risk becomes too great. Passive investors must hold whatever stocks the index contains, regardless of performance.
- **Tax management:** Although this strategy can trigger capital gains taxes, advisors can tailor tax management strategies to individual investors -- for example, by selling losing investments to offset the taxes on big winners.

But active strategies also come with these drawbacks:

- **Very high fees:** Active buying and selling generates transaction costs, on top of the salaries paid to analyst teams who research stock picks. Over decades of investing, these fees can erode returns. In 2020, actively managed mutual funds charged an average expense ratio of 0.71%, compared with just 0.06% for passive funds.
- **Active risk:** Active managers are free to buy any investment they believe will deliver high returns -- which is great when analysts are right, but costly when they are wrong.
- **Poor track record:** The data show that very few actively managed portfolios beat their passive benchmarks, especially after accounting for taxes and fees. In fact, over medium- to long-term time horizons, only a handful of actively managed mutual funds outperform their benchmark indices.

## How to Start Passive Investing

Purchasing index funds is a common passive investment strategy. Index funds are designed to mirror the activity of a market index, such as the Russell 2000 Index. By reducing trading frequency, index funds aim to achieve maximum returns over the long term.

Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are another popular choice for passive investors. ETFs can be passively or actively managed. Index-based ETFs, like index funds, track a securities index.

## What Are the Costs Associated with Passive Investing?

Passive investing is typically less expensive than active investing because fund managers are not picking stocks or bonds. Passive funds allow a given index to guide which securities are traded, meaning there are no additional research analyst costs.

Even passively managed funds charge fees, however. Investigating the associated costs is essential when deciding which fund to invest in.

## How Do Return Expectations Compare for Passive vs. Active Investing?

Active investing aims to drive returns through frequent trading, but those returns are diminished by the fees associated with professional management and frequent buying and selling. Research shows that few actively managed funds are able to deliver above-benchmark returns to investors over extended periods.

Passive investing targets strong long-term returns by minimizing the frequency of buying and selling, but it is unlikely to beat the market and generate excess returns in the short term. Active investing can potentially deliver larger returns but also carries higher risk than passive investing.

## Conclusion

Passive investing has both advantages and disadvantages compared with active investing. The strategy can deliver lower fees and greater tax efficiency, but may offer limited short-term returns and will generally underperform active investing in absolute terms. For investors seeking lower-risk returns over a longer time horizon, passive investing is an attractive option.
