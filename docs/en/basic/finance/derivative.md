# What Is a Derivative?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The term "derivative" refers to a financial contract whose value depends on an underlying asset, group of assets, or benchmark. A derivative is set between two or more parties who can trade it either on an exchange or over the counter (OTC).

These contracts can be used to trade any number of assets and carry their own risks. Derivative prices are derived from fluctuations in the underlying asset. These financial securities are commonly used to gain exposure to specific markets and may be traded to hedge risk. Derivatives can be used to mitigate risk (hedging) or to assume risk in expectation of commensurate reward (speculation). They can transfer risk -- and the accompanying returns -- from the risk-averse to the risk-seeking.

### Key Takeaways

- Derivatives are financial contracts set between two or more parties whose value is derived from an underlying asset, group of assets, or benchmark.
- Derivatives can trade on an exchange or over the counter.
- Derivative prices are derived from fluctuations in the underlying asset.
- Derivatives are typically leveraged instruments, which amplifies their potential risks and rewards.
- Common derivatives include futures contracts, forwards, options, and swaps.

## Understanding Derivatives

A derivative is a complex type of financial security set between two or more parties. Derivatives can take many forms, from stock and bond derivatives to economic indicator derivatives.

Traders use derivatives to access specific markets and trade different assets. Derivatives are generally considered a form of advanced investing. The most common underlying assets are stocks, bonds, commodities, currencies, interest rates, and market indices. Contract values depend on changes in the price of the underlying asset (the primary instrument).

Derivatives can be used to hedge a position, speculate on the directional movement of an underlying asset, or add leverage to holdings. They are commonly traded on exchanges or OTC and purchased through brokerage firms. The Chicago Mercantile Exchange (CME) is one of the world's largest derivatives exchanges. [1]

It is important to remember that when companies hedge, they are not speculating on the price of a commodity. Rather, hedging is simply a way for each party in a transaction to manage risk. Each party has its profit or margin built into the price, and hedging helps protect those profits from being eroded by adverse commodity price movements.

OTC-traded derivatives generally carry greater counterparty risk -- the danger that one of the parties involved in a transaction may default. These contracts trade between two private parties and are unregulated. To hedge this risk, an investor can purchase a currency derivative to lock in a specific exchange rate. Derivatives that can be used to hedge such exposure include currency futures and currency swaps.

**Important:** Exchange-traded derivatives are more standardized and more strictly regulated than OTC derivatives.

## Special Considerations

Derivatives were originally used to ensure balanced exchange rates for internationally traded goods. International traders needed a system to account for the different values of national currencies.

Suppose a European investor has investment accounts denominated entirely in euros (EUR). They use U.S. dollars (USD) to purchase shares of a U.S. company through a U.S. exchange. This exposes them to exchange rate risk -- specifically, the risk that the euro appreciates against the dollar. If that happens, any profits realized from selling the stock will be worth less when converted back to euros.

A speculator who expects the euro to appreciate against the dollar could profit by using a derivative that rises in value as the euro strengthens. When using derivatives to speculate on the price movement of an underlying asset, the investor does not need to hold a position in or a portfolio of the underlying asset.

**Note:** Many derivative instruments are leveraged, meaning that a small amount of capital can control a large amount of value in the underlying asset.

## Types of Derivatives

Today, derivatives are based on a wide variety of transactions and have far more uses. There are even derivatives based on weather data, such as rainfall or the number of sunny days in a particular region.

Many different types of derivatives can be used for risk management, speculation, and leveraged positions. The derivatives market continues to grow, offering products to suit nearly any need or risk tolerance.

Derivative products fall into two categories: "lock" and "option." Lock products (such as futures, forwards, or swaps) bind the respective parties to the agreed-upon terms throughout the life of the contract. Option products (such as stock options) give the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell the underlying asset or security at a specific price on or before the expiration date. The most common types of derivatives are futures, forwards, swaps, and options.

**Futures** contracts -- or simply futures -- are agreements between two parties to buy and deliver an asset at an agreed-upon price on a future date. Futures are standardized contracts that trade on exchanges. Traders use futures to hedge risk or speculate on the price of an underlying asset. The parties are obligated to fulfill the commitment to buy or sell the underlying asset.

For example, suppose on November 6, 2021, Company A purchases an oil futures contract at $62.22 per barrel, expiring December 19, 2021. The company does this because it needs oil in December and is concerned that prices will rise before it needs to make the purchase. Buying an oil futures contract hedges the company's risk because the seller is obligated to deliver oil at $62.22 per barrel upon contract expiration. If oil prices rise to $80 per barrel by December 19, 2021, Company A can accept delivery from the futures seller. If it no longer needs the oil, it can sell the contract before expiration and keep the profit.

In this example, both the futures buyer and seller hedge their risk. Company A needed oil in the future and wanted to offset the risk of a December price increase by going long on an oil futures contract. The seller may be an oil company concerned about falling oil prices, seeking to eliminate that risk by selling -- or shorting -- a futures contract that fixes the price it will receive in December.

It is also possible that one or both parties are speculators with opposing views on the direction of December oil prices. In that case, one party benefits from the contract and the other does not. For example, consider the West Texas Intermediate (WTI) crude oil futures contract, which trades on the CME and represents 1,000 barrels of oil. If oil prices rise from $62.22 to $80 per barrel, the long position (buyer) profits by $17,780 [($80 - $62.22) x 1,000 = $17,780]. The short position (seller) loses $17,780.

Not all futures contracts are settled by delivery of the underlying asset at expiration. If both parties are speculative investors or traders, neither is likely to arrange for delivery of large quantities of crude oil. Speculators can end their obligation to buy or deliver the underlying commodity by closing (unwinding) their contract with an offsetting contract before expiration.

In fact, many derivatives are cash-settled, meaning the gain or loss in the trade is simply an accounting cash flow in the trader's brokerage account. Cash-settled futures include many interest rate futures, stock index futures, and more unusual instruments such as volatility futures or weather futures.

**Forwards** -- or forward contracts -- are similar to futures but do not trade on an exchange. These contracts trade only OTC. When a forward contract is created, the buyer and seller can customize the terms, size, and settlement process. As OTC products, forwards carry greater counterparty risk for both parties.

Counterparty risk is a form of credit risk -- the possibility that a party may be unable to meet the obligations outlined in the contract. If one party becomes insolvent, the other may have no recourse and could lose the value of its position.

Once created, the parties in a forward contract can offset their position with other counterparties, which can increase the potential for counterparty risk as more traders become involved in the same contract.

**Swaps** are another common type of derivative, often used to exchange one kind of cash flow for another. For example, a trader might use an interest rate swap to switch from a floating-rate loan to a fixed-rate loan, or vice versa.

Suppose XYZ Company borrows $1,000,000 and pays a variable interest rate currently at 6%. XYZ may be concerned that rising rates will increase the cost of the loan, or may encounter a lender that is unwilling to extend additional credit while the company carries this floating-rate exposure.

Suppose XYZ creates a swap with QRS Company, which is willing to exchange the payments owed on the variable-rate loan for the payments owed on a fixed-rate loan at 7%. This means XYZ will pay QRS 7% on its $1,000,000 principal, and QRS will pay XYZ 6% interest on the same principal. At the inception of the swap, XYZ pays QRS only the 1-percentage-point difference between the two swap rates.

If rates fall so that the variable rate on the original loan is now 5%, XYZ must pay QRS the 2-percentage-point difference on the loan. If rates rise to 8%, QRS must pay XYZ the 1-percentage-point difference between the two swap rates. Regardless of how rates change, the swap achieves XYZ's original objective of converting a variable-rate loan into a fixed-rate loan.

Swaps can also be structured to exchange currency exchange rate risk or the risk of loan default, or cash flows from other business activities. Swaps related to the cash flows and potential defaults of mortgage bonds were an extremely popular type of derivative -- in fact, they were too popular. It was the counterparty risk of this type of swap that ultimately escalated into the 2008 credit crisis.

**Options** contracts are similar to futures in that they are agreements between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a future date. The key difference is that with an option, the buyer is not obligated to exercise the agreement. It represents an opportunity, not an obligation -- unlike a futures contract. As with futures, options can be used to hedge or speculate on the price of the underlying asset.

**Tip:** Regarding the timing of when you can exercise your rights, it depends on the option "style." American-style options allow the holder to exercise at any time up to and including the expiration date. European-style options can be exercised only on the expiration date. Most stocks and ETFs have American-style options, while equity indices -- including the S&P 500 -- have European-style options.

Suppose an investor owns 100 shares of a stock valued at $50 per share. They believe the stock's value will rise in the future but are concerned about potential downside risk and decide to hedge with an option. The investor can purchase a put option, which grants the right to sell 100 shares of the underlying stock at $50 per share (the strike price) until a specified future date (the expiration date).

Suppose the stock falls to $40 per share by expiration and the put option buyer decides to exercise the option, selling the shares at the original $50 strike price. If the put option cost $200, the investor's only loss is the premium paid, because the strike price equals the price at which the stock was originally purchased. A strategy like this is called a protective put because it hedges against downside risk in the stock.

Alternatively, suppose an investor does not currently own the $50 stock but believes its value will rise over the next month. This investor can purchase a call option granting the right to buy the stock at $50 before or at expiration. Suppose the call option costs $200 and the stock rises to $60 before expiration. The buyer can now exercise the option, purchasing shares worth $60 at the $50 strike price, yielding an initial profit of $10 per share. A call option represents 100 shares, so the actual profit is $1,000, minus the cost of the option (the premium) and any brokerage commissions.

In both examples, the seller is obligated to fulfill their side of the contract if the buyer chooses to exercise. However, if the stock price is above the strike price at expiration, the put expires worthless and the seller (option writer) keeps the premium, as the option will not be exercised. If the stock price is below the strike price at expiration, the call expires worthless and the call seller keeps the premium.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Derivatives

As the examples above illustrate, derivatives can be useful tools for both businesses and investors. They provide a way to:

- Lock in prices
- Hedge against adverse rate movements
- Mitigate risk

These benefits often come at a limited cost.

Derivatives can also frequently be purchased on margin, meaning traders use borrowed funds to acquire them, making them even more affordable.

However, derivatives are difficult to value because they are based on the price of another asset. The risks of OTC derivatives include counterparty risk that is difficult to predict or estimate. Most derivatives are also sensitive to:

- Changes in time to expiration
- The cost of holding the underlying asset
- Interest rates

These variables make it difficult to perfectly match a derivative's value with its underlying asset.

Because derivatives have no intrinsic value -- their value comes solely from the underlying asset -- they are vulnerable to market sentiment and market risk. Supply and demand factors can cause a derivative's price and liquidity to rise and fall independently of what is happening to the price of the underlying asset.

Finally, derivatives are typically leveraged instruments, and leverage cuts both ways. While it can amplify returns, it also accelerates losses.

### Pros

- Lock in prices
- Hedge risk
- Can be leveraged
- Diversify portfolios

### Cons

- Difficult to value
- Subject to counterparty default (if OTC)
- Complex to understand
- Sensitive to supply and demand factors

## What Are Derivatives?

Derivatives are securities whose value depends on, or is derived from, an underlying asset. For example, an oil futures contract is a derivative whose value is based on the market price of oil. Derivatives have become increasingly popular in recent decades, with the total value of outstanding derivatives estimated at $610 trillion as of June 30, 2021. [3]

## What Are Some Examples of Derivatives?

Common examples include futures contracts, options contracts, and credit default swaps. Beyond these, there is a vast array of derivative contracts tailored to the needs of various counterparties. In fact, since many derivatives trade OTC, they can in principle be infinitely customized.

## What Are the Main Benefits and Risks of Derivatives?

Derivatives can be a very convenient way to achieve financial objectives. For example, a company looking to hedge its commodity exposure can do so by buying or selling energy derivatives such as crude oil futures. Similarly, a company can hedge its currency risk by purchasing currency forward contracts. Derivatives can also help investors leverage their positions -- for example, by purchasing equities through stock options rather than shares. The main drawbacks of derivatives include counterparty risk, the inherent risks of leverage, and the fact that complex webs of derivative contracts can lead to systemic risk.
