# What Is the Price-to-Earnings (P/E) Ratio?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio measures a company's current stock price relative to its earnings per share (EPS). Often referred to as the price multiple or earnings multiple, the P/E ratio helps assess the relative value of a company's stock. It is useful for comparing a company's valuation against its own historical performance, against peers within the same industry, or against the broader market.

The P/E ratio can be estimated on either a historical (trailing) or forward (projected) basis.

### Key Takeaways

- The price-to-earnings (P/E) ratio relates a company's share price to its earnings per share.
- A high P/E ratio may indicate that a stock is overvalued or that investors expect high growth rates.
- Companies with no earnings or negative earnings have no P/E ratio, as there is nothing to put in the denominator.
- The two most commonly used forms are the forward P/E and trailing P/E.
- The P/E ratio is most valuable when comparing similar companies within the same industry or evaluating a single company over time.

## P/E Ratio Formula and Calculation

The formula is as follows:

$$ \text{P/E Ratio} = \frac{\text{Market Value per Share}}{\text{Earnings per Share}} $$

To determine the P/E ratio, divide the stock price by earnings per share (EPS).

The stock price (P) can be found by searching for a company's ticker symbol on any reputable financial website. While this figure reflects what investors are currently paying for the stock, EPS is tied to earnings reported over different time periods.

EPS is generally presented in two ways. Trailing twelve months (TTM) represents the company's performance over the most recent 12-month period. The other form is found in earnings releases, which typically provide EPS guidance -- the company's own forward-looking estimate of future earnings. These different versions of EPS form the basis of the trailing P/E and forward P/E, respectively.

## Understanding the P/E Ratio

The P/E ratio is one of the most widely used metrics for evaluating a stock's relative valuation. It helps determine whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued. A company's P/E can also be benchmarked against other stocks in the same industry or the broader market, such as the S&P 500.

Analysts interested in long-term valuation trends may examine the P/E 10 or P/E 30, which average the past 10 or 30 years of earnings. These measures are commonly used to gauge the overall value of a stock index such as the S&P 500, as they smooth out fluctuations across multiple business cycles.

The S&P 500's P/E ratio has been tracked since 1927, reaching a low of 5.9 in mid-1949 and a high of 122.4 in mid-2009 (following the financial crisis). The long-term average P/E for the S&P 500 is approximately 17.6, meaning the constituent stocks are collectively priced at more than 17 times their weighted average earnings. [1] This average serves as a benchmark for assessing whether the market is valued above or below historical norms.

## Forward P/E

The two most common forms are the forward P/E and trailing P/E. A third, less common variant combines the actual earnings from the two most recent quarters with estimated earnings for the next two quarters.

The forward (or leading) P/E uses projected future earnings rather than trailing figures. Sometimes called the "estimated P/E," this forward-looking indicator helps compare current earnings with future expectations and can illustrate what earnings would look like absent changes and other accounting adjustments.

However, the forward P/E metric has limitations. Companies may underestimate earnings so that they can beat consensus estimates when the next quarter's results are announced. Additionally, external analysts may provide estimates that diverge from the company's own guidance, creating confusion.

## Trailing P/E

The trailing P/E relies on past performance, dividing the current share price by total EPS over the preceding 12 months. It is the most popular P/E metric because it is considered objective -- assuming the company has accurately reported its earnings. However, the trailing P/E has its own drawbacks, including the fact that past performance does not necessarily predict future earnings.

Investors often base purchase decisions on prospective earnings rather than historical results. Using the trailing P/E can be problematic because it relies on a fixed EPS figure while the stock price fluctuates continuously. If a significant event has a positive or negative impact on the stock price, the trailing P/E may not reflect this accurately.

The trailing P/E changes with each movement in the stock price since earnings are released only quarterly while stocks trade continuously during market hours. Consequently, some investors prefer the forward P/E. If the forward P/E is lower than the trailing P/E, analysts expect earnings to increase; if the forward P/E is higher, analysts expect earnings to decline.

## Valuation from the P/E Ratio

Beyond indicating whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued, the P/E ratio reveals how a stock's valuation compares to its industry group or a benchmark like the S&P 500.

The P/E ratio represents how much an investor can expect to invest in a company to receive $1 of that company's earnings. For this reason, it is sometimes called the price multiple, as it shows how much investors are willing to pay per dollar of earnings. If a company trades at a P/E of 20x, investors are paying $20 for every $1 of current earnings.

The P/E ratio also helps investors assess a stock's market value relative to the company's earnings. A high P/E may suggest that the stock's price is elevated relative to earnings and potentially overvalued. Conversely, a low P/E may indicate that the stock price is modest relative to earnings.

## P/E Ratio Example

Consider FedEx Corporation (FDX). As of February 9, 2024, the company's stock closed at $242.62, and its trailing twelve-month EPS was $16.85. [2]

FDX's P/E ratio was therefore approximately 14.4.

Now consider two energy companies for a relative value comparison.

As of the February 9, 2024 close, Hess Corporation (HES) had the following data (using diluted EPS to account for the potential exercise of all convertible securities):

- Stock price: $142.07
- Diluted trailing 12-month EPS: $4.49
- P/E ratio: 31.64 ($142.07 / $4.49) [3]

HES was thus trading at roughly 31 times trailing earnings. However, a P/E of 31 is not meaningful in isolation -- it must be compared against something, such as the stock's industry group, a benchmark index, or HES's own historical P/E range.

HES's P/E was higher than that of the S&P 500, which as of February 9, 2024 stood at approximately 22 times trailing earnings. [4] To compare HES with a peer, consider Marathon Petroleum Corporation (MPC):

- Stock price: $169.97
- Diluted trailing 12-month EPS: $23.64
- P/E ratio: 7.19 [5]

When comparing HES's P/E of 31 with MPC's P/E of 7, HES appears significantly richer relative to both the S&P 500 and MPC. Alternatively, HES's higher P/E could mean that investors expect far greater future earnings growth compared with MPC.

No single ratio tells the full story about a stock. Before investing, it is prudent to employ a variety of financial tools to determine whether a stock is fairly valued.

## Investor Expectations

In general, a high P/E indicates that investors expect higher earnings growth than companies with a lower P/E. A low P/E may suggest that a company is undervalued or that it is performing exceptionally well relative to its historical results. When a company has no earnings or is reporting losses, the P/E is expressed as N/A. Although a negative P/E can be calculated, this convention is uncommon.

The P/E ratio can also standardize the value of $1 of earnings across the stock market. In theory, by taking the median P/E over several years, one could formulate a standardized P/E that serves as a benchmark to indicate whether a stock is worth buying.

**N/A Meaning:** A P/E of N/A means the ratio is unavailable for that stock. A company newly listed on an exchange that has not yet reported earnings -- such as an IPO -- may carry an N/A P/E. It can also indicate zero or negative earnings.

## P/E Ratio vs. Earnings Yield

The inverse of the P/E ratio is the earnings yield (which can be thought of as the E/P ratio). The earnings yield is defined as EPS divided by the stock price, expressed as a percentage.

If Stock A trades at $10 with a TTM EPS of $0.50, it has a P/E of 20 ($10 / $0.50) and an earnings yield of 5% ($0.50 / $10). If Stock B trades at $20 with a TTM EPS of $2, it has a P/E of 10 ($20 / $2) and an earnings yield of 10% ($2 / $20).

The earnings yield is less widely used than the P/E ratio. It is useful for investors focused on the rate of return on investment. For equity investors who receive regular investment income, this may be a secondary consideration -- which is why many investors prefer value-based metrics such as the P/E ratio.

The earnings yield is also useful when a company has zero or negative earnings. Since this is common among high-tech, high-growth, or startup companies, EPS may be negative, producing an undefined P/E (displayed as N/A). However, a company with negative earnings will have a negative earnings yield, which can still be used for comparison.

## P/E Ratio vs. PEG Ratio

Even a P/E ratio calculated with forward earnings estimates does not always indicate whether the P/E is appropriate for the company's expected growth rate. To address this limitation, investors turn to the price/earnings-to-growth (PEG) ratio.

The PEG ratio measures the relationship between the P/E ratio and earnings growth to provide investors with a more complete picture. Investors use it to determine whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued by analyzing both earnings and the company's expected growth rate. The PEG ratio is calculated by dividing a company's trailing P/E by its earnings growth rate over a given period.

Because it incorporates both historical earnings and future earnings growth, the PEG is generally considered more informative than the P/E alone. For example, a low P/E might suggest a stock is undervalued and worth buying. However, factoring in the company's growth rate to derive its PEG ratio may tell a different story. PEG ratios can be termed "trailing" if based on historical growth rates or "forward" if based on projected growth rates.

Although earnings growth rates vary across industries, a stock with a PEG below 1 is generally considered undervalued because its price is low relative to expected earnings growth. A PEG above 1 may be considered overvalued, as it suggests the stock price is high relative to the company's expected growth. [6]

## Absolute P/E vs. Relative P/E

Analysts also distinguish between absolute and relative P/E in their analysis.

The numerator is typically the current stock price, and the denominator may be the TTM EPS, the estimated EPS for the next 12 months (forward P/E), or a blend of trailing EPS from the most recent two quarters and forward EPS for the next two quarters.

When distinguishing between absolute and relative P/E, note that absolute P/E represents the P/E of the current period. For example, if today's stock price is $100 and TTM earnings are $2 per share, the P/E is 50 ($100 / $2).

Relative P/E compares the absolute P/E to a benchmark or a range of past P/Es over a relevant period, such as the last 10 years. Relative P/E shows what portion or percentage of past P/Es the current P/E has reached. Relative P/E is most often compared to the highest value in the historical range, but investors may also compare it to the low end, measuring how close the current P/E is to the historical floor.

If the current P/E is below the past range (whether compared to the historical high or low), the relative P/E will be below 100%. If the relative P/E reaches 100% or more, it indicates the current P/E has matched or exceeded past levels.

## Limitations of Using the P/E Ratio

Like any fundamental metric, the P/E ratio has important limitations. Companies with no profits and negative EPS present a challenge for P/E calculation. Analysts differ on how to handle this: some report a negative P/E, others assign a P/E of zero, and most simply state that the P/E does not exist (N/A) until the company becomes profitable.

A primary limitation of the P/E ratio is that it is unreliable for comparing companies across different industries. Because companies earn money in different ways and on different timelines, valuation and growth rates can vary substantially across sectors.

Therefore, the P/E ratio should be used as a comparison tool only among companies in the same industry, as this is the only context in which it yields meaningful insights. For instance, comparing the P/E of a retailer with that of an oil and gas drilling company might suggest that one is a superior investment, but this is not a compelling conclusion. When an entire industry exhibits high P/E ratios, an individual company's high P/E is less of a concern.

## Other P/E Ratio Considerations

Since a company's debt can affect both its share price and its earnings, leverage can distort P/E ratios. For example, consider two similar companies that differ primarily in the amount of debt they carry. The more leveraged company may have a lower P/E than the less leveraged one. However, if business is solid, the more leveraged company may earn higher returns precisely because of the risk it has assumed.

Another critical limitation lies in the P/E formula itself. The P/E ratio depends on accurate representations of a stock's market value and EPS estimates. The market determines the stock price across many venues. However, the source of earnings information is the company itself -- and therefore subject to potential manipulation. Analysts and investors must trust that management is providing truthful information. If that trust is broken, the stock will be deemed riskier and less valuable.

To mitigate these risks, the P/E ratio is just one of many metrics that analysts review. If a company deliberately manipulates its results, it would be difficult to ensure all metrics align in a way that conceals the manipulation. This is why the P/E remains a central data point -- but never the only one -- when analyzing a publicly traded company.

## What Is a Good P/E Ratio?

The answer depends on the industry. Certain industries tend to have higher average P/E ratios. For example, as of February 2024, the Communication Services Select Sector Index had a P/E of 17.60, while the Technology Select Sector Index had a P/E of 29. [7][8] To gauge whether a specific P/E is high or low, compare it with the average P/E of other companies in the same industry, then with other industries and the market overall.

## Is a Higher or Lower P/E Ratio Better?

Many investors argue that it is better to buy shares of companies with lower P/E ratios because you are paying less for every $1 of earnings. A lower P/E is like a lower price tag, making it attractive to bargain-seeking investors. In practice, however, there may be a reason behind a company's low P/E. If the P/E is low because the business model is in decline, the apparent bargain may be illusory.

## What Does a P/E Ratio of 15 Mean?

A P/E of 15 means the company's current market value equals 15 times its annual earnings. Taken literally, if you were to hypothetically buy 100% of the company's shares, it would take 15 years to recoup your initial investment through the company's ongoing profits. However, that estimate would change if the company grows or if its earnings fluctuate.

## The Bottom Line

The P/E ratio is one of many fundamental financial metrics used to evaluate a company. It is calculated by dividing a stock's current market price by its earnings per share. It reflects investor expectations and helps determine whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued relative to its earnings. The P/E ratio is useful for comparing companies within the same industry -- for example, insurer versus insurer or telecom versus telecom. It provides insight into market sentiment and investment prospects. However, it should be used alongside other financial metrics, as it does not account for future growth prospects, debt levels, or industry-specific factors.
