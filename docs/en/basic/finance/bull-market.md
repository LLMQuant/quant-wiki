# What Is a Bull Market?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A bull market is a condition in financial markets characterized by rising -- or expected rising -- prices. The term "bull market" is most commonly used in reference to the stock market but can apply to anything that is traded, such as bonds, real estate, currencies, and commodities.

Because security prices rise and fall essentially continuously during trading, the term "bull market" is typically reserved for extended periods in which a large portion of security prices are rising. Bull markets tend to last months or even years.

### Key Takeaways

- A bull market is a period of rising asset or security prices in financial markets.
- The commonly accepted definition of a bull market is when stock prices rise by 20% or more.
- Traders employ multiple strategies, such as increased buy-and-hold and retracement, to profit from bull markets.
- The opposite of a bull market is a bear market, in which prices trend downward.

## Understanding Bull Markets

Bull markets are characterized by optimism, investor confidence, and the expectation that strong results will continue for an extended period. It is difficult to predict consistently when market trends might shift. Part of the difficulty is that psychological effects and speculation can sometimes play a significant role in markets.

There is no single specific and universal metric for identifying a bull market. Nevertheless, perhaps the most common definition is a situation in which stock prices rise 20% or more from recent lows.

Since bull markets are difficult to predict, analysts can typically recognize this phenomenon only after it has occurred. One notable bull market in recent history was the period between 2003 and 2007. During that time, the S&P 500 increased significantly after a prior decline; as the 2008 financial crisis took effect, major declines occurred again after the bull market run. [1][2]

Bull markets usually occur when the economy is strengthening or already strong. They tend to coincide with robust gross domestic product (GDP) growth, declining unemployment, and rising corporate profits. Investor confidence also tends to climb throughout a bull market. Overall demand for stocks is positive, as is the general tone of the market. Additionally, the volume of initial public offering (IPO) activity typically increases during bull markets.

Notably, some of the above factors are more easily quantifiable than others. While corporate profits and unemployment are quantifiable, it can be more difficult, for example, to gauge the general tone of market commentary. Supply and demand for securities will see-saw: supply tends to be weak while demand is strong. Investors are eager to buy securities while few are willing to sell. In a bull market, investors are more willing to participate in the stock market to capture gains.

During a bull market, several characteristics can be observed. These include increased trading volume as more investors are willing to buy and hold securities in hopes of achieving capital gains. Securities in a bull market also tend to receive higher valuations because investors are willing to pay more for them due to perceived upside potential.

Furthermore, bull markets are typically characterized by greater liquidity, as demand for securities is higher and fewer sellers are available, making it easy for investors to buy and sell at reasonable prices. Companies performing well during a bull market may also choose to reward shareholders by increasing dividends, which can be attractive to income-focused investors. Finally, during bull markets, more companies may go public and raise capital through IPOs, giving investors the opportunity to participate in the growth of new, promising companies.

## Bull Markets vs. Bear Markets

The opposite of a bull market is a bear market, which is characterized by falling prices and typically shrouded in pessimism. The common belief about the origin of these terms suggests that the use of "bull" and "bear" to describe markets derives from the way each animal attacks its opponents. A bull thrusts its horns upward, while a bear swipes its paws downward. These actions are metaphors for market movement. If the trend is up, it is a bull market. If the trend is down, it is a bear market.

Bull and bear markets often coincide with the economic cycle, which consists of four phases: expansion, peak, contraction, and trough. The onset of a bull market is often a leading indicator of economic expansion. Because public sentiment about future economic conditions drives stock prices, the market frequently rises even before broader economic measures -- such as GDP growth -- begin to tick up. Similarly, bear markets usually set in before economic contraction takes hold. A review of a typical U.S. recession reveals that the stock market decline typically precedes the drop in GDP by several months.

## How to Take Advantage of a Bull Market

Investors who want to profit from a bull market should buy early in order to take advantage of rising prices and sell when prices have reached their peak. Although it is difficult to determine when the bottom and peak will occur, most losses will be minimal and are usually temporary. Below are several prominent strategies investors use during bull markets. However, because it is difficult to assess the current state of the market, these strategies involve at least some degree of risk.

One of the most basic strategies in investing is to buy a particular security and hold it, potentially selling it at a later date. This strategy necessarily involves confidence on the part of the investor: why hold a security unless you expect its price to rise? For this reason, the optimism that accompanies bull markets helps fuel the buy-and-hold approach.

Increased buy-and-hold is a variation of the straightforward buy-and-hold strategy and involves additional risk. The premise behind the increased buy-and-hold approach is that an investor will continue adding to holdings in a particular security as long as its price continues to increase. One common method suggests that an investor purchase an additional fixed quantity of shares for every preset increase in the stock price.

A retracement is a brief period during which the general trend in a security's price reverses. Even during a bull market, stock prices are unlikely to only ascend. Rather, there are likely to be shorter periods of time in which small dips occur as well, even as the overall trend continues upward.

Some investors watch for retracements within a bull market and move to buy during these periods. The thinking behind this strategy is that, assuming the bull market continues, the price of the security in question will quickly move back up, retroactively providing the investor with a discounted purchase price.

Perhaps the most aggressive way to attempt to capitalize on a bull market is the process known as full swing trading. Investors employing this strategy take very active roles, using short selling and other techniques to squeeze maximum gains as shifts occur within the context of a broader bull market.

## Why Is It Called a "Bull" Market When Prices Go Up?

The actual origin of the term "bull" is subject to debate. The terms "bear" (for down markets) and "bull" (for up markets) are thought by some to derive from the way each animal attacks its opponents. That is, a bull thrusts its horns upward, while a bear swipes its paws downward. These actions were then metaphorically linked to the movement of the market. If the trend is up, it is considered a bull market. If the trend is down, it is considered a bear market.

Others point to Shakespeare's plays, which reference battles involving bulls and bears. In *Macbeth*, the ill-fated title character says his enemies have tied him to a stake but "bear-like, I must fight the course." In *Much Ado About Nothing*, the bull is a savage but noble beast. Several other explanations also exist.

## Were We in a Bull Market as of 2023?

The S&P 500 entered a bull market on June 8, 2023, after rallying 20% from its October 2022 low. The index had been in a bear market since June 2022. The DJIA and the Nasdaq had been in bull markets since November 30 and May 8, respectively.

## What Causes Stock Prices to Rise in a Bull Market?

Bull markets often exist alongside a strong and growing economy. Stock prices are informed by future expectations of profits and firms' ability to generate cash flows. A strong production economy, high employment, and rising GDP all suggest profits will continue to grow, and this is reflected in rising stock prices. Low interest rates and low corporate tax rates are also favorable for corporate profitability.

## Why Do Bull Markets Sometimes Falter and Become Bear Markets?

When the economy hits a rough patch -- for example, in the face of recession or a spike in unemployment -- it becomes difficult to sustain rising stock prices. Moreover, recessions are often accompanied by a negative turn in investor and consumer sentiment, where market psychology becomes more focused on fear or reducing risk than on greed or risk-taking.

## Conclusion

A bull market is a financial market characterized by rising prices and investor optimism. It is most commonly used in reference to the stock market but can also refer to bond, real estate, currency, and commodity markets. Bull markets tend to last for extended periods and are marked by rising demand for securities, increasing corporate profits and GDP, and falling unemployment. The opposite of a bull market is a bear market, characterized by falling prices and investor pessimism. The terms "bull" and "bear" are believed to derive from the way these animals attack their opponents.
