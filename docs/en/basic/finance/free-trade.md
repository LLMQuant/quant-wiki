# What Is a Free Trade Agreement (FTA)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A free trade agreement is a pact between two or more nations to reduce barriers to imports and exports among them. Under a free trade policy, goods and services can be bought and sold across international borders with few or no government tariffs, quotas, subsidies, or prohibitions to inhibit their exchange.

The concept of free trade is the opposite of trade protectionism or economic isolationism.

### Key Takeaways

- Free trade agreements reduce or eliminate barriers to cross-border trade.
- Free trade is the opposite of trade protectionism.
- In the United States and the European Union, free trade agreements are not without regulation and oversight.

## How Free Trade Agreements Work

In the modern world, free trade policies are typically implemented through formal, mutual agreements between the nations involved. However, a free trade policy may simply mean the absence of any trade restrictions.

A government does not need to take specific action to promote free trade. This hands-off stance is referred to as "laissez-faire trade" or trade liberalization.

Governments that adopt free trade policies or enter into free trade agreements do not necessarily abandon all control over imports and exports, nor do they eliminate all protectionist policies. In modern international trade, few free trade agreements achieve completely free trade.

**Important:** The benefits of free trade were outlined by economist David Ricardo in his 1817 work *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*. [1]

For example, a country might allow free trade with another nation while making exceptions to prohibit the import of certain drugs not approved by its regulators, unvaccinated animals, or processed foods that do not meet its standards.

It may also implement policies that exempt specific products from tariff-free status to protect domestic producers from foreign competition.

In principle, free trade at the international level is no different from trade between neighbors, towns, or states.

However, it allows businesses in each country to focus on producing and selling the goods that best utilize their resources, while other businesses import goods that are scarce or unavailable domestically. This combination of local production and foreign trade enables economies to experience faster growth while better meeting consumer needs.

This view was first popularized by economist David Ricardo in 1817 in his work *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*. He argued that free trade expands the variety of goods available to a country, lowers prices, and makes better use of its domestic resources, knowledge, and specialized skills. [1]

## Free Trade Models

Before the 19th century, global trade was largely dominated by mercantilist theory. This approach prioritized achieving a favorable trade balance relative to other nations and accumulating more gold and silver.

To achieve a favorable trade balance, countries frequently erected trade barriers -- such as taxes and tariffs -- to discourage their residents from buying foreign goods. This incentivized consumers to purchase domestically manufactured products, thereby supporting local industry.

Ricardo introduced the law of comparative advantage, which holds that countries gain the most from free trade. He demonstrated that if countries prioritize producing goods they can make more cheaply than other nations -- that is, goods in which they have a comparative advantage -- they can collectively produce more goods than if trade were restricted. [2]

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Free Trade

Free trade has enabled many nations to achieve rapid economic growth. By focusing on exports and resources in which they hold a strong comparative advantage, many countries have attracted foreign investment capital and provided relatively high-paying jobs for local workers.

For consumers, free trade creates a competitive environment in which countries strive to offer the lowest prices for their resources. This in turn allows manufacturers to offer lower prices for finished goods, ultimately raising purchasing power for all consumers.

However, when a country opens its borders to free trade, there are economic losers as well. Domestic industries may be unable to compete with foreign rivals, leading to local job losses. Large industries may relocate to countries with more lenient environmental and labor laws, resulting in child labor or pollution.

Free trade can also make countries more dependent on global markets. For example, while certain goods may be cheaper on world markets, producing them domestically may be strategically important. In the event of war or crisis, a country may be forced to rebuild those industries from scratch.

### Pros

- Allows consumers access to the cheapest goods on world markets.
- Enables countries with relatively inexpensive labor or resources to benefit from foreign exports.
- According to Ricardian theory, countries can collectively produce more goods by trading based on their respective advantages.

### Cons

- Competition with foreign exports may cause local unemployment and business closures.
- Industries may migrate to jurisdictions with weaker regulations, leading to environmental damage or abusive labor practices.
- Countries may become dependent on global markets for critical goods, placing them at a strategic disadvantage during crises.

## Public Opinion on Free Trade

Free trade divides economists and the general public. Research shows that American economists support free trade policies at significantly higher rates than the public at large. [3]

Indeed, American economist Milton Friedman said: "The economics profession has been almost unanimous on the subject of the desirability of free trade." [4]

Free trade policies are less popular among the general public. Key concerns include unfair competition from countries with lower labor costs, which can undercut prices, and the loss of well-paying jobs to manufacturers overseas.

Calls for the public to "Buy American" rise and fall with the political winds but never disappear entirely.

Unsurprisingly, financial markets see the other side of the coin. Free trade represents an opportunity to open another part of the world to domestic producers.

Moreover, free trade is now an integral part of the financial system and the investment world. American investors can now access most foreign financial markets and a wider range of securities, currencies, and other financial products.

However, completely free trade in financial markets is unlikely in the current era. There are numerous supranational regulatory organizations for world financial markets, including the Basel Committee on Banking Supervision, the International Organization of Securities Commissions (IOSCO), and the Committee on Capital Movements and Invisible Transactions.

## Examples of Free Trade Agreements

The European Union is a notable example of free trade today. Member states have formed an essentially borderless single entity for trade purposes, and most have adopted the euro, further smoothing the way. [5] [6]

It should be noted that this system is governed by a central bureaucracy that must manage the many trade-related issues that arise among member state representatives. [7]

The United States currently has numerous free trade agreements. These include multilateral agreements such as the United States-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA), which covers Canada and Mexico, and the Central America-Dominican Republic Free Trade Agreement (CAFTA-DR), which includes Costa Rica, the Dominican Republic, El Salvador, Guatemala, Honduras, and Nicaragua. There are also bilateral trade agreements with countries ranging from Australia to Peru. [8] [9] [10]

Collectively, these agreements mean that, according to government data, roughly half of all industrial goods entering the United States are tariff-free. The average import tariff on industrial goods is 2%. [11]

All of these agreements combined still do not add up to free trade in its most laissez-faire form. American special interest groups have successfully lobbied for trade restrictions on hundreds of imports, including steel, sugar, automobiles, milk, tuna, beef, and denim.

## Why Were Free Trade Zones Established in China?

Beginning in 2013, China began establishing free trade zones around major ports and coastal areas. These zones relaxed national regulations to encourage foreign investment and commercial development. [12]

## What Is a Free Trade Area?

A free trade area is a group of countries that agree to reduce or eliminate trade barriers on regional trade among themselves. This allows participating nations to benefit from lower tariffs while maintaining existing protections on trade with countries outside the area.

## What Are the Arguments Against Free Trade?

Critics frequently argue that free trade invites foreign competition that damages domestic industries, leading to unemployment and harming critical sectors. In some cases, free trade has led manufacturers to relocate to countries with weaker regulations, rewarding companies that pollute or engage in abusive labor practices. In other cases, countries with weak intellectual property laws may steal foreign companies' technology.

## The Bottom Line

Free trade refers to policies that allow inexpensive imports and exports without tariffs or other trade barriers. Under a free trade agreement, a group of countries agrees to lower tariffs or other barriers to facilitate greater exchange with trading partners. This enables all participating countries to benefit from lower prices and access to each other's resources.
