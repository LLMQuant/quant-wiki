# What Are Growth Rates?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A growth rate refers to the percentage change of a specific variable within a specific time period. Growth rates can be positive or negative, depending on whether the variable is increasing or decreasing over time. Originally used by biologists to study population sizes, growth rates are now widely applied to economic activity, corporate management, and investment returns.

For investors, growth rates typically represent the compound annualized rate of return on an investment, or the growth in a company's revenues, earnings, or dividends. Growth rates also apply to broader macroeconomic concepts such as gross domestic product (GDP) and unemployment. Both forward-looking expected growth rates and backward-looking historical growth rates are commonly used in analysis.

### Key Takeaways

- Growth rates express the annual change of a variable as a percentage.
- A positive growth rate indicates the variable is increasing over time; a negative growth rate indicates it is decreasing.
- Growth rates help assess company performance and forecast future performance.
- The growth rate is calculated by dividing the difference between the ending and beginning values of the analysis period by the beginning value.
- Common time horizons for growth rates include annual, quarterly, monthly, and weekly periods.

## Understanding Growth Rates

At the most fundamental level, growth rates express the annual change of a variable as a percentage. An economy's growth rate, for example, is derived from the year-over-year rate of change in GDP. This growth rate is used to gauge the contraction or expansion of the economy. If a country's income declines for two consecutive quarters, it is considered to be in recession.

Conversely, if the country's income grows for two consecutive quarters, it is considered to be expanding.

## How to Calculate Growth Rates

Growth rates can be calculated in several ways, depending on the information to be conveyed. A simple growth rate divides the difference between the ending and beginning values by the beginning value: (EV - BV) / BV. The economic growth rate of a nation's GDP can be computed as:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{Economic Growth} = \frac { \text{GDP}_2 - \text{GDP}_1 }{ \text{GDP}_1 } \\ &\textbf{where:} \\ &\text{GDP} = \text{Gross domestic product of the nation} \\ \end{aligned} $$

This approach, however, may be overly simplistic.

The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) is a variant commonly used to evaluate the performance of an investment or company. CAGR is not a true rate of return but rather describes the rate at which an investment would have grown if it had grown at the same rate every year with profits reinvested at the end of each year. The CAGR formula is:

$$ \begin{aligned} &CAGR= \left ( \frac{EV}{BV} \right ) ^{\frac{1}{n}}-1\\ &\textbf{where:}\\ &EV = \text{Ending value}\\ &BV = \text{Beginning value}\\ &n = \text{Number of years} \end{aligned} $$

The CAGR calculation assumes that growth is steady over a given time period. Due to its simplicity and flexibility, CAGR is a widely used metric, and many firms employ it to report and forecast earnings growth.

Financial theory holds that a company's stock can be reasonably valued using a dividend discount model (DDM), which is based on the premise that today's price is the present value of all future dividend payments. Therefore, the dividend growth rate is important for stock valuation.

The Gordon Growth Model (GGM) is a popular approach used to determine the intrinsic value of a stock based on a future series of dividends growing at a constant rate. Since mature companies typically seek to increase the dividends they pay to investors, the dividend growth rate is a key input in stock valuation.

## Using Growth Rates

Analysts, investors, and a company's management use growth rates to periodically assess growth and make predictions about future performance. Most often, growth rates are calculated for a firm's earnings, sales, or cash flow, but investors also look at growth rates for other metrics such as price-to-earnings ratios or book value. When public companies report quarterly earnings, the headline figures are typically earnings and revenue along with the growth rates -- quarter-over-quarter or year-over-year -- for each.

For example, Amazon reported full-year 2023 revenues of $575 billion, representing 12% growth from 2022 revenues of $514 billion. [1]

The internal growth rate (IGR) is a specific type of growth rate used to measure the return of an investment or project, or a company's performance. It represents the highest level of growth achievable without external financing, and a firm's maximum IGR is the level of business operations that can continue to fund and grow the company.

Investors typically examine rate of return (RoR) calculations to determine the growth rate of their portfolios or investments. While these generally follow the growth rate or CAGR formula, investors may also want to know their real or after-tax rate of return. Thus, growth rates for investors will net out the impact of taxes, inflation, and transaction costs or fees.

**Note:** Because stock prices are thought to reflect the discounted value of a firm's future cash flows, a rising stock market implies improving forecasted growth rates for the company.

Industry-specific growth rates also exist. Each industry has a unique benchmark growth rate against which its performance is measured. Companies at the forefront of technology, for instance, are more likely to have higher annual growth rates than a mature industry such as retail. Industry growth rates can serve as a comparison point against which a company evaluates its performance relative to peers.

Using historical growth rates is one of the simplest methods for estimating future industry growth. However, historically high growth rates do not always indicate high future growth, since industrial and economic conditions are constantly changing and often cyclical. For example, the auto industry has higher revenue growth rates during periods of economic expansion, but in recessions, consumers tend to be more frugal and do not spend discretionary income on a new car.

Beyond GDP growth, retail sales growth is another important growth rate for an economy because it can reflect consumer confidence and spending habits. When the economy is healthy and consumers are confident, spending increases, which is reflected in retail sales. When the economy is in recession, consumers cut back and retail sales decline.

For example, Ireland's Central Statistics Office reported in August 2023 that domestic retail sales had grown 5.9% over the preceding twelve months. However, retail sales excluding motor trades remained essentially flat, rising just 0.3%, suggesting the relative importance of automobile sales. Department stores, electrical goods, and food and beverage sectors experienced negative growth. [2]

## Examples of Growth Rates

Suppose we are comparing the annual GDP growth rates of two countries.

- Country A is a developed economy with a large, skilled population. Its GDP over three years:
  - Year 1: $1.20 trillion
  - Year 2: $1.26 trillion (growth Y1 to Y2 = 6/120 = 5.0%)
  - Year 3: $1.29 trillion (growth Y2 to Y3 = 3/126 = 2.4%)
- Country B is a developing economy with a rapidly growing population and a moderately skilled but low-cost labor force. Its GDP over the same period:
  - Year 1: $20 billion
  - Year 2: $25 billion (growth Y1 to Y2 = 50/200 = 25.0%)
  - Year 3: $35 billion (growth Y2 to Y3 = 100/250 = 40.0%)

Country A's growth rates are more modest and declining over the three years, while Country B is growing rapidly at an accelerating pace. This is not unusual for large, mature nations versus emerging market economies. But notably, by Year 3, Country A's economy is still more than 36 times larger than Country B's.

We can also consider the CAGR for each country between Year 1 and Year 3:

- CAGR_A = (1.29/1.20)^(1/2) - 1 = 3.68% per year
- CAGR_B = (35/20)^(1/2) - 1 = 32.29% per year

Note that the annualized CAGR is slightly lower than the arithmetic average of the two individual-year growth rates.

## Limitations of Growth Rates

While growth rates are important for understanding how quickly things change over time, they have several significant limitations. First, growth rates focus only on the net change between two points in time without accounting for price movements or volatility in between. For example, if a variable is valued at 10.00 today and 10.00 a year from now, the growth rate is zero. However, over those 12 months, it may have fluctuated wildly or remained perfectly flat. The annual growth rate conveys nothing about this.

Growth rates also ignore the nominal amounts involved. For example, a company's earnings may grow from $100,000 to $150,000, representing 50% growth, but only a $50,000 change. Meanwhile, a much larger Company B may grow earnings by only 5% per year (10 times lower in growth rate terms), but that could represent millions of dollars in revenue.

Finally, growth rates are difficult to compare across industries or other dissimilar variables. A 5% growth rate means very different things depending on whether it belongs to a growth-focused tech startup or a large, established consumer goods manufacturer. Similarly, a 4% decline in unemployment does not necessarily carry the same implications as a 4% increase in GDP.

## How Do You Calculate the GDP Growth Rate?

Following the formula above, the GDP growth rate takes the difference between the current and prior GDP level and divides that by the prior GDP level. The real economic (real GDP) growth rate will account for the effects of inflation, replacing real GDP in the numerator and denominator, where Real GDP = GDP / (1 + inflation rate since the base year).

## What Is a Normal Growth Rate for a Company?

What constitutes a good growth rate for a company depends on several factors, including the industry, whether the company is a startup or a mature firm, the company's size, and the overall state of the economy. In general, a company's growth rate should exceed the nominal GDP growth rate and the rate of inflation. Newer companies in higher-risk industries need higher growth rates to meet investor expectations.

## What Is a Good Growth Rate for a Startup?

Startups, especially those in high-technology industries, are generally expected to grow quite rapidly. According to Y Combinator, the well-known tech incubator, a good growth rate is considered to be 5% to 7% in weekly revenue growth, while an exceptional growth rate is 10% per week. [3] Accordingly, a startup might grow 150% or more in its first few months. As the company matures, the growth rate will moderate.

## How Do You Calculate a Growth Rate in Excel?

Because growth rate calculations follow a fairly straightforward formula, they can easily be transferred into a spreadsheet program like Microsoft Excel to speed up calculations and reduce the chance of human error. You simply need the beginning value, ending value, and the number of periods (for example, when using CAGR). Note that newer versions of Excel also have a built-in rate of return function called RRI that can compute CAGR in one step. RRI uses three arguments: number of periods, start value, and end value.

## How Do You Calculate the Population Growth Rate?

Like any other growth rate calculation, a population's growth rate can be computed by taking the current population size and subtracting the previous population size. Divide that amount by the previous size and multiply by 100 to get the percentage.

## Conclusion

Growth rates measure how quickly a variable is increasing or decreasing, showing the net change in value over a given period. Originally applied to the study of biological populations and diseases, growth rates have become essential considerations for economists, policymakers, corporate managers, entrepreneurs, and investors. Computing a growth rate simply requires dividing the change in value over the observation period (such as a year) by the starting value.
