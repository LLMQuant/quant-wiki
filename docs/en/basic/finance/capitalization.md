# What Is Capitalization?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Capitalization is an accounting method in which a cost is included in the value of an asset and expensed over the useful life of that asset, rather than being expensed in the period the cost was incurred. Capitalization records a cash outlay as an asset on the balance sheet, rather than as an expense on the income statement.

Capitalization can also refer to a quantitative assessment of a firm's capital structure, including the cost of capital in the form of a corporation's stock, long-term debt, and retained earnings.

### Key Takeaways

- In accounting, capitalization allows an asset to be depreciated over its useful life and appear on the balance sheet rather than the income statement.
- Capitalization may refer to the book value of capital or the total of a company's debt and equity.
- An undercapitalized company does not have sufficient funds to meet all of its obligations.

## Types of Capitalization

- Accounting: The matching principle requires companies to record expenses in the same accounting period as the related revenue. However, an asset may benefit a business over multiple accounting periods, typically in the case of fixed assets such as office buildings. The cost of these items is recorded in the general ledger as the historical cost of the asset. These costs are therefore said to be capitalized rather than expensed. A capitalized asset is not fully expensed in the current accounting period. A company may make a large purchase but spread the expense over many years depending on the type of property, plant, or equipment involved.
- Finance: Capitalization may refer to the book value cost of capital, which is the sum of a company's long-term debt, stock, and retained earnings. The alternative to book value is market value, which depends on the company's share price. Market capitalization equals the number of outstanding shares multiplied by the share price. If the total number of outstanding shares is one billion and the current share price is $10, then market capitalization is $10 billion. Companies with a high market capitalization are referred to as large caps.

**Important:** Because assets are used to generate revenue for the company over time, a portion of the cost is allocated to each accounting period. This process is known as depreciation for fixed assets and amortization for intangible assets.

## Capitalization Thresholds

Typically, a company sets a "capitalization threshold." Any cash outlay exceeding that amount is capitalized if it is appropriate to do so. Companies set capitalization thresholds because materiality varies by size and industry. For example, a small local shop may set a capitalization threshold of $500, while a global technology company may set its capitalization threshold at $100,000.

Financial statements can be manipulated when costs are incorrectly capitalized or expensed. If a cost is incorrectly expensed, net income in the current period will be lower than it should be, and the company will pay lower taxes in the current period. If a cost is incorrectly capitalized, net income in the current period will be higher than it should be. In addition, assets on the balance sheet will be overstated.

## What Is the Difference Between Undercapitalization and Overcapitalization?

A company may be either overcapitalized or undercapitalized. Undercapitalization occurs when earnings are insufficient to cover the cost of capital, such as interest payments to bondholders or dividend payments to shareholders. Overcapitalization occurs when there is no need for outside capital because earnings are sufficiently high and earnings have been underestimated.

## How Does Capitalization Affect Leased Equipment?

For leased equipment, capitalization converts an operating lease into a capital lease by classifying the leased asset as a purchased asset, which is then reflected on the company's balance sheet as one of its assets. The Financial Accounting Standards Board (FASB) requires that all leases exceeding twelve months be capitalized as assets and recorded as liabilities on the lessee's books to reflect the rights and obligations of the lease. ## What Expenses Can Be Capitalized?

Expenses that provide benefits to future periods should be capitalized so that the cost of the asset is recognized in the same period in which the benefits are received. In general, examples of expenses that can be capitalized include development costs, construction costs, and capital assets such as equipment or vehicles.

## Summary

In accounting, capitalization refers to long-term assets with future benefits. Rather than expensing the cost immediately, it is depreciated over time so that the expense is recognized when the benefit is received. In finance, capitalization refers to a company's financing structure and its book value cost of capital.
