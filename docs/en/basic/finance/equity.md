# What Is Equity?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Equity -- commonly referred to as shareholders' equity (or owners' equity for privately held companies) -- represents the amount that would be returned to shareholders if all of a company's assets were liquidated and all of its debts repaid. In the context of an acquisition, equity is the value of the company's sales minus any liabilities that do not transfer with the sale.

Shareholders' equity can also represent a company's book value. Equity may sometimes be offered as payment in kind. It also represents proportionate ownership in a company's shares. Equity appears on a company's balance sheet and is one of the most commonly referenced data points used by analysts to assess financial health.

### Key Takeaways

- Equity represents the value that would be returned to shareholders if all company assets were liquidated and all debts repaid.
- Equity can also be thought of as the degree of residual ownership in a company or asset after deducting all associated debts.
- Equity represents a shareholder's stake in the company and is identified on the balance sheet.
- The formula for equity is total assets minus total liabilities, and it is used in several key financial ratios such as return on equity (ROE).
- Home equity is the value of a homeowner's property (net of debt), which is another common usage of the term.

## How Shareholders' Equity Works

By comparing specific figures that reflect everything a company owns and everything it owes, the "assets minus liabilities" equity equation provides a clear picture of a company's finances that is readily interpreted by investors and analysts. Equity serves as capital raised by a company, which is then used to purchase assets, invest in projects, and fund operations. A company can typically raise capital by issuing debt (in the form of loans or bonds) or equity (by selling stock). Investors generally favor equity investments because they offer greater participation in a company's profits and growth.

Equity is important because it represents an investor's stake in the company, proportional to the number of shares held. Owning shares in a company entitles shareholders to capital gains and dividends. Equity ownership also gives shareholders the right to vote on corporate actions and board elections. These benefits of equity ownership foster ongoing shareholder interest in the company.

Shareholders' equity can be positive or negative. A positive figure indicates that the company has sufficient assets to cover its liabilities. A negative figure means the company's liabilities exceed its assets -- if this condition persists, it constitutes balance sheet insolvency. Investors generally view companies with negative shareholders' equity as risky or unsafe investments. However, shareholders' equity alone does not provide a complete picture of a company's financial health; combined with other tools and metrics, investors can more thoroughly analyze an organization's condition.

## Components of Shareholders' Equity

Retained earnings are a component of shareholders' equity, representing the portion of net income that has not been distributed to shareholders as dividends. Think of retained earnings as savings -- they represent accumulated profits that have not been paid out as distributions. Retained earnings grow over time as the company continues to reinvest a portion of its income.

At some point, accumulated retained earnings may exceed the amount of equity capital contributed by shareholders. For companies that have been operating for many years, retained earnings are often the largest component of shareholders' equity.

Treasury stock consists of shares the company has repurchased from existing shareholders (not to be confused with U.S. Treasury securities). Companies may repurchase shares when management cannot deploy all available equity capital at optimal returns. Repurchased shares become treasury stock, and their dollar value is recorded in a contra account that offsets the investor capital and retained earnings accounts. Companies can reissue treasury stock to shareholders when they need to raise funds.

Many consider shareholders' equity to represent a company's net assets -- in other words, the amount shareholders would receive if all assets were liquidated and all debts repaid.

## Other Forms of Equity

The concept of equity extends beyond corporate valuation. More broadly, equity can be thought of as the degree of ownership in any asset after deducting all associated debts.

Here are several common variants of equity:

- A stock or any other security representing an ownership interest in a company.
- On a company's balance sheet, the amount of funds contributed by owners or shareholders plus retained earnings (or losses). Also called shareholders' equity.
- In a margin trading account, the value of securities minus the amount borrowed from the brokerage.
- In real estate, the difference between a property's current fair market value and the amount the owner still owes on the mortgage. This is the amount the owner would receive after selling the property and paying off any liens -- also called "real property value."
- When a business goes bankrupt, equity is the amount remaining after the business repays its creditors. Often called "owners' equity" or "risk capital."

## Private Equity

For a public company, the market value of equity is readily available through its stock price and market capitalization. For private companies, no market mechanism exists, so other valuation methods must be used to estimate value.

Private equity generally refers to the valuation of companies that are not publicly traded. The equity stated on the balance sheet -- assets minus liabilities -- provides a book value estimate. Privately held companies can then seek investors by selling shares directly. These private equity investors may include pension funds, university endowments, insurance companies, or accredited individuals.

Private equity is often sold to funds and investors that specialize in direct investments in private companies or leveraged buyouts (LBOs) of public companies. In an LBO transaction, a company receives a loan from a private equity firm to fund the acquisition of a division of another company. Cash flows or assets of the acquired company typically serve as collateral for the loan. Mezzanine debt is a form of private loan, usually provided by a commercial bank or mezzanine venture capital firm. Mezzanine transactions typically involve a blend of debt and equity in the form of subordinated loans, warrants, common stock, or preferred stock.

Private equity plays a role at different stages of a company's life cycle. Typically, young companies with no established revenue or earnings cannot afford to borrow and must obtain capital from friends and family or individual angel investors. Venture capitalists enter the picture when a company has finally developed a product or service and is ready to bring it to market. In the technology sector, some of the largest and most successful companies -- including Google, Apple, Amazon, and Meta -- started with venture capital financing.

Venture capitalists (VCs) provide the bulk of private equity financing in exchange for an early minority stake. Sometimes VCs take board seats at their portfolio companies to ensure active involvement in guiding the business. VCs look to realize substantial returns early and exit investments within five to seven years. An LBO is among the most common types of private equity financing and typically occurs when a company is more mature.

Another form of private equity is private investment in public equity (PIPE). A PIPE involves a private investment firm, mutual fund, or other qualified investor purchasing stock in a company at a discount to the current market value in order to raise capital.

Unlike shareholders' equity, private equity is not accessible to ordinary individuals. Only "accredited" investors -- those with a net worth of at least $1 million -- can participate in private equity or venture capital partnerships. Depending on the size, these activities may require filing Form 4. For investors who do not meet this threshold, there is the option of investing in private equity exchange-traded funds (ETFs).

## Home Equity

Home equity is roughly comparable to the value embodied in homeownership. The equity a person holds in their residence represents the portion of the home they own outright, net of mortgage debt. Home equity is derived from mortgage payments -- including the down payment -- and appreciation in property value.

Home equity is often an individual's greatest source of collateral, and homeowners can use it to obtain a home equity loan -- sometimes called a second mortgage -- or a home equity line of credit (HELOC). Equity extraction is the process of drawing money from or borrowing against a property.

For example, suppose Sam owns a home with a mortgage. The home has a current market value of $175,000, and the outstanding mortgage balance is $100,000. Sam has $75,000 in home equity -- that is, $175,000 (total assets) minus $100,000 (total liabilities).

## Brand Equity

When determining equity in an asset, particularly for large corporations, it is important to note that these assets may include both tangible assets (such as property) and intangible assets (such as a company's reputation and brand identity). Through years of advertising and customer development, a company's brand can acquire intrinsic value. This value is sometimes called "brand equity," which measures the worth of a brand relative to a generic or store-brand alternative.

For example, many soft drink enthusiasts will reach for a Coca-Cola rather than a store-brand cola because they prefer the taste or are more familiar with the brand. If a bottle of store-brand cola costs $1 and a bottle of Coca-Cola costs $2, then Coca-Cola has $1 of brand equity.

Negative brand equity also exists -- when consumers are willing to pay more for a generic or store-brand product than for a particular branded product. Negative brand equity is rare and typically arises from negative publicity, such as a product recall or a disaster.

## Equity vs. Return on Equity

Return on equity (ROE) is a measure of financial performance calculated by dividing net income by shareholders' equity. Because shareholders' equity equals a company's assets minus its debt, ROE can be considered the return on net assets. ROE is regarded as a measure of how effectively management uses a company's assets to generate profits.

As discussed, equity has multiple meanings but typically represents ownership in an asset or a company -- such as a shareholder's equity stake. ROE is a financial metric that measures how much profit is generated by a company's shareholders' equity.

## Equity and Financial Accounting

The fundamental accounting equation states that assets equal liabilities plus equity. This equation forms the basis of the balance sheet, which summarizes a company's financial position at a specific point in time. In all examples discussed in this article, the foundation for calculating equity is rooted in this accounting equation.

Liabilities are a company's obligations to external parties, such as loans, accounts payable, and accrued expenses. Equity represents the residual claim on assets after liabilities are satisfied. A company can pay for something either by borrowing (a liability) or by using its own funds (equity). Thus, the equation reflects the principle that all of a company's resources (assets) can be financed through one of these two means.

## What Is Equity in Finance?

Equity is an important concept in finance, and its specific meaning varies depending on the context. Perhaps the most common type of equity is "shareholders' equity," calculated by taking a company's total assets and subtracting its total liabilities.

Shareholders' equity is therefore essentially a company's net worth. If the company were to liquidate, shareholders' equity is the amount that would theoretically be returned to shareholders.

## What Are Some Other Terms That Describe Equity?

Other terms sometimes used to describe this concept include shareholders' equity, book value, and net asset value. Depending on the context, the precise meanings of these terms may differ, but generally they refer to the value of an investment remaining after all liabilities associated with that investment have been paid off. The term is also used in real estate investing to refer to the difference between a property's fair market value and the outstanding mortgage balance.

## How Do Investors Use Equity?

Equity is a very important concept for investors. For instance, when evaluating a company, an investor might use shareholders' equity as a benchmark for determining whether a particular purchase price is expensive. If a company has historically traded at a price-to-book ratio of 1.5, an investor might think twice before paying more than that valuation -- unless they believe the company's prospects have fundamentally improved. On the other hand, an investor might be willing to buy shares in an underperforming company at a relatively low price if the price paid is low enough relative to equity.

## How Is Equity Calculated?

Equity equals total assets minus total liabilities. These figures can all be found on a company's balance sheet. For a homeowner, equity is the value of the home minus any outstanding mortgage or liens.

## Conclusion

Financial equity represents the ownership interest in a company's assets after deducting liabilities. It reflects the value that belongs to shareholders or business owners. Equity can also refer to other concepts, such as brand equity or other non-financial notions.
