# What Is Gross National Product (GNP)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Gross national product (GNP) is an estimate of the total value of all final products and services produced in a given period by the means of production owned by a country's residents. [1] GNP is commonly calculated by adding personal consumption expenditures, private domestic investment, government expenditure, net exports, and any income earned by residents from overseas investments, minus income earned within the domestic economy by foreign residents. Net exports represent the difference between what a country exports minus any imports of goods and services.

GNP is related to another important economic measure, gross domestic product (GDP), which takes into account all output produced within a country's borders regardless of who owns the means of production. GNP starts from GDP, adds income earned by residents from overseas investments, and subtracts income earned by foreign residents within the country. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- GNP measures the output of a country's residents, regardless of the location of the actual underlying economic activity.
- Income from overseas investments by a country's residents is counted in GNP, while foreign investment within a country's borders is not. This contrasts with GDP, which measures economic output and income based on location rather than nationality.
- GNP and GDP can have different values, and a large gap between the two may indicate a high degree of integration with the global economy.

## Understanding GNP

GNP measures the total monetary value of the output produced by a country's residents. Therefore, any output produced by foreign residents within the country's borders must be excluded, while any output produced by the country's residents outside its borders must be counted.

GNP does not include intermediate goods and services to avoid double-counting, since they are already incorporated into the value of final goods and services.

The United States used GNP as its primary measure of economic activity until 1991. After that, it began using GDP instead for two main reasons. [2] First, GDP corresponds more closely with other U.S. economic data of interest to policymakers, such as employment and industrial production, which -- like GDP -- measure activity within U.S. borders and disregard nationality. Second, the switch to GDP was intended to facilitate cross-country comparisons, since most other nations already used GDP as their primary metric at the time. [3]

## The Difference Between GNP and GDP

GNP and GDP are very closely related concepts, and the main differences between them arise from the fact that there may be companies owned by foreign residents that produce goods domestically, and companies owned by domestic residents that produce goods for the rest of the world and repatriate earned income to domestic residents.

For example, there are a number of foreign companies that produce goods and services in the United States and transfer any income earned to their foreign residents. Likewise, many U.S. corporations produce goods and services outside U.S. borders and earn profits for U.S. residents. If income earned by domestic corporations outside the United States exceeds income earned within the United States by foreign-resident corporations, then U.S. GNP is higher than its GDP.

Calculating GNP and GDP can produce different results in terms of total output. For example, in 2021 (according to third-quarter data), U.S. GDP was $23.8 trillion, while its GNP was $23.9 trillion. [4]

While GDP is the most widely followed measure of a country's economic activity, GNP is still worth examining because large differences between GNP and GDP may indicate that a country is becoming increasingly engaged in international trade, production, or financial operations. The larger the difference between a country's GNP and GDP, the greater the degree to which its income and investment activity involves transnational activities such as foreign direct investment.

## What Does GNP Measure?

Gross national product is one measure of a nation's economic output. It is the value of all products and services produced by a country's citizens both domestically and internationally, minus income earned by foreign residents. For example, if a country has production facilities in a neighboring country as well as its home country, GNP would account for both production outputs.

## What Is the Difference Between GNP and GDP?

GNP accounts for its citizens' production both within and outside its borders. It then subtracts income earned by foreign residents within the country. By contrast, GDP measures the total production of goods and services by both citizens and foreign residents within a country's borders. [1]

## What Is an Example of GNP?

Consider a country whose GNP exceeds its GDP. This indicates that its citizens, businesses, and corporations are providing net inflows to the country through their overseas operations. Consequently, this higher GNP may signal that the country is increasing its international financial operations, trade, or production.
