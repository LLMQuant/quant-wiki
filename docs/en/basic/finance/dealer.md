# What Is a Dealer?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A dealer is an individual or firm that buys and sells securities for its own account, whether through a broker or otherwise. A dealer acts as a principal in a transaction, whereas a broker acts as an agent executing orders on behalf of clients.

Dealers play an important role in the market. They make markets in securities, underwrite securities, and provide investment services to investors. This means dealers are the market makers who provide the bid and ask quotes you see when you look up the price of a security in the over-the-counter market. They also help create liquidity in the markets and drive long-term growth.

In the United States, dealers are a separate registration category, while in Canada the term is commonly short for "investment dealer," the equivalent of a U.S. broker-dealer.

### Key Takeaways

- A dealer buys and sells securities for its own account.
- Dealers play an important role in the market as market makers, creating liquidity and promoting long-term market growth.
- Dealers must register with the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and comply with all state requirements before commencing business.
- Dealers differ from traders and brokers: a dealer buys and sells for its own account, while a broker does not trade for its own portfolio.
- Dealers are regulated by the SEC.

## Understanding Dealers

A dealer in the securities market is an individual or firm that stands ready and willing to buy a security for its own account (at its bid price) or sell from its own account (at its ask price). A dealer seeks to profit from the spread between the bid and ask prices while also providing liquidity to the market. A dealer neither conducts business on behalf of a client nor facilitates transactions between parties.

**Tip:** An entity that arranges the buying and selling of securities but does not purchase and hold securities in its own account is not classified as a dealer.

A dealer is distinct from a trader. A dealer buys and sells securities as part of its regular business, while a trader buys and sells securities for personal account, not on a commercial basis.

In recent years, dealer profitability has been challenged by several factors, including increased technology requirements to keep pace with rapidly changing markets, industry consolidation, and a heightened regulatory environment that has raised compliance costs.

## Regulation of Dealers

Dealers are regulated by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). As part of this regulation, all dealers and brokers must register with the SEC and become members of the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA).

Individuals who typically need to register as dealers include those who:

- Continuously hold themselves out as being willing to buy and sell a particular security (i.e., make a market in that security).
- Run a matched book of repurchase agreements.
- Issue or originate securities and also buy and sell those securities.

Under SEC guidance, dealers owe certain duties when dealing with customers. These include prompt order execution, disclosure of material information and conflicts of interest to investors, and charging reasonable prices in the prevailing market.

Dealers are not permitted to begin conducting business until their SEC registration has been approved. They must also join a self-regulatory organization (SRO), become members of the Securities Investor Protection Corporation (SIPC), and comply with all applicable state requirements.

## Dealers vs. Brokers

These two roles are both commonly associated with the buying and selling of securities. Although they can function similarly in certain respects, they have distinct differences.

Unlike a dealer, a broker does not trade for its own portfolio but instead facilitates transactions by bringing buyers and sellers together. In practice, most dealers also act as brokers and are therefore referred to as broker-dealers. Broker-dealers range from small independent firms to subsidiaries of some of the largest banks. Firms operating as broker-dealers perform both services depending on market conditions and the size and type of securities involved in a particular transaction.

Another key distinction lies in how each charges for its services. A dealer charges a markup when selling from its own inventory because the dealer is the principal in the transaction, while a broker charges a commission for executing trades on behalf of clients.

**Important:** A dealer is different from a Registered Investment Advisor (RIA), who is held to a fiduciary standard requiring them to place the client's interests above their own.

## Dealer Markets

The environment where multiple dealers come together to buy and sell securities for their own accounts is known as a dealer market. In this market, dealers can transact with one another and use their own funds to close transactions -- in contrast to a broker market, where they act as agents for buyers and sellers. Brokers are not permitted to trade in a dealer market. Dealers provide all the terms of a transaction, including price.

## Other Dealers in the Market

While the term "dealer" predominates in the securities market, the same distinction applies in other fields. A dealer can also refer to a business or person that trades in or executes the purchase and sale of a specific product or service. For example, someone who sells automobiles is called an automobile dealer, and someone who deals in antiques is called an antiques dealer.

## Dealer FAQ

After buying securities such as stocks and bonds, a dealer sells those securities to other investors at a price above the purchase price. The difference between the dealer's bid price (purchase price) and ask price (selling price) is known as the dealer's spread. The spread equals the dealer's profit on the transaction.

When you open an account at a broker-dealer, you are typically required to provide certain information.

Before opening an account with anyone, you should check the broker's background and disciplinary record. The SEC's website provides guidance on how to find a broker's background or disciplinary history.

Brokers will generally ask for the following personal information:

- Name
- Social Security number (or taxpayer identification number)
- Address
- Phone number
- Email address
- Date of birth
- Driver's license, passport, or other government-issued identification
- Employment status and occupation
- Whether you are employed by a brokerage firm
- Annual income
- Net worth
- Investment objectives and risk tolerance

You will also need to decide what type of brokerage account to open. Broker-dealers typically offer two types of accounts: cash accounts and margin accounts.

Finally, you will need to make some investment decisions for your account. You may also choose to grant "discretionary authority" to another person to make decisions on your behalf.

According to FINRA data, there are more than 3,400 securities firms. Some of the largest broker-dealers include Fidelity Investments, Charles Schwab, and Edward Jones.

Broker-dealers can be individuals or firms (general partnerships, limited partnerships, limited liability companies, corporations, or other entities). According to the latest FINRA data, the number of broker-dealers to choose from exceeds 3,400.

## Conclusion

A dealer is an individual or firm that buys and sells securities for its own account, whether through a broker or otherwise. Dealers are regulated by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC). Dealers are important because they make markets in securities, underwrite securities, and provide investment services to investors.
