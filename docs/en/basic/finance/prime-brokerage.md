# What Is Prime Brokerage?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Prime brokerage is a bundled suite of services offered by investment banks and other financial institutions to hedge funds and other large investment clients. These clients need to borrow securities or cash to engage in netting and achieve absolute returns.

The services encompassed by prime brokerage include securities lending, leveraged trade execution, and cash management, among others. Most major financial services firms -- such as Goldman Sachs, UBS, and Morgan Stanley -- provide prime brokerage services, with their dedicated units dating back to the 1970s.

### Key Takeaways

- Prime brokerage refers to a bundle of services that investment banks and other major financial institutions offer to hedge funds and similar clients.
- Prime brokerage services may include cash management, securities lending, and more.
- Prime brokerage services help hedge funds access research, find new investors, borrow securities or cash, and more.
- Prime brokerage provides large institutions with a mechanism to outsource many of their investment activities, allowing them to focus on investment objectives and strategy.
- Financial institutions dealing with prime brokers must meet minimum account size requirements, which vary by broker along with fees.

## Understanding Prime Brokerage

At its core, prime brokerage facilitates the complex, active trading operations of large financial institutions such as hedge funds. A prime broker enables hedge funds to borrow securities and increase their leverage, while also acting as an intermediary between hedge funds and counterparties such as pension funds and commercial banks.

Prime brokerage firms -- sometimes referred to as prime brokers -- are generally larger financial institutions that have dealings with other major institutions and hedge funds. Most large banks have prime brokerage units that serve hundreds of clients. Although prime brokerage firms offer a wide range of services, clients are not required to participate in all of them and can engage other firms for specific services as needed.

## Prime Brokerage Services

Prime brokerage offers a range of services to qualifying clients. A designated broker may provide settlement agent services along with financing for leverage, asset custody services, and daily account statement preparation.

Prime brokers offer information and resources that many institutions may not have the capacity to maintain in-house. In essence, prime brokerage provides large institutions with a mechanism to outsource many of their investment activities, enabling them to focus on investment goals and strategy.

Additionally, prime brokerage firms may offer concierge-type services. These can include risk management, capital introduction, securities financing, and cash financing. Some even offer the opportunity to sublease office space and provide access to other facility-based benefits. As with traditional services, participation in any concierge service is optional.

In the case of securities lending, collateral is generally required by the prime broker. This allows them to minimize risk while also gaining quick access to funds when needed.

## Prime Brokerage Account Requirements

Most prime brokerage clients are large-scale investors and institutions. Money managers and hedge funds typically qualify, as do arbitrageurs and a variety of other professional investors. In the case of hedge funds, prime brokerage services are often considered critical to a fund's success.

Two common types of clients are pension funds (a form of institutional investor) and commercial banks. These investors typically deal with large amounts of investment cash but lack the internal resources to manage investments independently.

The minimum account size to open a prime brokerage account is $500,000 in equity, though such an account is unlikely to receive benefits beyond what discount brokers offer.

**Note:** Some of the largest prime brokers in the United States are investment banks, including Bank of America, J.P. Morgan, Goldman Sachs, and Citigroup.

For a hedge fund or other institutional client to receive the kinds of services that make having a prime brokerage account worthwhile -- particularly reduced trading fees -- an account size of $50 million in equity is a likely starting point.

Even so, these services are highly sought after by clients, and leading banks accept only those clients most likely to be beneficial to them over time. For this reason, a hedge fund would probably need to have as much as $200 million in equity to qualify for the best treatment.

## Prime Brokerage Example

Hedge Fund ABC just launched with $75 million raised from investors. It is a small hedge fund that employs 15 people. The majority of them are traders, researchers, and a few administrative personnel. The fund has limited resources to allocate to the various needs of the business.

To alleviate some of these burdens, ABC transacts with J.P. Morgan's prime brokerage unit. The two parties sign a prime brokerage agreement detailing that J.P. Morgan will assume the responsibilities of managing ABC's cash management, computing its net asset value (NAV) on a monthly basis, and performing risk management analysis on its portfolio. For these services, the parties agree that J.P. Morgan will charge a monthly fee of $20,000.

After six months, ABC has grown, and its investment strategy has become more complex. It needs to borrow securities as part of its investment strategy and transacts with J.P. Morgan to access securities lending services. For this, J.P. Morgan charges 5% on the borrowed amount. ABC also engages J.P. Morgan for capital introduction services, whereby J.P. Morgan introduces ABC to potential investors and charges 2% of the invested amount from each investor.

All of these services provided by J.P. Morgan to Hedge Fund ABC constitute prime brokerage services.

## Prime Brokerage FAQs

A broker is an individual or entity that facilitates the buying and selling of securities, such as purchasing stocks and bonds for an investment account. A prime broker is a large institution that provides a multitude of services to other large institutions, including cash management, securities lending, and risk management.

Prime brokers charge different rates to different clients. Each prime broker has its own fee structure. Fees also depend on the volume of transactions a client conducts, the number of services used, and other factors.

Margin refers to a prime broker lending money to a client so that the client can purchase securities. It is also known as margin financing. The prime broker has no risk on the underlying positions, only on the client's ability to make margin payments. Margin terms are typically agreed upon in advance to determine lending limits.

A prime brokerage agreement is an agreement between a prime broker and its client that stipulates all of the services for which the prime broker will be contractually obligated, including all terms such as fees, minimum account requirements, minimum transaction levels, and any other details needed between the two parties.

Revenue sources for prime brokerage include overall fees, trading commissions, and borrowing charges.

## Conclusion

Prime brokerage is an important set of services provided to large institutions that helps facilitate their business and outsource activities, allowing them to focus on their core responsibilities.

Prime brokerage is a significant part of the financial sector, creating jobs for thousands of people and contributing meaningfully to the economy. For many large institutions, a prime broker can serve as a one-stop shop that greatly simplifies their business operations.
