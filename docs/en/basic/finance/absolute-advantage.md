# What Is Absolute Advantage?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Absolute advantage is the ability of an individual, company, region, or country to produce a greater quantity of a good or service with the same amount of inputs per unit of time, or to produce the same quantity using fewer inputs per unit of time, compared with its competitors.

Absolute advantage can be achieved by producing goods or services at a lower absolute cost per unit using fewer inputs, or through a more efficient production process.

### Key Takeaways

- Absolute advantage is the ability of a producer to provide a greater quantity of goods or services at the same cost, or the same quantity at a lower cost, than its competitors.
- A concept introduced by Adam Smith, absolute advantage can serve as the basis for large-scale trade between producers of different goods who each hold different absolute advantages. [1]
- Through specialization, division of labor, and trade, producers with different absolute advantages can always gain more than they would by producing and consuming in isolation.
- Absolute advantage can be contrasted with comparative advantage, which is the ability to produce goods and services at a lower **opportunity cost**.

## Understanding Absolute Advantage

The concept of absolute advantage was developed by the 18th-century economist Adam Smith in his work *The Wealth of Nations* to illustrate how countries can benefit from trade by specializing in producing and exporting goods they make more efficiently than other countries. Countries with an absolute advantage can choose to specialize in producing and exporting specific goods or services and use the proceeds to purchase goods and services from other countries.

Smith argued that when countries specialize in the products for which they have an absolute advantage and then trade, all countries can be made better off -- provided each has at least one product in which it holds an absolute advantage over others.

Absolute advantage explains why it makes sense for individuals, firms, and nations to trade with one another. Since each entity has advantages in producing certain goods and services, both parties can benefit from exchange.

This mutual gain from trade forms the basis of Smith's argument that specialization, division of labor, and subsequent trade lead to an overall increase in prosperity from which everyone can benefit. Smith saw this as the fundamental source of the "wealth of nations." [1]

## Absolute Advantage vs. Comparative Advantage

Absolute advantage can be contrasted with comparative advantage, which refers to a producer's ability to produce goods or services at a lower opportunity cost than other producers. Opportunity cost is the potential benefit that an individual, investor, or business foregoes when choosing one alternative over another. [2]

Absolute advantage leads to unambiguous gains from specialization and trade only when each producer has an absolute advantage in producing some good. If a producer lacks any absolute advantage, Adam Smith's argument does not necessarily apply.

However, even in that case, producers and their trading partners can still gain from trade if they specialize according to their respective comparative advantages. [3] David Ricardo, in his work *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, showed that even when a country has an absolute advantage in the production of multiple goods, it can still benefit from trade if it has different comparative advantages relative to other countries. [4]

## Assumptions of Absolute Advantage Theory

Both Smith's theory of absolute advantage and Ricardo's theory of comparative advantage rely on certain assumptions and simplifications to explain the benefits of trade. [4]

Both theories assume the absence of trade barriers. They do not account for transportation costs or tariffs that one country might impose on another's imports. [4] In practice, however, transportation costs affect the likelihood of trade between importers and exporters, and countries can use tariffs to create advantages for themselves or disadvantages for competitors.

Both theories also assume that factors of production are immobile. In these models, workers and businesses do not relocate in search of better opportunities -- an assumption that was realistic in the 18th century.

In modern trade, however, globalization has made it easy for companies to move factories overseas. It has also increased immigration rates, which affect a country's available labor force. In certain industries, companies work with governments to create immigration opportunities for workers critical to their business operations.

More importantly, both theories assume that a country's absolute advantage is constant and scale-invariant. In other words, they assume that the unit cost of producing a small quantity of goods is the same as for a large quantity, and that countries cannot alter their absolute advantages.

In reality, countries frequently make strategic investments to create greater advantages in certain industries. Absolute advantages can also change for reasons beyond investment -- natural disasters, for instance, can destroy farmland, factories, and other factors of production.

## Pros and Cons of Absolute Advantage

One strength of the theory of absolute advantage is its simplicity: it provides an elegant explanation of the benefits of trade, showing how countries can gain by focusing on their absolute advantages.

However, absolute advantage theory does not fully explain why countries benefit from trade. That explanation would later come from Ricardo's theory of comparative advantage: even when a country has an absolute advantage in producing two types of goods, it can still benefit from trade. In other words, if a country can produce all goods more cheaply than its trading partner, it can still gain from trading with other countries.

Moreover, the theory assumes that absolute advantages are static -- that a country cannot change its absolute advantage and that advantages do not become more efficient at scale. Practical experience shows this to be untrue: many countries have successfully created absolute advantages through strategic investment in key industries. [3]

In fact, the theory has been used to justify exploitative post-colonial economic policies. Arguing that all countries should focus on their strengths, major institutions such as the World Bank and the International Monetary Fund have often pressured developing countries to concentrate on agricultural exports rather than industrialization. As a result, many of these countries remain at lower levels of economic development. [5] [6]

### Pros

- Provides a straightforward explanation of why countries can benefit from leveraging their strengths through trade.

### Cons

- Lacks the explanatory power of comparative advantage theory.
- Does not account for the costs or barriers of trade.
- Has been used to justify exploitative policies.

## Example of Absolute Advantage

Consider two hypothetical countries, Atlantica and Pacifica, with identical populations and resource endowments, each producing two products: butter and bacon. Each year, Atlantica can produce 12 tubs of butter or 6 slabs of bacon, while Pacifica can produce 6 tubs of butter or 12 slabs of bacon.

Each country needs at least 4 tubs of butter and 4 slabs of bacon to survive. In a state of self-sufficiency, Atlantica could spend one-third of the year making butter and two-thirds making bacon, producing a total of 4 tubs of butter and 4 slabs of bacon.

Pacifica could spend one-third of the year making bacon and two-thirds making butter, producing the same totals: 4 tubs of butter and 4 slabs of bacon. This leaves each country at the edge of subsistence, with barely enough of each product to survive. Note, however, that Atlantica has an absolute advantage in producing butter, while Pacifica has an absolute advantage in producing bacon.

If each country specializes in its area of absolute advantage, Atlantica can produce 12 tubs of butter and no bacon in a year, while Pacifica produces 12 slabs of bacon and no butter. Through specialization, the two countries divide their labor.

If they then trade 6 tubs of butter for 6 slabs of bacon, each country ends up with 6 tubs of butter and 6 slabs of bacon -- better off than before, when each could only produce 4 of each good on its own.

## How Does Absolute Advantage Benefit a Country?

The concept of absolute advantage was introduced by Adam Smith in *The Wealth of Nations* to show how countries can gain from trade by specializing in producing and exporting goods they make more efficiently than others, while importing goods that other countries produce more efficiently. Specializing in and trading products where they hold an absolute advantage can benefit both countries, so long as each has at least one product in which it holds an absolute advantage. [1]

## How Does Absolute Advantage Differ from Comparative Advantage?

Absolute advantage is the ability of an entity to produce a product or service at a lower absolute unit cost, using fewer inputs or a more efficient process, than another entity producing the same good or service. Comparative advantage is the ability to produce goods and services at a lower opportunity cost -- not necessarily at greater volume or higher quality. [2] [3]

## Which Countries Have Examples of Absolute Advantage?

Saudi Arabia provides a clear example, with abundant oil supplies that give it an absolute advantage over other nations. [7]

Other examples include Colombia and its climate -- ideal for growing coffee -- and Zambia, home to some of the world's richest copper mines. [8] [9] For Saudi Arabia to attempt growing coffee, or for Colombia to drill for oil, would be an extremely expensive and likely futile undertaking.

## Conclusion

The theory of absolute advantage represents Adam Smith's explanation of why countries benefit from trade: exporting goods in which they hold an absolute advantage and importing others. While the theory offers an elegant and simple account of the benefits of trade, it does not fully explain the gains from international commerce. That fuller explanation would later come from David Ricardo's theory of comparative advantage. [1] [4]
