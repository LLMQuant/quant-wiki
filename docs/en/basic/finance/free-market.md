# What Is a Free Market?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A free market is an economic system based on supply and demand with little or no government control. A central principle of the free market is the concept of voluntary exchange, defined as any transaction in which two parties freely trade goods or services.

Free markets are characterized by a spontaneous and decentralized order that emerges through individual economic decision-making. [1] Depending on a country's political and legal framework, a free market economy can range from very broad to entirely illegal.

### Key Takeaways

- A free market is one in which the laws of supply and demand provide the sole basis for the economic system, without government intervention.
- A central principle of the free market is voluntary exchange -- transactions in which buyers and sellers freely trade goods and services.
- While no purely free market economy exists in practice and all markets are constrained to some degree, economists who measure market freedom have found a broadly positive relationship between free markets and measures of economic well-being.

## Understanding the Free Market

The term "free market" is sometimes used as a synonym for laissez-faire capitalism. When most people discuss the "free market," they mean an economy with unimpeded competition and only private transactions between buyers and sellers. However, a more inclusive definition should encompass any voluntary economic activity, so long as it is not controlled by a coercive central authority. [2]

Using this description, laissez-faire capitalism and voluntary socialism are both examples of a free market, even though the latter involves common ownership of the means of production. The critical feature is the absence of coercive impositions or restrictions on economic activity. [3] In a free market, coercion can occur only through prior mutual agreement in a voluntary contract, such as contractual remedies enforced through tort law.

## The Free Market's Connection to Capitalism and Individual Liberty

No modern country operates a completely unfettered free market. That said, the freest markets tend to coincide with countries that value private property, capitalism, and individual rights. [2] This makes sense, because political systems that avoid regulating or subsidizing individual behavior necessarily reduce interference with voluntary economic transactions. Moreover, free markets are more likely to flourish in systems where property rights are well protected and capitalists have an incentive to pursue profits.

## Free Markets and Financial Markets

In a free market, financial markets can develop to meet the financing needs of those who cannot or choose not to self-fund. For example, some individuals or businesses specialize in acquiring savings by consistently spending less than their current wealth. Others specialize in deploying savings to pursue entrepreneurial activities such as starting or expanding a business. These participants can benefit from trading financial securities such as stocks and bonds.

For instance, savers can purchase bonds, exchanging their current savings for an entrepreneur's promise to repay those savings plus interest in the future. With stocks, savings are traded for an ownership claim on future earnings. No modern example of a purely free financial market exists.

## Common Constraints on the Free Market

All constraints on the free market employ implicit or explicit threats of force. Common examples include: prohibitions on specific transactions, taxation, regulations, mandates on specific terms of trade, licensing requirements, fixed exchange rates, competition from publicly provided services, price controls, and quotas on production, purchasing, or hiring.

Common political justifications for imposing constraints on free markets include consumer safety, fairness among various advantaged or disadvantaged groups, and the provision of public goods. Whatever the stated rationale, business firms and other interest groups in society often lobby to shape these constraints in their own favor -- a phenomenon known as rent-seeking. When free market behavior is regulated, the scope of the free market is curtailed but not usually eliminated entirely, and voluntary exchanges may still occur within the framework of government regulations.

Some transactions also take place on illegal markets in defiance of government rules and regulations. In some respects, illegal markets can be considered an underground version of the free market. However, market transactions remain severely constrained because, in illegal markets, competition often takes the form of violent conflict between rival producer or consumer groups rather than free market competition or rent-seeking through the political system. Consequently, competitive advantage tends to flow to those with a relative advantage in violence, making monopolistic or oligopolistic behavior likely and barriers to entry high as weaker participants are driven out.

## Measuring Economic Freedom

To study the effects of free markets on the economy, economists have developed several well-known indices of economic freedom. These include the Index of Economic Freedom published by the Heritage Foundation [4] and the Economic Freedom of the World and Economic Freedom of North America indices published by the Fraser Institute. [5] [6] These indices incorporate items such as the security of property rights, the regulatory burden, and the openness of financial markets. Empirical analysis comparing these indices with various measures of economic growth, development, and living standards shows overwhelming evidence of a relationship between free markets and material well-being across countries. [7] [8]

## What Are Some Examples of Free Market Economies?

Most countries exhibit a blend of free market and planned economic characteristics. Even countries with limited government regulation maintain a degree of intervention. Countries that rank highly on indices of economic freedom -- based on factors associated with free markets such as low taxation and minimal regulation -- include Singapore, Switzerland, and Ireland.

## What Is the Opposite of a Free Market?

The opposite of a free market is a planned or command economy. In such a system, the government controls most or all of the means of production and the distribution of wealth. The government may also dictate the prices of goods, services, and labor.

## What Is an Example of Voluntary Exchange in Everyday Life?

Examples of voluntary exchange abound in everyday American life. You might visit a coffee shop each morning, for instance. The shop owner is free to set prices as they wish. As a consumer, you are free to decide whether or not to buy.

## Conclusion

A free market is an economic system grounded in voluntary exchange and the laws of supply and demand. Crucially, a free market is defined by the absence of government control. While no modern country has a completely free market, those with relatively free markets tend to value private property, capitalism, and individual liberty.
