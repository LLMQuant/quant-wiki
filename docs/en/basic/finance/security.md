# What Is a Security?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The term "security" refers to a broad range of investments such as stocks, bonds, investment contracts, notes, and derivatives. A security may represent ownership in a corporation in the form of stock, a creditor relationship with a government body or corporation in the form of a bond, or ownership rights in the form of an option.

### Key Takeaways

- Securities are fungible and tradable financial instruments used to raise capital in public and private markets.
- There are primarily three types of securities: equity, which provides holders with ownership rights; debt, which is essentially a loan repaid through periodic payments; and hybrids, which combine aspects of both debt and equity.
- Public sales of securities are regulated by the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).
- Self-regulatory organizations such as the NASD, NFA, and FINRA also play an important role in regulating derivative securities.

## Understanding Securities

The Securities Act of 1933 was the first federal legislation to regulate the U.S. stock market, which had previously been governed at the state level. Under the law, anyone wishing to sell investment contracts to the public must publish certain information about the proposed offering, the issuing company, and its key principals.

These requirements are designed to protect the investing public from deceptive or misleading marketing practices. The company and its principal officers bear strict liability for any inaccuracies in their financial statements, whether intentional or not. Subsequent legislation established the SEC, the regulatory body responsible for oversight and enforcement.

Although the term "security" is commonly associated with stocks, bonds, and similar instruments, the U.S. Supreme Court has interpreted it more broadly. In SEC v. W.J. Howey Co. (1946), the Court ruled that the sale of land and agricultural services constituted an "investment contract" -- even in the absence of any stock or bond certificate.

That case established the four-pronged Howey Test, which holds that an investment may be regulated as a security if it meets certain conditions.

Under this rule, it does not matter whether the offering takes the form of a formal legal contract or stock certificate; any type of investment offering can qualify as a security. On multiple occasions, courts have enforced securities provisions against unconventional assets such as whiskey, beavers, and chinchillas. The SEC has also sought to bring enforcement actions against issuers of cryptocurrencies and non-fungible tokens.

## Types of Securities

Securities can be broadly categorized into two types: equity and debt. However, some hybrid securities combine elements of both.

Equity securities represent an ownership interest held by shareholders in an entity (a corporation, partnership, or trust), realized in the form of shares of capital stock, which include both common and preferred shares.

Holders of equity securities are typically not entitled to regular payments -- although equities often pay dividends -- but they can profit from capital gains when they sell the securities (assuming the value has increased).

Equity securities grant holders proportional control over the company through voting rights. In the event of bankruptcy, equity holders share only in the residual interest remaining after all obligations to creditors have been satisfied. They are sometimes offered as payment-in-kind.

Debt securities represent borrowed money that must be repaid, with terms stipulating the loan size, interest rate, and maturity or renewal date.

Debt securities -- including government and corporate bonds, certificates of deposit (CDs), and collateralized securities such as CDOs and CMOs -- generally entitle holders to regular interest payments and repayment of principal (regardless of the issuer's performance), along with any other stipulated contractual rights (excluding voting rights).

They are typically issued for a fixed term, at the end of which they can be redeemed by the issuer. Debt securities may be secured (backed by collateral) or unsecured; if secured, they may be contractually prioritized over other unsecured, subordinated debt in bankruptcy.

As the name suggests, hybrid securities combine characteristics of both debt and equity securities.

Examples of hybrid securities include equity warrants (options issued by the company itself, granting shareholders the right to purchase stock within a specific timeframe at a specific price), convertible bonds (bonds that can be converted into common shares of the issuing company), and preferred stock (company shares whose interest, dividends, or other capital returns may be prioritized over those of other shareholders).

**Important:** Although preferred stock is technically classified as an equity security, it is often treated as a debt security because it "behaves like a bond." Preferred stock offers a fixed dividend rate and is a popular instrument for income-seeking investors. It is essentially a fixed-income security.

Derivatives are financial contracts whose price is determined by the value of some underlying asset, such as a stock, bond, or commodity. The most commonly traded derivatives include call options (which gain value when the underlying asset appreciates) and put options (which gain value when the underlying asset depreciates).

Asset-backed securities represent a share of a large pool of similar assets, such as loans, leases, credit card debt, mortgages, or any other income-generating asset. Over time, cash flows from these assets are pooled and distributed among different investors.

## How Securities Trade

Publicly traded securities are listed on stock exchanges, where issuers can seek listings and attract investors by ensuring a liquid, regulated market. In recent years, informal electronic trading systems have become increasingly common, and securities are now frequently traded "over the counter" (OTC) -- directly between investors online or by telephone.

An initial public offering (IPO) represents a company's first major sale of equity securities to the public. Following an IPO, any newly issued stock -- while still sold on the primary market -- is referred to as a secondary offering.

Alternatively, securities may be offered privately to a restricted and qualified group in what is known as a private placement -- an important distinction in both corporate law and securities regulation. Companies sometimes sell shares through a combination of public and private placements.

In the secondary market (also known as the aftermarket), securities are simply transferred as assets from one investor to another: shareholders can sell their securities to other investors in exchange for cash and/or capital gains.

The secondary market thus supplements the primary market. The secondary market is less liquid for privately placed securities, since they are not publicly traded and can only be transferred among qualified investors.

## Investing in Securities

The entity that creates securities for sale is known as the issuer, while those who buy them are investors. Generally, securities represent an investment and serve as a means by which municipalities, corporations, and other commercial enterprises raise new capital. Companies can generate substantial funds when they go public -- for example, by selling stock in an IPO.

City, state, or county governments can raise funds for a particular project by issuing municipal bonds. Depending on an institution's market demand or pricing structure, raising capital through securities may be a preferable alternative to financing through bank loans.

On the other hand, purchasing securities with borrowed money -- an act known as buying on margin -- is a popular investment technique. In essence, a company may deliver property rights, in the form of cash or other securities, either at inception or upon default, to pay its obligations to another entity. These collateral arrangements have been growing in recent years, particularly among institutional investors.

## Regulation of Securities

In the United States, the SEC regulates the public offering and sale of securities.

Public offerings, sales, and trades of U.S. securities must be registered and filed with the SEC's state securities departments. Self-regulatory organizations (SROs) within the brokerage industry also assume regulatory roles. Examples of SROs include the National Association of Securities Dealers (NASD) and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA).

## Residual Securities

Residual securities are a type of convertible security -- that is, they can be changed into another form, typically common stock. A convertible bond, for example, is a residual security because it allows the bondholder to convert the security into common shares.

Preferred stock may also have a convertible feature. Corporations may offer residual securities to attract investment capital when competition for funds is intense.

When a residual security is converted or exercised, it increases the number of outstanding common shares. This can dilute the total share pool and its price. Dilution also affects financial analysis metrics such as earnings per share, since the company's earnings must be divided among a greater number of shares.

Conversely, if a publicly traded company takes measures to reduce its total number of outstanding shares, the company is said to have consolidated them. The net effect of this action is to increase the value of each individual share. This is often done to attract more or larger investors, such as mutual funds.

## Other Types of Securities

Certificated securities are those represented in physical, paper form. Securities may also be held in the direct registration system, which records shares in book-entry form. In other words, a transfer agent maintains the shares on behalf of the company without the need for physical certificates.

Modern technologies and policies have largely eliminated the need for certificates and for the issuer to maintain a complete security register. A system has been developed whereby issuers can deposit a single global certificate representing all outstanding securities into a universal depository known as the Depository Trust Company (DTC).

All securities traded through DTC are held in electronic form. It is important to note that certificated and uncertificated securities do not differ in terms of the rights or privileges of the shareholder or issuer.

Bearer securities are negotiable and entitle the shareholder to the rights under the security. They are transferred between investors, in some cases by endorsement and delivery. In terms of proprietary nature, pre-electronic bearer securities were always undivided, meaning each security constitutes a separate asset, legally distinct from others in the same issue.

Depending on market practice, undivided security assets may be fungible or (less commonly) non-fungible, meaning that upon lending, the borrower can return assets equivalent to the original asset or the specific identical asset at the end of the loan. In some cases, bearer securities may be used to facilitate tax evasion and may therefore be viewed negatively by issuers, shareholders, and fiscal regulatory bodies. They are rare in the United States.

Registered securities bear the holder's name and other necessary details, maintained by the issuer in a register. Transfers of registered securities are made through amendments to the register. Registered debt securities are always undivided, meaning the entire issue constitutes a single asset, with each security being a part of the whole. Undivided securities are fungible by nature. Secondary market shares are also always undivided.

Letter securities are not registered with the SEC and cannot be sold publicly on the market. Letter securities -- also known as restricted securities, letter stock, or letter bonds -- are sold directly by the issuer to the investor. The term derives from the SEC's requirement for a buyer's "investment letter" stating that the purchase is for investment purposes and not for resale. When changing hands, these letters often require SEC Form 4.

Cabinet securities are listed under a major financial exchange such as the NYSE but are not actively traded. Held by an inactive investment crowd, they are more likely to be bonds than stocks. The "cabinet" refers to the historical physical location where bond orders were stored off the trading floor. Cabinets typically hold limit orders, which are kept on file until they expire or are executed.

## Issuing Securities: An Example

Consider the case of XYZ, a successful startup interested in raising capital to fuel its next stage of growth. Until now, the startup's ownership has been divided between its two founders. It has several options for accessing capital. It can tap public markets by conducting an IPO, or it can raise funds by offering its shares to investors in a private placement.

The former method enables the company to generate more capital, but it comes with hefty fees and disclosure requirements. In the latter method, shares are traded on the secondary market and not subject to public scrutiny. Both cases, however, involve the distribution of shares that dilute the founders' stakes and confer ownership rights upon investors. This is an example of equity securities.

Next, consider a government interested in raising money to revitalize its economy. It uses bonds -- debt securities -- to raise the amount, promising regular payments to coupon holders.

Finally, look at the case of startup ABC. It raises money from private investors, including family and friends. The startup's founders offer their investors a convertible note that converts into shares of the startup upon a later qualifying event. Most such events are funding rounds. The note is essentially a debt security, since it is a loan made by investors to the startup's founders.

At a later stage, the note converts into equity in the form of a predefined number of shares, giving investors a stake in the company. This is an example of a hybrid security.

## What Is the Difference Between Stocks and Securities?

Stocks, or equity shares, are one type of security. Each share of stock represents fractional ownership of a public corporation and may include the right to vote for company directors or to receive a small portion of the profits. There are many other types of securities as well, such as bonds, derivatives, and asset-backed securities.

## What Are Marketable Securities?

Marketable securities are any type of stock, bond, or other security that can be readily bought and sold on a public exchange. For example, shares of public companies can be traded on a stock exchange, and Treasury bonds can be bought and sold on the bond market.

Conversely, non-marketable securities are those that cannot be legally sold to the public. For instance, shares in unlisted companies can only be bought and sold under very limited circumstances.

## What Are Treasury Securities?

Treasury securities are debt instruments issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury to raise funds for the government. Because these bonds are backed by the government, they are considered very low-risk and are highly attractive to risk-averse investors.

## The Bottom Line

Securities represent the most common types of investment contracts. When saving for retirement, most people choose to invest a portion of their savings in equity or debt securities. These securities markets are also vital to the broader economy, as they allow companies to raise capital from the public.
