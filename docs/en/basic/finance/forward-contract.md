# What Is a Forward Contract?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A forward contract is a customized agreement between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a future date. Forward contracts can be used for hedging or speculation, although their non-standardized nature makes them particularly well suited for hedging.

### Key Takeaways

- A forward contract is a customizable derivative contract between two parties to buy or sell an asset at a specified price on a future date.
- Forward contracts can be tailored to a specific commodity, amount, and delivery date.
- Forward contracts do not trade on a centralized exchange and are therefore considered over-the-counter (OTC) instruments.
- For example, forward contracts can help producers and users of agricultural products hedge against changes in the price of an underlying asset or commodity.
- Financial institutions that initiate forward contracts face a higher degree of settlement and default risk compared to contracts that are regularly marked to market.

## Understanding Forward Contracts

Unlike standard futures contracts, a forward contract can be customized to a specific commodity, amount, and delivery date. Commodities traded can include grains, precious metals, natural gas, oil, or even poultry. Settlement of a forward contract can occur on a cash or delivery basis, depending on the terms of the contract.

Forward contracts do not trade on a centralized exchange and are therefore considered over-the-counter (OTC) instruments. While their OTC nature makes it easier to customize terms, the lack of a centralized clearinghouse also gives rise to a higher degree of default risk.

**Important:** Because of their potential for default risk and the absence of a centralized clearinghouse, forward contracts are not as readily available to retail investors as futures contracts.

## Forward Contracts vs. Futures Contracts

Both forward and futures contracts involve an agreement to buy or sell a commodity at a set price in the future, but there are several subtle differences between the two. Forward contracts do not trade on an exchange, while futures contracts do.

Forward contracts are settled at the end of the contract, whereas futures contracts settle on a daily basis. Most importantly, futures contracts are standardized agreements and are not customized between the two parties. [1]

## Risks of Forward Contracts

The forward contract market is enormous because many of the world's largest corporations use it to hedge currency and interest rate risk. However, since the details of forward contracts are limited to the buyer and seller and are not publicly disclosed, the size of this market is difficult to estimate.

The large size and unregulated nature of the forward contract market means that, in a worst-case scenario, it may be susceptible to a cascading series of defaults. While banks and financial corporations mitigate this risk by carefully selecting their counterparties, the possibility of large-scale default still exists.

Another risk arising from the non-standard nature of forward contracts is that they are only settled on the settlement date and are not marked to market like futures contracts. What happens if the forward rate specified in the contract diverges widely from the spot rate at the time of settlement?

In this case, the financial institution that originated the forward contract faces a greater degree of risk than it would with a contract that is regularly marked to market, which increases the exposure to client default or non-settlement.
