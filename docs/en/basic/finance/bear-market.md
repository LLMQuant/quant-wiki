# What Is a Bear Market?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A bear market is a prolonged period of declining prices in a financial market, typically defined as a drop of 20% or more from recent highs. Bear markets are usually accompanied by widespread investor pessimism, large-scale selling of securities and other assets, and economic weakness.

Bear markets are generally associated with declines in an overall market or index such as the S&P 500, but individual securities or commodities can also be considered in a bear market if they experience a decline of 20% or more over a sustained period (usually two months or longer). Bear markets may also coincide with broader economic downturns such as recessions. They are considered the opposite of upward-trending bull markets.

### Key Takeaways

- A bear market occurs when market prices decline more than 20%, typically accompanied by negative investor sentiment and a weakening economy.
- Bear markets can be cyclical or secular. The former lasts several weeks or months, while the latter can persist for years or even decades.
- Short selling, put options, and inverse ETFs are among the ways investors can profit during a bear market as prices fall.

## Understanding Bear Markets

Stock prices generally reflect investor expectations for corporate performance. When a company's earnings fall short of expectations, or growth lags behind analyst forecasts, investors may respond by selling the stock, driving the overall price lower. Herd behavior and fear can trigger a rush to minimize losses, which in turn can lead to a prolonged period of depressed asset prices.

One definition holds that a market enters bear territory when stocks decline at least 20% on average from their highs. But 20% is an arbitrary figure, just as a 10% decline is an arbitrary benchmark for a correction. Another definition of a bear market is when investors are more risk-averse than risk-seeking. This type of bear market can last months or years as investors shun speculation in favor of dull but reliable investments.

The causes of bear markets vary, but in general, a weak or slowing economy, bursting market bubbles, pandemics, wars, geopolitical crises, and dramatic paradigm shifts in the economy (such as the transition to an online economy) are all factors that can trigger one. Signs of a weak or slowing economy typically include:

- Low employment
- Low disposable income
- Weak productivity
- Declining corporate profits

Government intervention in the economy can also trigger a bear market. For example, changes in tax rates or the federal funds rate can lead to bear markets. Similarly, a decline in investor confidence may signal the onset of a bear market. When investors believe something is about to happen, they take action -- in an approaching bear market, selling stocks to avoid losses.

Bear markets can last several years or only a few weeks. A secular bear market can last 10 to 20 years, characterized by consistently below-average returns. There may be rallies within secular bear markets where stocks or indices stage temporary recoveries, but the gains do not hold and prices revert to lower levels. A cyclical bear market, on the other hand, can last anywhere from a few weeks to several months.

On December 24, 2018, U.S. major market indices came close to bear market territory, falling just short of a 20% drawdown. [1] More recently, major indices including the S&P 500 and the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) fell sharply into bear market territory between March 11 and March 12, 2020. [2] Before that, the last prolonged bear market in the United States occurred during the 2007-2009 financial crisis and lasted approximately 17 months. The S&P 500 lost 50% of its value during that period. [3]

In February 2020, global stocks entered a sudden bear market in the wake of the global coronavirus pandemic, with the DJIA falling 38% from its all-time high of 29,568.77 on February 12 to a low of 18,213.65 on March 23 -- a span of just over one month. [4] However, both the S&P 500 and the Nasdaq 100 reached new highs by August 2020. [5]

## Phases of a Bear Market

Bear markets typically unfold in four distinct phases.

The first phase is characterized by high prices and elevated investor sentiment. Toward the end of this phase, investors begin to exit the market and take profits.

In the second phase, stock prices start to fall sharply, trading activity and corporate profits decline, and formerly positive economic indicators begin to drop below average. Some investors start to panic as sentiment deteriorates. This is known as capitulation.

The third phase sees speculators enter the market, pushing some prices and trading volumes higher.

In the fourth and final phase, stock prices continue to decline, but at a slower pace. Low prices and positive news begin to attract investors again, and the bear market gives way to a bull market.

**"Bear" and "Bull":** The bear market phenomenon is thought to get its name from the way a bear attacks its prey -- swiping its paws downward. This is why a market with falling stock prices is called a bear market. Similarly, a bull market may be named after the way a bull attacks by thrusting its horns upward into the air.

## Bear Markets vs. Corrections

A bear market should not be confused with a correction, which is a short-term trend lasting less than two months.

While corrections offer value investors an opportunity to find entry points into the stock market, bear markets rarely provide suitable entry points. This barrier exists because it is nearly impossible to identify the bottom of a bear market. Trying to recover losses can be an uphill battle unless investors are short sellers or use other strategies to profit in declining markets.

Between 1900 and 2018, the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) experienced approximately 33 bear markets, averaging roughly one every three years. [6] One of the most notable bear markets in recent history coincided with the global financial crisis from October 2007 to March 2009. During that period, the DJIA fell 54%. [3] The global COVID-19 pandemic caused the most recent 2020 bear market in the S&P 500 and the DJIA. The Nasdaq Composite most recently entered bear market territory in March 2022 amid concerns about the war in Ukraine, economic sanctions on Russia, and high inflation. [7]

## Short Selling in a Bear Market

Investors can make money in a bear market through short selling. This technique involves selling borrowed shares and buying them back at a lower price. It is an extremely risky trade that can cause heavy losses if it fails. A short seller must borrow shares from a broker before placing a short sell order. The profit or loss is the difference between the price at which the shares are sold and the price at which they are bought back, referred to as "covering."

For example, an investor shorts 100 shares at $94. The price falls and the shares are covered at $84. The investor pockets a profit of $10 x 100 = $1,000. If the stock unexpectedly rises, the investor is forced to buy back shares at a premium, potentially causing heavy losses.

Short selling is a risky trading strategy that can result in substantial losses. It is not suitable for inexperienced investors.

## Put Options and Inverse ETFs in a Bear Market

A put option gives the owner the freedom, but not the obligation, to sell a stock at a specific price on or before a certain date. Put options can be used to speculate on falling stock prices and to hedge against declining prices to protect long-only portfolios. Investors must have options privileges in their accounts to make such trades. Outside of a bear market, buying puts is generally safer than short selling.

Inverse ETFs are designed to change in value in the opposite direction of the index they track. For example, if the S&P 500 falls 1%, an inverse ETF on the S&P 500 would rise 1%. There are many leveraged inverse ETFs that magnify the returns of the index they track by two and three times. Like options, inverse ETFs can be used to speculate or protect portfolios.

## Real-World Examples of Bear Markets

The ballooning housing mortgage default crisis caught up with the stock market in October 2007. At that time, the S&P 500 had touched a high of 1,565.15 on October 9, 2007. [8] By March 5, 2009, it had crashed to 682.55 as the extent and ramifications of housing mortgage defaults on the overall economy became clear. [9] U.S. major market indices again approached bear market territory on December 24, 2018, falling just short of a 20% drawdown. [1]

Most recently, the DJIA entered a bear market on March 11, 2020, and the S&P 500 entered a bear market on March 12, 2020. [10] This followed the index's longest bull market on record, which began in March 2009. Stocks were driven down by the impact of the COVID-19 pandemic, which brought widespread lockdowns and the fear of a collapse in consumer demand. During this period, the Dow dropped sharply from near 30,000 to below 19,000 in the span of a few weeks. From February 19 to March 23, the S&P 500 declined 34%. [11][12]

Other examples include the aftermath of the dot-com bubble bursting in March 2000, which wiped out approximately 49% of the S&P 500's value and lasted until October 2002; [13] and the Great Depression, which began with the stock market crash on October 28-29, 1929. [14]

## What Is the Key Difference Between Bear and Bull Markets?

The key difference between bear and bull markets is that a bear market represents a significant downturn in financial markets, while a bull market represents a significant upturn. During bull markets, the market performs well; during bear markets, it performs poorly.

## Is It Good to Buy During a Bear Market?

Long-term investors can find many valuable stocks at lower prices during a bear market, and if you have the ability to wait for your investments to recover, a bear market is a good time to buy. Traders seeking short-term profits may need to employ other strategies during a bear market, such as short selling.

## Should I Sell My Stocks During a Bear Market?

For most investors, a buy-and-hold strategy is the best way to make money through investing rather than rushing to buy or sell with every market shift. If you hold a balanced, diversified portfolio that includes assets such as government bonds, defensive stocks, and cash alongside equities, you do not need to sell during a bear market. In fact, selling stocks during a bear market out of fear that they will fall further could mean missing out on substantial profits when the market eventually rebounds.

## The Bottom Line

A bear market is a downward trend in financial markets that signals a weak economy and a loss of investor confidence. Markets are generally considered to be in bear territory when prices fall more than 20%. Bear markets can be as short as a few weeks or as long as several years.

Buy-and-hold investors can often take advantage of lower prices during bear markets to add valuable stocks to their portfolios. However, day traders and other short-term investors may need to use strategies such as short selling, put options, and inverse ETFs to profit during a bear market.
