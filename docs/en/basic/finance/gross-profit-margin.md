# What Is Gross Profit Margin?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Gross profit margin is a financial metric that analysts use to assess a company's financial health. It represents profit after deducting the cost of goods sold (COGS).

Put simply, a company's gross profit margin is the money it earns after accounting for operating costs. The metric is usually expressed as a percentage of sales and may also be referred to as the gross margin ratio.

**Gross Profit vs. Gross Profit Margin:** Gross profit is the total profit a company earns after deducting operating costs -- that is, total sales or revenue minus COGS. This figure is expressed in dollar terms. Gross profit margin, on the other hand, expresses the profit a company earns as a percentage.

### Key Takeaways

- Gross profit margin is an analytical metric expressed as a company's net sales minus the cost of goods sold.
- Gross profit margin is typically shown as gross profit as a percentage of net sales.
- Gross profit margin shows the amount of profit earned before deducting selling, general, and administrative expenses, which yield the company's net profit margin.

## Gross Profit Margin Formula and Calculation

A company's gross profit margin is calculated using the following formula:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{Gross Profit Margin}=\frac{\text{Net Sales }-\text{ COGS}}{\text{Net Sales}}\\ \end{aligned} $$

This requires first subtracting COGS from the company's net sales (gross revenue minus returns, allowances, and discounts). That figure is then divided by net sales to calculate the gross profit margin as a percentage.

**Note:** Analysts use a company's gross profit margin to compare its business model with that of its competitors.

## How Gross Profit Margin Works

Gross profit is the total profit a company earns after deducting operating costs. Put simply, gross profit equals total sales or revenue minus COGS. This figure is expressed in dollar terms. Gross profit margin, on the other hand, is the company's profit expressed as a percentage using the formula above.

It is one of the key metrics analysts and investors track because it helps determine whether a company is financially healthy. Companies can also use it to identify areas where they can improve by cutting costs and/or increasing sales. Both sales and COGS are found on a company's income statement. A higher gross profit margin is desirable, indicating that the company is operating efficiently, while a lower margin signals areas that need improvement.

Product pricing adjustments can affect gross profit margin. All else being equal, a company that sells its products at a premium earns a higher gross margin. But this can be a delicate balancing act -- if prices are set too high, fewer customers may purchase the product. As a result, the company may lose market share.

Significant fluctuations in margins can signal poor management and/or inferior product quality. However, such changes may be justified when a company is making comprehensive operational changes. Any temporary volatility should therefore not be a cause for concern. For example, if a company automates parts of its supply chain, the initial investment may be high, but the cost of goods ultimately decreases due to lower labor costs associated with automation.

**Important:** It is essential to compare gross profit margins among companies in the same industry. This allows you to determine which companies are leaders and which are lagging.

## Gross Profit Margin vs. Net Profit Margin vs. Operating Profit Margin

Gross profit margin is one of the key profitability metrics analysts and investors monitor. The other two are net profit margin and operating profit margin.

Net profit margin is also a critical financial metric that speaks to a company's financial health. Also known as net margin, it indicates the amount of profit generated as a percentage of the company's revenue. Simply put, a company's net profit margin is the ratio of net income to revenue.

Management can use the net profit margin to identify inefficiencies and assess whether the current business model is working.

Net profit margin is calculated by subtracting COGS, operating and other expenses, interest, and taxes from a company's revenue. That figure is then divided by total revenue for the period. To express it as a percentage, multiply by 100.

A company's operating profit margin, or operating profit, shows how much profit it generates from its core operations after taking all operating expenses into account. This type of margin considers additional expenses such as interest and overhead.

Operating profit margin is calculated by subtracting all COGS, operating expenses, depreciation, and amortization from total revenue. Like gross profit margin and net profit margin, it is expressed as a percentage by multiplying the result by 100.

## Gross Profit Margin Example

Suppose both Company ABC and Company XYZ produce widgets with identical characteristics and comparable quality levels.

If Company ABC finds a way to manufacture its product at one-fifth the cost, it achieves a higher gross margin due to lower COGS. This gives ABC a competitive edge in the market. To compensate for its margin loss, Company XYZ doubles its product prices to boost revenue.

This strategy has a potential drawback: if customers are deterred by the higher price, it could backfire. In that scenario, XYZ loses both gross margin and market share.

## What Does Gross Profit Margin Indicate?

A company's gross profit margin indicates the profit it earns after accounting for the direct costs associated with operations. Simply put, it shows how well a company converts its sales into profit. Expressed as a percentage, it represents revenue minus COGS, which includes labor and materials.

## What Is the Difference Between High and Low Gross Profit Margins?

Companies strive for high gross profit margins because they signal greater profitability. When a company carries a high margin, it is operating efficiently. As long as operating expenses remain under control, the company can sustain this level.

Conversely, a low gross profit margin is a concern. It affects the bottom line and indicates that there are areas ripe for improvement.

## How Can a Company Improve Its Gross Profit Margin?

A company can improve its gross profit margin -- and therefore its profitability -- through several approaches, including reducing input costs, improving operational efficiency, raising prices strategically, and optimizing product mix.

## The Bottom Line

As an investor, you need to examine several key financial metrics to make informed decisions about the companies you add to your portfolio. Start by looking at the gross profit margins of the businesses that interest you. You can calculate it by subtracting COGS from a company's revenue -- both figures can be found on the income statement.

The higher the margin, the more profitable and efficient the company. But be sure to compare margins among companies in the same industry, since variables are similar.
