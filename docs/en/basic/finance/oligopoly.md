# What Is an Oligopoly?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

An oligopoly is a market structure in which a small number of firms dominate the industry. In an oligopolistic market, producers may directly or indirectly restrict output or set prices to achieve above-normal returns. A defining feature of oligopoly is that no single firm can prevent other participants from exerting significant influence over the market. Unlike a monopoly, where one company controls the entire market, an oligopoly involves shared market power among several firms.

### Key Takeaways

- An oligopoly is a market structure in which a handful of producers work to limit output or fix prices in order to earn above-normal market returns.
- Economic, legal, and technological factors can contribute to the formation, maintenance, or dissolution of oligopolies.
- The central difficulty facing oligopolists is the prisoner's dilemma, which incentivizes each member to cheat on the group's cooperative agreements.
- Government policy can either discourage or encourage oligopolistic behavior; firms in mixed economies often seek government approval to limit competition.

## Understanding Oligopolies

Market structures vary in form and scale. The term is used to distinguish among industries composed of different firms selling products and services. Most market structures aspire to perfect competition -- a theoretical construct that does not exist in practice.

Oligopolistic markets are characterized by a few dominant firms that set prices -- either collectively (as in a cartel) or under the leadership of a single firm -- rather than accepting prices determined by the market. As a result, profit margins are higher than in more competitive environments.

Barriers to entry in an oligopoly include economies of scale, regulatory hurdles, access to supply and distribution channels, capital requirements, and brand loyalty. These obstacles prevent new participants from entering the market.

Historically, oligopolies have existed in steel manufacturing, oil production, railroads, tire manufacturing, grocery chains, and wireless telecommunications. The economic and legal concern is that an oligopoly can block new entrants, slow innovation, and raise prices -- all to the detriment of consumers.

## Special Considerations

Governments sometimes respond to oligopolies through laws prohibiting price-fixing and collusion. However, a cartel can engage in price manipulation if it operates beyond government reach or with government endorsement. Oligopolies in mixed economies frequently seek -- and lobby for -- favorable government policies in order to operate under the regulation, or even direct oversight, of government agencies.

The principal challenge confronting firms within an oligopoly is that each has an incentive to cheat. If all members agree to collectively restrict supply and keep prices high, any individual firm stands to capture substantial market share by breaking the agreement and undercutting its rivals. This competition can occur through pricing or simply through an individual firm expanding the volume of output it brings to market.

**Important:** Firms in an oligopoly benefit from price-fixing, setting prices collectively or under the direction of one firm, rather than relying on free-market forces.

## Characteristics of an Oligopoly

Oligopolies tend to be stable. One of the primary reasons for this stability is that participating firms must perceive the benefits of cooperation as outweighing the costs of competition before they agree not to compete.

These firms sometimes find creative ways to avoid the appearance of price-fixing, such as using lunar phases to schedule price changes. Price-fixing involves setting prices by agreement rather than allowing them to be determined by market forces. Another approach is for firms to follow a recognized price leader: when the leader raises prices, the others follow suit.

Conditions that enable oligopolies include high entry costs in the form of capital expenditure, legal privileges (such as licenses to use wireless spectrum or railroad right-of-way), and platforms that gain value with additional users, such as social media networks.

Global shifts in technology and trade have altered some of these conditions. For example, the rise of offshore production and mini-mills has reshaped the steel industry. In office software applications, Microsoft (MSFT) became a target for Google Docs, which Google funded with cash generated by its web search business.

## Oligopoly and Game Theory

Game theorists have developed models for oligopolistic scenarios that form a type of prisoner's dilemma. When costs and benefits reach an equilibrium such that no firm has an incentive to break from the group, the result is considered a Nash equilibrium for the oligopoly. This can be achieved through contractual or market conditions, legal constraints, or strategic relationships among members that enable punishment of defectors.

Sustaining an oligopoly and broadly coordinating action among buyers and sellers involves shaping the payoffs of various prisoner's dilemma and related coordination games that repeat over time.

As a result, many of the same institutional factors that reduce prisoner's dilemma problems and facilitate market economies -- such as secure contract enforcement, cultural norms of high trust and reciprocity, and laissez-faire economic policies -- may also help encourage and sustain oligopolies.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Oligopolies

One of the primary advantages of an oligopoly is that competition is significantly limited due to the small number of market participants. With few competitors, oligopolists can earn higher profits.

Oligopolies also present higher barriers to entry for newcomers. The elevated costs of doing business, the regulatory environment, and challenges in accessing supply and distribution channels make market entry difficult.

On the other hand, the lack of competition may diminish the incentive to innovate. With limited product diversity, consumers tend to remain loyal to familiar offerings.

#### Advantages

- Limited competition
- Higher corporate profits
- Greater consumer demand concentration

#### Disadvantages

- High barriers to entry for new participants
- Lack of innovation
- Limited consumer choice

## Examples of Oligopolies

Numerous examples of oligopolies exist across global markets. One of the most prominent is the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries (OPEC). Founded in Baghdad in 1960 with five member nations, the organization had expanded to 13 oil-producing countries by 1975. [1]

OPEC is widely regarded as an oligopoly because no overarching authority governs the group. Each member nation holds a significant share of the collective market. These countries wield considerable power over supply, demand, and pricing -- not individually, but as a group. When OPEC reduces supply in the face of declining demand, prices rise; the reverse occurs when demand increases.

## What Are the Negative Effects of an Oligopoly?

An oligopoly is a market structure in which a small number of firms exert significant control. These firms may collude to set prices, ultimately offering non-competitive pricing. Other adverse effects include restricting new entrants and reducing innovation. Oligopolies have been found in the oil industry, railroads, wireless carriers, and major technology sectors.

## Is the Current U.S. Airline Industry an Oligopoly?

As of 2021, just four companies controlled nearly two-thirds of all domestic flights in the United States, leading many observers to characterize the airline industry as an oligopoly. These four companies are Delta Air Lines, United Airlines Holdings, Southwest Airlines, and American Airlines. According to a White House report, "reduced competition has contributed to higher fees such as baggage and cancellation charges. These fees tend to rise in lockstep, suggesting a lack of meaningful competitive pressure, and are often hidden from consumers at the point of purchase." [2] Notably, the Airline Deregulation Act of 1978 stripped the Civil Aeronautics Board of its authority to regulate the industry. [3] Prior to deregulation, the airline industry operated more like a public utility, and fare prices had been declining in the two decades before deregulation was introduced.

## The Bottom Line

Perfect competition does not exist in actual markets. However, different market structures do exist, including oligopolies. These markets are characterized by a small number of participating firms that may collectively set prices.
