# What Is Compounding?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Compounding is the process by which an asset's earnings -- from either capital gains or interest -- are reinvested to generate additional earnings over time. This growth is calculated using an exponential function because the investment generates returns not only on the initial principal but also on the accumulated earnings from prior periods.

Compounding therefore differs from linear growth, where only the principal earns interest each period.

### Key Takeaways

- Compounding is the process of crediting interest on both the existing principal and the interest already paid.
- Compounding can thus be interpreted as "interest on interest" -- the effect of which is to magnify returns over time, a phenomenon known as the "miracle of compounding."
- When banks or financial institutions credit compound interest, they use a compounding period such as annual, monthly, or daily.
- Compounding can work on the growth side of investments, where returns accumulate faster, or on debt, where the amount owed can grow even as payments are being made.
- Compounding occurs naturally in savings accounts; certain dividend-paying investments can also benefit from compounding.

## Understanding Compounding

Compounding generally refers to the increasing value of an asset as a result of the interest earned on both the principal and the accumulated interest. This phenomenon, a direct realization of the time value of money concept, is also known as compound interest.

Compounding is crucial in finance, and the gains attributable to its effects are the motivation behind many investment strategies. For example, many corporations offer dividend reinvestment plans (DRIPs) that allow investors to reinvest their cash dividends to purchase additional shares. Reinvesting in these dividend-paying shares accelerates investor returns because the increased number of shares will consistently boost future dividend income, assuming dividends remain steady.

Investing in dividend growth stocks on top of reinvesting dividends adds yet another layer of compounding, which some investors refer to as "double compounding." In this case, not only are dividends being reinvested to buy more shares, but these dividend growth stocks are also increasing their per-share payout.

## The Compounding Formula

The future value (FV) formula for a current asset relies on the concept of compounding. It takes into account the present value of an asset, the annual interest rate, the frequency of compounding per year (or the number of compounding periods), and the total number of years. The general formula for compounding is:

$$ \begin{aligned}&FV = PV \times \Big (1 + \frac{ i }{ n } \Big ) ^ {nt} \\&\textbf{where:} \\&FV = \text{Future value} \\&PV = \text{Present value} \\&i = \text{Annual interest rate} \\&n = \text{Number of compounding periods per cycle} \\&t = \text{Number of time periods} \\\end{aligned} $$

This formula assumes no changes to the original principal balance other than interest.

## Increasing Compounding Periods

The effects of compounding strengthen as the frequency of compounding increases. Assume a one-year time period. The more compounding periods within that year, the higher the future value of the investment; naturally, two compounding periods per year are better than one, and four are better than two.

To illustrate this effect, consider the following example. Assume an investment of $1,000,000 earning 20% per year. The resulting future value, based on varying numbers of compounding periods, is as follows:

- Annual compounding (n = 1): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/1)]^(1 x 1) = $1,200,000
- Semi-annual compounding (n = 2): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/2)]^(2 x 1) = $1,210,000
- Quarterly compounding (n = 4): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/4)]^(4 x 1) = $1,215,506
- Monthly compounding (n = 12): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/12)]^(12 x 1) = $1,219,391
- Weekly compounding (n = 52): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/52)]^(52 x 1) = $1,220,934
- Daily compounding (n = 365): FV = $1,000,000 x [1 + (20%/365)]^(365 x 1) = $1,221,336

As evidenced, even though the number of compounding periods increases dramatically, the incremental gain in future value begins to plateau. The frequency of compounding over a fixed time period has a limited effect on investment growth. This limit is known as continuous compounding, which can be calculated using the formula:

$$ \begin{aligned}&FV=P\times e^{rt}\\&\textbf{where:}\\&e=\text{Irrational number 2.7183}\\&r=\text{Interest rate}\\&t=\text{Time}\end{aligned} $$

In the example above, the future value with continuous compounding equals: FV = $1,000,000 x 2.7183^(0.2 x 1) = $1,221,404.

**Note:** Compounding is an example of a "snowball effect," in which a small initial impact compounds over time into progressively larger and more significant states.

## The Impact of Compounding on Investments and Debt

Compounding works on both assets and liabilities. While compounding rapidly boosts the value of an asset, it can also increase the amount owed on a loan because interest accrues on both the unpaid principal and previous interest charges. Even while making loan payments, compound interest can cause the total amount owed to grow over time.

The concept of compounding is especially tricky for credit card balances. Credit card debt carries not only high interest rates, but interest charges may be added to the principal balance, generating interest on themselves going forward. For this reason, the effect of compounding is not inherently "good" or "bad." The impact of compounding may work in favor of or against an investor depending on their specific financial situation.

## Compounding Example

To illustrate how compounding works, suppose $10,000 is held in an account that pays 5% interest annually. After the first compounding period, the total in the account rises to $10,500, reflecting $500 in interest added to the $10,000 principal. In Year 2, the account earns 5% on both the original principal and the $500 of first-year interest, resulting in a second-year gain of $525 and a balance of $11,025.

| Compounding Example |   |   |   |
|---|---|---|---|
| Compounding Period | Starting Balance | Interest | Ending Balance |
| 1 | $10,000.00 | $500.00 | $10,500.00 |
| 2 | $10,500.00 | $525.00 | $11,025.00 |
| 3 | $11,025.00 | $551.25 | $11,576.25 |
| 4 | $11,576.25 | $578.81 | $12,155.06 |
| 5 | $12,155.06 | $607.75 | $12,762.82 |
| 6 | $12,762.82 | $638.14 | $13,400.96 |
| 7 | $13,400.96 | $670.05 | $14,071.00 |
| 8 | $14,071.00 | $703.55 | $14,774.55 |
| 9 | $14,774.55 | $738.73 | $15,513.28 |
| 10 | $15,513.28 | $775.66 | $16,288.95 |

After 10 years with no withdrawals and a steady 5% annual interest rate, the account would grow to $16,288.95. Without any additions or withdrawals of principal, the effect of compounding causes the periodic balance change to grow from $500 in the first period to $775.66 in the tenth period.

Additionally, after 10 years, the investment has grown by $6,288.95. Had the investment paid only simple interest (5% on the original investment), the annual interest would have been just $500 per year, or $5,000 total over ten years.

## What Is the Rule of 72?

The Rule of 72 is a heuristic used to estimate how long an investment or savings will take to double in value under compounding (or compound returns). The rule states that the number of years required is approximately 72 divided by the interest rate. If the interest rate is 5% with compounding, it would take roughly 14 years and 5 months to double. [4]

## What Is the Difference Between Simple Interest and Compound Interest?

Simple interest pays interest only on the principal amount invested or deposited. For example, if $1,000 is deposited at 5% simple interest, it earns $50 each year. Compound interest, however, pays "interest on interest," so in the first year you earn $50, but in the second year you earn $52.50 ($1,050 x 0.05), and so on.

## How Can I Make My Money Compound?

In addition to compound interest, investors can earn compounding returns by reinvesting dividends. This means using dividend cash received to purchase additional shares of the company, which will themselves pay dividends in the future.

## Which Average Is Best for Compounding?

There are different types of average calculations used in finance. When computing the average return of an investment or savings account with compounding, it is best to use the geometric mean. In finance, this is sometimes called the time-weighted average return or the compound annual growth rate (CAGR).

## What Is the Best Example of Compounding?

A high-yield savings account is a good example of compounding. Suppose you deposit $1,000 into a savings account. In the first year, you earn a certain amount of interest. If you do not spend any of the funds in the account and the interest rate remains at least at the prior year's level, you will earn more interest in the second year. This is because the savings account adds earned interest to the cash balance, resulting in a higher base on which interest accrues.

## Summary

Compounding and compound interest play a significant role in an investor's financial success. If you harness compounding, you will earn money faster; if you bear the burden of compounding debt, you will remain mired in growing obligations for longer. Through compounding, financial balances can grow exponentially -- much faster than under simple linear interest.
