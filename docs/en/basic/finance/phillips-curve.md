# What Is the Phillips Curve?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Phillips Curve is an economic theory positing a stable, inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. Proposed by William Phillips, it asserts that economic growth brings inflation, which in turn should lead to more jobs and less unemployment.

The original concept of the Phillips Curve has been partially discredited by the occurrence of stagflation in the 1970s -- a period characterized by simultaneously high inflation and high unemployment. [1] [2]

### Key Takeaways

- The Phillips Curve states that inflation and unemployment have an inverse relationship: higher inflation is associated with lower unemployment, and vice versa.
- The Phillips Curve was a concept used to guide macroeconomic policy in the 20th century but was called into question during the stagflation of the 1970s.
- Understanding the Phillips Curve through the lens of consumer and worker expectations suggests that the relationship between inflation and unemployment may not hold in the long run -- and possibly not even in the short run.

## Understanding the Phillips Curve

The concept behind the Phillips Curve holds that changes in unemployment within an economy have a predictable effect on price inflation. The inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation is depicted as a downward-sloping, concave curve, with inflation on the Y-axis and unemployment on the X-axis. Rising inflation decreases unemployment, and vice versa. Alternatively, a focus on reducing unemployment also increases inflation, and vice versa. [3]

The prevailing belief in the 1960s was that any fiscal stimulus would increase aggregate demand and trigger the following effects: labor demand rises, the pool of unemployed workers shrinks, companies raise wages to compete for a smaller talent pool, the increased wage costs are then passed on to consumers in the form of higher prices.

This belief system led many governments to adopt a "stop-go" strategy -- setting a target inflation rate and using fiscal and monetary policy to expand or contract the economy to achieve it. However, with the onset of stagflation in the 1970s, the stable trade-off between inflation and unemployment broke down, casting doubt on the validity of the Phillips Curve. [1] [2]

## The Phillips Curve and Stagflation

Stagflation occurs when an economy experiences economic stagnation, high unemployment, and high price inflation simultaneously. This scenario directly contradicts the theory underlying the Phillips Curve. The United States did not experience stagflation until the 1970s, when rising unemployment was not accompanied by falling inflation. [4] Between 1973 and 1975, the U.S. economy posted six consecutive quarters of declining GDP while inflation tripled. [5]

## Expectations and the Long-Run Phillips Curve

The stagflation phenomenon and the breakdown of the Phillips Curve prompted economists to look more closely at the role of expectations in the relationship between unemployment and inflation. Because workers and consumers can adjust their expectations of future inflation based on current inflation and unemployment rates, the inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment may hold only in the short run. [2]

When a central bank raises the inflation rate to push unemployment lower, it may cause an initial shift along the short-run Phillips Curve. However, as workers' and consumers' inflation expectations adapt to the new environment, the Phillips Curve itself may shift outward over the longer term.

This is thought to occur especially around the natural rate of unemployment, or NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment), which essentially represents the normal rate of frictional and institutional unemployment in the economy. Thus, in the long run, if expectations can adapt to changes in inflation rates, the long-run Phillips Curve resembles a vertical line at the NAIRU; monetary policy simply raises or lowers the inflation rate once expectations have adjusted. [6] [2]

During periods of stagflation, workers and consumers may even begin to rationally expect rising inflation rates as soon as they become aware that the monetary authority plans to pursue expansionary policy. This can cause the short-run Phillips Curve to shift outward even before the expansionary monetary policy is implemented, so that even in the short run the policy has little effect on lowering unemployment. In effect, the short-run Phillips Curve also becomes a vertical line at the NAIRU. [2]

## Why Do Some Economists Still Believe in the Phillips Curve?

While the Phillips Curve has its limitations, some economists still consider it worth examining. [7] Policymakers may use it as a general framework for thinking about the relationship between inflation and unemployment -- both key measures of economic performance. Others caution that it fails to capture the complexities of modern markets.

## Why Does the Debate Over the Phillips Curve's Relevance Matter?

Disagreements over the reliability of the Phillips Curve can lead to different economic policies. For example, a policymaker who believes that lower unemployment is associated with higher inflation may seek to implement measures to curb inflation, such as raising interest rates. [8] Another policymaker might disagree with that response.

## Why Has the Phillips Curve Flattened?

During certain periods, unemployment has fallen even as inflation has remained low, suggesting that the Phillips Curve has "flattened." To some extent, this can be attributed to the Federal Reserve's concerted effort to keep inflation low and stable, which may have weakened the link between inflation and labor market performance. [9]

## Conclusion

The Phillips Curve is an economic theory asserting an inverse relationship between inflation and unemployment. It resonated with economists in the 20th century but became increasingly controversial in the 1970s, when inflation and unemployment rose simultaneously. Today, economists have developed new models to explain the relationship between unemployment and inflation. However, some economists still maintain that, despite its limitations, the Phillips Curve is worth considering.
