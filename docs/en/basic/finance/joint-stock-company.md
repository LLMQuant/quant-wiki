# What Is a Joint-Stock Company?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
A joint-stock company is a business owned by its investors, with each investor holding a share of the company proportional to their investment. It is the forerunner of the modern corporation and other types of registered business entities in the United States.

Joint-stock companies were created to finance ventures too expensive for any individual -- or even a government -- to undertake. The owners of a joint-stock company expected to share in its profits.

Today, U.S. businesses are not organized as joint-stock companies. While the term can loosely describe any business with shareholders, there is no such registration option. Instead, businesses organize as corporations, partnerships, or limited liability companies (LLCs).

### Key Takeaways

- The joint-stock company is the ancestor of the modern corporation, though legal differences exist.
- A joint-stock company is a business owned by its shareholders, who can freely buy and sell shares.
- Historically, shareholders of a joint-stock company could face unlimited liability for the company's debts.
- In the United States, corporate registration limits shareholder liability to the par value of their shares. [1]
- The British East India Company is a notable example of a joint-stock company.

## Characteristics of a Joint-Stock Company

Shareholders of a joint-stock company bear unlimited liability for the company's debts. In the United States, the legal process of incorporating as a corporation or LLC limits liability to the par value of the stock held by shareholders or the amount contributed by LLC members. [1] In the United Kingdom, the term "limited" carries a similar meaning. [2]

Shares in a joint-stock company are transferable. If the company is publicly traded, its shares trade on a registered stock exchange. Shares of a private joint-stock company can be transferred between parties, but the transfer process may be restricted by agreement -- for example, limited to family members. [1]

Historically, because of the unlimited liability nature of joint-stock companies, shareholders' personal assets could be seized to settle the company's debts in the event of a failure. [3]

## Types of Joint-Stock Companies

Companies register with state and local authorities to be legally permitted to operate under their chosen organizational form (e.g., corporation, S corporation, limited liability partnership, LLC, etc.).

A **chartered company** is one incorporated under a national royal charter. It may enjoy certain privileges related to conducting business operations.

A **statutory company** is one established by an act of the national legislature to provide public services that benefit the populace. Its liabilities and privileges are detailed in the founding statute.

## Advantages of a Joint-Stock Company

- A joint-stock company can raise substantial capital from numerous shareholders, which can be used to establish and grow the business.
- Shareholders have a direct voice in decisions related to company management and the right to elect the board of directors.
- Shares of publicly traded companies can be freely bought and sold on stock exchanges. Private company shares can be traded as permitted or restricted by the company.
- Modern joint-stock companies provide shareholders with limited liability for company debts.
- If additional capital is needed, the company can issue new shares or bonds.
- For public companies, there is no cap on the number of shareholders (private companies may impose such limits).
- Investment risk is distributed among many shareholders rather than borne by one or two individuals.
- Publicly traded joint-stock companies support sound corporate governance and make their externally audited financial statements available for public review.

**Note:** The Muscovy Company was a joint-stock company chartered by Queen Mary of England in 1555. Its shareholders included the explorer Sebastian Cabot and numerous London merchants. The company was granted exclusive rights to trade with Russia.

## Joint-Stock Company vs. Public Company

While the joint-stock company is not a specific legal form of business entity in the United States, the term can be applied to corporations, partnerships, LLCs, or public companies -- in fact, any company with multiple shareholders.

Historically, joint-stock companies were associated with unlimited liability and the potential seizure of personal assets to settle outstanding debts. A modern publicly traded company with shareholders is still a form of joint-stock company, but its legal status limits shareholder liability to the amount of their ownership stake or contribution.

## A Brief History of Joint-Stock Companies

Records of joint-stock companies being formed in Europe date back to the 13th century. However, they appear to have multiplied dramatically from the 16th century onward, when adventurous investors began speculating on opportunities discovered in the New World. [4]

European exploration of the Americas was largely financed by joint-stock companies. Governments were eager to claim new territories but unwilling to bear the enormous costs and risks associated with these ventures. [4]

This led entrepreneurs to devise a business model: they would raise capital by selling shares in their ventures to numerous investors to fund voyages to the New World. The prospect of exploiting resources and developing trade attracted many investors. Others sought to acquire real estate in the New World and establish new communities free from religious persecution. [4]

In American history, the Virginia Company of London was among the earliest and most famous joint-stock companies. In 1606, King James I signed a royal charter granting the company exclusive rights to establish a colony in what is now Virginia. The Virginia Company's business plan was ambitious, ranging from exploiting the region's gold resources (there were none) to finding a viable route to China (there was none). [4]

After considerable hardship, the company successfully established the Jamestown colony in Virginia and began growing and exporting tobacco. However, in 1624, an English court ordered the dissolution of the company and converted Virginia into a royal colony. The investors in the Virginia Company never earned a profit. [4]

## Why Were Joint-Stock Companies Important in American History?

Joint-stock companies played a vital role in financing the settlement of the original colonies. They could raise capital from many investors without exposing any single investor to excessive risk, enabling them to amass sufficient resources to successfully launch settlements in the New World. A prominent example is the Virginia Company of London, which funded the settlement of Jamestown. [4]

## Do Joint-Stock Companies Still Exist?

Yes. Today's equivalent of the joint-stock company in the United States includes corporations, partnerships, and limited liability companies. While there is no legal business registration form labeled "joint-stock company," the term can be used to describe any business entity that issues stock and has shareholders.

## What Are the Advantages of a Joint-Stock Company?

Joint-stock companies -- both historical and modern -- can raise substantial amounts of capital by issuing shares to individuals. This made them practical for ventures during the American colonial era that could not be funded by a single person or entity. All shareholders, of course, expect to receive a share of any returns proportional to their investment.

## What Is Different About Today's Joint-Stock Companies?

The legal liability that shareholders must bear is different. In the past, any shareholder could be held fully liable for debts owed by the company that it could not repay. A shareholder's personal assets could be seized by creditors or authorities as payment. Today, shareholder liability is typically limited to their investment in the company.

## What Are Some Famous Joint-Stock Companies?

The British East India Company is perhaps the most well-known joint-stock company. It was established to trade with India and Asia. Over its 250-year history, the EIC effectively controlled the colonization and exploitation of India and other overseas territories. [5]

## The Bottom Line

A joint-stock company is collectively owned by its shareholders. This type of company has existed since at least the 13th century. Historically, they exposed shareholders to unlimited liability, which discouraged investment.

Fortunately, corporate law has since limited shareholder liability. In the United States, it is restricted to the par value of a shareholder's stock.
