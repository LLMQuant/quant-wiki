# What Is the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), also known as efficient market theory, posits that stock prices reflect all available information and that consistently generating excess returns is impossible.

According to the EMH, stocks always trade at their fair value on exchanges, making it impossible for investors to purchase undervalued stocks or sell stocks at inflated prices. As a result, beating the overall market through expert stock selection or market timing is not achievable; the only way to obtain higher returns is to purchase riskier investments.

### Key Takeaways

- The Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), or efficient market theory, holds that share prices reflect all available information.
- The EMH assumes that stocks trade at their fair market value on exchanges.
- Proponents of the EMH argue that investors benefit from investing in low-cost, passive portfolios.
- Opponents of the EMH contend that it is possible to beat the market and that stocks can deviate from their fair market values.

## Understanding the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH)

Although the EMH is a cornerstone of modern financial theory, it is highly controversial and frequently challenged. Adherents argue that searching for undervalued stocks or attempting to predict market trends through either fundamental or technical analysis is futile.

Theoretically, neither technical analysis nor fundamental analysis can consistently produce risk-adjusted excess returns (alpha); only inside information could deliver abnormal risk-adjusted returns.

While academics point to a substantial body of evidence supporting the EMH, an equal measure of dissent exists. For instance, investors such as Warren Buffett have consistently outperformed the market over long periods -- an outcome that is, by the EMH's definition, impossible.

Detractors of the EMH also cite events such as the 1987 stock market crash, when the Dow Jones Industrial Average fell more than 20% in a single day, and asset bubbles as evidence that stock prices can deviate significantly from fair value.

## Special Considerations

Proponents of the EMH argue that, given the randomness of the market, investors may achieve better returns by investing in low-cost, passive portfolios.

Data compiled by Morningstar in its June 2019 Active/Passive Barometer study supports the EMH. Morningstar compared the returns of active managers across all categories with a composite of related index funds and exchange-traded funds (ETFs). The study found that over the 10 years beginning in June 2009, only 23% of active managers outperformed their passive counterparts. Foreign stock funds and bond funds showed higher success rates, while U.S. large-cap stock funds had lower success rates. Overall, investors fared better by investing in low-cost index funds or ETFs.

While a percentage of active managers do outperform passive funds at certain points, the challenge for investors is identifying which managers can sustain that outperformance over the long term. More than 25% of top-performing active managers proved unable to consistently beat their passive peers over time.

## What Does Market Efficiency Mean?

Market efficiency refers to the degree to which prices reflect all available information. The EMH argues that markets are efficient, leaving little room for generating excess profits through investment, because all prices already reflect fair and accurate valuations. This implies that the prospects for beating the market are slim, although passive index investing can match market returns.

## Does the Efficient Market Hypothesis Hold?

The validity of the EMH has been questioned on both theoretical and empirical grounds. Some investors, such as Warren Buffett, have beaten the market by focusing on undervalued stocks, accumulating billions in returns and setting an example for countless followers. There are also portfolio managers who have outperformed their peers and investment houses whose research is more highly regarded than others. EMH proponents counter that those who outperform the market do so not through skill but through luck, given the laws of probability: in a market with a large number of participants, some will outperform the mean while others underperform.

## Can Markets Be Inefficient?

Some markets are indeed less efficient than others. In an inefficient market, asset prices do not accurately reflect their true value, which can arise from a number of causes. Market inefficiencies may exist due to information asymmetries, insufficient buyers and sellers (low liquidity), high transaction costs or delays, market psychology, and human emotion. Inefficiency often leads to a misallocation of resources. In practice, most markets exhibit some degree of inefficiency, and in extreme cases, an inefficient market can result in market failure.

Accepting the pure (strong) form of the EMH can be difficult, as it asserts that all information in a market -- whether public or private -- is already reflected in stock prices. However, modified forms of the EMH exist to capture the varying degrees to which it holds in practice:

- Semi-strong efficiency: This form of the EMH holds that all publicly available (but not non-public) information is already incorporated into a stock's current price. Neither fundamental nor technical analysis can produce excess returns.
- Weak efficiency: This form asserts that all of a stock's past prices are reflected in today's stock price. Therefore, technical analysis cannot be used to predict or outperform the market.

## How Can Markets Become More Efficient?

The more participants in a market, the more efficient it becomes, as more people compete and bring more -- and more varied -- information to bear on prices. As markets become more active and liquid, arbitrageurs emerge to profit by exploiting small inefficiencies, quickly restoring efficiency.
