# What Is Quantitative Easing?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Quantitative easing (QE) is a form of monetary policy used by central banks, such as the U.S. Federal Reserve, to increase the domestic money supply and stimulate economic activity by purchasing securities in the open market.

QE creates new bank reserves, providing banks with more liquidity and encouraging lending and investment. In the United States, the Federal Reserve implements QE policy.

### Key Takeaways

- Quantitative easing is a monetary policy tool used by central banks to increase the domestic money supply and spur economic activity.
- Under QE, a central bank purchases government bonds and other financial instruments, such as mortgage-backed securities (MBS).
- Quantitative easing is typically deployed when interest rates are near zero and economic growth has stalled.
- In the United States, the Federal Reserve implements quantitative easing policies.

## Understanding Quantitative Easing

Quantitative easing is typically deployed when interest rates are near zero and economic growth has stalled. Central banks have limited monetary policy tools, such as lowering interest rates, to influence economic growth. When rates cannot be lowered further, central banks must strategically increase the money supply to compensate.

To execute QE, central banks purchase government bonds and other securities, injecting bank reserves into the economy. This process of increasing the money supply provides liquidity to the banking system and further pushes down interest rates, enabling banks to lend more freely.

Government fiscal policy may be implemented simultaneously to expand the money supply. While the Federal Reserve can influence the money supply in the economy, the U.S. Treasury can create new money and implement new tax policies through fiscal policy, injecting funds into the economy directly or indirectly.

Quantitative easing can combine both monetary and fiscal policy.

## Does Quantitative Easing Work?

Most economists believe that the Federal Reserve's QE programs helped rescue the U.S. and global economy in the aftermath of the 2007-2008 financial crisis; however, the effects of QE are difficult to quantify.

Globally, central banks have also attempted to deploy QE to stave off recession and deflation, with similarly inconclusive results. While QE policies have been effective at lowering interest rates and boosting stock markets, their broader impact on the overall economy is not clear-cut.

According to Stephen Williamson, a former economist at the Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis, QE generally benefits borrowers while penalizing savers and non-investors, so its effects are a mixed bag. [1]

**Note:** During the COVID-19 pandemic, QE was deployed, and the Federal Reserve increased its holdings until the first quarter of 2021, when its holdings of Treasury securities accounted for 56% of issuance. [2]

## Risks of Quantitative Easing

As money is injected into the economy, the risk of inflation rises. Although liquidity works its way through the system gradually, central banks must remain vigilant, as the time lag between increases in the money supply and the inflation rate is typically 12 to 18 months. [4]

If QE fails to stimulate the expected economic growth and instead leads to inflation, a situation known as stagflation may arise -- where both the inflation rate and unemployment are elevated.

Despite increased liquidity for banks, central banks like the Fed cannot force banks to increase lending activity, nor can they force individuals and businesses to borrow and invest. This can lead to a credit crunch, where cash is hoarded by banks or corporations hold cash reserves due to an uncertain business environment. [5]

As the money supply increases, QE can also lead to a depreciation of the domestic currency. While a weaker currency helps domestic manufacturers because their exported goods become cheaper in the global market, a falling currency value makes imports more expensive, increasing production costs and consumer price levels.

## Real-World Examples of Quantitative Easing

To combat the Great Recession, the U.S. Federal Reserve implemented a QE program from 2009 to 2014. The Fed's balance sheet expanded with bonds, mortgages, and other assets. By 2017, U.S. bank reserves had swelled to over $4 trillion, providing liquidity to support lending and stimulate overall economic growth. However, banks held $2.8 trillion in excess reserves, an unexpected outcome of the Fed's QE program. [6]

In 2020, facing the economic and market turmoil from COVID-19 lockdowns, the Fed announced plans to purchase $700 billion in assets as an emergency QE measure. [7] In 2022, however, the Federal Reserve significantly adjusted its monetary policy, including substantial interest rate increases and a reduction in asset holdings, to address the persistent high inflation trend that emerged in 2021. [8]

Following the 1997 Asian financial crisis, Japan fell into an economic recession. The Bank of Japan embarked on an aggressive QE program to curb deflation and stimulate the economy, transitioning from buying Japanese government bonds to purchasing private debt and stocks. The effects of this QE campaign were only temporary; although Japan's GDP rose from $4.1 trillion in 1998 to $6.27 trillion in 2012, it fell back to $4.44 trillion by 2015. [9][10]

The Swiss National Bank (SNB) also employed a QE strategy after the 2008 financial crisis. Eventually, the SNB's total assets exceeded the entire country's annual economic output. Although economic growth improved, the extent to which the subsequent recovery could be attributed to the SNB's QE program remained unclear. [11][12]

In August 2016, the Bank of England (BoE) launched a QE program to address potential economic consequences of Brexit. The plan called for purchasing 60 billion pounds in government bonds and 10 billion pounds in corporate debt to prevent interest rates from rising and to stimulate business investment and employment. [13]

By June 2018, the Office for National Statistics reported that gross fixed capital formation had seen a quarterly compound average growth rate of 0.2% over the prior decade, but 0.8% when excluding recessionary dips, compared to 0.6% in the preceding decade. [14]

UK economists could not conclusively determine whether growth would have been notably different without the QE program. [15]

## How Does Quantitative Easing Work?

Quantitative easing is a form of monetary policy in which a nation's central bank attempts to increase liquidity in the financial system by purchasing long-term government bonds from major banks, thereby injecting money into the economy and stimulating growth by encouraging banks to lend or invest more freely.

## Is Quantitative Easing Money Printing?

Critics have argued that QE is effectively a form of money printing, citing historical examples in which money printing led to hyperinflation. [16] However, proponents of QE counter that banks act as intermediaries rather than placing cash directly into the hands of individuals and businesses, so QE carries less risk of producing runaway inflation. [1]

## How Does Quantitative Easing Increase Bank Lending?

QE effectively increases the money supply by replacing bonds in the banking system with cash, making it easier for banks to free up capital. As a result, they can underwrite more loans and purchase other assets.

## Conclusion

Quantitative easing is a monetary policy implemented by central banks such as the U.S. Federal Reserve that involves purchasing securities through open market operations to increase the money supply and encourage bank lending and investment. QE policies have been implemented around the world, but their effects on national economies are often debated.
