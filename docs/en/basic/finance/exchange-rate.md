# What Is an Exchange Rate?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
An exchange rate is the rate at which one currency can be exchanged for another. It influences trade and capital flows between countries.

Exchange rates are affected by the value of the domestic currency and the foreign currency. As of April 2024, the exchange rate of the U.S. dollar against the euro was 1.07, meaning it took $1.07 to purchase one euro. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- An exchange rate is the rate at which one currency is exchanged for another.
- Most exchange rates are defined as free-floating. They rise and fall based on supply and demand in the market.
- Some exchange rates are pegged or fixed to the value of a specific country's currency.
- Exchange rate fluctuations affect businesses by altering the cost of supplies purchased from other countries and changing foreign demand for their products.

## Understanding Exchange Rates

The exchange rate between two currencies is generally determined by economic activity, market interest rates, gross domestic product, and unemployment rates in each country. Commonly referred to as the market exchange rate, it is set in global financial markets where banks and other financial institutions trade currencies around the clock based on these factors. Changes can occur hourly or daily and may be small increments or large swings. [2]

Exchange rates are typically quoted using abbreviations that represent each national currency. "USD" represents the U.S. dollar. "EUR" represents the euro. To quote the currency pair for the dollar and the euro, it would be expressed as EUR/USD.

**Important:** For the Japanese yen, the notation is USD/JPY, or the U.S. dollar to the Japanese yen. An exchange rate of 100 means that 1 dollar equals 100 yen.

## How Exchange Rates Fluctuate

Exchange rates can be free-floating or fixed. A free-floating exchange rate rises and falls in response to changes in the foreign exchange market. A fixed exchange rate is pegged to the value of another currency. The Hong Kong dollar is pegged to the U.S. dollar in a range of 7.75 to 7.85, so the value of the Hong Kong dollar against the U.S. dollar remains within this band. [3]

Exchange rates have a spot rate, or cash value, which is the current market price. They may also have a forward rate, which is based on expectations that the currency will appreciate or depreciate relative to its spot price.

Forward rate values fluctuate as expectations for future interest rates in one country relative to another change. If traders speculate that the eurozone will ease monetary policy while the United States tightens, they may buy dollars against euros, pushing the value of the euro lower.

## Exchange Rate Example

A traveler from the United States heading to Germany wants to convert $200 into euros upon arrival. The selling rate is the price at which the traveler sells foreign currency in exchange for local currency. The buying rate is the price at which foreign currency is bought back from the traveler in exchange for local currency.

If the current exchange rate is 1.05, then $200 will net 190.48 euros. In this case, the equation is: dollars / exchange rate = euros.

Suppose the traveler has 66 euros left at the end of the trip. If the exchange rate has dropped to 1.02, converting the euros back to dollars yields $67.32.

Japanese yen calculations work differently. In this case, the dollar is placed before the yen, as in USD/JPY. The equation is: dollars x exchange rate = yen.

At an exchange rate of 110, a traveler heading to Japan who wants to convert $100 into yen would receive 11,000 yen. To convert yen back to dollars, divide the currency amount by the exchange rate.

## How Do Exchange Rates Affect the Supply and Demand for Goods?

Exchange rate movements affect businesses by altering the cost of supplies purchased from different countries and changing overseas demand for their products.

## What Is the Foreign Exchange Market?

The foreign exchange market allows banks, funds, and individuals to buy, sell, or exchange currencies. The market operates five days a week, 24 hours a day, with trillions of dollars in daily trading activity as traders seek to profit by betting on one currency's value rising or falling relative to another.

## What Is a Restricted Currency?

Exchange rates within the same country may differ. Some countries have restricted currencies that limit their exchange within the country's borders, and there is often both an onshore and an offshore rate. A more favorable exchange rate can typically be found within the country than outside it, and the value of a restricted currency is set by the government.

China is one example of a country with this type of exchange rate structure and a government-controlled currency. The Chinese government sets a daily midpoint rate for the yuan, allowing it to trade within a 2% band around that rate. [4]

## Conclusion

An exchange rate is the rate at which one currency is exchanged for another. Most exchange rates are floating and rise or fall based on market supply and demand, though some are pegged or fixed to the value of a specific country's currency. Exchange rate fluctuations affect businesses as well as the cost of supplies and demand for products in international markets.
