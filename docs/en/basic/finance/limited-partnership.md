# What Is a Limited Partnership (LP)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A limited partnership (LP) is a business entity owned by two or more parties. These parties must include at least one general partner, who manages the business and assumes unlimited liability for its debts. Limited partners bear liability only up to the amount of their investment. A limited partnership is distinct from a limited liability partnership (LLP).

The limited partnership structure is commonly used as a vehicle for individuals to pool capital for investments in real estate or other assets.

### Key Takeaways

- A limited partnership (LP) is a business entity that requires at least one general partner and one or more limited partners.
- The general partner assumes unlimited financial liability, while the other partners' liability is limited to their investment.
- An LP is a pass-through entity with minimal or no reporting requirements.
- Most U.S. states govern the formation of limited partnerships and require registration of the entity.

## How a Limited Partnership (LP) Works

A limited partnership must have at least one general partner and one or more limited partners. [1]

The general partner has full management control of the business and assumes unlimited financial liability for its obligations. Limited partners have little or no involvement in management, and their liability is restricted to the amount they have invested in the LP.

Hedge funds and real estate investment partnerships are frequently structured as LPs to shield their investors from the financial consequences of failure.

**Note:** A partnership agreement should be drafted to outline the specific responsibilities and rights of both general and limited partners.

## Types of Partnerships

Generally, any partnership is a business owned by two or more individuals. Partnerships take three forms: limited partnerships, general partnerships, and limited liability partnerships.

In all forms, each partner contributes resources -- such as property, capital, skills, or labor -- and shares in the business's profits and losses. At least one partner is responsible for decisions regarding the day-to-day affairs of the business. That partner is typically known as the general partner.

Most limited partnerships are formed by investors pooling capital to invest in assets such as real estate. What distinguishes an LP from other partnerships is that, apart from the general partner, all other partners have limited liability -- meaning they are not responsible for business debts exceeding their initial investment.

The general partner manages the limited partnership on a day-to-day basis and is personally responsible for the company's financial obligations, including debts and litigation. All other contributors, known as limited (or silent) partners, provide capital but cannot make management decisions and are not liable for any debts beyond their initial investment.

**Important:** If limited partners take on a more active role in the LP, they may become personally liable.

A general partnership (GP) is a corporate structure requiring all partners to share jointly in the business's profits, management responsibilities, and debt liability. Unless the legal partnership agreement stipulates otherwise, partners share profits and responsibilities equally. [2]

A joint venture is a form of general partnership established to accomplish a specific project and dissolved upon its completion. All partners have equal rights to control the business and share in profits or losses. They also have a fiduciary duty to act in the best interests of the other members and the venture. [3]

A limited liability partnership (LLP) is a type of corporation that grants all partners limited financial liability. All partners may also participate in management decisions. [4]

This differs from a limited partnership, in which at least one general partner must assume unlimited liability and limited partners cannot participate in management. LLP partners are not liable for the misconduct or negligence of other partners. [4]

LLPs are most commonly used as business structures for groups of professionals such as attorneys or accountants.

## How to Form a Limited Partnership

Nearly all U.S. states govern the formation of limited partnerships under the Uniform Limited Partnership Act, which was first introduced in 1916 and has been amended multiple times since. The majority of U.S. jurisdictions -- 49 states and the District of Columbia -- have adopted these provisions, with Louisiana being the sole exception. [5]

To form a limited partnership, the partners must register the entity in the applicable state, typically through the local Secretary of State's office. Required business licenses and permits vary by locality, state, and industry.

**Tip:** The U.S. Small Business Administration (SBA) lists all local, state, and federal permits required to start a business. [6]

In addition to external filings, the partners of a limited partnership must draft a partnership agreement. This internal document defines how the business will operate, outlining each partner's rights, responsibilities, and expectations.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of LPs

The primary advantage of an LP for its limited partners is that it shields them from personal financial liability beyond their investment. The general partner accepts the greatest risk in order to raise capital for investments.

An LP is a pass-through entity for all partners, meaning the entity files an informational return on Form 1065. Partners receive a Schedule K-1 to report their share of income or loss on their personal tax returns. [7] Limited partners are not subject to self-employment tax because they are not active participants in the business.

Another advantage is the simplicity of creating and maintaining a limited partnership. Because the partnership structure is less formal than other entity types, it may be simpler to set up. Maintenance is also straightforward: partners are not required to attend any form of annual meetings.

On the downside, an LP requires the general partner to assume unlimited liability. The general partner is responsible for all management decisions and is personally accountable for any debts or mismanagement. [4]

If limited partners wish to preserve their liability protection, they must remain uninvolved in business operations. If their role is deemed active, they may lose their personal liability shield.

The LP structure also has drawbacks compared with other entities such as limited liability companies (LLCs). Ownership transfers can be more difficult, and there is less flexibility in reassigning management roles within the business.

#### Advantages

- Personal liability protection for limited partners
- Pass-through entity for tax purposes
- Easy to create and report
- Limited partners are not subject to self-employment tax

#### Disadvantages

- Unlimited personal liability for the general partner
- Limited partners may not participate in management decisions
- Ownership transfers can be difficult
- Less flexibility in changing management roles

## LP vs. LLC

A limited liability company (LLC) and a limited partnership share several similarities. Both entities offer a degree of flexibility in defining member roles and entity structure, including control over voting rights, financial terms, and fiduciary duties.

Both entity types also receive pass-through tax treatment, meaning each investor reports their share of the entity's profits on their personal tax return. Neither type of entity pays federal income tax at the entity level. [7]

However, there are differences between the two, starting with corporate structure. A limited partnership consists of general partners and limited partners, while an LLC can have any number of members. Generally, all members of an LLC may participate in managing the business, whereas limited partners in an LP cannot be active participants. [4]

Another key distinction involves liability. A general partner in an LP assumes unlimited personal liability and may be personally accountable for the company's debts and obligations. Limited partners are typically not liable for the partnership's obligations. An LLC, on the other hand, generally provides corporate-like protection for its members, who are not directly responsible for the company's debts. [4]

Finally, LLCs enjoy greater tax flexibility. An LLC can elect to be taxed as a C corporation, S corporation, or disregarded entity. [8] Both LLCs and LPs default to partnership taxation.

- Composed of general partners and limited partners
- Limited partners do not actively participate in day-to-day management
- The general partner may be personally liable
- Taxed as a partnership

#### LLC

- Can have many owners, all referred to as members
- All members may participate in management
- No personal liability
- Can be taxed as a partnership, C corporation, S corporation, or disregarded entity

## Limited Partnerships and Taxes

For tax purposes, a limited partnership is treated similarly to a general partnership. An LP is classified as a pass-through entity and files an informational return on Form 1065. The LP also provides each partner with a Schedule K-1, enabling each partner to report their share of business income and losses on their individual tax return. [9]

If the limited partnership incurs a loss, each partner may deduct that loss on their personal return up to the amount of their investment. If the loss exceeds their cumulative investment, the partner may carry the loss forward to future years.

Income or loss from a limited partnership is classified as passive income or loss because each partner does not actively participate in the business.

This is especially important for tax purposes, since passive activities can only be offset by other passive income; passive losses can only be used to offset passive gains.

This also plays a critical role in self-employment taxes. Limited partners are not subject to self-employment tax on most payments because they are not active participants in the business. General partners are typically required to pay self-employment tax. [10]

## What Type of Business Is a Limited Partnership?

Businesses that form as limited partnerships typically own or operate specific assets, such as the properties held by a real estate investment partnership. The general partner controls the assets, manages the business, and may be personally liable for its debts. All limited partners are investors who have no management role and are not liable for debts beyond their investment.

## What Is the Difference Between an LP and an LLP?

An LP (limited partnership) and an LLP (limited liability partnership) have similar structures. However, an LP has general partners and limited partners, whereas an LLP has no general partners. All partners in an LLP have limited liability. [4]

## What Is Limited Partnership Taxation?

Limited partnerships are taxed as pass-through entities, meaning each partner receives a Schedule K-1 for inclusion on their personal tax return. [7]

## What Are the Benefits of a Limited Partnership?

Limited partnerships are ideal vehicles for raising capital for a specific investment or set of assets. They allow most partners to invest while keeping their liability limited.

## The Bottom Line

Limited partnerships are commonly used by hedge funds and investment partnerships because they provide the ability to raise capital without relinquishing control.

Limited partners invest in an LP and have little or no control over the entity's management, but their liability is capped at their personal investment. Meanwhile, general partners manage and operate the LP, but their liability is unlimited.
