# What Is the Compound Annual Growth Rate (CAGR)?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The compound annual growth rate (CAGR) is the rate of return an investment would need to earn each year in order to grow from its beginning balance to its ending balance over a given time interval. CAGR assumes that all profits are reinvested at the end of each period over the investment's life.

### Key Takeaways

- CAGR is one of the most accurate ways to calculate and measure the return on any investment whose value can rise or fall over time.
- It measures a smoothed rate of return.
- Investors can compare the CAGR of two or more investments to evaluate how well a stock performed relative to peers or a market index.
- CAGR is therefore a useful tool for evaluating the performance of different investments over time or against a benchmark.
- Importantly, CAGR does not reflect investment risk.

## How to Calculate CAGR

$$ \begin{aligned}&CAGR=\left(\left(\frac{EV}{BV}\right)^{\frac1n}-1\right)\times100\\&\textbf{where:}\\&EV=\text{Ending value}\\&BV=\text{Beginning value}\\&n=\text{Number of years}\end{aligned} $$

The CAGR formula produces an annualized rate of return, which is useful for comparing the performance of different investments over time.

## What CAGR Can Tell You

The compound annual growth rate is not a true rate of return but rather a representational figure. It essentially describes the rate at which an investment would have grown if it had grown at the same rate every year, with profits reinvested at the end of each year. For stock market investors, this is particularly useful for comparing the performance of different stocks. CAGR does not account for the discount rate, which is critical when evaluating the present value of future returns.

In reality, such consistent performance is unlikely. However, CAGR can be used to smooth returns so they are easier to understand and compare against alternatives.

## CAGR in Practice

Suppose you invested $10,000 in a portfolio with the following returns:

- From January 1, 2018 to January 1, 2019, the portfolio grew to $13,000 (30% growth in Year 1).
- On January 1, 2020, the portfolio was worth $14,000 (7.69% growth from January 2019 to January 2020).
- On January 1, 2021, the portfolio ended at $19,000 (35.71% growth from January 2020 to January 2021).

The year-to-year annual growth rates of the portfolio varied significantly, as shown in the parentheses above.

CAGR, on the other hand, smooths the investment's performance and ignores the large divergences between 2018 and 2020 relative to 2019. The CAGR over this period was 23.86%, calculated as:

$$ CAGR=\left(\frac{\$19,000}{\$10,000}\right )^{\frac{1}{3}}-1\times100=23.86\% $$

The 23.86% CAGR over the three-year investment period can help an investor compare alternatives or forecast future values. For example, imagine an investor is comparing the performance of two uncorrelated investments.

In any given year during the period, one investment may be rising while the other is falling. This might be the case when comparing high-yield bonds with equities, or a real estate investment with emerging markets. Using CAGR would smooth the annual returns over the period, making the two alternatives easier to compare.

As another example, suppose an investor purchased 55 shares of Amazon (AMZN) in December 2017 at $1,180 per share, for a total investment of $64,900. Three years later, by December 2020, the stock price had risen to $3,200 per share, making the investment worth $176,000. [1] What is the CAGR?

Using the CAGR formula, we need:

- Ending balance: $176,000
- Beginning balance: $64,900
- Number of years: 3

Therefore: [($176,000 / $64,900)^(1/3)] - 1 = 39.5%.

## Other Uses of CAGR

CAGR can be used to calculate the average growth of a single investment. As we saw in the example above, due to market volatility, year-to-year growth of an investment may appear erratic and uneven.

For example, an investment may increase by 8% in one year, decline by -2% the next, and then increase by 5% the year after. CAGR helps smooth returns when growth rates are expected to be volatile and inconsistent.

The geometric mean produced by CAGR can be used to compare different types of investments against one another. For example, suppose in 2015, an investor placed $10,000 into an account for a five-year period at a fixed annual interest rate of 1%, and another $10,000 into a stock mutual fund. Returns in the stock fund will be uneven over the coming years, making a comparison between the two investments difficult.

Assume that at the end of five years, the savings account balance is $10,510.10, and although the other investment has grown unevenly, the ending balance of the stock fund is $15,348.52. Using CAGR to compare the two investments can help an investor understand the difference in returns:

$$ \text{Savings Account CAGR} =\, \left ( \frac{\$ 10,510.10}{\$ 10,000} \right )^{\frac{1}{5}}-1 \times 100= 1.00\% $$

and:

$$ \text{Stock Fund CAGR} =\, \left ( \frac{\$ 15,348.52}{\$ 10,000} \right )^{\frac{1}{5}}-1 \times 100= 8.95\% $$

On the surface, the stock fund appears to be a far superior investment, with a return nearly nine times that of the savings account. On the other hand, one of the drawbacks of CAGR is that by smoothing returns, it cannot tell the investor how volatile or risky the stock fund was. However, CAGR can be used in the MAR ratio to adjust for risk. Pairing CAGR with metrics such as net present value (NPV) can provide a more comprehensive picture of an investment's potential.

CAGR can also be used to track the performance of one or more business measures across companies. For example, over a five-year period, Big-Sale Stores' market-share CAGR was 1.82%, but its customer-satisfaction CAGR over the same period was -0.58%. Comparing CAGRs of measures within a company in this way reveals relative strengths and weaknesses.

Comparing CAGRs of business activities across similar companies helps evaluate competitive strengths and weaknesses. For example, Big-Sale's customer-satisfaction CAGR might not seem so low when compared with SuperFast Cable's customer-satisfaction CAGR of -6.31% during the same period.

## How Investors Use CAGR

Understanding the formula used to calculate CAGR is an introduction to many other ways investors evaluate past returns or estimate future profits. The formula can be manipulated algebraically into a formula for finding the present or future value of money, or for calculating a hurdle rate of return.

For example, imagine an investor knows they need $50,000 for a child's college education in 18 years, and they have $15,000 to invest today. What average rate of return is required to reach that goal? The CAGR calculation can provide the answer:

$$ \text{Required Return} =\, \left ( \frac{\$ 50,000}{\$ 15,000} \right )^{\frac{1}{18}}-1 \times 100= 6.90\% $$

This version of the CAGR formula is simply a rearranged present value and future value equation. For example, if an investor knows they need $50,000 and considers an expected annual return of 8% to be reasonable, they can use this formula to find out how much they need to invest to meet their goal.

## What Is a Good CAGR?

What counts as a good CAGR depends on the context. Generally, investors will evaluate it by considering the opportunity cost and the risk of the investment. For example, if a company grew at 25% in an industry with an average CAGR closer to 30%, the result may seem lackluster by comparison. But if the broader industry growth rate were lower, say 10% or 15%, then that company's CAGR might be quite impressive.

In general, the higher the CAGR, the better.

## Modifying the CAGR Formula

Investments are rarely made on the first day of the year and sold on the last. Suppose an investor wants to evaluate the CAGR of a $10,000 investment made on June 1, 2013, and sold for $16,897.14 on September 9, 2018.

Before performing the CAGR calculation, the investor needs to know the fractional remainder of the holding period. The position was held for 213 days in 2013, a full year each in 2014, 2015, 2016, and 2017, and 251 days in 2018. This investment was held for 5.271 years, calculated as follows:

- 2013 = 213 days
- 2014 = 365
- 2015 = 365
- 2016 = 365
- 2017 = 365
- 2018 = 251

The total number of days the investment was held is 1,924. To calculate the number of years, divide the total days by 365 (1,924/365), which equals 5.271 years.

The total number of years the investment was held can be placed in the exponent's denominator inside the CAGR formula as follows:

$$ \text{Investment CAGR} =\, \left ( \frac{\$ 16,897.14}{\$ 10,000} \right )^{\frac{1}{5.271}}-1 \times 100= 10.46\% $$

## Limitations of the Smoothed Growth Rate

The most important limitation of CAGR is that because it calculates a smoothed growth rate over a period, it ignores volatility and implies that growth during that period was steady. Investment returns are uneven over time, except for bonds held to maturity, certificates of deposit, and similar instruments.

Additionally, CAGR does not account for when an investor adds funds to or withdraws funds from a portfolio during the measurement period.

For example, if an investor held a portfolio for five years and injected funds into the portfolio during that period, the CAGR would be inflated. The CAGR would calculate the return rate based on the beginning and ending balances over the five years and would essentially count the deposited funds as part of the annual growth rate, which would be inaccurate.

## Other CAGR Limitations

Beyond the smoothed-growth limitation, CAGR has other constraints. When evaluating investments, a second limitation is that no matter how steady a company's or investment's past growth has been, investors cannot assume the rate will remain the same in the future. The shorter the time frame used in the analysis, the less likely the realized CAGR will match the expected CAGR when relying on historical results.

A third limitation relates to representation. Say an investment fund was worth $100,000 in 2016, $71,000 in 2017, $44,000 in 2018, $81,000 in 2019, and $126,000 in 2020. If the fund managers claimed in 2021 that their CAGR was a stellar 42.01% over the past three years, they would be technically correct. However, they would be omitting some very important information -- namely that the fund's CAGR over the past five years was a modest 4.73%.

## CAGR vs. IRR

CAGR measures the return of an investment over a certain period of time. The internal rate of return (IRR) also measures investment performance but is more flexible than CAGR.

The most important distinction is that CAGR is straightforward enough to be calculated by hand. In contrast, more complicated investments and projects, or those that have many different cash inflows and outflows, are best evaluated using IRR. The best tools for IRR calculations include financial calculators, Excel, or portfolio accounting systems.

## What Is an Example of CAGR?

CAGR is a measurement used by investors to calculate the rate at which a quantity grew over time. The word "compound" denotes the fact that CAGR takes into account the effects of compounding, or reinvestment, over time. For example, suppose you have a company with revenue that grew from $3 million to $30 million over a span of 10 years. In that scenario, the CAGR would be approximately 25.89%.

## What Is the Difference Between CAGR and a Growth Rate?

The main difference between CAGR and a growth rate is that CAGR assumes the growth rate was repeated, or "compounded," each year, whereas a traditional growth rate does not. Many investors prefer CAGR because it smooths out the volatile nature of year-by-year growth rates. For example, even a highly profitable and successful company will likely have several years of poor performance during its lifetime. These bad years could have a large effect on individual years' growth rates but would have a comparatively small impact on the company's CAGR.

## Can CAGR Be Negative?

Yes. A negative CAGR would indicate losses over time rather than gains.

## What Is Risk-Adjusted CAGR?

To compare the performance and risk characteristics of various investment alternatives, investors can use a risk-adjusted CAGR. A simple method for calculating a risk-adjusted CAGR is to multiply the CAGR by (1 minus the investment's standard deviation). If the standard deviation (i.e., its risk) is zero, the risk-adjusted CAGR is unaffected. The larger the standard deviation, the lower the risk-adjusted CAGR.

## Summary

CAGR is a valuable metric for investors evaluating how their investments have performed over time. By smoothing annual returns, it provides a clearer view of an investment's growth trajectory. However, while CAGR can help compare different investments and forecast future values, it does not account for volatility or risk. Investors should use CAGR in conjunction with other metrics to make well-informed decisions, keeping in mind its limitations in reflecting the full picture of investment performance.
