# What Is the Secondary Market?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The secondary market is where investors buy and sell securities. Trades occur among investors and traders rather than with the issuing company. People commonly associate the secondary market with the stock market. National exchanges such as the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the Nasdaq are secondary markets. The secondary market is where securities trade after they have been offered to the public for the first time.

### Key Takeaways

- The secondary market provides investors and traders with a venue for trading securities that were first sold on the primary market.
- On the secondary market, investors trade with one another rather than with the issuing entity.
- Through a large volume of independent but interconnected transactions, the secondary market drives security prices toward their fair value.
- The secondary market provides liquidity to the financial system and allows smaller traders to participate.
- Stock markets and over-the-counter (OTC) markets are types of secondary markets.

## How the Secondary Market Works

As noted above, securities first sold on the primary market are subsequently bought and sold among investors on the secondary market. For this reason, most people refer to the secondary market simply as the stock market.

Transactions on the secondary market are termed "secondary" because they are one step removed from the transaction that originally created the securities in question. For example, a financial institution writes a mortgage for a consumer, creating the mortgage security. The bank can then sell it on the secondary market to an entity such as Fannie Mae.

Although stocks are among the most commonly traded securities, there are other types of secondary markets as well. Investment banks, corporations, and individual investors buy and sell mutual funds and bonds on the secondary market. Entities such as Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac also purchase mortgages on the secondary market.

The importance of the secondary market can be understood in several ways. First, it provides liquidity to investors. Centralized exchanges can ensure a large number of participants and guarantee that security values are not lost in the process of buying and selling. It also lowers barriers to entry, allowing smaller traders to participate in the market.

## Types of Secondary Markets

Stock markets consist of centralized exchanges where buyers and sellers come together to trade equities and other assets. There is no direct contact between the trading parties -- whether physical or otherwise. The vast majority of trades are executed electronically. Traders must comply with rules and regulations established by relevant regulatory bodies, including the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC).

Examples of stock markets (secondary markets) include the NYSE and Nasdaq in the United States, as well as the London Stock Exchange (LSE), the Hong Kong Stock Exchange, the Bombay Stock Exchange (BSE), and the Frankfurt Stock Exchange.

The over-the-counter (OTC) market involves the trading of stocks, bonds, and other financial assets through broker-dealer networks rather than centralized exchanges. Assets traded OTC are not listed on formal exchanges. OTC stocks are typically those of smaller companies that have not met exchange listing requirements.

OTC markets include:

- **OTCQX:** The highest tier, where listed stocks must trade at a minimum of $5 per share.
- **OTCQB:** The middle tier, known as the Venture Market, featuring a wide range of developing companies.
- **Pink Sheets:** The Pink Sheets allow investors to trade shares of companies that have not met the listing requirements of major exchanges. Many of these are penny stocks.

**Important:** As new financial products emerge, the number of secondary markets continues to grow. In the case of mortgages, for example, several secondary markets may exist. Mortgage bundles are often repackaged into securities -- such as Ginnie Mae pools -- and resold to investors.

## Secondary Market vs. Primary Market

Understanding the distinction between the secondary market and the primary market is essential. When a company first issues stock or bonds and sells these securities directly to investors, that transaction occurs on the primary market.

Some of the most common and most publicized primary market transactions are IPOs. During an IPO, a primary market transaction takes place between the purchasing investor and the investment bank underwriting the offering. All proceeds from the sale of stock on the primary market go to the issuing company, after deducting the bank's administrative fees.

If these initial investors later decide to sell their stake in the company, they can do so on the secondary market. All secondary market transactions occur between investors, and the proceeds of each sale go to the selling investor -- not to the company that issued the securities or to the underwriting bank.

Primary market prices are typically set in advance, while secondary market prices are determined by the fundamental forces of supply and demand. If the majority of investors believe a stock's value will rise and rush to buy, the price generally increases. If a company loses investor confidence or fails to post sufficient earnings, its stock price declines as demand for the security falls.

## Is the Secondary Market the Same as the Stock Market?

Most people think of the stock market as the secondary market. It is where securities trade after they are first issued on the primary market. For instance, Company X would conduct its IPO on the primary market. Once the offering is complete, its shares can then be traded on the secondary market. Major stock exchanges such as the NYSE and Nasdaq are secondary markets.

## Who Are the Main Participants in the Secondary Market?

Key participants in the secondary market include broker-dealers who facilitate transactions, investors who initiate buying and selling activity, and intermediaries such as banks, financial institutions, and advisory firms.

## Why Is the Secondary Market Important?

The secondary market is where securities trade after passing through the primary market. It is a vital component of the financial system, providing market liquidity. It also offers traders a centralized venue for transactions. Investors of all sizes -- from large institutions to small retail traders -- can participate.
