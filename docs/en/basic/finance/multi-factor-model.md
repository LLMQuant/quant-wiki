# What Is a Multi-Factor Model?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A multi-factor model is a financial modeling strategy that employs multiple factors to explain market phenomena and/or equilibrium asset prices. A multi-factor model can be used to explain either an individual security or a portfolio of securities by comparing two or more factors to analyze the relationships among variables and the resulting performance.

### Key Takeaways

- A multi-factor model is a financial modeling strategy that uses multiple factors to analyze and explain asset prices.
- Multi-factor models reveal which factors have the most influence on the price of an asset.
- Multi-factor portfolios can be constructed using various methods, including intersectional, combinational, and sequential approaches.
- The beta of a security measures the systematic risk of that security relative to the overall market.
- The Fama-French three-factor model is a well-known tool that builds on the Capital Asset Pricing Model by adding size and value factors to the market risk factor.

## Understanding Multi-Factor Models

Multi-factor models are used to construct portfolios with specific characteristics (such as risk) or to track indexes. When building a multi-factor model, it is difficult to decide how many and which factors to include. Moreover, models are typically judged on historical numbers, which may not accurately predict future values.

Multi-factor models also help explain the weight of different factors used in the model, indicating which factor has a more significant impact on an asset's price.

## Multi-Factor Model Formula

Factors are compared using the following formula:

Ri = ai + Bi(m) * Rm + Bi(1) * F1 + Bi(2) * F2 + ... + Bi(N) * FN + ei

Where:

- Ri is the return of the security
- Rm is the market return
- F(1, 2, 3 ... N) is each of the factors used
- B is the beta with respect to each factor, including the market (m)
- e is the error term
- a is the intercept

## Types of Multi-Factor Models

Multi-factor models can be divided into three categories: macroeconomic models, fundamental models, and statistical models.

**Macroeconomic models:** Macroeconomic models compare a security's return to factors such as employment, inflation, and interest rates.

**Fundamental models:** Fundamental models analyze the relationship between a security's return and its underlying financial metrics, such as earnings, market capitalization, and debt levels.

**Statistical models:** Statistical models are used to compare the returns of different securities based on the statistical performance of each security on its own, typically using historical data for modeling.

## Construction of Multi-Factor Models

The three most commonly used methods for constructing multi-factor models are the combination model, the sequential model, and the intersectional model.

**Combination model:** In a combination model, multiple single-factor models (each using a single factor to distinguish stocks) are combined to create a multi-factor model. For example, stocks may be sorted based solely on momentum in the first pass, and subsequent passes use other factors such as volatility for further classification.

**Sequential model:** A sequential model sorts stocks by a single factor in a sequential manner to create a multi-factor model. For example, stocks within a particular market capitalization segment may be sequentially analyzed for various factors, such as value and momentum.

**Intersectional model:** In the intersectional model, stocks are sorted based on their intersections across factors. For example, stocks may be classified based on intersections in value and momentum.

## Measurement of Beta

The beta of a security measures its systematic risk relative to the overall market. A beta of 1 indicates that the security theoretically experiences the same degree of volatility as the market and moves in tandem with it.

A beta greater than 1 means the security is theoretically more volatile than the market. A beta less than 1 means the security is theoretically less volatile than the market.

When investment managers use multi-factor models to assess investment risk, beta is an important factor they can employ.

## The Fama-French Three-Factor Model

One widely used multi-factor model is the Fama-French three-factor model. The model has three factors: the size of firms, book-to-market values, and excess return on the market. In other words, the three factors used are SMB (small minus big), HML (high minus low), and the portfolio's return minus the risk-free rate.

SMB accounts for publicly traded companies with small market capitalizations that generate higher returns, while HML accounts for value stocks with high book-to-market ratios that generate higher returns relative to the market.
