# What Is Margin?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

In finance, margin is the collateral that an investor must deposit with a broker or exchange to cover the credit risk the investor poses. An investor creates credit risk when borrowing cash from the broker to purchase financial instruments, borrowing financial instruments to sell them short, or entering into a derivative contract.

Buying on margin refers to the practice of borrowing from a broker to purchase an asset. It typically describes the initial payment the investor makes to the broker for the asset; the investor uses the marginable securities held in the brokerage account as collateral.

In a general business context, margin is the difference between a product's or service's selling price and its cost of production, or the ratio of profit to revenue. Margin can also refer to the portion of the interest rate added to the adjustment-index rate in an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM).

### Key Takeaways

- Margin is money borrowed from a broker to purchase an investment and represents the difference between the total value of the investment and the loan amount.
- Margin trading refers to the practice of using borrowed funds from a broker to trade financial assets; the assets serve as collateral for the loan.
- A margin account is a standard brokerage account in which an investor is allowed to use the cash or securities in the account as collateral for a loan.
- The leverage obtained through margin tends to amplify both gains and losses. In the event of a loss, the broker may liquidate positions without prior consent.

## Understanding Margin and Margin Trading

Margin refers to the equity an investor holds in a brokerage account. "Buying on margin" means using money borrowed from a broker to purchase securities. To do so, you need a margin account rather than a standard brokerage account. A margin account is one in which the broker lends the investor money to buy more securities than the account balance alone would allow.

Using margin to purchase securities is essentially using the existing cash or securities already in your account as collateral for a loan. This collateralized loan carries a periodic interest rate that the investor must repay. Because the investor is using borrowed money, both losses and gains are amplified. Margin investing can be advantageous when the investor expects to earn a higher return on the investment than the interest paid on the loan.

For example, if you have an initial margin requirement of 60% for your margin account and you want to purchase $10,000 worth of securities, your margin would be $6,000, and you could borrow the remainder from the broker.

**Important:** The SEC states that margin accounts "can be very risky and they are not appropriate for everyone." [1]

## How It Works

Buying on margin is the process of borrowing money from a broker to buy stock. Think of it as a loan from your brokerage firm. Margin trading allows you to buy more stock than you would normally be able to afford.

To trade on margin, you need a margin account. This is different from a regular cash account, in which you trade using the money in the account. In a margin account, you deposit cash, which serves as collateral for a loan to purchase securities. You can borrow up to 50% of the investment cost. So if you deposit $5,000, you will be able to purchase up to $10,000 worth of securities.

Your broker will charge interest on the loan, which you must repay over time. When you sell securities, the proceeds go first toward repaying the loan, and you keep the remainder.

The Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA) and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) regulate margin trading, setting rules for how much you must deposit, how much you can borrow, and how much you must maintain in your account. [2]

## Components of Margin Trading

By law, your broker is required to obtain your consent to open a margin account. The margin account may be part of your standard account opening agreement or may be a completely separate agreement. A minimum investment of $2,000 is required to open a margin account, though some brokerages may require more. This deposit is known as the minimum margin. [2]

Once the account is opened and operational, you can borrow up to 50% of the purchase price of a stock. The portion of the purchase price you deposit is known as the initial margin. It is important to know that you do not have to leverage all the way up to 50%. You can borrow less, such as 10% or 25%. Note that some brokerages require you to deposit more than 50% of the purchase price. [2]

You can hold the loan for as long as you like, provided you pay the interest on time. When you sell stock in a margin account, the proceeds go to your broker to repay the loan until it is fully paid off.

There is also a restriction called the maintenance margin -- the minimum account balance you must maintain to avoid your broker requiring you to deposit more funds or sell stock to pay down the loan. When this happens, it is called a margin call. A margin call is essentially a demand from your brokerage firm to add money to your account or close positions to bring the account back to the required level. If you fail to meet the margin call, your broker can close any open positions without your consent to restore the account to its minimum value. Your broker has discretion over which positions to liquidate.

Additionally, your broker may charge commissions on trades. You are responsible for any losses incurred during this process, and your broker may liquidate enough shares or contracts to exceed the initial margin requirement.

## Special Considerations

Because using margin is a form of borrowing, it comes with costs, and the marginable securities in the account serve as collateral. The primary cost is the interest you must pay on the loan. Interest charges accrue to your account unless you make payments. Over time, accruing interest increases your debt level. As debt increases, interest charges rise further. Therefore, buying on margin is primarily used for short-term investments. The longer you hold an investment on margin, the greater the return needed to break even. If you hold a margin investment for a long time, the odds of turning a profit diminish.

Not all stocks, ETFs, or other investment securities are eligible for margin purchases. Moreover, not all exchanges or brokers permit margin on every product they handle. For instance, margin trading in certain cryptocurrencies may be restricted.

The Federal Reserve Board regulates which stocks are eligible for margin. [3] As a general rule, brokers will not allow customers to purchase penny stocks or initial public offerings (IPOs) on margin because of the day-to-day risks associated with these types of securities. Individual brokerages may also decide not to margin certain stocks, so check with them for restrictions on your margin account.

**Note:** Large-scale margin calls can create a cascading effect on other investors. If a major investor faces a significant margin call, the forced liquidation could drive down the value of collateral securities held by other margin traders, potentially triggering margin calls for those investors as well.

## Pros and Cons of Margin Trading

#### Pros

- Potential for larger gains due to leverage
- Increased purchasing power
- Generally more flexible than other types of loans
- Can create a self-reinforcing cycle of opportunity, as rising collateral values increase leveraging capacity

#### Cons

- Potential for larger losses due to leverage
- Account fees and interest charges
- May trigger margin calls requiring additional equity deposits
- May result in forced liquidation of securities (typically at a loss)

The primary reason investors trade on margin is to use leverage. Margin trading is centered on amplifying purchasing power by using capital as collateral for a loan. Investors can use that capital to purchase more securities than their own funds would allow.

Consequently, margin trading can amplify profits. Holding more securities means that increases in value produce correspondingly larger gains, since you hold more through borrowed money. At the same time, if the securities used as collateral appreciate in value, you may be able to leverage further because your collateral base has grown.

Margin trading is typically more flexible than other forms of borrowing, with potentially no fixed repayment schedule and broker maintenance margin requirements that may be simple or automated. For most margin accounts, the loan remains open until the securities are sold, at which point final payment is usually due from the borrower.

Investors who use margin trading primarily to amplify gains must also recognize that margin amplifies losses. If margin-purchased securities decline rapidly in value, the investor may owe not only the initial equity investment but also additional funds to the lender. Margin trading also has costs; brokers typically charge interest, which accrues regardless of how the margin account performs.

Because of margin and equity requirements, investors may face margin calls. This is a broker's demand to deposit additional funds into the margin account when equity drops below a certain level. Investors must remain vigilant to meet these additional capital requirements.

If the investor is unable to post additional equity, or if the account value declines so quickly that it breaches certain margin requirements, a forced liquidation may occur. This forced liquidation sells the purchased securities and may result in losses in order to satisfy the broker's requirements.

## Margin Example

Suppose you deposit $10,000 in a margin account. Because you put up 50% of the purchase price, this means you have $20,000 worth of buying power. If you then buy $5,000 worth of stock, you still have $15,000 in buying power remaining. You have enough cash to cover this transaction and have not yet tapped into margin. You only begin borrowing when you purchase securities worth more than $10,000.

Note that a margin account's buying power changes daily based on the price movements of marginable securities.

## Other Uses of the Term Margin

In business accounting, margin refers to the difference between revenue and expenses, and businesses typically track their gross profit margin, operating margin, and net profit margin. Gross profit margin measures the relationship between a company's revenue and its cost of goods sold (COGS). Operating profit margin accounts for COGS and operating expenses and compares the result to revenue, while net profit margin factors in all these expenses, taxes, and interest.

An adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) offers a fixed interest rate for an initial period, after which the rate adjusts. To determine the new rate, the bank adds a margin to an established benchmark index. In most cases, the margin stays the same over the life of the loan, but the index rate changes. To illustrate, consider an ARM with a 4% margin indexed to the Treasury rate. If the Treasury index stands at 6%, the mortgage interest rate is the 6% index rate plus the 4% margin, totaling 10%. [4][5]

## What Is Margin Trading?

Margin trading means borrowing money from a brokerage firm in order to carry out trades. When trading on margin, investors first deposit cash that serves as collateral for the loan and then pay ongoing interest on the money they borrow. This loan increases the investor's purchasing power, allowing them to buy a larger quantity of securities. The securities purchased automatically serve as collateral for the margin loan.

## What Is a Margin Call?

A margin call is a notice issued by a broker to an investor who has taken out a margin loan, demanding that the investor increase the collateral in the margin account. When faced with a margin call, investors typically need to deposit additional cash into their account, sometimes by selling other securities. If the investor refuses, the broker has the right to forcibly sell the investor's positions to raise the necessary funds. Many investors dread margin calls because they can force the sale of positions at unfavorable prices.

## Does the Term "Margin" Have Other Meanings?

Outside of margin lending, the term "margin" has other uses in finance. For example, it is used as a catch-all term to refer to various profit margins, such as gross profit margin, pre-tax profit margin, and net profit margin. The term is sometimes also used to refer to interest rates or risk premiums.

## What Are the Risks of Margin Trading?

When investing on margin, an investor risks losing more than the amount deposited in the margin account. This can occur when the value of the securities held declines, forcing the investor to either provide additional funds or face a forced liquidation of securities.

## Summary

Investors looking to amplify both the potential gains and potential losses of their trades may consider margin trading. Margin trading involves borrowing money, depositing cash as collateral, and using the borrowed funds to trade. Through the use of debt and leverage, margin may result in higher profits than an investor could achieve with their own capital alone. On the other hand, should security values decline, the investor may face the risk of owing more than the collateral posted.
