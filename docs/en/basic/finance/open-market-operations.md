# What Are Open Market Operations (OMOs)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Open market operations (OMOs) refer to the Federal Reserve's buying and selling of securities on the open market. The Fed conducts OMOs to regulate the money supply held in bank reserves across the U.S. banking system. The Fed purchases Treasury securities to increase the money supply and sells them to reduce it.

Through OMOs, the Fed can adjust the federal funds rate, which in turn influences other short-term rates, long-term rates, and foreign exchange rates. This mechanism alters the amount of money and credit available in the economy, affecting key economic variables such as unemployment, output, and the cost of goods and services.

### Key Takeaways

- Open market operations are one of three tools the Fed uses to influence the supply of money and credit.
- The term refers to a central bank's buying and selling of securities on the open market to influence the money supply.
- The Fed uses OMOs to manipulate interest rates, beginning with the federal funds rate used for interbank lending.
- Purchasing securities injects money into the system, lowering rates, easing credit conditions, and stimulating economic activity.
- Selling securities removes money from the system, raising rates, tightening credit, and slowing economic activity.

## Understanding Open Market Operations

To understand OMOs, one must first understand how the Federal Reserve -- the U.S. central bank -- implements monetary policy.

To keep the economy running smoothly and prevent the harmful effects of uncontrolled inflation or deflation, the Board of Governors sets a target for the federal funds rate. [1]

The federal funds rate is the rate at which depository institutions charge one another for overnight loans. This continuous flow of funds allows banks to earn a return on excess cash held in their Federal Reserve accounts while maintaining the reserves necessary to meet customer demand. [2]

As a benchmark, the federal funds rate influences a wide range of other rates, from savings deposit rates to mortgage rates and credit card rates. [1]

OMOs are one of the tools the Fed uses to keep the federal funds rate at its target level.

The central bank can lower rates by purchasing securities (injecting money into the money supply). It can raise rates by selling securities from its balance sheet, withdrawing money from circulation and putting upward pressure on rates.

The Board of Governors sets the federal funds rate target, and the Federal Open Market Committee (FOMC) executes open market operations to achieve that rate. [3]

## Types of Open Market Operations

There are two types of OMOs: permanent and temporary.

**Permanent open market operations** involve the outright purchase or sale of securities for the Fed's portfolio. Permanent OMOs are used to pursue traditional policy objectives -- for example, adjusting the Fed's holdings to put downward pressure on long-term rates and improve financial conditions for consumers and businesses. They are also used to reinvest principal received on maturing securities. [4]

**Temporary open market operations** are used to add or drain reserves from the banking system on a short-term basis. They address reserve needs deemed to be transitory. Unlike permanent OMOs, temporary operations take the form of repurchase agreements (repos) or reverse repurchase agreements (reverse repos). [4]

A repo is a transaction in which the Fed's trading desk buys securities with an agreement to sell them back on a future date. A reverse repo is a transaction in which the Fed sells securities and agrees to repurchase them later. Overnight reverse repos are currently used by the Fed to maintain the federal funds rate within the FOMC's target range. [4]

**Note:** U.S. Treasuries -- government-issued notes and bonds -- are purchased by individual consumers and held in large quantities by financial institutions. Backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government, they are considered safe investments. Treasuries are initially issued by the government and subsequently traded on the secondary market.

## Expansionary and Contractionary Monetary Policy

Fed monetary policy can be either expansionary or contractionary. [5]

**Expansionary policy** aims to increase the money supply and stimulate demand. The Fed purchases Treasuries to inject cash into banks, encouraging them to lend excess reserves to consumers and businesses. As banks compete for customers, interest rates gradually decline. Consumers can borrow more to spend, and businesses are eager to borrow for expansion. [5]

**Contractionary policy** aims to shrink the money supply and dampen demand. The Fed sells Treasuries to withdraw money from the system. With less money in the economy, interest rates gradually rise, borrowing decreases, consumer spending slows, and businesses scale back growth plans. Economic activity decelerates. [5]

## Advantages of Open Market Operations

OMOs allow the Federal Reserve (or any central bank) to combat inflation or deflation without directly intervening in the market economy. Rather than using regulations to control lending, the Fed simply raises or lowers the cost of borrowing.

This enables the Fed to smooth the business cycle and mitigate economic shocks. During recessions, the central bank lowers borrowing costs to encourage business activity and growth. During periods of overheating, the Fed raises borrowing costs to cool speculation and deflate potential bubbles.

OMOs can also be used to support employment growth. By lowering interest rates, the Fed makes it easier to start businesses and hire workers, thereby boosting employment.

## Example of Open Market Operations

In 2019, the Federal Reserve used temporary OMOs (term and overnight repos) to support a healthy supply of bank reserves during what it described as "periods of sharp increases in non-reserve liabilities," and to "mitigate the risk of money market pressures that could adversely affect policy implementation." [6]

The Fed also used repos to offset pressures caused by the COVID-19 crisis in 2020, ensuring banks could maintain adequate reserves and supporting "the smooth functioning of short-term U.S. dollar funding markets." [6]

## Open Market Operations vs. Quantitative Easing

OMOs are a routine policy tool used by the Fed to expand or contract the money supply and support or slow economic activity.

Quantitative easing (QE) is an unconventional tool also used for monetary policy purposes. It involves large-scale purchases of securities to stimulate or stabilize the economy.

The Fed typically deploys QE after conventional tools have been exhausted but additional stimulus is still needed. For example, QE may be used when interest rates are already near zero but economic output remains below what the Fed considers healthy.

## Why Does the Federal Reserve Conduct Open Market Operations?

The Fed uses OMOs to move the federal funds rate and influence the broader rate environment. The objective is either to stimulate or slow the economy. By purchasing securities (typically Treasuries), the Fed increases the money supply and lowers the federal funds rate. By selling securities from its balance sheet, it raises the federal funds rate, withdrawing money from circulation and pushing rates upward.

## What Are Permanent Open Market Operations?

Permanent OMOs refer to the outright purchase or sale of securities (not reversed in the short term) to adjust the money supply. They contrast with temporary OMOs, which involve repos and reverse repos designed to temporarily add or drain reserves from the banking system. [7]

## What Is the Federal Funds Rate?

The federal funds rate is the overnight rate at which depository institutions lend available balances held at the Federal Reserve to one another.

## How Does the Federal Funds Rate Affect Banks?

Financial institutions generally set consumer and business loan rates based on the federal funds rate. As the Fed conducts OMOs to raise or lower the federal funds rate, banks and credit card companies adjust their rates accordingly.

## Conclusion

In open market operations, the Federal Reserve buys and sells securities on the open market to raise or lower interest rates. OMOs are one of the tools available to the Fed to promote or slow national economic activity. Through OMOs, the Fed can inject money into or withdraw money from the nation's money supply.

OMOs can be permanent or temporary. Permanent OMOs involve outright purchases (or sales) of securities. Temporary OMOs involve buying or selling securities with an agreement to reverse the transaction in the near future.
