# What Is Face Value?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Face value is a financial term used to describe the nominal or dollar value of a security, as stated by its issuer. For bonds, face value is the amount paid to the holder at maturity. The face value of a bond is commonly referred to as "par value" or simply "par."

For stocks, face value is the original cost listed on the certificate. In practice, the face value of a stock is largely irrelevant. In countries such as the United States, par value can be set at an arbitrarily low amount. Many U.S. companies deliberately issue stock with very low par values due to specific state regulations. These rules tie a company's registration fees to the par value of its registered shares. By setting a low par value, companies can minimize their registration costs.

Below, we review other important information about par or face value.

### Key Takeaways

- Face value describes the nominal or dollar value of a security, as stated by its issuer.
- The face value of a stock is its initial cost as listed on its certificate.
- The face value of a bond is the dollar amount to be paid to the investor at maturity.
- The actual market price of a stock or bond does not reliably indicate its face value, because many other influencing forces -- such as supply and demand -- are at play.

## Understanding Face Value

In bond investing, face value (par value) is the amount paid to a bondholder at the maturity date, provided the bond issuer does not default. However, bonds sold on the secondary market fluctuate with interest rates. For example, if interest rates are higher than the bond's coupon rate, the bond is sold at a discount (below par).

Conversely, if interest rates are lower than the bond's coupon rate, the bond sells at a premium (above par). While the face value of a bond provides a guaranteed return, the face value of a stock is generally not an indicator of its actual worth.

## Face Value and Bonds

A bond's face value is the amount the issuer pays to the bondholder at maturity. A bond may have an additional coupon rate, or the profit may be based solely on the increase from a below-par original issue price to the face value at maturity.

**Important:** While the par value of bonds is generally fixed, there is a notable exception: inflation-linked bonds, whose par value is adjusted for inflation rates over preset periods.

## Face Value and Stocks

The cumulative face value of all of a company's shares establishes the legal capital that a corporation is obligated to maintain. Only capital above and beyond the legal capital may be released to investors in the form of dividends. In essence, the funds that cover the face value function as a type of default reserve.

However, there is no requirement that businesses list a face value upon issuance. This gives companies the flexibility to use very low values to determine the size of the reserve. For example, AT&T Inc. (T) shares have a par value of $1 per common share, while Apple Inc. (AAPL) shares have a par value of $0.00001.

## Face Value vs. Market Value

The face value of a stock or bond does not equal its actual market value. Market value is determined by the principles of supply and demand and reflects the dollar amount at which investors are willing to buy and sell the security at a specific point in time. Depending on market conditions, face value and market value may have very little correlation.

In the bond market, interest rates (compared to the bond's coupon rate) may determine whether a bond sells above or below par. Zero-coupon bonds, or those where investors receive no income other than buying the bond below face value, are generally sold only below par because that is the only feasible way for an investor to earn a profit.

## Is Face Value the Same as Par Value?

Yes. Face value refers to the dollar value of a financial instrument when it is issued. The face value of a bond is the price the issuer pays at maturity, also called "par value." By comparison, the face value of a stock is the price set by the issuer when the stock is first issued.

## What Is the Difference Between Face Value and Market Value?

While face value is the original price of a stock as set by its issuer, market value is influenced by supply and demand. Market value is the price the market will bear, and it can differ significantly from a stock's initial price. For example, Apple stock has a face value of $0.00001, while its market value as of June 10, 2024 was $193.12 per share.

## What Is the Difference Between Face Value and Bond Price?

A bond's face value is fixed and is often issued in denominations of $1,000. In contrast, its price fluctuates in response to market interest rates, time to maturity, and the issuer's credit rating. Based on these conditions, a bond may be priced above or below par. For example, if interest rates rise, bond prices will fall, trading at a discount to face value on the secondary market.

## What Is Book Value?

Book value is the net value of a company's assets as recorded on its balance sheet. It is calculated by subtracting total liabilities from total assets. In essence, book value reflects the amount that would remain for shareholders if the company liquidated all its assets and paid off all its debts. This metric provides insight into a company's intrinsic value and is frequently used by investors to assess whether a stock is overvalued or undervalued.

## Summary

In finance, face value refers to the nominal or dollar value of a security as declared by its issuer, commonly known as "par value," especially in relation to bonds. Face value differs from market value, which is the price of a security determined by supply and demand. For bonds, face value is the amount paid to the bondholder at maturity. However, like stocks, a bond's market price can fluctuate if sold on the secondary market.

Historically, ensuring a face value prevented companies from selling stock below a certain price. In an era of limited information, face value also protected shareholders. For issuers, face value created a value expectation when selling shares.
