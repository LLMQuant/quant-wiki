# What Is Value Investing?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Value investing is an investment strategy that involves selecting stocks that appear to trade below their intrinsic value or book value. Value investors actively seek stocks they believe the market has undervalued. They believe the market overreacts to good and bad news, causing stock price movements that do not correspond to a company's long-term fundamentals. This overreaction creates an opportunity to profit by buying stocks at a discount.

Warren Buffett is arguably the most well-known value investor today, but there are many others, including Benjamin Graham (Buffett's professor and mentor), David Dodd, Charlie Munger (Buffett's business partner), Christopher Browne (another Graham student), and billionaire hedge fund manager Seth Klarman.

### Key Takeaways

- Value investing is a strategy of selecting stocks that appear to trade below their intrinsic or book value.
- Value investors actively look for stocks they believe the market has undervalued.
- Value investors use financial analysis, avoid following the crowd, and invest for the long term in quality companies.

## Understanding Value Investing

The basic concept behind value investing is straightforward: if you know the true worth of something, you can save a great deal when you buy it on sale. Most people would agree that whether you buy a new television at full price or at a discount, you get the same TV with the same screen size and picture quality.

Stock prices work in a similar fashion, meaning a company's share price can fluctuate even when the company's underlying value remains unchanged. Strictly speaking, no absolute or intrinsic value exists for a given company's stock. But relative value does exist.

Market participants can buy and sell stocks without being anchored to an objective price figure. Stocks, like televisions, go through periods of higher and lower demand, leading to price fluctuations. If a company's fundamentals are intact and future opportunities remain unchanged, the stock's value is essentially the same regardless of the price.

**Important:** Value investing originated from a concept developed by Columbia Business School professors Benjamin Graham and David Dodd in 1934 and was later popularized in Graham's 1949 book, *The Intelligent Investor*.

Just as savvy shoppers argue there is no point in paying full price for a TV because televisions go on sale multiple times a year, savvy value investors believe stocks work the same way. Unlike televisions, of course, stocks do not go on sale at predictable times of the year, and the discounted prices are not publicly advertised.

Value investing is the process of doing detective work to find these secret sales on stocks and buying them at a discount to their market value. In exchange for buying and holding these value stocks for the long term, investors can be handsomely rewarded.

## Intrinsic Value and Value Investing

In the stock market, the equivalent of a stock being cheap or discounted is when its shares are undervalued. Value investors aim to profit from shares they believe are deeply discounted.

Investors use various metrics to determine a stock's valuation or intrinsic value. Intrinsic value is derived through a combination of financial analysis -- examining a company's financial performance, revenue, earnings, cash flow, profit, and fundamental factors including the company's brand, business model, target market, and competitive advantage. Common metrics used to value a company's stock include:

- **Price-to-Book (P/B):** Measures the value of a company's assets relative to its stock price. If the price is lower than the value of the assets, and the company is not in financial distress, the stock is undervalued.
- **Price-to-Earnings (P/E):** Shows the company's earnings track record to determine whether the stock price is not fully reflecting all earnings or is undervalued.
- **Free Cash Flow:** The cash generated from a company's revenue or operations after the cost of expenditures has been deducted.

Free cash flow is the cash remaining after expenses are paid, including operating expenses and capital expenditures (large purchases such as equipment or manufacturing plant upgrades). If a company generates free cash flow, it has money left over to invest in the future of the business, pay down debt, pay dividends to shareholders, or buy back stock.

Of course, many other metrics are used in the analysis, including debt, equity, sales, and revenue growth. After reviewing these metrics, a value investor may decide to purchase shares if the comparative value -- the stock's current price relative to its intrinsic value -- is attractive enough.

## Margin of Safety

Value investors require some room for error in their estimation of value and often set their own "margin of safety" based on their individual risk tolerance. The margin of safety principle is one of the keys to successful value investing. It rests on the premise that buying stocks at bargain prices gives you a better chance of earning a profit when you later sell them. The margin of safety also reduces the potential for loss if the stock does not perform as expected.

If you believe a stock is worth $100 and buy it for $66, you stand to earn a $34 profit simply by waiting for the stock's price to rise to its $100 true value. On top of that, the company might grow and become more valuable, giving you an even larger return. If the stock price rises to $110, you earn $44 since you bought at a discount. Had you purchased it at the full $100 price, your profit would be only $10.

**Note:** Benjamin Graham, the father of value investing, recommended purchasing stocks at two-thirds or less of their liquidation value. This was the margin of safety he believed was necessary to minimize risk and achieve the best returns. [1]

## Value Investors Believe the Market Is Not Efficient

Value investors do not subscribe to the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH), which holds that stock prices already incorporate all available information about a company and therefore always reflect fair value. Instead, value investors believe that stocks can be overpriced or underpriced for a variety of reasons.

For example, a stock may be underpriced because the economy is performing poorly and investors are selling in a panic (as during the Great Recession). Or a stock may be overpriced because investors have become overly excited about an unproven new technology (as during the dot-com bubble). Psychological biases can push stock prices up or down based on news, such as disappointing or unexpected earnings announcements, product recalls, or litigation.

## Value Investors Do Not Follow the Herd

Value investors possess many characteristics of contrarians -- they do not follow the crowd. Not only do they reject the EMH, but when everyone else is buying, they are often selling or standing aside. When everyone else is selling, they are buying or holding. Value investors do not chase popular stocks (because those tend to be overvalued). Instead, they invest in companies that are not household names, provided the financials check out. They also take a second look at stocks that are household names when those stocks' prices have plunged, believing that such companies can recover from setbacks if their fundamentals remain strong and their products and services retain quality.

Value investors care only about a stock's intrinsic value. They think of buying a stock as buying a percentage ownership of a company, and they want to own companies that they know have sound principles and solid financials -- regardless of what everyone else is saying or doing.

## Value Investing Requires Diligence and Patience

Estimating the true intrinsic value of a stock involves financial analysis as well as a fair degree of subjectivity -- meaning it can be as much an art as a science. Two different investors can analyze the exact same valuation data for a company and arrive at different conclusions. Consequently, you need to develop an approach that works for you.

Some investors focus only on existing financial statements and place little weight on future growth estimates. Others focus primarily on a company's future growth potential and projected cash flows. And some do both: noted value investing practitioners Warren Buffett and Peter Lynch, who managed Fidelity's Magellan Fund for many years, are both well known for analyzing financial statements and looking at valuation multiples to identify cases where the market has mispriced stocks.

Despite the variety of approaches, the underlying logic of value investing is to buy assets for less than they are currently worth, hold them for the long term, and profit when they return to intrinsic value or above. It does not deliver instant gratification. You cannot expect to buy a stock for $50 on Tuesday and sell it for $100 on Thursday. Instead, you may have to wait years for your stock investments to pay off, and you will occasionally take losses. The good news is that for most investors, long-term capital gains are taxed at a lower rate than short-term investment gains.

Like all investment strategies, you must have the patience and diligence to stick with your investment philosophy. Some stocks may attract you because their fundamentals are strong, but you should wait if the price is too high. You want to buy the most attractively priced stock, and if no stock currently meets your criteria, you must sit and wait, letting your cash remain idle until an opportunity presents itself.

## Why Stocks Become Undervalued

If you do not believe in the EMH, you can identify reasons why stocks trade below their intrinsic value. Here are several factors that can push a stock's price down and make it undervalued.

People sometimes make irrational investment decisions based on psychological biases rather than market fundamentals. When a particular stock's price is rising -- or when the overall market is rising -- they buy. They see that if they had invested 12 weeks ago, they could have earned 15% by now, and a fear of missing out develops.

Conversely, when a stock's price is falling or when the overall market is declining, loss aversion compels people to sell. Rather than holding their positions and waiting for the market to change direction, they accept a certain loss by selling. This investor behavior is so widespread that it affects individual stock prices, exaggerating both upward and downward market movements and creating excessive volatility.

"Bubbles" are a product of investor enthusiasm that pushes prices ever higher. When the market reaches unsustainable heights, a bubble typically bursts. Because price levels become untenable, investors eventually panic and rush to sell the related assets. This triggers a market crash. The dot-com bubble of the early 2000s was such a case, when technology stocks soared far beyond what companies were actually worth. The housing bubble that burst in 2006 produced a similar market crash over the following years.

Look beyond the headlines. You may find genuinely attractive investment opportunities among small-cap stocks or even foreign equities that do not receive widespread coverage. Most investors want in on the next big thing -- a tech startup, for example -- rather than a boring, established consumer durables manufacturer.

For instance, stocks like Meta (formerly Facebook), Apple, and Google are more likely to be affected by herd mentality than diversified conglomerates like Procter & Gamble or Johnson & Johnson.

Even excellent companies face setbacks such as litigation and recalls. However, just because a company has experienced one adverse event does not mean it no longer has intrinsic value or that its stock will not recover. In other cases, a particular segment or division may weigh on profitability. But if the company divests or closes that line of business, the situation may change.

Analysts have a poor track record of predicting the future, yet investors often panic when a company's earnings miss analyst estimates. Value investors who can see past downgrades and negative news can buy stock at deeper discounts because they are able to recognize a company's long-term value.

Cyclicality is defined as the fluctuations that affect a business. Companies are not immune to the ups and downs of the economic cycle, whether due to seasonality or to shifts in consumer attitudes and sentiment. All of these factors can influence profit levels and share prices but do not affect a company's long-term value.

## Value Investing Strategies

The key to buying undervalued stocks is to thoroughly research the company and make commonsense decisions. Value investor Christopher H. Browne recommends asking whether a company is likely to increase its revenue through the following means:

- Raising prices on products
- Increasing sales figures
- Decreasing expenses
- Selling off or closing unprofitable divisions [2]

Browne also suggests studying a company's competitors to evaluate its future growth prospects. But the answers to all of these questions tend to be speculative, without hard supporting data. Simply put, there are no quantitative software programs that can reliably generate these answers, which is why value investing is sometimes more of an educated estimation. This is why Warren Buffett recommends investing only in industries you have personally worked in or whose consumer goods you are familiar with, such as cars, clothing, appliances, and food.

One thing investors can do is choose the stocks of companies that sell high-demand products and services. While it is difficult to predict when innovative new products will capture market share, it is straightforward to evaluate how long a company has been in business and how it has adapted to challenges over time.

Insider buying is worth noting. Insiders are a company's senior managers and directors, as well as any shareholders who own at least 10% of the company's stock. [3] Managers and directors possess unique knowledge of the companies they run, so if they are buying stock, it is reasonable to assume the company's outlook is favorable.

Likewise, investors who own at least 10% of a company's stock would not have bought so much if they did not see profit potential. Conversely, insider selling does not necessarily signal poor expected performance -- the insider may simply need cash for personal reasons. However, if mass sell-offs are occurring, the situation warrants further analysis.

At some point, value investors must examine a company's financial statements to see how it is performing and compare it with industry peers.

Financial reports present a company's annual and quarterly results. The annual report is SEC Form 10-K, and the quarterly report is SEC Form 10-Q. Companies are required to file these reports with the SEC. [4] You can find them on the SEC website or on the company's investor relations page. [5]

A company's annual report contains a wealth of information. It explains the products and services offered and the direction the company is heading.

## Financial Statements

Financial statements are typically included in a company's regulatory filings and provide a comprehensive view of its financial health. Public companies are required to file three statements -- the balance sheet, the income statement, and the cash flow statement.

The balance sheet consists of two sections: one listing the company's assets and the other listing its liabilities and shareholders' equity. The assets section is broken down into cash and cash equivalents, investments, accounts receivable, inventory, and fixed assets such as plant and equipment.

The liabilities section lists accounts payable, accrued liabilities, short-term debt, and long-term debt. The shareholders' equity section reflects how much money has been invested in the company, the number of shares outstanding, and the amount of retained earnings. Retained earnings function as a savings account holding cumulative profits that can be used for paying dividends and are regarded as a sign of a healthy, profitable company.

The income statement shows revenue generated, expenses incurred, and profit earned. Reviewing the annual income statement rather than a quarterly statement provides a better picture of the company's overall position, as many companies experience seasonal fluctuations in sales.

The cash flow statement records the sources and uses of a company's cash. It identifies which activities generated cash inflows -- such as operations, investing, or financing activities -- and which generated cash outflows.

**Important:** Research has shown that value stocks outperform growth stocks and the overall market over the long run.

## Couch Potato Investing

It is possible to become a value investor without ever reading a 10-K. Couch potato investing is a passive strategy of buying and holding a few investment vehicles for which someone else has already done the investment analysis -- namely, mutual funds or exchange-traded funds. In the case of value investing, those funds would be ones that follow a value strategy and buy value stocks, or that track the moves of high-profile value investors like Warren Buffett.

Investors can buy shares of his holding company, Berkshire Hathaway, which owns or has a stake in dozens of companies that the "Oracle of Omaha" has researched and evaluated.

## Risks of Value Investing

As with any investment strategy, there is a risk of loss with value investing, even though it is a low-to-medium-risk approach. Below are some of those risks and why losses can occur.

Many investors rely on financial statements when making value investing decisions. If you depend on your own analysis, make sure you have the most up-to-date information and that your calculations are accurate. If not, you may make a poor investment or miss a good one. If you are not yet confident in your ability to read and analyze financial statements, continue studying the subject and do not place any trades until you are truly ready.

One approach is to read the footnotes. These are the notes in Form 10-K or Form 10-Q that explain a company's financial statements in greater detail. The notes follow the statements and clarify the company's accounting methods and elaborate on reported results. If the footnotes are unintelligible or the information they present seems unreasonable, you will have a clearer signal to pass on the stock.

A company's income statement may contain one-time items that should be considered extraordinary. These are generally beyond the company's control and are referred to as extraordinary items -- gains or extraordinary items -- losses. Examples include lawsuits, restructurings, and even natural disasters. If you exclude these from your analysis, you can form a better picture of the company's future performance.

However, think critically about these items and use your judgment. If a company reports the same extraordinary item year after year, it may not be truly extraordinary. Also, if there are unexpected losses every year, it could be a sign that the company is experiencing financial difficulties. Extraordinary items are supposed to be unusual and nonrecurring. Also watch for a pattern of write-downs.

Earlier tutorial sections discussed the calculation of various financial ratios that help investors diagnose a company's financial health. There is no single way to determine financial ratios, which can be problematic. The following factors can affect how ratios are interpreted:

- Ratios can be calculated using pre-tax or after-tax figures.
- Some ratios do not give precise results and instead produce estimates.
- Depending on how "earnings" is defined, a company's earnings per share (EPS) may differ.
- Comparing different companies using the same ratios can be tricky if those companies employ different accounting practices.

Overpaying for a stock is one of the main risks for value investors. Paying too much can mean losing part or all of your investment. The same holds true for buying a stock close to its fair market value. Buying undervalued stock means your risk of loss is reduced, even if the company does not perform well.

Recall that one of the fundamental principles of value investing is to build a margin of safety into all investments. This means purchasing stocks at a price of roughly two-thirds or less of their intrinsic value. Value investors want to risk as little capital as possible on potentially overvalued assets, so they aim to avoid overpaying for investments.

Conventional investment wisdom says that investing in individual stocks can be a high-risk strategy. Instead, we are taught to invest in multiple stocks or stock indices to gain exposure to a variety of companies and economic sectors. However, some value investors believe you can have a diversified portfolio even if you hold only a small number of stocks, as long as they represent different industries and sectors of the economy. Value investor and investment manager Christopher H. Browne recommends owning a minimum of 10 stocks. [6] According to Benjamin Graham, you should look to choose 10 to 30 stocks if you want to diversify your holdings. [7]

Another set of experts disagrees. If you want big returns, try choosing just a few stocks, according to the authors of *Value Investing for Dummies*, Second Edition. They contend that having more stocks in your portfolio is likely to lead to average returns. [8] Of course, this advice assumes you are skilled at picking winners, which may not be the case, particularly for beginners.

Ignoring emotions when making investment decisions is difficult. Even if you can maintain a detached, critical stance when evaluating data, fear and excitement may creep in when it comes time to use your hard-earned money to buy stocks. More importantly, once you have purchased a stock, you may be tempted to sell it if the price falls.

Remember that the point of value investing is to resist the temptation to panic and go with the herd. Do not fall into the trap of buying when share prices rise and selling when they drop. Such behavior will erode your returns (since herd investing can quickly become a dangerous game).

## Value Investing in Practice

Value investors seek to profit from market overreactions, which often occur following quarterly earnings releases. As a historical real-world example, on May 4, 2016, Fitbit released its Q1 2016 earnings report and saw a sharp decline in after-hours trading. After the dust settled, the company had lost nearly 19% of its market capitalization. However, while large declines in a company's share price following an earnings release are not uncommon, Fitbit not only met analyst expectations for the quarter but also raised its 2016 guidance. [9]

The company posted Q1 2016 revenue of $505.4 million, up more than 50% year over year. Furthermore, Fitbit projected Q2 2016 revenue of $565 million to $585 million, above the $531 million analysts had forecast. [9]

The company looked strong and was growing. However, because Fitbit invested heavily in R&D during the first quarter, earnings per share (EPS) declined relative to the prior year. This was enough to trigger selling by average investors, which drove the stock price down. A value investor with an eye on the fundamentals, however, would have recognized that Fitbit was an undervalued security with significant upside potential.

As a case in point, Fitbit's revenue exceeded $1.4 billion in 2019, and in 2021 Google finalized the acquisition of Fitbit for $2.1 billion. [10][11] A value investor who purchased Fitbit stock at the depressed price of $5.35 on February 9, 2017, would have come out well ahead, as the stock was converted to cash at $7.35 per share upon the completion of the acquisition. [12][13]

## What Is Value Investing?

Value investing is an investment philosophy that involves purchasing assets at a discount to their intrinsic value. This is also known as a security's margin of safety. Benjamin Graham, known as the father of value investing, first established this term with his landmark book, *The Intelligent Investor*, in 1949. Notable proponents of value investing include Warren Buffett, Seth Klarman, Mohnish Pabrai, and Joel Greenblatt.

## What Is an Example of Value Investing?

Common sense and fundamental analysis underlie many of the principles of value investing. The margin of safety -- the discount at which a stock trades relative to its intrinsic value -- is one of the leading principles. Fundamental metrics such as the price-to-earnings ratio (P/E), for example, illustrate company earnings relative to price. A value investor may invest in a company with a low P/E ratio because it provides one barometer for determining whether a stock is undervalued or overvalued.

## What Are Common Value Investing Metrics?

In addition to the P/E ratio, common value investing metrics include price-to-book (P/B), free cash flow (FCF), and the debt-to-equity ratio (D/E).

## Who Is "Mr. Market"?

"Mr. Market" was first coined by Benjamin Graham as a metaphor for a hypothetical investor prone to sharp mood swings of fear, apathy, and euphoria. "Mr. Market" represents the consequences of emotionally reacting to the stock market rather than relying on rational or fundamental analysis. As a behavioral archetype, "Mr. Market" illustrates the inherent price fluctuations in markets and the extreme emotions -- such as greed and fear -- that can drive them.

## Conclusion

Value investing is a long-term strategy. Warren Buffett, for example, buys stocks with the intention of holding them almost indefinitely. He has said: "I never attempt to make money on the stock market. I buy on the assumption that they could close the market the next day and not reopen it for five years."

When you are ready to make a major purchase or retire, you will probably want to sell your stocks, but by holding a diversified portfolio and maintaining a long-term outlook, you can sell your stocks only when their price exceeds their fair market value (and the price you paid).
