# What Is Capitalism?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Capitalism is an economic system in which private individuals or businesses own capital goods. At the same time, business owners employ workers who receive only wages; labor does not own the means of production but uses them on behalf of the capital owners.

Under capitalism, the production of goods and services is based on supply and demand in the general market -- also known as a market economy. This stands in contrast to a planned or command economy, in which prices are set through central planning.

The purest form of capitalism is free-market or laissez-faire capitalism. Here, private individuals are unrestrained. They may determine where to invest, what to produce or sell, and at what prices to exchange goods and services. The laissez-faire marketplace operates without checks or controls. Today, most countries practice a mixed capitalist system that includes some degree of government regulation of business and selective ownership of certain industries. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- Capitalism is an economic system characterized by private ownership of the means of production, where labor receives only wages.
- Capitalism depends on the enforcement of private property rights, which provide incentives for investment and the productive use of capital.
- Capitalism evolved from feudalism and mercantilism in Europe and dramatically expanded industrialization and the large-scale availability of mass-market consumer goods.
- Pure capitalism can be contrasted with pure socialism, in which all means of production are collectively or state-owned.

## Understanding Capitalism

Capitalism is one type of system for economic production and resource allocation. Instead of planning economic decisions through centralized political methods, as in socialism or feudalism, economic planning under capitalism occurs through decentralized, competitive, and voluntary decisions.

Capitalism is essentially an economic system in which the means of production (factories, tools, machines, raw materials, etc.) are organized by one or more business owners, also known as capitalists. Capitalists then hire workers to operate the means of production in exchange for wages. Workers have no claim of ownership over the means of production or the profits generated by their labor; these belong to the capitalists. [1]

Private property rights are thus fundamental to capitalism. Most modern concepts of private property stem from John Locke's homesteading theory, in which human beings claim ownership by mixing their labor with unclaimed resources. Once owned, the only legitimate means of transferring property are through voluntary exchange, gift, inheritance, or re-homesteading of abandoned property. [2]

Private property promotes efficiency by incentivizing resource owners to maximize the value of their property. The more valuable the resource, the greater the trading power it provides to its owner. In a capitalist system, the person who owns the property is entitled to any value associated with that property.

For individuals or businesses to deploy their capital goods with confidence, a system must exist that protects their legal rights to own or transfer private property. Capitalist societies rely on contracts, fair dealing, and tort law to facilitate and enforce these private property rights.

When property is not privately owned but shared by the public, a problem known as the tragedy of the commons can emerge. With a common-pool resource that all people can use and no one can restrict access to, all individuals have an incentive to extract as much use value as possible and no incentive to conserve or reinvest in the resource. Privatizing the resource is one possible solution, along with various voluntary or involuntary collective action approaches. [3]

**Note:** Under capitalist production, the business owners retain ownership of the goods produced. If a worker at a shoe factory were to take home a pair of shoes they had made, it would be considered theft. This concept is known as the alienation of workers from their labor.

## Capitalism and the Profit Motive

Profits are closely associated with the concept of private property. By definition, an individual enters into a voluntary exchange of private property only when they believe the exchange benefits them in some psychic or material way. In such trades, each party gains extra subjective value -- or profit -- from the transaction.

The profit motive, or the desire to earn profits from business activity, is the driving force of capitalism. It creates a competitive environment in which businesses vie to be the low-cost producer of a particular good in order to gain market share. If it is more profitable to produce a different type of good, a business has the incentive to switch. [4]

Voluntary trade is another related mechanism driving activity in a capitalist system. Resource owners compete with one another over consumers, who in turn compete with other consumers for goods and services. All of this activity is built into the price system, which balances supply and demand to coordinate the allocation of resources. [1]

A capitalist earns the highest profit by using capital goods most efficiently while producing the highest-value good or service. Conversely, a capitalist suffers losses when capital resources are not used efficiently and instead create less valuable output.

**Important:** Capitalism is a system of economic production. Markets are systems of distribution and allocation of goods that have already been produced. While they often go hand in hand, capitalism and free markets refer to two distinct systems.

## Precursors to Capitalism: Feudalism and Mercantilism

Capitalism is a relatively new social arrangement for producing goods in an economy. It largely arose with the advent of the Industrial Revolution, around the late 17th century. [5] Before capitalism, other systems of production and social organization were prevalent.

Capitalism grew out of European feudalism. Until the 12th century, only a very small fraction of the European population lived in towns. Skilled workers lived in cities but received their keep from feudal lords rather than a real wage, and most workers were serfs of landed nobles. However, by the late Middle Ages, rising urbanization made cities increasingly important as centers of industry and trade.

Under feudalism, society was segmented into social classes based on birth or family lineage. Lords (nobility) were the landowners, while serfs (peasants and laborers) did not own land but were employed by the landed nobility.

The advent of industrialization revolutionized trade and encouraged more people to move to towns where they could earn more money working in factories rather than exchanging labor for mere subsistence.

Mercantilism gradually replaced the feudal economic system in Western Europe and became the primary system of commerce from the 16th to the 18th centuries. Mercantilism started as trade between towns, but it was not necessarily competitive. Initially, each town had distinctly different products and services that were slowly homogenized by demand over time. [6]

After the homogenization of goods, trade was carried out in broader and broader circles: town to town, county to county, province to province, and finally, nation to nation. When too many nations were offering similar goods for trade, competition took on a nationalistic edge, sharpened by strong sentiments of nationalism on a continent frequently embroiled in wars.

Colonialism flourished alongside mercantilism, but the nations seeding the world with settlements were not trying to increase trade. Most colonies were set up with an economic system that smacked of feudalism, with raw materials going back to the motherland. In the case of the British colonies in North America, they were forced to buy back finished goods with a pseudo-currency that prevented them from trading with other nations.

Economist Adam Smith observed that mercantilism was a regressive system that created trade imbalances between nations and prevented them from advancing. His ideas for a free market opened the world to capitalism. [7]

Adam Smith's ideas were well timed, as the Industrial Revolution was beginning to cause tremors that would soon shake the Western world. The (often literal) gold mine of colonialism had brought new wealth and new demand for the products of domestic industries, driving expansion and mechanization of production.

As technology advanced by leaps and bounds and factories no longer had to be built near waterways or windmills to function, industrialists began building in the cities where there were now thousands of people to supply labor.

**Note:** Capitalism involves restructuring society into social classes based not on ownership of land, but on ownership of capital (in other words, businesses). Capitalists are able to earn profits from the surplus labor of the working class, who earn only wages. Thus, the two social classes defined by capitalism are the capitalists and the laboring class. [8]

Industrial tycoons were the first people to amass their wealth in their lifetimes, often outstripping both the landed nobles and many of the lending/banking families. For the first time in history, common people could have hopes of becoming wealthy. The new money crowd built more factories that required more labor, while also producing more goods for people to purchase.

During this period, the term "capitalism" -- originating from the Latin word "capitalis," meaning "head of cattle" -- gained currency. In 1850, French socialist Louis Blanc used the term to signify a system of exclusive ownership of industrial means of production by private individuals rather than shared ownership. [9]

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Capitalism

#### Advantages

- More efficient allocation of capital resources
- Competition leads to lower consumer prices
- Wages and overall standard of living rise across the board
- Stimulates innovation and invention

#### Disadvantages

- Creates inherent class conflict between capital and labor
- Generates vast wealth disparities and social inequality
- Can incentivize corruption and crony capitalism in pursuit of profit
- Produces negative externalities such as pollution

More efficient allocation of capital resources: Under this system, labor and the means of production follow capital, as supply follows demand.

Competition leads to lower consumer prices: Capitalists compete with one another and therefore seek to increase profits by cutting costs, including labor and materials costs. Mass production generally benefits consumers as well.

Wages and overall standard of living rise across the board: With the help of labor unions, wages under capitalism have increased. More and better goods have become cheaply available to the general population, raising standards of living in ways previously unimaginable.

Stimulates innovation and invention: In capitalism, inequality is the driving force that encourages innovation, which in turn drives economic development.

Creates inherent class conflict between capital and labor: While capitalists enjoy the potential for high profits, workers may be exploited for their labor, with wages consistently below the true value of the work performed.

Generates vast wealth disparities and social inequality: Capitalism creates significant gaps between the rich and the poor, as well as social inequality.

Can incentivize corruption and crony capitalism in pursuit of profit: Capitalism can provide incentives for corruption that arises from favoritism and close relationships between business people and the state.

Produces negative externalities such as pollution: Capitalism typically leads to a range of negative externalities, such as air and noise pollution, the costs of which are borne by society rather than by the producers who cause them.

## Capitalism vs. Socialism

In terms of political economy, capitalism is often contrasted with socialism. The fundamental difference between the two lies in the ownership and control of the means of production.

In a capitalist economy, property and businesses are owned and controlled by individuals. In a socialist economy, the state owns and manages the vital means of production. However, other differences exist in terms of equity, efficiency, and employment.

The capitalist economy is not concerned with equitable arrangements. The socialist model's primary concern is the redistribution of wealth and resources from the rich to the poor, out of fairness, and to ensure equality of opportunity and equality of outcome. Equality is valued above high achievement, and the collective good is seen above the opportunity for individual advancement.

The capitalist argument is that the profit incentive drives corporations to develop innovative products that are desired by consumers and have demand in the marketplace. It is argued that state ownership of the means of production leads to inefficiency because, without the motivation to earn more money, management, workers, and developers are less likely to put in the extra effort to push new ideas or products.

In a capitalist economy, the state does not directly employ the workforce. This lack of government-run employment can lead to unemployment during economic recessions and depressions.

In a socialist economy, the state is the primary employer. During times of economic hardship, the socialist state can order hiring, so there is full employment. In addition, there tends to be a stronger "safety net" in socialist systems for workers who are injured or permanently disabled. Those who can no longer work have fewer options available to help them in capitalist societies.

**Karl Marx, Capitalism, and Socialism:** Philosopher Karl Marx was famously critical of the capitalist system of production because he viewed it as an engine for creating social ills, vast inequality, and self-destructive tendencies. Marx argued that over time, capitalist businesses would drive each other out of business through fierce competition, while at the same time the laboring class would swell and begin to resent their unfair conditions. His solution was socialism, whereby the means of production would be handed over to the laboring class in an egalitarian fashion. [10]

## Varieties of Capitalism

Today, many countries operate with capitalist production, but this also exists on a spectrum. In reality, there are elements of pure capitalism operating alongside socialist institutions.

The standard spectrum of economic systems places laissez-faire capitalism at one extreme and a completely planned economy -- such as communism -- at the other. Everything in between can be said to be a mixed economy. The mixed economy has elements of both central planning and unplanned private enterprise. By this definition, nearly every country in the world has a mixed economy. [1]

When a government owns some but not all of the means of production, and can legally circumvent, replace, limit, or otherwise regulate private economic interests, it is known as a mixed economy or mixed economic system. A mixed economy respects property rights but places limits on them.

Property owners are restricted in how they exchange with one another. These restrictions come in many forms, such as minimum wage laws, tariffs, quotas, windfall taxes, licensing restrictions, prohibited products or contracts, direct public expropriation, anti-trust legislation, legal tender laws, subsidies, and eminent domain. Governments in mixed economies also fully or partially own and operate certain industries, especially those considered public goods.

In contrast, pure capitalism -- also known as laissez-faire capitalism or anarcho-capitalism -- features all industries privately owned and operated, including public goods, with no central government authority providing regulation or oversight of general economic activity. [11]

## What Is an Example of Capitalism?

An example of capitalist production is if an entrepreneur starts a new widget company and opens a factory. This individual uses available capital that they own or from outside investors to buy the land, build the factory, order the machinery, and source the raw materials. The entrepreneur then hires workers to operate the machines and produce widgets. Note that the workers do not own the machines they use or the widgets they produce. Instead, they receive only wages in exchange for their labor. Those wages represent only a small fraction of the total revenue the entrepreneur derives from the enterprise.

## Who Benefits from Capitalism?

Capitalism tends to benefit capitalists the most. These include business owners, investors, and other owners of capital. While capitalism has been praised for raising the standard of living for many people across the board, it has by far most benefited those at the top.

## Why Is Capitalism Harmful?

Because of how it is structured, capitalism will always pit business owners and investors against the working class. Capitalists compete with each other and therefore seek to increase their profits by cutting costs, including labor costs. At the same time, workers seek higher wages, fairer treatment, and better working conditions. These two incentives are fundamentally at odds with each other, creating class conflict.

## Is Capitalism the Same as Free Enterprise?

Capitalism and free enterprise are often seen as synonymous. In truth, they are closely related but distinct terms with overlapping features. It is possible to have a capitalist economy without complete free enterprise, and it is possible to have a free market without capitalism. Any economy is capitalist as long as private parties control the factors of production. However, a capitalist system can still be regulated by government laws, and the profits of capitalist endeavors can still be taxed heavily. "Free enterprise" can be roughly understood as economic exchanges free from coercive government influence.

## The Bottom Line

Capitalism is an economic and political system in which trade and industry are controlled by private owners for profit. Its core principles are accumulation, ownership, and profiting from capital. In its purest form, capitalism works best when private owners are confident that the wealth they generate will remain in their own pockets -- often a contentious assertion.

Capitalism is the dominant economic system in the world, although it is seldom practiced in its pure form. In many countries, state intervention -- a core characteristic of socialism -- is common. Businesses are allowed to pursue profits within the boundaries set by the government. Most political theorists and nearly all economists argue that capitalism is the most efficient and productive system of exchange.
