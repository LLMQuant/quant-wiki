# What Is Fixed Income?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Fixed income refers to investment securities that pay investors a fixed level of interest or dividends until maturity. At maturity, investors receive the principal amount they originally invested. Government and corporate bonds are the most common fixed-income products.

Unlike equities -- which may not pay income to investors -- or variable-rate securities whose payments fluctuate based on an underlying benchmark such as short-term interest rates, fixed-income securities pay predetermined amounts that remain constant throughout the term.

In addition to purchasing individual fixed-income securities directly, investors can access this asset class through fixed-income exchange-traded funds (ETFs) and mutual funds.

### Key Takeaways

- Fixed income refers to assets and securities that pay investors a set level of income, typically in the form of fixed interest or dividends.
- Government and corporate bonds are the most common fixed-income products.
- Fixed-income securities generally offer lower returns and lower risk than equities.
- For many fixed-income securities, investors receive their original principal at maturity in addition to interest income.
- In a bankruptcy, fixed-income investors typically have priority over common shareholders.

## Understanding Fixed Income

Companies and governments issue debt securities to raise capital for day-to-day operations and to finance large projects. These fixed-income instruments reward investors by paying a fixed interest rate in return for their capital. At the maturity date, the investor receives the original amount invested -- the principal.

For example, a company might issue a 5% bond with a $1,000 face value maturing in five years. The investor pays $1,000 and will not receive principal repayment until the five-year period ends. Over those five years, the company makes annual coupon payments of $50 (5% x $1,000). After five years, the investor receives back the $1,000 principal. Investors can also find fixed-income investments that pay coupons monthly, quarterly, or semi-annually.

Fixed-income securities are recommended for conservative investors seeking a diversified portfolio. The percentage allocated to fixed income depends on the investor's style. There is also an opportunity to diversify with a mix of fixed-income products and equities -- for example, 50% fixed income and 50% equities.

Treasury bonds, municipal bonds, and certificates of deposit (CDs) are all examples of fixed-income products. Each has unique advantages and limitations.

## Types of Fixed-Income Products

The most common examples of fixed-income securities are government or corporate bonds. Bonds trade in the bond market and on the secondary market over the counter (OTC).

The following are common types of fixed-income products:

- **Treasury bills (T-bills):** Short-term securities maturing within one year that do not pay coupons. Investors purchase T-bills at a discount to face value and earn the difference at maturity.
- **Treasury notes (T-notes):** Maturities of 2 to 10 years with a fixed interest rate, sold in $100 increments. Investors receive semi-annual interest payments and receive principal at maturity.
- **Treasury bonds (T-bonds):** Similar to T-notes but with maturities of 20 or 30 years. Also sold in $100 increments.
- **Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS):** Protect investors against inflation. The principal amount adjusts with inflation and deflation.
- **Municipal bonds:** Similar to Treasuries but issued by state and local governments to fund local expenditures. Municipal bonds may offer tax-exempt interest at the federal level and may also be exempt from state and local taxes.
- **Corporate bonds:** Available in many varieties. Price and interest rate depend primarily on the company's financial stability and creditworthiness. Higher-rated bonds typically pay lower coupon rates.
- **Junk bonds (high-yield bonds):** Corporate bonds that pay higher coupon rates due to elevated default risk. Default occurs when the issuer fails to make interest or principal payments.
- **Certificates of deposit (CDs):** Fixed-income instruments offered by financial institutions with maturities of up to five years. They pay higher rates than typical savings accounts and are protected by the FDIC or NCUA.

**Important:** Traditional portfolio theory holds that an efficient investment strategy should balance risk and return and should diversify between equities and bonds. Equities generally carry higher risk and greater potential returns, while fixed-income securities are relatively safer with typically lower returns.

## How to Invest in Fixed Income

Investors looking to include fixed-income securities in their portfolios have several options. Most brokerage firms today offer clients direct access to various bond markets, including Treasuries, corporate debt, and municipal bonds.

1. **Fixed-income mutual funds (bond funds):** Provide exposure to a diversified portfolio of bonds and debt instruments. These funds offer income streams and professional portfolio management.

2. **Fixed-income ETFs:** Function similarly to mutual funds but may be more accessible and cost-effective for individual investors. They may target specific credit ratings, maturities, or other factors. ETFs also carry a management expense ratio.

3. **Bond laddering:** A strategy in which investors purchase a series of bonds with staggered maturities. As bonds mature, the returned principal is reinvested into new bonds that extend the ladder. This approach provides available capital and protects against missing out on rising interest rates.

For example, a $60,000 investment might be divided equally among one-year, two-year, and three-year bonds ($20,000 each). When the one-year bond matures, the $20,000 is reinvested in a bond maturing after the original three-year term. As each subsequent bond matures, the proceeds roll into new issues, providing a steady income stream and the ability to capture higher rates.

**Note:** Fixed-income investing is generally a conservative strategy, with returns generated from predictable, low-risk securities.

## Fixed-Income Example

Suppose PepsiCo (PEP) issues a fixed-rate bond to finance a new bottling plant in Argentina. The 5% bond has a $1,000 face value and matures in five years. The company plans to repay the debt from the new plant's revenue.

You purchase 10 bonds for a total cost of $10,000 and will receive $500 annually in interest (0.05 x $10,000 = $500).

The company receives $10,000 and uses it to build the overseas facility. The interest is fixed, providing you with a steady income stream. After five years, the company repays the $10,000 principal. Over five years, total interest earned is $2,500 ($500 x 5 years).

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Fixed Income

### Income Generation

Fixed-income investments provide a steady income stream throughout the life of the bond or debt instrument. They also give issuers access to needed capital. Predictable income allows investors to plan expenditures, which is why these products are popular in retirement portfolios.

### Relatively Low Volatility

Fixed-income interest payments can help stabilize the risk-return profile of an investment portfolio -- known as market risk. For investors holding equities, price fluctuations can produce large gains or losses. The steady coupon payments from fixed-income products can partially offset losses from declining stock prices, helping diversify overall portfolio risk.

### Guarantees

U.S. Treasury fixed-income investments are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government.

Corporate bonds, while not insured, are backed by the issuer's financial viability. If a company declares bankruptcy or liquidation, bondholders have a higher claim on assets than common shareholders.

Bonds held at brokerage firms are protected by the Securities Investor Protection Corporation (SIPC) for up to $500,000 in cash and securities per account. CDs are protected by the FDIC for up to $250,000 per depositor.

**Tip:** A fixed rate is suitable for low-risk investing, but once locked in, the rate cannot increase. During periods of inflation, fixed-income securities may underperform because newly issued bonds offer higher yields.

### Credit and Default Risk

As noted, Treasuries and CDs are backed by the government and the FDIC. Corporate bonds, while less secure, rank above equity in the capital structure. When evaluating corporate bond investments, consider the credit rating of both the bond and the underlying company. Bonds rated below BBB are considered low quality (junk) and carry elevated loss risk.

Company-specific credit risk can affect a fixed-income instrument's valuation. If a company encounters financial difficulty, its bonds may trade at a discount on the secondary market. Liquidity may also be an issue -- such bonds may be difficult to sell at fair value on the open market.

Bond prices can fluctuate during the life of the instrument. If the investor holds to maturity, price fluctuations are immaterial. However, if sold before maturity, the investor receives the prevailing market price, which may result in a gain or a loss.

### Interest Rate Risk

Fixed-income investors face interest rate risk. In a rising-rate environment, the fixed coupon paid by an existing bond may lag behind market rates. The bond loses value on the secondary market (when rates rise, bond prices fall), and the investor's capital is locked in and unavailable for higher-yielding opportunities.

For example, if an investor buys a two-year bond paying 2.5% and two-year rates subsequently rise to 5%, the investor is locked into the 2.5% return regardless of market conditions.

### Inflation Risk

Rising prices erode the purchasing power of fixed-income returns. If a fixed-income security yields 2% and inflation rises to 1.5%, the investor's real return is only 0.5%.

#### Advantages

- Steady, predictable income stream
- More stable returns than equities
- Higher claim on assets in bankruptcy
- Some securities backed by government and FDIC guarantees

#### Disadvantages

- Returns generally lower than other investments
- Credit and default risk exposure
- Sensitive to interest rate risk
- Sensitive to inflation risk

**Tip:** Some government bonds, such as Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), are indexed to inflation to moderately protect investors.

## Fixed-Income Analysis: Factors to Consider

Many investors perform fixed-income analysis when deciding which product to invest in. This analysis helps assess which investments best fit their profile.

Fixed-income analysis typically begins with risk. Every investment involves a risk-return tradeoff. Higher-risk investments should offer higher returns, all else being equal.

For fixed-income securities, risk is closely tied to the issuer's credit rating, the security's term, and the industry in which the company operates. U.S. government fixed-income securities generally offer lower returns because of their lower default risk. Conversely, corporate issuers -- especially those with cash flow challenges -- may carry higher risk but offer higher returns.

Some fixed-income securities make periodic payments, providing cash flow during the investment period. This also reduces risk, since not all capital depends on repayment at maturity.

Finally, different fixed-income securities have different features. Some are callable, meaning the issuer can repay the bond before maturity. Others allow conversion into common stock. Understanding these features is essential, as they can affect investor returns.

## Fixed-Income Securities: Examples

Fixed-income securities are debt instruments that pay a fixed rate of interest. These include bonds issued by governments or corporations, certificates of deposit, money market funds, and commercial paper. Preferred stock is sometimes classified as fixed income because it is a hybrid security combining debt and equity characteristics.

## What Is the Difference Between Fixed Income and Equities?

Fixed-income securities are debt instruments that pay interest and return principal at maturity. Equities represent ownership shares in a company. Stocks do not mature and do not guarantee income in the form of dividends. In general, equities carry higher risk and higher potential returns than corporate debt.

## How Does Inflation Affect Fixed Income?

Inflation typically has a negative effect on the value of fixed-income securities. As interest rates rise, bond prices fall. The prices of bonds and other fixed-income securities are inversely related to interest rate changes.

## What Is the Difference Between Fixed-Rate and Floating-Rate Bonds?

A fixed-rate bond pays the same interest rate throughout its term. A floating-rate or variable-rate bond periodically resets its coupon based on prevailing market rates.

## Conclusion

Fixed income refers to the fixed interest rate and principal repayment that debt securities provide to investors. Fixed-income securities include various types of bonds and certificates of deposit.

As an asset class, fixed income is generally less volatile than equities and is considered a more conservative investment. A well-diversified portfolio should include some allocation to fixed income. For some investors, this allocation increases as the investment horizon shortens -- for example, as they approach retirement.
