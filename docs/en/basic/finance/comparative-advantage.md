# What Is Comparative Advantage?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Comparative advantage is an economy's ability to produce a particular good or service at a lower opportunity cost than its trading partners. It is used to explain why companies, countries, or individuals can benefit from trade.

In the context of international trade, comparative advantage refers to the products a country can produce more cheaply or easily than others. However, some contemporary economists argue that an exclusive focus on comparative advantage can lead to the exploitation and depletion of a country's resources.

The law of comparative advantage is commonly attributed to English political economist David Ricardo and his 1817 work *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*, though it was likely Ricardo's mentor James Mill who originated the analysis. [1]

### Key Takeaways

- Comparative advantage is an economy's ability to produce a particular good or service at a lower opportunity cost than its trading partners.
- The theory of comparative advantage introduces opportunity cost as a factor in choosing among different production possibilities.
- Comparative advantage suggests that countries will trade with each other, exporting the goods in which they have a relative advantage.
- A sole focus on a country's comparative advantage can also carry drawbacks, potentially exploiting its labor and natural resources.
- Absolute advantage refers to a country's unambiguous superiority in producing a particular good.

## Understanding Comparative Advantage

Comparative advantage is one of the most important concepts in economic theory and a foundational principle of the argument that all participants can benefit from cooperation and voluntary trade at all times. It is also a bedrock principle of international trade theory.

The key to understanding comparative advantage lies in a firm grasp of opportunity cost. In simple terms, opportunity cost is the potential benefit that someone forgoes when choosing one option over another.

In the context of comparative advantage, one company's opportunity cost -- the potential benefit forgone -- is lower than another's. The company with the lower opportunity cost, and therefore the smallest forgone potential benefit, holds the advantage.

Another way to think about comparative advantage is as the best option given a trade-off. If you are comparing two different options, each with its own trade-off (some benefits and some drawbacks), the one with the best overall package has the comparative advantage.

People learn their comparative advantage through wages. This drives individuals toward the jobs at which they are comparatively best. If a skilled mathematician earns more as an engineer than as a teacher, then both they and everyone who trades with them are better off when they pursue engineering.

Wider gaps in opportunity costs allow for higher levels of value production by organizing labor more efficiently. The greater the diversity of people and their skills, the greater the opportunity for beneficial trade through comparative advantage.

## Example of Comparative Advantage

Consider a famous athlete like Michael Jordan. As a renowned basketball and baseball star, Michael Jordan is an exceptional athlete whose physical abilities surpass most other people. He could likely paint his house quickly, thanks to his ability and impressive height.

Hypothetically, Michael Jordan could paint his house in eight hours. In those same eight hours, he could also film a television commercial that would earn him $50,000. Meanwhile, Jordan's neighbor Joe could paint Jordan's house in 10 hours. In that same period, he could work at a fast-food restaurant and earn $100.

In this example, Joe has the comparative advantage as a house painter because his opportunity cost is lower -- even though Michael Jordan could paint the house faster and better. The optimal trade is for Michael Jordan to film the commercial and pay Joe to paint his house. As long as Jordan earns the expected $50,000 and Joe earns more than $100, the deal is a winner. Because of their diverse skills, Michael Jordan and Joe would find this arrangement mutually beneficial.

## Comparative Advantage vs. Absolute Advantage

Comparative advantage contrasts with absolute advantage. Absolute advantage is the ability to produce more or better goods and services than someone else. Comparative advantage is the ability to produce goods and services at a lower opportunity cost -- not necessarily in greater volume or higher quality.

To see the difference, consider an attorney and their secretary. The attorney is better at providing legal services and is also a faster typist and organizer. In this case, the attorney has an absolute advantage in both legal services and secretarial work.

Nevertheless, they benefit from trade thanks to their comparative advantages and disadvantages. Suppose the attorney produces $175 per hour in legal services and $25 per hour in secretarial duties. The secretary can produce $0 per hour in legal services and $20 per hour in secretarial duties. Here, opportunity cost is crucial.

To produce $25 of income from secretarial work, the attorney must forgo $175 of income from not practicing law. The opportunity cost of secretarial work is high. The attorney is better off providing an hour of legal services and hiring the secretary to type and organize. The secretary is much better off typing and organizing for the attorney; the opportunity cost of doing so is low. That is where the comparative advantage lies.

**Important:** Comparative advantage provides a key insight: trade still occurs even when one country has an absolute advantage in all products.

## Comparative Advantage vs. Competitive Advantage

Competitive advantage refers to a company's, economy's, country's, or individual's ability to provide stronger value to consumers than its competitors. It is similar to, but distinct from, comparative advantage.

To achieve a competitive advantage over others in the same field, at least one of the following must be accomplished: the firm should be a low-cost provider of its goods or services, it should offer superior goods or services compared to competitors, or it should focus on a particular segment of the consumer pool.

## Comparative Advantage in International Trade

David Ricardo famously demonstrated how England and Portugal both benefit by specializing and trading according to their comparative advantages. In this case, Portugal was able to produce wine at low cost, while England could manufacture cloth cheaply. Ricardo predicted that each country would eventually recognize these facts and stop attempting to produce the product that was more costly to make.

Indeed, over time England stopped producing wine and Portugal stopped manufacturing cloth. Both countries found it advantageous to stop producing these goods domestically and to trade with each other instead.

**Note:** Comparative advantage is closely associated with free trade, which is seen as beneficial, while tariffs are closely associated with restricted trade and a zero-sum game.

A contemporary example: China's comparative advantage vis-a-vis the United States lies in cheap labor. Chinese workers produce simple consumer goods at a much lower opportunity cost. The United States' comparative advantage lies in specialized, capital-intensive labor. American workers produce sophisticated goods and investment opportunities at a lower opportunity cost. Specializing and trading along these lines benefits both parties.

The theory of comparative advantage helps explain why protectionism is typically unsuccessful. Proponents of this analytical approach argue that countries engaged in international trade have already worked to find partners with comparative advantages.

If a country withdraws from international trade agreements and imposes tariffs, it may produce local benefits in the form of new jobs and industries. However, this is not a long-term solution to trade problems. Eventually, that country will be at a disadvantage relative to its neighbors, which are already better able to produce those goods at a lower opportunity cost.

**Note:** The classical understanding of comparative advantage does not account for certain disadvantages of over-specialization. For example, an agricultural country that focuses on cash crops and relies on the world market for food may find itself vulnerable to global price shocks.

## Criticism of Comparative Advantage

Why is there not open trade between all countries? When free trade exists, why do some countries remain poor at the expense of others? Perhaps comparative advantage does not work as well as the theory suggests. There may be many reasons for this, but the most influential is what economists call rent-seeking behavior. Rent-seeking occurs when an organized group lobbies the government to protect its interests.

For example, suppose American shoe producers understand and agree with the free trade argument but also recognize that their narrow interests would be negatively affected by cheaper foreign footwear. Even if workers would be most productive shifting from making shoes to making computers, no one in the shoe industry wants to lose their job or see profits decline in the short run.

This desire leads shoemakers to lobby for, say, special tax breaks for their products or extra tariffs (or even outright bans) on foreign footwear. Appeals to save American jobs and preserve a time-honored American craft abound -- even though, in the long run, American workers would be made relatively less productive and American consumers relatively poorer by such protectionist tactics.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Comparative Advantage

In international trade, the law of comparative advantage is often used to justify globalization, since countries can achieve higher material outcomes by producing only the goods in which they have a comparative advantage and trading those goods with other nations. Countries like China and South Korea have made major productivity gains by specializing their economies in certain export-oriented industries where they hold a comparative advantage.

Following comparative advantage can improve production efficiency because it focuses only on those tasks or products that can be completed more cheaply. Products that are more expensive or time-consuming to produce can be purchased from elsewhere. In turn, this increases a company's or country's overall profit margins because costs associated with less efficient production are eliminated.

On the other hand, over-specialization can also have negative effects, particularly for developing countries. While free trade allows developed countries to access cheap industrial labor, it also carries high human costs due to the exploitation of local workforces.

By offshoring manufacturing to countries with less stringent labor laws, companies can benefit from child labor and coercive employment practices that would be illegal at home.

Similarly, an agricultural country that focuses exclusively on certain export crops may find itself suffering from soil depletion and destruction of natural resources, as well as harm to indigenous peoples. Moreover, there are strategic disadvantages to over-specialization, since such a country would find itself dependent on global food prices. [2]

### Advantages and Disadvantages of Comparative Advantage

#### Pros

- Greater efficiency
- Improved profit margins
- Reduced need for government protectionism

#### Cons

- Developing countries may be at a relative disadvantage
- May facilitate unfair or exploitative working conditions elsewhere
- Can lead to resource depletion
- Risk of over-specialization
- May incentivize rent-seeking behavior

## Who Developed the Law of Comparative Advantage?

The law of comparative advantage is usually attributed to David Ricardo, who described the theory in his 1817 publication *On the Principles of Political Economy and Taxation*. However, the idea of comparative advantage may have originated with Ricardo's mentor and editor, James Mill, who also wrote on the subject. [3]

## How Is Comparative Advantage Calculated?

Comparative advantage is usually measured in terms of opportunity costs -- the value of alternative goods that could be produced with the same resources. This is then compared with the opportunity cost of another economic actor producing the same good. For example, if Factory A can make 100 pairs of shoes with the same resources needed to produce 500 belts, then each pair of shoes has an opportunity cost of 5 belts. If competitor Factory B can produce three belts with the resources needed for one pair of shoes, then Factory A has a comparative advantage in producing belts and Factory B has a comparative advantage in producing shoes.

## What Is an Example of Comparative Advantage?

An interesting example of comparative advantage often arises with senior executives, who may consider hiring an assistant to respond to their emails and perform certain secretarial functions. The executive may even be better at performing these duties than the assistant -- but the time they spend on secretarial work could be more profitably spent doing executive work. Likewise, even if the assistant is mediocre at secretarial work, they would likely be even more unsuited for executive work. When both focus on their comparative advantages, they end up being more productive.

## The Bottom Line

Comparative advantage is one of the most important concepts in economics. In classical economics, the idea explains why people, nations, and businesses can experience greater collective benefits through trade and exchange than they can produce alone. However, contemporary economists also point out that these gains can be one-sided or result in the exploitation of weaker parties.
