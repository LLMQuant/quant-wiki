# What Is Financial Leverage?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Financial leverage refers to the use of borrowed capital as a funding source. Companies typically employ leverage when investing in themselves for expansion, acquisitions, or other growth initiatives.

Leverage is also an investment strategy that uses borrowed funds -- specifically various financial instruments or borrowed capital -- to amplify the potential return on an investment.

### Key Takeaways

- Leverage involves using debt (borrowed funds) to magnify returns on an investment or project.
- Companies can use leverage to invest in growth strategies.
- Some investors use leverage to increase their purchasing power in the market.
- A range of financial leverage ratios gauge a company's financial strength, the most common being the debt-to-asset ratio and the debt-to-equity ratio.

## Understanding Financial Leverage

Leverage is the use of debt or borrowed capital to undertake an investment or project. It is commonly used to boost an entity's equity base. Both investors and companies employ the concept of leverage:

- Investors use leverage to significantly increase the returns an investment may deliver. They leverage their investments through various instruments including options, futures, and margin accounts.
- Companies can use leverage to finance their assets. Rather than issuing equity to raise capital, companies can use debt financing to invest in business operations and fuel growth.

Investors who are not comfortable using leverage directly can gain indirect exposure in several ways. They can invest in companies that use leverage in the normal course of business to finance or expand operations -- without increasing their own outlay.

The purpose and effect of financial leverage is to multiply the potential returns on a project. At the same time, leverage also multiplies the potential downside risk if the investment does not succeed. When a company, property, or investment is described as "highly leveraged," it means that it carries more debt than equity.

## How to Calculate Financial Leverage

A full suite of leverage financial ratios is used to calculate the degree of debt leverage a company employs to maximize profits. Below are several common leverage ratios.

You can analyze a company's leverage by calculating its debt-to-asset ratio. This ratio indicates how much debt the company uses to generate its assets. A high debt ratio signals that the company relies on leverage to finance its assets. A ratio of 1.0 means the company has $1 of debt for every $1 of assets. Below 1.0 indicates more assets than debt; above 1.0 indicates more debt than assets.

Keep in mind that when calculating this ratio, you are using all debt, including both short-term and long-term debt instruments.

Rather than focusing on what the company owns, you can measure leverage by looking strictly at how assets are financed. The debt-to-equity (D/E) ratio compares the money a company has borrowed against the capital raised from private investors or shareholders.

A D/E ratio greater than 1.0 means the company has more debt than equity. However, this does not necessarily mean the company is highly leveraged. Each company and industry typically operates in a specific way that may warrant a higher or lower ratio.

For example, a startup technology company may struggle to secure financing and must often turn to private investors. A 0.5 D/E ratio (one dollar of debt for every two dollars of equity) may still be considered high for that industry.

You can also compare a company's debt to the income it generates over a given period using earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization (EBITDA). The debt-to-EBITDA ratio indicates how much income is available to service debt before operating expenses are deducted.

A company with a high debt-to-EBITDA ratio carries a significant amount of debt relative to its earnings. The higher the ratio, the greater the company's leverage.

**Important:** One issue with using EBITDA is that it does not accurately reflect earnings because it excludes expenses that must be accounted for. It is a non-GAAP metric that some companies use to create an appearance of higher profitability.

Debt is not directly referenced in the equity multiplier. However, it is inherently included because total assets and total equity both have a direct relationship to total debt. The equity multiplier seeks to understand a company's ownership weighting by analyzing how its assets are financed. A company with a low equity multiplier has financed the majority of its assets with equity, meaning it is not highly leveraged.

DuPont analysis uses the equity multiplier to measure financial leverage. It is calculated by dividing total assets by total equity. Once determined, total financial leverage is multiplied by total asset turnover and the profit margin to produce return on equity.

For example, if a publicly traded company has total assets valued at $500 million and shareholder equity valued at $250 million, the equity multiplier is 2.0 ($500 million / $250 million). This indicates the company has financed half of its total assets with equity. But if it had $500 million in assets and only $100 million in equity, the equity multiplier would be 5.0. A larger equity multiplier suggests further investigation is warranted because more financial leverage may be in use.

Fundamental analysts can also use the degree of financial leverage (DFL) ratio. DFL is calculated by dividing the percentage change in a company's earnings per share (EPS) by the percentage change in its earnings before interest and taxes (EBIT) over a given period.

The goal of DFL is to understand how sensitive a company's EPS is to changes in operating income. A higher ratio indicates a higher degree of leverage, and a company with a high DFL is likely to have more volatile earnings.

The formulas above are used to evaluate a company's use of leverage in its operations. However, households can also use leverage. By taking on debt and using personal income to cover interest expenses, households employ leverage as well.

Consumer leverage is derived by dividing household debt by disposable income. Households with higher calculated consumer leverage carry high levels of debt relative to their income and are therefore more leveraged.

If consumer leverage is too high, individuals may eventually find it difficult to obtain loans. For instance, lenders typically set debt-to-income limits when households apply for a mortgage.

**Important:** Financial ratios are most valuable when compared over time or against competitors. Exercise caution when analyzing leverage ratios across different companies, as different industries may require different financing structures.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Financial Leverage

Some investors and traders use leverage to amplify profits. When an initial investment is multiplied by additional upfront capital, trades can become exponentially more valuable. Leverage also provides access to more expensive investment options that would otherwise be out of reach with a small amount of upfront capital.

Leverage is best used for short-term, low-risk situations that require significant capital. For example, during an acquisition, a growth company may have a short-term need for capital that leads to a strong medium-to-long-term growth opportunity. Rather than gambling with additional capital on risky ventures, leverage enables savvy companies to execute opportunities at ideal moments with the intention of exiting their leveraged positions quickly.

If leverage can amplify investment returns, it can also amplify losses. Using leverage creates higher downside risk, sometimes resulting in losses that exceed the initial capital investment. On top of that, brokers and contract traders typically charge fees, premiums, and margin rates, and require the maintenance of a margin account with a specific balance. This means that even if a trade goes against you, additional costs are still owed.

Leverage also has the potential for complexity. Investors must understand their financial position and the risks they inherit when entering a leveraged position. This may require closer attention to one's portfolio and the contribution of additional capital if the trading account does not have sufficient funds per brokerage requirements.

### Leverage

#### Pros

- Can amplify returns, creating the potential for outsized profits
- Reduces barriers to entry by allowing investors to access more expensive trading opportunities
- Strategic way for companies to meet short-term financing needs for acquisitions

#### Cons

- Can amplify downside by creating the potential for losses and increased debt
- More expensive than other types of trading
- Incurs fees, margin rates, and contract premiums regardless of whether the trade succeeds
- More complex, as trades may require additional capital and time based on portfolio needs

## Financial Leverage vs. Margin

Margin is a special type of leverage that involves using existing cash or securities as collateral to increase one's purchasing power in financial markets. Margin allows you to borrow money from a broker at a fixed rate to purchase securities, options, or futures contracts in anticipation of substantial returns. [1]

You can use margin to create leverage, thereby increasing your purchasing power by the total amount in your margin account. For example, if you need $1,000 of collateral to purchase $10,000 worth of securities, your margin is 1:10 or 10x leverage.

## Example of Financial Leverage

Suppose a company is formed with a $5 million investment from investors. This equity is the capital available for operations. If the company takes on $20 million in debt financing, it now has $25 million to invest in business operations and greater opportunity to increase value for shareholders. However, its debt-to-equity ratio would be high. Depending on the industry and its average ratios, such a high ratio may be expected -- or cause for concern.

These types of leveraged positions occur frequently. For example, Apple (AAPL) issued $4.7 billion in green bonds for the third time in March 2022. [2] Through debt financing, Apple can expand low-carbon manufacturing and create recycling opportunities while using carbon-free aluminum. This type of leverage strategy can work when revenue generated exceeds the debt created from issuing bonds.

## What Is Financial Leverage?

Financial leverage is the strategic use of borrowed money to invest in assets. The goal is for the return on those assets to exceed the cost of borrowing. The objective of financial leverage is to increase profitability without deploying additional personal capital.

## What Is an Example of Financial Leverage?

An example of financial leverage is purchasing a rental property. If an investor puts down only 20%, they borrow the remaining 80% from a lender. The investor then rents out the property, using rental income to cover the monthly principal and debt payments. If the income covers the obligations, the investor has successfully used leverage to gain personal resources (ownership of the home) and potential residual income.

## How Is Financial Leverage Calculated?

Financial leverage can be calculated in several different ways. A suite of financial ratios known as leverage ratios analyzes the level of indebtedness a company experiences relative to various assets. The two most common financial leverage ratios are the debt-to-equity ratio (total debt / total equity) and the debt-to-asset ratio (total debt / total assets).

## What Is a Good Financial Leverage Ratio?

Generally, a D/E ratio greater than 1 indicates that a company has chosen to take on more debt rather than finance through shareholders. While this is not inherently problematic, the company may face greater risk due to the inflexibility of debt obligations. The company must be compared with similar firms in the same industry or through its historical financial data to determine whether it has a sound leverage ratio.

## Why Is Financial Leverage Important?

Financial leverage is important because it creates opportunities for investors and businesses. For investors, that opportunity comes with high risk, as leverage amplifies losses during downturns. For businesses, leverage creates additional debt that can be difficult to service if the ensuing years bring a slowdown.

## The Bottom Line

Individuals and companies can increase their equity base in multiple ways. Financial leverage is one such method. For businesses, financial leverage involves borrowing money to fuel growth. It allows investors to access certain instruments with less initial outlay.

Because risk accompanies the use of leverage, it is important to weigh the advantages and disadvantages and determine whether financial leverage truly fits your financial situation and goals.
