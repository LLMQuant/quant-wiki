# What Is Debt Restructuring?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Debt restructuring is a process used by companies, individuals, and even sovereign nations to avoid the risk of default -- for example, by negotiating lower interest rates. When a debtor faces financial distress, restructuring offers a less costly alternative to bankruptcy and can benefit both the borrower and the lender.

### Key Takeaways

- Debt restructuring is available to companies, individuals, and sovereign nations.
- The process may involve reducing loan interest rates or extending repayment terms.
- A debt-for-equity swap, in which creditors agree to cancel some or all outstanding debt in exchange for equity in the company, is one common form of restructuring.
- A nation seeking to restructure its debt may shift obligations from the private sector to public-sector institutions.

## How Debt Restructuring Works

Some companies pursue debt restructuring before facing bankruptcy. The process typically involves persuading lenders to agree to reduced interest rates, extended maturities, or both. These concessions improve the company's ability to service its obligations and continue operating. Creditors recognize that they would likely recover even less through a formal bankruptcy or liquidation process.

Debt restructuring can be a win-win: the business avoids bankruptcy, and lenders generally recover more than they would under insolvency proceedings.

The same principles apply to individuals and sovereign nations, though at very different scales.

**Important:** Individuals seeking to restructure their debt may engage a debt relief company to assist with negotiations, but should verify the firm's reputation to avoid scams.

## Types of Debt Restructuring

Companies can use several tools for debt restructuring.

**Debt-for-equity swaps** occur when creditors agree to cancel a portion or all of the outstanding debt in exchange for equity in the company. This approach is generally preferred when both the outstanding debt and the company's assets are substantial, and forcing the business to cease operations would be counterproductive. Creditors may prefer to take control of a distressed company as a going concern.

**Haircuts on bonds:** A company may renegotiate with its bondholders to write off a portion of outstanding interest payments or principal balances.

**Callable bonds:** Companies often issue callable bonds to protect themselves against an inability to make interest payments. A callable bond can be redeemed early by the issuer if interest rates decline, allowing the company to refinance existing debt at lower rates.

**Income bonds:** In rare cases, a company may issue income bonds, which commit to repaying the principal but make no promise of interest or dividend payments.

**Sovereign debt restructuring:** Nations can face sovereign debt default, a recurrent phenomenon throughout history. In modern times, some countries opt to restructure their debt with bondholders. This may involve transferring debt from the private sector to public-sector institutions better equipped to absorb the impact of default.

Sovereign creditors may also be required to accept a haircut -- agreeing to receive a reduced percentage of outstanding debt, such as 25% of face value. Bond maturities may also be extended, giving the government more time to secure funds for repayment.

Unfortunately, this type of restructuring lacks robust international oversight, even when obligations cross national borders.

**Individual debt restructuring:** Individuals facing potential bankruptcy can renegotiate terms with creditors and tax authorities. For example, someone unable to continue payments on a $250,000 mortgage might reach an agreement with the lender to reduce the mortgage to 75%, or $187,500 (75% x $250,000). In return, the lender might receive 40% of the proceeds when the property is sold.
