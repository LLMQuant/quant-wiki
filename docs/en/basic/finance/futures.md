# What Are Futures?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Futures are contracts obligating the buyer to purchase, or the seller to sell, a specific underlying asset at a predetermined price on a future date. The underlying asset can be a commodity, a security, or another financial instrument. Futures trading requires the buyer to acquire the underlying asset at the set price upon expiration, or the seller to deliver it, regardless of the prevailing market price.

Futures trading commonly refers to futures whose underlying assets are securities in the stock market. These contracts derive their value from the future price of individual company stocks or stock market indices such as the S&P 500, the Dow Jones Industrial Average, or the Nasdaq. [1] Futures traded on exchanges such as the Chicago Mercantile Exchange can also have underlying "assets" that include physical commodities, bonds, or weather events. [2]

### Key Takeaways

- Futures are derivatives -- financial contracts whose value is derived from price changes in an underlying asset.
- Stock market futures obligate the buyer to purchase a given stock or basket of stocks at a predetermined future date and price, and the seller to deliver accordingly.
- Futures contracts can be used to hedge against price movements in a company's stock, a basket of stocks, or an index, helping to prevent losses from adverse price changes.

## Underlying Assets

Futures traders can lock in the price of an underlying asset. These contracts have expiration dates and known, fixed prices. Stock futures have specific expiration dates, organized by month. Underlying assets for futures contracts may include: [3]

- Commodity futures based on physical commodities such as crude oil, natural gas, corn, and wheat
- Cryptocurrency futures based on the price movements of assets such as Bitcoin or Ethereum
- Currency futures covering currencies including the euro and the British pound
- Energy futures covering crude oil, natural gas, gasoline, and heating oil
- Stock futures based on individual stocks and baskets of stocks traded in the market
- Interest rate futures used to speculate on or hedge against future changes in Treasury and other bond yields
- Precious metal futures for gold and silver
- Stock index futures with underlying assets such as the S&P 500 Index

**Important:** The buyer of a futures contract is obligated to take delivery of the underlying stock or shares at expiration, not before. However, the buyer may sell the position prior to expiration. There is a distinction between futures and options: American-style options grant the holder the right, but not the obligation, to buy or sell the underlying asset at any time before expiration. [4]

## How Futures Trading Works

Futures contracts are standardized in terms of quantity, quality, and asset delivery, enabling them to be traded on futures exchanges. They obligate the buyer to purchase shares of a stock or index at a predetermined date and price. [5] This ensures market transparency, enhances liquidity, and facilitates accurate price discovery.

Stock futures have specific expiration dates organized by month. For example, futures on major indices such as the S&P 500 may expire in March, June, September, and December. [6] The nearest expiring contract is called the "front-month" contract and typically sees the most trading activity. As a contract approaches expiration, traders wishing to maintain their positions generally roll into the next available contract month. Short-term traders tend to focus on front-month contracts, while longer-term investors may look further out along the curve. [1]

For example, when trading S&P 500 index futures, a trader might buy a futures contract agreeing to purchase shares of the index at a set price six months from now. If the index rises, the futures contract increases in value and the trader can sell it at a profit before expiration. Selling futures works in reverse: if a trader believes a stock will decline and sells a futures contract, and the market falls as expected, the trader can repurchase the contract at a lower price and profit from the difference.

**Tip:** Settlement of a futures contract depends on the asset. Physical delivery is standard for commodities such as oil, gold, or wheat. However, futures based on stocks and stock indices are settled in cash. [1]

## Speculation

Futures contracts allow traders to speculate on commodity prices. If a trader buys a futures contract and the price of the underlying asset rises above the contract price at expiration, the trader earns a profit. Conversely, if the price falls below the purchase price specified in the contract, the trader incurs a loss. Before expiration, a long futures position can be closed by selling the contract at the current price.

Traders can also take a short speculative position if they anticipate a price decline. If the price falls, the trader takes an offsetting position to close the contract. The net difference is settled at expiration. The investor profits if the underlying asset's price is below the contract price; the investor loses if the current price is above the contract price.

Suppose a trader selects an S&P 500 futures contract. The index stands at 5,000 points, and the futures contract settles in three months. Each contract is valued at $50 per index point, so one contract is worth $250,000 (5,000 x $50). Without leverage, the trader would need $250,000. In futures trading, the trader is only required to post margin -- a fraction of the total contract value. [7] If the initial margin is 10% of the contract value, the trader need deposit only $25,000 (10% of $250,000) to enter the position. If the index falls 10% to 4,500, the contract's value drops to $225,000 (4,500 x $50). The trader faces a $25,000 loss -- equivalent to a 100% loss on the initial margin.

## Hedging

Futures can be used to hedge against price movements in the underlying asset. [2] The objective is to prevent losses from adverse price changes rather than to speculate. Suppose a mutual fund manager oversees a $100 million portfolio that tracks the S&P 500. Anticipating potential short-term market volatility, the manager uses S&P 500 futures contracts to hedge the portfolio against a possible downturn.

Assume the S&P 500 is at 5,000 and each futures contract is based on the index multiplied by $250 per index point. Since the portfolio closely mirrors the S&P 500, the hedge ratio is assumed to be one-to-one. The hedged value per contract is 5,000 x $250 = $1,250,000. The number of futures contracts required is the portfolio value divided by the hedged value per contract: $100,000,000 / $1,250,000 = approximately 80. Selling 80 futures contracts should effectively hedge the portfolio, with two possible outcomes:

- The S&P 500 falls 10% over three months to 4,500, meaning the portfolio could lose approximately 10%, or $10 million. However, the short futures position gains in value, offsetting the loss. Each contract gains (5,000 - 4,500) x $250 = $125,000. The total gain on 80 contracts is 80 x $125,000 = $10,000,000 -- effectively neutralizing the portfolio's decline.
- The S&P 500 rises over three months. The portfolio gains value, but the loss on the futures position offsets the gain. This outcome is acceptable because the primary objective is to hedge against downside risk.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Futures Trading

Futures trading has both benefits and drawbacks. It typically involves leverage, with brokers requiring an initial margin -- a small fraction of the contract's total value. The required amount depends on the contract size, the investor's creditworthiness, and the broker's terms and conditions.

Futures contracts can serve as valuable tools for hedging against price volatility. Companies can plan budgets and protect potential profits against adverse price movements. However, futures contracts also carry drawbacks. Due to the leverage involved, investors face the risk of losses that exceed their initial margin.

#### Advantages

- Potential for speculative gains
- Effective hedging capability
- Ease of trading

#### Disadvantages

- Elevated risk due to leverage
- Missed gains from favorable price movements when hedging
- Margin is a double-edged sword

**Regulation of Futures:** The futures market is regulated by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission (CFTC). The CFTC is a federal agency created by Congress in 1974 to ensure the integrity of futures market pricing, including the prevention of abusive trading practices and fraud, and to regulate brokerage firms engaged in futures trading. [8]

## Why Trade Futures Instead of Stocks?

A key advantage of trading futures rather than stocks is the high degree of leverage, which allows investors to control a large asset position with a relatively small amount of capital. This also entails greater risk. Additionally, futures markets are open nearly around the clock, offering the flexibility to trade outside traditional market hours and to respond quickly to global events.

## Are Futures or Options More Profitable?

The relative profitability of futures versus options depends heavily on the investor's strategy and risk tolerance. Futures tend to offer higher leverage, which can make them more profitable when predictions prove correct -- but they also carry correspondingly higher risk. Options offer the safety of a non-binding contract, capping potential losses.

## What Happens If an Investor Holds a Futures Contract Until Expiration?

When the underlying asset is a stock, a trader who holds a futures contract to expiration settles the position in cash. Depending on whether the underlying asset rose or fell during the holding period, the trader either receives or pays a cash settlement. In some cases, however, futures contracts require physical delivery. In that event, the investor holding the contract at expiration takes delivery of the underlying asset.

## Summary

As an investment instrument, futures contracts offer opportunities for price speculation and risk hedging against potential market downturns. However, they also have drawbacks. Taking an opposing position as a hedge can generate additional losses if market predictions prove inaccurate. Moreover, the daily mark-to-market settlement of futures prices introduces volatility, causing the value of a position to change materially from one trading session to the next.
