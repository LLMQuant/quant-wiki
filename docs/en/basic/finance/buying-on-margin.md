# What Is Buying on Margin?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Buying on margin refers to the practice of borrowing money from a bank or broker to purchase an asset. It describes the initial payment the investor makes toward the asset -- for example, 10% down with 90% financed. The investor uses the marginable securities in the brokerage account as collateral.

The buying power an investor holds in a brokerage account reflects the total dollar amount of purchases they can make using any available margin capacity. Short sellers of stock also use margin to trade.

### Key Takeaways

- Buying on margin means you are investing with borrowed money.
- Buying on margin amplifies both gains and losses.
- If your account falls below the maintenance margin, your broker can sell some or all of your portfolio to restore balance.

## Understanding Buying on Margin

The Federal Reserve Board sets the rules for margin securities. As of 2023, under Federal Reserve Regulation T, an investor must fund at least 50% of a security's purchase price with cash or other collateral. The investor may borrow the remaining 50% from a broker or dealer.

However, many brokers impose higher margin requirements on their clients. In addition, certain securities cannot be purchased on margin at all.

As with any loan, when an investor buys securities on margin, the borrowed amount must eventually be repaid along with interest, which varies by brokerage firm. Monthly interest on the principal is charged to the investor's brokerage account.

In essence, buying on margin means an individual is investing with borrowed money. Although there are benefits, the practice is risky for investors with limited capital.

## Buying on Margin Example

To illustrate how buying on margin works, we will simplify the process by setting aside monthly interest costs. Although interest affects returns and losses, its impact is not as significant as the margin principal itself.

Consider an investor who purchases 100 shares of XYZ Company stock at $100 per share. The investor funds half of the purchase price with personal capital and finances the other half on margin, making the initial cash outlay $5,000. One year later, the share price rises to $200. The investor sells the shares for $20,000 and repays the $5,000 borrowed from the broker.

Ultimately, in this scenario the investor triples the original money, earning $15,000 on a $5,000 investment. Had the investor purchased the same number of shares using only personal funds, the investment would only have doubled from $5,000 to $10,000.

Now consider that instead of doubling in a year, the share price falls to $50. The investor sells at a loss, receiving $5,000. Since this exactly equals the amount owed to the broker, the investor loses 100% of the investment. Had the investor not used margin, the loss would have been only 50% -- $2,500 rather than $5,000.

## How to Buy on Margin

The broker sets a minimum or initial margin and a maintenance margin that the investor must maintain in the account before margin trading can begin. The required amount is largely based on the investor's creditworthiness. A maintenance margin is the minimum balance the broker requires in the investor's brokerage account.

Suppose an investor deposits $15,000 and the maintenance margin is 50%, or $7,500. If the investor's equity falls below $7,500, a margin call may be issued. At that point, the investor is required to deposit funds with the broker to bring the account back to the required maintenance margin.

The investor can deposit cash or sell securities purchased with borrowed money. If the investor does not comply, the broker may liquidate the investor's holdings to restore the maintenance margin.

## Who Should Buy on Margin?

Generally speaking, buying on margin is not for beginners. It requires a certain degree of risk tolerance, and any trade using margin must be closely monitored. Watching a stock portfolio lose and gain value over time is stressful enough for someone without additional leverage. Moreover, the potential for outsized losses during a stock market crash makes margin trading a challenging proposition even for the most experienced investors.

However, certain types of trading, such as commodity futures, almost always involve the use of margin, while other securities, such as options contracts, have traditionally been purchased with all cash. Options buyers can now purchase equity options and equity index options on margin, provided the option has more than nine months until expiration. The initial (maintenance) margin requirement is 75% of the cost (market value) of a listed, long-term equity or equity index put or call option.

For most individual investors focused primarily on stocks and bonds, buying on margin introduces unnecessary risk.

## Pros and Cons of Buying on Margin

Buying on margin allows investors to make larger trades than their own assets alone would support. For skilled traders, this represents an opportunity to capitalize on market moves even with relatively limited investment capital.

Margin trading also lets traders leverage their existing assets without having to sell them. If a trader were to liquidate existing stock holdings for cash, it could trigger a taxable event, potentially offsetting investment gains. However, a trader who uses existing stock as margin collateral can trade without selling and thus avoid that tax consequence.

While margin traders may earn higher profits, they may also face larger losses. There is even the possibility that a margin trader's losses can exceed the original investment -- meaning additional assets are needed to cover the shortfall.

In addition to the risk, traders must pay additional fees on margin positions. These costs typically run around 10%, depending on the federal funds rate.

For example, as of January 2023, Fidelity Investments charged between 8.25% and 12.575% on margin loans depending on the size of the margin position. If a position takes a long time to generate a profit, these costs can erode any expected gains.

### Pros

- Higher returns
- No need to liquidate existing assets

### Cons

- Higher risk
- Additional margin costs

## How Does Buying on Margin Work?

Margin traders deposit cash or securities as collateral to borrow money for trading. In the stock market, they can typically borrow up to 50% of the total cost of the trade, with the remainder covered by their margin collateral. They then use the borrowed cash to make speculative trades. If the trader loses too much money, the broker will liquidate the trader's collateral to cover the losses.

## Why Can Buying on Margin Be Problematic?

Before the 1929 stock market crash, buying on margin encouraged speculation because traders could achieve rapid gains on relatively small investments. Those gains in turn encouraged even more margin buying, inflating asset prices in a bubble. When the bubble burst, many margin traders owed debts they could not repay.

## Why Is Buying on Margin Risky?

The profit potential of buying on margin is higher than with conventional investing, but so is the exposure to losses. In a bull market, the returns can be highly attractive, but when trades go wrong, investors may end up owing more than the capital they originally had available to trade.

## Conclusion

Buying on margin is the practice of borrowing money, using securities as collateral, to make speculative trades. In a bull market, margin trading can deliver returns far beyond what investing only available assets would produce. However, margin trading can also lead to substantially larger losses.
