# What Is Inflation?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Inflation is the gradual loss of purchasing power, reflected in a broad rise in the prices of goods and services over time.

The inflation rate is calculated as the average increase in prices of a selected basket of goods and services over the course of a year. High inflation means prices are rising rapidly, while low inflation means prices are rising more slowly. Inflation is the opposite of deflation, which occurs when prices fall and purchasing power increases.

### Key Takeaways

- Inflation measures the rate at which prices of goods and services rise.
- Inflation is sometimes classified into three types: demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation, and built-in inflation.
- The most commonly used inflation indexes are the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Wholesale Price Index (WPI).
- Inflation can be viewed positively or negatively depending on the individual perspective and the rate of change.
- Those who hold tangible assets such as real estate or stockpiled commodities may welcome some inflation, as it raises the value of their assets.

## How Inflation Affects Consumers

While it is easy to measure price changes for individual products over time, human needs extend far beyond one or two products. Individuals require a large and diversified set of products as well as a range of services to live comfortably. These include food grains, metals, fuel, utilities such as electricity and transportation, and services such as healthcare, entertainment, and labor.

Inflation aims to measure the overall impact of price changes across a diverse set of products and services. It provides a single-value representation of the increase in the price level of goods and services in an economy over a period of time.

Prices rise, which means that one unit of currency buys fewer goods and services. This loss of purchasing power affects the cost of living for the general public, ultimately leading to a deceleration in economic growth. The consensus among economists is that sustained inflation occurs when a nation's money supply growth outpaces economic growth.

**3.4%:** Over the 12 months ending April 2024, the Consumer Price Index for All Urban Consumers (CPI-U) rose 3.4% on an unadjusted basis. Prices in April 2024 increased 0.3% from the prior month on a seasonally adjusted basis. [1]

To combat this, the monetary authority (in most cases, the central bank) takes the necessary steps to manage the money supply and credit to keep inflation within permissible limits and keep the economy running smoothly.

Theoretically, monetarism is a popular theory that explains the relationship between inflation and an economy's money supply. For example, following the Spanish conquest of the Aztec and Inca empires, massive amounts of gold and especially silver flowed into the Spanish and other European economies. Since the money supply had rapidly increased, the value of money fell, contributing to rapidly rising prices. [2]

Inflation is measured in various ways depending on the types of goods and services. It is the opposite of deflation, which indicates a general decline in prices when the inflation rate falls below 0%. Keep in mind that deflation should not be confused with disinflation, a related term referring to a slowdown in the (positive) rate of inflation.

## What Causes Inflation

An increase in the money supply is the root of inflation, though this can play out through different mechanisms in the economy. A country's money supply can be increased by the monetary authority through:

- Printing more money and distributing it to citizens
- Legally devaluing (reducing the value of) the legal tender currency
- Loaning new money into existence as reserve account credits through the banking system by purchasing government bonds from banks on the secondary market (the most common method)
- Critical supply bottlenecks and shortages of key commodities that drive up other prices

In all of these cases, the money ultimately loses its purchasing power. The mechanisms that drive inflation can be classified into three types: demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation, and built-in inflation.

Demand-pull inflation occurs when an increase in the supply of money and credit stimulates overall demand for goods and services to rise faster than the economy's production capacity. This increases demand and leads to price rises.

When people have more money, it leads to positive consumer sentiment. This in turn leads to higher spending, which pulls prices higher. It creates a demand-supply gap with higher demand and less flexible supply, which results in higher prices.

Cost-push inflation is a result of rising prices working through the production process inputs. When additions to the money and credit supply are channeled into commodity or other asset markets, costs for all kinds of intermediate goods rise. This is especially evident when there is a negative economic shock to the supply of key commodities.

These developments lead to higher costs for the finished product or service and work their way into rising consumer prices. For example, when the money supply is expanded, it creates a speculative boom in oil prices. This means energy costs can rise and contribute to rising consumer prices, which is reflected in various measures of inflation.

Built-in inflation is related to adaptive expectations -- the idea that people expect current inflation rates to continue in the future. As the price of goods and services rises, people may anticipate a continued increase at a similar rate in the future.

As a result, workers may demand higher costs or wages to maintain their standard of living. Their increased wages lead to higher costs of goods and services, and this wage-price spiral continues as one factor induces the other and vice versa.

## How to Protect Your Finances During Inflation

Individuals can take a number of steps to protect their finances from inflation.

For example, one may choose to invest in asset classes that outperform the market during inflationary periods. These may include commodities such as grains, beef, oil, electricity, and natural gas. Commodity prices tend to lead product prices by a step, and rising commodity prices are often seen as an indicator of future inflation. However, commodities can also be highly volatile and vulnerable to natural disasters, geopolitics, or conflicts.

Real estate income may also help cushion against inflation, as landlords can raise rents to keep pace with overall price increases.

The U.S. government also offers Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), a type of inflation-indexed security designed to protect against a decline in purchasing power.

## Types of Price Indexes

Depending on the selected set of goods and services, multiple types of baskets of goods are calculated and tracked as price indexes. The most commonly used price indexes are the Consumer Price Index (CPI) and the Wholesale Price Index (WPI).

The CPI is a measure that examines the weighted average of prices of a basket of goods and services primarily relevant to consumer needs. These include transportation, food, and healthcare.

The CPI is calculated by taking price changes for each item in the predetermined basket of goods and averaging them based on their relative weight in the overall basket. The prices considered are the retail prices of each item, available for purchase by individual citizens. The CPI affects the value of a currency relative to the currencies of other nations.

Changes in the CPI are used to assess price changes associated with the cost of living, making it one of the most frequently used statistics for identifying periods of inflation or deflation. In the United States, the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) reports the CPI monthly and has calculated it as far back as 1913. [3]

**Important:** The CPI-U was introduced in 1978 and represents the buying habits of approximately 88% of the non-institutional U.S. population. [4]

The WPI is another popular measure of inflation. It measures and tracks changes in the price of goods at stages before the retail level.

While WPI items vary from one country to another, they mostly include items at the producer or wholesale level. For example, it includes cotton prices for raw cotton, cotton yarn, cotton gray goods, and cotton clothing. [6]

Although many countries and organizations use the WPI, many other countries, including the United States, use a similar variant called the Producer Price Index (PPI). [7]

The PPI is a family of indexes that measures the average change in selling prices received by domestic producers of intermediate goods and services. PPI measures price changes from the perspective of the seller, differing from the CPI, which measures price changes from the buyer's perspective. [8]

In all of these variants, it is possible that a rise in the price of one component (e.g., oil) cancels out a price decline in another (e.g., wheat) to a certain degree. Overall, each index represents the average weighted price change for the given constituents, which may apply at the overall economy, sector, or commodity level.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Inflation

Inflation can be interpreted as either a good thing or a bad thing, depending on which side one takes and how rapidly the change occurs.

Individuals with tangible assets priced in domestic currency, such as property or stocked commodities, may like to see some inflation as that raises the price of their assets, which they can sell at a higher rate.

Inflation often leads businesses to speculate on risky projects and individuals to invest in company stocks because they expect returns better than inflation.

An optimal level of inflation is often promoted to encourage spending to a certain degree instead of saving. If the purchasing power of money falls over time, then there may be a greater incentive to spend now rather than save and spend later. It may increase spending, which may boost economic activities in a country. A balanced approach is thought to keep the inflation value in an optimum and desirable range.

Buyers of such assets may be unhappy with inflation, as they will be required to pay more. People who hold assets denominated in domestic currency, such as cash or bonds, may not like inflation, as it erodes the real value of their holdings.

As such, investors looking to protect their portfolios from inflation should consider inflation-hedged asset classes such as gold, commodities, and real estate investment trusts (REITs). Inflation-indexed bonds are another popular option for investors to profit from inflation.

High and variable rates of inflation can impose major costs on an economy. Businesses, workers, and consumers must all account for the effects of generally rising prices in their buying, selling, and planning decisions.

This introduces an additional source of uncertainty into the economy because they may guess wrong about the rate of future inflation. Time and resources spent researching, estimating, and adjusting economic behavior are expected to rise to the general level of prices. This is in contrast to real economic fundamentals, which inevitably represent a cost to the economy as a whole.

Even low, stable, and easily predictable rates of inflation, which some consider optimal, may lead to serious problems in the economy. This is because of the way in which, where, and when new money enters the economy.

Whenever new money and credit enter the economy, it is always in the hands of specific individuals or business firms. The process of price level adjustment to the new money supply then takes place as they spend the new money, and it circulates from hand to hand and account to account through the economy.

Inflation does push up some prices first and other prices later. This sequential change in purchasing power and prices (known as the Cantillon effect) means that inflation does not merely raise the general price level over time. But it also distorts relative prices, wages, and rates of return along the way. [11]

Economists generally understand that distortions of relative prices away from their economic equilibrium are not good for the economy, and Austrian economists even believe this process is a major driver of cycles of recession in the economy. [12]

#### Advantages

- Leads to higher resale value for assets
- An optimal level of inflation encourages spending

#### Disadvantages

- Buyers must pay more for products and services
- Imposes higher prices on the economy
- Pushes up some prices first, then others

## How to Control Inflation

A country's financial regulator bears the important responsibility of keeping inflation in check. This is done by implementing measures through monetary policy, which refers to the actions of a central bank or other committees that determine the size and rate of growth of the money supply.

In the United States, the Fed's monetary policy goals include moderate long-term interest rates, price stability, and maximum employment. Each of these goals is intended to promote a stable financial environment. The Federal Reserve clearly communicates long-term inflation goals in order to maintain a steady long-term rate of inflation, which is considered beneficial for the economy.

Price stability -- or a relatively constant level of inflation -- allows businesses to plan for the future, since they know what to expect. The Fed believes that this will promote maximum employment, which is determined by non-monetary factors that fluctuate over time and are therefore subject to change.

For this reason, the Fed does not set a specific goal for maximum employment, and it is largely determined by employer assessments. Maximum employment does not mean zero unemployment, as at any given time there is a certain level of volatility as people vacate and start new jobs. [13]

**Note:** Hyperinflation is often described as a period of inflation of 50% or more per month. [15]

Monetary authorities also take extraordinary measures in extreme economic conditions. For example, following the 2008 financial crisis, the U.S. Fed held interest rates near zero and pursued a bond-buying program called quantitative easing (QE). [16]

Some critics of the program alleged it would cause a spike in inflation in the U.S. dollar, but inflation peaked in 2007 and declined steadily over the following eight years. There are many complex reasons why QE didn't lead to inflation or hyperinflation, but the simplest explanation is that the recession itself was a very prominent deflationary environment, and quantitative easing supported its effects. [17]

Consequently, U.S. policymakers have attempted to keep inflation steady at around 2% per year. The European Central Bank (ECB) has also pursued aggressive quantitative easing to counter deflation in the eurozone, with some areas even experiencing negative interest rates. This is due to fears that deflation could take hold in the eurozone and lead to economic stagnation. [19]

Moreover, countries with higher growth rates can absorb higher inflation rates. India targets about 4% (with an upper tolerance of 6% and a lower tolerance of 2%), while Brazil targets 3.25% (with an upper tolerance of 4.75% and a lower tolerance of 1.75%). [21]

## Understanding Inflation, Deflation, and Disinflation

While a high inflation rate means prices are rising, a low inflation rate does not mean prices are falling. Counterintuitively, when the inflation rate falls, prices are still rising -- just at a slower rate than before. When the inflation rate declines (but remains positive), this is called disinflation.

Conversely, if the inflation rate turns negative, it means prices are falling. This is called deflation, and it can have negative effects on the economy. Because purchasing power increases over time, consumers have less incentive to spend in the short term, leading to decreased economic activity.

## Hedging Against Inflation

Stocks are considered one of the best hedges against inflation because the rise in stock prices is inclusive of the effects of inflation. Since increases in the money supply in virtually all modern economies occur through bank credit injection into the financial system, much of the immediate effect on prices occurs in financial assets denominated in domestic currency, such as stocks.

Special financial instruments exist that can be used to safeguard investments against inflation. They include Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities (TIPS), which are low-risk Treasury securities indexed to inflation, where the principal amount invested is increased by the percentage of inflation. [23]

One can also opt for a TIPS mutual fund or TIPS-based exchange-traded fund (ETF). To access stocks, ETFs, and other funds that can help avoid the dangers of inflation, you will likely need a brokerage account. Choosing a stockbroker can be a tedious process due to the variety among them.

Gold is also considered a hedge against inflation, though looking back, this has not always been the case.

## Examples of Inflation

Since all world currencies are fiat money, the money supply could increase rapidly for political reasons, resulting in rapid price level increases. The most famous example is the hyperinflation that struck Germany's Weimar Republic in the early 1920s.

The victorious nations of World War I demanded reparations from Germany, which could not be paid in German paper currency because its value was suspect due to government borrowing. Germany attempted to print paper notes, buy foreign currency with them, and use that to pay its debts.

This policy resulted in the rapid devaluation of the German mark, accompanied by hyperinflation. German consumers responded to the cycle by trying to spend their money as quickly as possible, understanding that the longer they waited, the less it would be worth. More and more money flooded the economy, and its value plummeted to the point where people would paper their walls with virtually worthless banknotes. Similar situations occurred in Peru in 1990 and in Zimbabwe between 2007 and 2008. [24]

## What Causes Inflation?

There are three primary causes of inflation: demand-pull inflation, cost-push inflation, and built-in inflation.

- Demand-pull inflation refers to situations in which there are not enough products or services being produced to keep up with demand, causing their prices to increase.
- Cost-push inflation, on the other hand, occurs when the cost of producing products and services rises, forcing businesses to raise their prices.
- Built-in inflation (sometimes called the wage-price spiral) occurs when workers demand higher wages to keep up with rising living costs. This in turn causes businesses to raise their prices to offset their rising wage costs, leading to a self-reinforcing loop of wage and price increases.

## Is Inflation Good or Bad?

Too much inflation is generally considered bad for an economy, while too little inflation is also considered harmful. Many economists advocate for a middle ground of low to moderate inflation, around 2% per year.

Generally speaking, higher inflation hurts savers because it erodes the purchasing power of the money they have saved; however, it can benefit borrowers because the inflation-adjusted value of their outstanding debts shrinks over time.

## What Are the Effects of Inflation?

Inflation can affect the economy in several ways. For example, if inflation causes a nation's currency to decline, this can benefit exporters by making their goods more affordable when priced in foreign currency.

On the other hand, this could hurt importers by making foreign-made goods more expensive. Higher inflation can also encourage spending, as consumers aim to purchase goods quickly before prices rise further. Savers, on the other hand, could see the real value of their savings erode, limiting their ability to spend or invest in the future.

## Why Is Inflation So High Now?

In 2022, inflation rates around the world rose to their highest levels since the early 1980s. While there is no single reason for the rapid rise in global prices, a series of events worked together to push inflation to such high levels. [27]

The COVID-19 pandemic led to lockdowns and other restrictions that greatly disrupted global supply chains, from factory closings to bottlenecks at maritime shipping ports. Governments also issued stimulus checks and increased unemployment benefits to offset the financial impact on individuals and small businesses. When vaccines became widely available and the economy bounced back, demand (fueled in part by stimulus money and low interest rates) quickly outpaced supply, which was still struggling to get back to pre-COVID levels.

Russia's unprovoked invasion of Ukraine in early 2022 led to economic sanctions and trade restrictions on Russia, limiting the world's supply of oil and gas, as Russia is a major producer of fossil fuels. Food prices also rose as Ukraine's large grain harvests could not be exported. As fuel and food prices rose, it led to similar increases down the value chain. The Fed raised interest rates to combat high inflation, which fell substantially in 2023 but remained above pre-pandemic levels. [29]

## The Bottom Line

Inflation is a rise in prices, which results in a decline in purchasing power over time. Inflation is a natural phenomenon, and the U.S. government targets an annual inflation rate of 2%; however, inflation can be dangerous when it increases too fast and by too much.

Inflation makes goods more expensive, especially when wages do not rise at the same rate. Additionally, inflation erodes the value of certain assets, especially cash. Governments and central banks attempt to control inflation through monetary policy.
