# What Is a Quotation?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

A quotation refers to the most recent trading price of a stock, bond, or any other traded asset. In addition, most asset classes quote both a bid price and an ask price, which together determine the final transaction price. The bid price is defined as the highest price a buyer is willing to pay for an asset, while the ask price is the lowest price a seller is willing to accept.

In normal trading conditions, stable and liquid assets typically exhibit narrow bid-ask spreads. However, these spreads tend to widen during periods of systemic risk -- such as geopolitical events or broad market selloffs. The emergence of volatility and uncertainty disrupts the supply-demand mechanism, directly affecting quotations.

### Key Takeaways

- A quotation represents the most recent transaction price of any asset trading in the market.
- Quotations also encompass the high, low, open, and close values for a given day.
- Most asset classes quote an ask price and a bid price that determine the final transaction price.
- The bid price is the highest price a buyer is willing to pay, while the ask price is the lowest price a seller is willing to accept.
- Market volatility can cause shifts in the supply-demand dynamics that affect quotations.

## How Quotations Work

For most asset classes, a quotation conveys two pieces of information: the price an investor would need to pay to purchase the asset at a given moment (the lowest price quoted by sellers) and the price the investor would receive if selling the same asset at the same moment (the highest price offered by potential buyers). The difference between these two prices represents the liquidity cost borne by investors when transacting, since they must buy at the ask and sell at the bid.

**Important:** When an asset's price begins to fall, the bid and ask prices diverge in parallel. This widening spread can reduce the asset's liquidity, making it harder to execute trades during periods of broad market volatility.

Quotations extend beyond bid and ask prices. They also include the high, low, open, and close values for a given day. A basic stock quote highlights these key data points to provide context for the current trading session's movement. The spread between the open and close, or between the high and low, often reflects the prevailing trend. For instance, a sharp change between open and close signals strong upward momentum and a potentially attractive trading opportunity.

## Types of Quotations

Most investors associate the term "quotation" with stock prices, but many other asset classes also record last-traded price quotations.

For example, fixed-income markets quote a bond's bid and ask prices during regular trading hours. In addition to the bid-ask spread, bond quotations display the asset's par value and yield to maturity.

Bonds have a par value of $1,000 and are quoted as a percentage of par, which is then converted to a point scale. For example, if a corporate bond is quoted at 97, it is trading at 97% of face value, meaning the actual purchase cost is $970.

Par value -- also known as nominal value or face value -- is typically converted to a numerical figure and multiplied by 10 to determine a bond's cost. Par value is an investment term referring to the original value, usually the value of a bond when first issued, commonly $100 or $1,000. For instance, suppose a bond is purchased at $100 and its value rises over time to $125. Although the bond is now worth $125, its par value remains $100. If the bond depreciates to $75, the par value is still $100.

Par value is important because it determines the bond's maturity value as well as the dollar amount of interest payments. This rate is commonly referred to as the bond's coupon rate.

**Important:** Stock quotations may be the first and foremost consideration when placing an order, but traders typically use additional information -- often technical indicators -- before executing a trade.

Futures contracts and commodities also use quotations to provide investors and the financial community with relevant information about the asset. Quotations work the same way as for other assets, with the distinction that the buyer of a futures contract agrees to purchase the asset at a specified price at a specific future date.

Many investors use futures contracts to hedge trades or speculate on market movements. A futures contract and "futures" are the same thing; investors commonly use just the term "futures" when referring to futures contracts.

As an example of a futures quotation, a trader might purchase an oil futures contract at $80 per barrel with delivery in one year. This means that one year from the purchase date, the buyer is obligated to purchase the oil at $80 per barrel, and the seller is obligated to deliver it. One advantage of trading futures is that the trader does not need to deposit the full transaction amount with the brokerage -- only an initial margin is required.

## Quotation Example

Apple Inc. (AAPL) is a heavily traded public company. Because of AAPL stock's extremely high liquidity, trading is straightforward and bid-ask spreads are very narrow. As an example, suppose AAPL closed at $165 per share. The day's trading range might have been $161 to $167, but at the close it settled at $165.

During the trading day, some traders wanted to buy AAPL shares while others wanted to sell. If at 10:30 a.m. AAPL was trading at $163, the buyer would see the bid-ask spread -- in this example, a bid of $162.99 and an ask of $163.01. This is an extremely tight spread of just two cents. The buyer would pay the ask price, the seller would deliver the shares, and the transaction would be complete.

Although bid and ask prices are fundamental elements of financial market trading, when people refer to a "quote," they are almost always referring to the stock's last-traded price. This is typically the first and largest number you see when researching a stock.

## Frequently Asked Questions

## How Do You Read a Stock Quote?

A stock quote can be read in several ways. If you simply want to know the price of a stock, look at the "quote" -- this is the price people reference when they say a stock is trading at a certain level. If you are trading the stock, you can check the bid price to see what sellers are asking or the ask price to see what buyers are willing to pay. There is always a difference between these two numbers, and it is in this spread that market makers earn their profit.

## What Is a Real-Time Stock Quote?

A real-time stock quote is the same as any other quote but is updated in real time by sophisticated trading platforms. It is most commonly used by day traders engaged in high-frequency trading (HFT). However, some critics argue that this style of trading advantages firms and individuals with the most powerful technology.

## What Is a Nominal Quotation?

A nominal quotation is a hypothetical price at which a stock or other security might trade. Traders use these as "what-if" scenarios to determine whether to place a trade in the future. They are typically prefixed with "For Your Information" (FYI) or "For Valuation Only" (FVO). They contrast with firm quotations, which represent actual, current prices.

## What Is an Interdealer Quotation System?

An interdealer quotation system (IQS) is a system designed to organize price quotations from brokers and dealer firms. Its purpose is to provide investors with accurate and relevant pricing information. There are several interdealer quotation systems, each with its own specialization. For example, in the United States, Nasdaq, the Nasdaq SmallCap Market, and its Over-the-Counter Bulletin Board (OTCBB) platform are all integrated into a single IQS.

## Summary

Finding a security's quotation is straightforward -- it is typically the first number you see. If you only want to know a stock's price, that is sufficient. If you intend to trade, however, there are many other quotation-related considerations, including the bid-ask spread and the time of the last execution. Quotations are updated regularly by powerful technology, though even so, the speed of updates can vary across trading platforms.
