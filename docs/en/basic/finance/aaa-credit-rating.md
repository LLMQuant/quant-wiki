# What Is a AAA Credit Rating?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
AAA is the highest possible rating assigned to a bond issuer by the major credit rating agencies. AAA-rated bonds carry the highest degree of creditworthiness because their issuers can readily meet financial commitments and bear the lowest risk of default.

The credit rating agencies Standard & Poor's (S&P) and Fitch Ratings use the letters AAA to designate bonds with the highest credit quality, while Moody's uses the slightly different notation Aaa to indicate a bond's top-tier credit rating.

### Key Takeaways

- The highest possible rating a bond can receive is AAA, awarded only to bonds that exhibit the greatest degree of creditworthiness.
- The AAA rating is used by Fitch and Standard & Poor's, while Moody's uses the similar Aaa designation.
- Bonds that receive a AAA rating are considered the least likely to default.
- Issuers of AAA-rated bonds generally have no difficulty attracting investors, although the yields offered on these bonds are lower than those of other tiers due to their high credit rating.

## Understanding AAA Credit Ratings

A credit rating is an assessment or opinion of the creditworthiness of a corporation or government entity. As such, credit ratings serve as an indicator of the likelihood that the issuing entity will meet its financial obligations, including paying bills. Investors can use these ratings to evaluate the safety of investing in a country's or issuer's bonds. Credit ratings are assigned by credit rating agencies, most notably S&P, Moody's, and Fitch.

AAA and Aaa ratings are considered investment grade. Because they carry the lowest perceived risk of default, these instruments typically offer investors the lowest yields among bonds of comparable maturity -- a direct reflection of the risk-return tradeoff. Default refers to a bond issuer's failure to meet its obligations, whether that means missing a semiannual interest payment or failing to repay the principal at maturity.

AAA ratings are granted to government debt and to corporate bonds of companies. The 2008 global credit crisis caused a number of companies to lose their AAA ratings, most notably General Electric (GE). As of August 2023, only two companies -- Microsoft (MSFT) and Johnson & Johnson (JNJ) -- still held AAA ratings. Apple (AAPL) carries a split rating, with Moody's assigning it Aaa while Standard & Poor's rates it AA+ (one notch below AAA).

Even the United States experienced a downgrade from Standard & Poor's to AA+ in 2011 after political disputes over raising the debt ceiling. Moody's and Fitch maintained their Aaa and AAA ratings, respectively. Then in August 2023, Fitch downgraded the U.S. long-term rating from AAA to AA+, citing projected fiscal deterioration and rising government debt over the following three years.

**Important:** Investors should not limit their fixed-income allocations solely to AAA-rated bonds. Instead, they should consider balancing these holdings with higher-yielding bonds, such as high-yield corporate issues.

## Types of AAA-Rated Bonds

Municipal bonds can be issued as either revenue bonds or general obligation bonds, each backed by different sources of income.

Revenue bonds are backed by fees and other specific income-generating sources, such as city pools and sporting venues. General obligation bonds, on the other hand, are backed by the issuer's ability to raise capital through taxation. State bonds rely on state income taxes, while local school district bonds are backed by property taxes.

Issuers can sell both secured and unsecured bonds, each carrying a different risk profile.

A secured bond means that specific assets are pledged as collateral. If the issuer defaults, creditors have a claim on those assets. Secured bonds may be collateralized by tangible items such as equipment, machinery, or real estate. Secured offerings may carry a higher credit rating than unsecured bonds issued by the same entity.

Unsecured bonds, by contrast, are backed only by the issuer's promise to repay. The credit rating of such instruments depends heavily on the issuer's revenue sources and business outlook.

## Benefits of a AAA Rating

A high credit rating lowers the cost of borrowing for the issuer. Companies with top-tier ratings are better positioned than lower-rated fixed-income instruments to borrow large sums at favorable terms. This low cost of capital gives firms easier access to credit, enabling them to grow their businesses and gain significant competitive advantages.

For example, a company may use proceeds from a new bond issuance to launch a new product line, open locations in new markets, or acquire a competitor. All of these initiatives can help a company increase its market share and thrive over the long term.

## Why Are Credit Ratings Important?

The credit rating an issuer receives has a material impact on borrowing costs in the open market. The higher the rating -- with AAA being the best -- the lower the cost of borrowing, and vice versa.

As an investor, the goal is to find the right balance between the level of risk you are willing to assume and the return you expect to earn.

## Who Determines a Debt Issuer's Credit Rating?

Three major credit rating agencies -- Standard & Poor's (S&P), Moody's, and Fitch -- evaluate the creditworthiness of debt issuers. They assess a range of factors including the issuer's ability to pay bond interest and principal, cash flow generation, outstanding debt levels, and business outlook.

## What Does a AAA Credit Rating Mean?

A AAA credit rating is reserved for the most creditworthy debt issuers, enabling investors to gauge the level of risk in their fixed-income portfolios. Conservative investors typically accept lower returns or yields in exchange for holding bonds with the highest credit ratings.

## Summary

Credit ratings are assigned to debt issues and bonds by three major agencies: S&P, Moody's, and Fitch. These ratings have a significant influence on an issuer's borrowing costs -- the higher the rating, the lower the cost.

AAA/Aaa is the highest rating a credit agency can assign and generally corresponds to the lowest borrowing costs or yields. Investors seeking higher returns should consider bond issuers with lower credit ratings and correspondingly higher yields.
