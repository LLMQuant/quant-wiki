# The Stock Market: How It Works, Key Participants, and Why It Matters
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The stock market is not a physical place you can visit but rather a network of transactions -- some conducted at physical locations but mostly online -- involving shares that represent partial ownership in companies. It serves both as a venue for companies to raise capital and as a barometer of economic health.

### Key Takeaways

- The stock market refers to the collective network of transactions involving stocks and their derivatives.
- It is the original form of crowdfunding and a core component of the modern economy, as companies can raise substantial capital to start, expand, or service debt.
- Companies listed on stock exchanges must be public, meaning their shares are available for trading on exchanges and other venues. Listed companies are subject to extensive reporting and transparency regulations.
- Stocks are sold to institutional investors and high-net-worth individuals, as well as ordinary people seeking dividend income, capital appreciation, or simply a voice in how a company is run.
- The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) and state regulators oversee the U.S. stock market. [1] [2]

Stock prices fluctuate based on supply and demand, company performance, economic conditions, and other factors -- including investor "sentiment," which may seem irrational but must be accounted for when buying or selling shares.

People buy stocks for many reasons. Some hold stocks for dividend income. Others believe a stock will rise and try to buy low and sell high. Still others may be interested in having a say in how a particular company operates, since shareholders can vote at annual meetings in proportion to their holdings.

The terms "stock market" and "stock exchange" are often used interchangeably, but they are not the same thing. Stock market participants trade on one or more stock exchanges, which are only part of the broader stock market. The major U.S. stock exchanges include the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the Nasdaq.

## How the Stock Market Works

The stock market is a vast, complex network of trading activity in which shares of companies are bought and sold, protected by laws designed to prevent fraud and other unfair trading practices. It plays a vital role in the modern economy by facilitating the flow of capital between investors and companies.

Sometimes the best way to understand how something works is to examine its components. Here is a review of the stock market's major elements -- from the companies that sell shares, to the stocks themselves, to the exchanges, to the indices that provide a snapshot of market health:

Not all companies can offer shares to the public. Only public companies -- those that have issued stock through an initial public offering (IPO) -- have shares that can be bought and sold on exchanges such as the NYSE or Nasdaq. From the time a company begins planning its IPO until its shares are sold to the public, it must comply with stringent regulations and financial disclosure laws.

The primary market may involve raising capital from friends, family, and others through direct transactions, making it the oldest method of dividing company ownership. Because the primary market is where companies sell their securities directly, it today encompasses IPOs, follow-on offerings, private placements, debt issuances, and other instances where a company sells part of itself to raise funds.

From there, stocks trade on the secondary market through exchanges or "over the counter." Today, more than 58,000 companies are publicly traded worldwide. [3]

When you purchase stock or shares, you acquire a piece of that company. How much of the company you own depends on the number of shares the company has issued and the number you hold. For a small private company, a single share may represent a significant portion of the business. Large public companies typically have millions or even billions of shares outstanding -- Apple Inc. (AAPL), for example, has billions of shares, so a single share represents only a tiny fraction of the company.

Owning stock entitles you to a share of the company's profits -- usually paid as dividends -- and sometimes the right to vote on corporate matters.

Once a company goes public, its shares can be freely traded on the stock market, meaning investors can buy and sell among themselves. This is the secondary market for stocks, and the vast majority of trading occurs through stock exchanges. This part of the stock market dates back at least to 1602 in Amsterdam and has since evolved into some of the world's most sophisticated institutions.

Stock exchanges are organized, regulated venues -- many now virtual -- where stocks and other securities are bought and sold. They play a crucial role in the financial system by providing a platform for companies to raise capital through the sale of shares and bonds to the public.

The NYSE and Nasdaq are leading examples, serving as central locations for buying and selling stocks. Major exchanges around the world include the London Stock Exchange, the Tokyo Stock Exchange, and the Shanghai Stock Exchange. Each has its own internal rules, and investors are subject to varying national and local laws designed to ensure fair trading practices and instill confidence. Exchanges also provide transparency by making real-time pricing information available, which is why current stock prices are easy to find on virtually any financial news website.

However, exchanges would be of little use if they did not provide liquidity -- the ability to buy or sell stocks with relative ease. This means that during trading hours, you can quickly purchase shares or sell them just as rapidly to raise cash.

Many exchanges also cross-list company shares, offering securities primarily listed on other exchanges. This gives companies access to more investors when raising capital and gives traders on specific exchanges a wider range of options.

**Note:** Although it is called the stock market, other securities -- such as exchange-traded funds (ETFs) -- also trade there.

Stocks and other securities are also traded "over the counter" (OTC). These OTC markets are venues where you deal directly with another investor, typically without the same level of regulation or public scrutiny as a stock exchange. OTC trading involves a network of brokers and dealers who negotiate directly by computer and telephone.

This type of trading is often used for smaller, less liquid companies that may not meet the stringent listing requirements of a stock exchange. This can make it harder for investors to obtain reliable information about the companies in which they invest.

In addition to common stock, many other assets trade through stock exchanges and OTC. These all fall under the umbrella of the "stock market":

- **American Depositary Receipts (ADRs):** Represent shares of foreign companies and trade on U.S. stock exchanges, allowing American investors to invest in foreign companies without dealing with foreign exchanges or currency conversion.
- **Derivatives:** A broad category including options and futures whose value is derived from an underlying asset such as a stock, bond, commodity, currency, interest rate, or market index. Derivative trades do not involve directly buying or selling the actual asset.
- **Funds:** Include mutual funds (which pool money from many investors to purchase a basket of stocks, bonds, and other securities) and ETFs (which trade on exchanges like individual stocks and track or seek to replicate the performance of a sector, index, or investment theme).
- **Preferred Stock:** Typically offers fixed dividends and, as the name implies, takes priority over common stock in receiving profit distributions or residual assets in the event of bankruptcy.
- **Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs):** Companies that own, manage, or finance real estate. Investors can buy shares in REITs, which are legally required to distribute at least 90% of their annual profits as dividends. [4]

More loosely, though they constitute separate markets, the following are often considered part of the "stock market":

- **Bonds:** Represent debt issued by governments and corporations to raise capital. Investors who purchase bonds are essentially lending money to the issuer in exchange for interest payments and the return of face value at maturity.
- **Commodities:** Across 50 major global commodity markets, raw materials such as oil, steel, wheat, and coal can be purchased directly or through futures contracts based on anticipated price movements.

Stock market participants include institutional investors -- such as pension funds, mutual funds, insurance companies, and hedge funds -- that manage large pools of capital and often have significant market impact due to the size of their trades. Retail investors buy and sell securities for their personal accounts rather than for organizations. They range from beginners to experienced traders, and most now use online platforms. Another key group consists of accredited investors -- high-net-worth individuals with sufficient capital and investment experience that the SEC permits them to access more complex investments, such as venture capital and private equity.

Generally, investors take a long-term perspective. They deploy capital into stocks, ETFs, mutual funds, and other securities, expecting their value to grow over time -- not the rapid-fire trading depicted in movies. These investors tend to focus on the fundamental strength of the companies or assets they invest in, such as financial performance, market position, and growth potential. They make investment decisions after research and analysis or on the advice of a financial advisor, aiming to build wealth steadily through a portfolio that appreciates over time.

Traders, by contrast, take a shorter-term approach. They aim to capitalize on market volatility by trading stocks, options, futures, and other financial instruments over timeframes ranging from seconds and minutes to days and months. Traders typically rely on technical analysis -- studying market trends, charts, and other statistical indicators to predict future price movements. While trading offers the potential for quick profits, it also carries significantly higher risk than long-term investing. Rapid buying and selling requires a keen understanding of the market and a more active, hands-on strategy.

Brokers in the stock market serve the same role as in insurance and other fields -- acting as intermediaries between investors and the securities market. They are licensed organizations that buy and sell stocks and other securities on behalf of individual and institutional clients. Brokerage firms may be small boutiques or multinational enterprises, offering investment advice, research, and wealth management services while executing trades for clients. Full-service brokers provide detailed financial advice, portfolio management, and personalized service, making them suitable for investors who prefer a comprehensive approach. Discount brokers offer a more hands-off experience, typically favored by investors who make their own trading decisions, at a lower cost.

Online brokerage platforms have become increasingly popular, offering user-friendly interfaces that allow investors to trade securities electronically at lower cost and greater convenience. These platforms often include educational resources, analytical tools, and real-time market data. Robo-advisors -- automated financial planning services offered at very low cost -- have also gained traction.

Regardless of type, brokers are regulated by the SEC and the Financial Industry Regulatory Authority (FINRA).

A critical aspect of the stock market -- determining what is traded and how -- is the regulatory framework. In the United States, the primary regulator is the SEC, an independent federal agency established in 1934 in the aftermath of the 1929 market crash and the Great Depression. The SEC's mission is to "protect investors, maintain fair, orderly, and efficient markets, and facilitate capital formation." [5]

The SEC enforces laws against market manipulation, insider trading, and other forms of fraud while ensuring that public companies disclose material financial information investors need before entrusting a company with their capital. The SEC also oversees stock exchanges, broker-dealers, investment advisors, mutual funds, and public utility holding companies.

In addition, exchanges have their own requirements, such as timely (typically quarterly) filing of company financial reports and immediate disclosure of relevant corporate developments, so that everyone who wants to trade has access to the same information.

FINRA oversees brokerage firms and their registered securities representatives and tends to focus more on protecting retail investors. Similar organizations exist around the world, which is essential because stock markets are global -- a crisis in one corner of the world can quickly spread to another.

While regulations vary considerably across countries to serve different populations and cultural expectations, general rules are enforced to ensure fair practices, protect investors, and bolster confidence in the broader stock market.

Textbook descriptions of stock pricing often begin with investors and traders gathering together, with a buyer and seller needing to agree on a price for a trade to occur. In practice, most investors see prices listed in an online brokerage account or as a chart tracking stock prices over time, rather than through face-to-face negotiation. That said, you do have to choose to buy a stock, and every investor or trader who makes that decision collectively shapes the demand for it, which combines with the existing supply to produce the prices on our screens.

The factors influencing these prices fall broadly into two categories: fundamentals and technicals. Fundamental factors are rooted in a company's earnings, operating profitability, and the goods or services it provides. Technical factors relate to market sentiment and statistical analysis of historical market activity and stock price trends.

A high stock price may indicate a company's success -- or at least market perception of success -- but can also result from stock splits, dividends, and share buybacks. When stock prices fall, it does not mean money has been lost from the market as a whole. Instead, it signals a decline in the market value of a particular stock. For example, if a company reports higher-than-expected profits, its stock price may rise as more investors seek to buy, anticipating future growth. Similarly, economic events such as interest rate changes or geopolitical issues can affect investor confidence and stock prices.

Most Americans first encounter the stock market through indices, since reporting on whether the Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) or the S&P 500 rose or fell has long been a staple of news broadcasts. Indices like the DJIA, which includes 30 large public companies, can paint a picture of the broader stock market. Broader indices, such as the S&P 500 -- representing the 500 largest U.S. public companies -- can provide a snapshot of the overall market. There are currently 11 sectors used to group specific industries, such as technology, healthcare, and consumer discretionary.

Indices matter because they serve as benchmarks for stocks and portfolios. If you invest in technology stocks, for instance, you would want to compare your performance against a technology index to better evaluate your returns.

## What Does the Stock Market Do?

The stock market serves several important functions:

**Corporate governance:** Public companies adhere to strict reporting regulations, which makes them more transparent and accountable. This information enables investors to make informed decisions and helps maintain market confidence. For ordinary citizens, it also provides insight into major companies that might otherwise be opaque.

**Economic indicator:** Stock market performance is widely regarded as a gauge of economic health. Rising stock prices correlate with corporate profitability and economic growth, while falling prices may signal trouble ahead.

**Investment opportunities:** The stock market offers opportunities to invest in companies and grow a portfolio over time. Historically, it has delivered returns that exceed inflation, making it an essential tool for retirement planning, wealth accumulation, and financial security. [6]

**Liquidity:** The stock market enables investors to quickly buy and sell company shares and other securities when needed.

**Capital formation:** Most importantly, the stock market provides a platform for companies to raise capital by issuing shares. This capital is critical for business expansion, research and development, and other corporate initiatives. By selling equity to the public, companies can access these funds without taking on debt.

**Resource allocation:** By reflecting the collective judgment of traders and investors through price movements, the stock market is said to help channel capital efficiently toward companies more likely to succeed -- and away from those that are not.

## Why Is the Stock Market So Important?

When the earliest stock markets formed, the global economy looked very different. Trade and commerce were primarily driven by physical goods, with agriculture, textiles, and early manufacturing dominating the economic landscape. Stock markets at the time were nascent institutions that mainly helped finance exploration and trade ventures -- that is, colonial enterprises aimed at acquiring goods and people from South Asia, the Americas, and Africa. These exchanges were already global investment operations. [7] However, they played a relatively minor role in everyday economic life.

Fast-forward to today, and the stock market is considered central to the global economy -- a prominence underscored by the growing dominance of financialization, financial markets, and financial institutions. This is not simply because more than a million Americans work in the financial industry. [8] Modern economies are characterized by a complex web of financial transactions and instruments, and the stock market serves not only as an economic barometer but also as a key mechanism for allocating and creating wealth.

Financialization also reflects broader socioeconomic shifts. Today's stock market is not merely a capital-raising platform; it is deeply intertwined with the retirement and investment strategies of millions of Americans. That is why during periods of crisis -- 2007-2008 and the pandemic being two prime examples -- the U.S. government and the Federal Reserve have felt more compelled than ever to intervene. This is not just about protecting the wealth of a few; it is because the savings of ordinary Americans are at stake. Not long ago, Americans hearing stock market news might have been gauging indirect effects on their jobs. Today, they are more often monitoring direct effects on their own portfolios, including 401(k) plans.

Of course, many people are paying off student loans, mortgages, and other debts, or are too financially precarious to own a portfolio of stocks or other assets. Nevertheless, they too feel the stock market's impact. Markets fund the technological advances we carry in our pockets -- smartphones -- and the medications we take, which require billions of dollars in R&D spending. This access to capital is critical for companies entering fields like artificial intelligence or novel medical devices, where costs far exceed what a company could borrow.

Stock market movements also affect the broader economy and, by extension, employment. Market performance influences corporate decisions on job creation (and the reverse dynamic, where layoffs can boost stock prices), expansion, and overall economic growth. A healthy stock market generally correlates with a stronger economy -- but it can also mean more capital concentrated in fewer hands, driving up property values in formerly middle-class neighborhoods across virtually every major U.S. city.

The stock market also indirectly affects public services and infrastructure. Pension funds -- a major component of spending for government employees at the local, state, and federal levels -- are heavily invested in the stock market. The returns these investments generate affect the financial health of pension funds and, consequently, the retirement security of millions of people. This is in addition to the many more Americans without pensions who invest directly in the market through 401(k) plans, mutual funds, and individual retirement accounts.

## What Is the Difference Between the Bond Market and the Stock Market?

According to the most recent available data, the global bond market is larger than the stock market, with approximately $130 trillion in outstanding bonds versus approximately $101 trillion in total stock market capitalization. [8] The bond market and the stock market serve different purposes and offer different risk-return profiles. In the bond market, investors buy and sell debt securities typically issued by governments (local, state, and federal) or corporations. When you invest in a bond, you are essentially lending money in exchange for periodic interest payments and the return of the bond's face value at maturity.

The stock market involves buying and selling shares of publicly traded companies and derivatives -- instruments whose value is linked in some way to specific stocks. Investing in stocks means purchasing partial ownership in a company. Stocks offer higher return potential than bonds, as investors can receive dividends when a company is profitable and benefit from rising stock prices. They also carry higher risk, since stock prices can be more volatile.

## What Are Alternative Trading Systems?

Alternative trading systems are platforms used to match large buy and sell orders that are not regulated as exchanges. Dark pools and many cryptocurrency exchanges are private exchanges or forums for securities and currency trading that operate within private groups.

## Who Helps Investors Trade on the Stock Market?

Stockbrokers act as intermediaries between the stock exchanges and investors, buying and selling stocks. Portfolio managers are professionals who invest portfolios -- collections of securities -- on behalf of clients. Investment bankers represent companies in various capacities, such as helping private companies go public through IPOs or planning mergers and acquisitions.

## The Bottom Line

The stock market is where shares of companies and other financial instruments are bought and sold. It is a network encompassing all stock trading, where investors and traders can buy and sell equities. These transactions determine stock prices, reflecting perceived company value and market conditions. The stock market is also where companies raise capital and where investors can grow their wealth. As such, it plays a vital role in the global economy. Even if you do not trade directly on the stock market, it affects the products you buy, the types of jobs available, and the retirement you may be planning.
