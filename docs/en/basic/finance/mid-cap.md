# What Is a Mid-Cap Company?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Mid-cap (or mid-capitalization) is a term used to describe companies with a market capitalization between $2 billion and $10 billion. As the name implies, mid-cap companies fall between large-cap (or big-cap) and small-cap companies. Classifications such as large-cap, mid-cap, and small-cap are approximations of a company's current value and may change over time.

### Key Takeaways

- Mid-cap refers to companies with a market capitalization between $2 billion and $10 billion.
- Mid-cap companies are attractive because they are expected to grow and increase profits, market share, and productivity; they are in the middle of their growth curve.
- Mid-cap stocks serve a unique role in portfolio diversification, offering a balance between growth and stability.

## Understanding Mid-Cap

There are two primary ways a company can raise capital when it needs funding: through debt or through equity. Debt must be repaid but can generally be borrowed at a lower rate than equity (due to tax advantages). Equity may cost more but does not need to be repaid in times of crisis. As a result, companies strive to strike a balance between debt and equity -- a balance known as the company's capital structure. Capital structure, particularly equity capital structure, can provide investors with valuable information about a company's growth prospects.

One way to gain insight into a company's capital structure and market depth is to calculate its market capitalization. Companies with low market capitalization, known as small caps, have a market cap of $2 billion or less. Large-cap companies have a market cap exceeding $10 billion, and mid-cap companies fall between the two (market cap of $2 billion to $10 billion). Additional categories include mega-cap (above $200 billion), micro-cap ($50 million to $500 million), and nano-cap (below $50 million).

Mid-cap companies can be appealing to investors because they are expected to grow and increase profits, market share, and productivity -- they are in the growth phase of their lifecycle. Because they are still considered to be in a growth stage, they are perceived as less risky than small caps but riskier than large caps. Successful mid-cap companies face the prospect of their market capitalization rising -- primarily due to share price appreciation -- to the point where they graduate out of the mid-cap category.

While a company's market cap depends on its market price, a stock priced above $10 is not necessarily a mid-cap stock. To calculate market capitalization, analysts multiply the current market price by the current number of outstanding shares. For example, if Company A has 10 billion shares outstanding at a price of $1 per share, its market cap is $10 billion. If Company B has 1 billion shares outstanding at $5 per share, its market cap is $5 billion. Despite Company A's lower share price, its market cap is higher than Company B's. Company B may have a higher stock price, but it has one-tenth the number of shares outstanding.

## Advantages of Mid-Cap Investing

Most financial advisors suggest that the key to minimizing risk is building a well-diversified portfolio; investors should hold a mix of small-cap, mid-cap, and large-cap stocks. However, some investors also view mid-cap stocks as a way to diversify risk in and of themselves. Small-cap stocks offer the greatest growth potential but also carry the most risk. Large-cap stocks offer the most stability but present relatively lower growth prospects. Mid-cap stocks represent a blend of the two, offering a balance of growth and stability.

No one can accurately predict when the market will favor a particular company size category -- large, mid, or small cap. Diversifying your portfolio is therefore essential. But the proportion of mid-cap stocks you hold should depend on your specific objectives and risk tolerance.

That said, mid-cap companies offer several advantages worth considering. When interest rates are low and capital is cheap, corporate growth tends to be steady. Mid-cap companies can typically obtain the credit they need to fuel expansion and tend to perform well during the expansion phase of the business cycle.

Mid-caps carry less risk than small-cap companies, meaning they tend to deliver relatively strong financial performance during periods of economic turbulence. Furthermore, many mid-cap companies are well established, often specializing in a particular business area with a long enough track record to have carved out a niche in their target market. Finally, because they are riskier than large caps, they may offer higher returns, which can appeal to investors with a moderate risk appetite.

Investors can buy mid-cap company stocks directly or invest in a mid-cap fund -- a mutual fund or ETF that focuses on mid-cap companies -- as a diversified vehicle.
