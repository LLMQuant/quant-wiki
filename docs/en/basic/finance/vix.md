# What Is the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) is a real-time index that represents the market's expectation of the relative strength of near-term price changes in the S&P 500 Index (SPX). Derived from the prices of near-term expiring SPX index options, it generates a 30-day forward projection of volatility. Volatility -- the speed at which prices move -- is commonly viewed as a gauge of market sentiment, particularly the level of fear among market participants.

The index is more commonly known by its ticker symbol and is often referred to simply as "the VIX." It was created by the CBOE Options Exchange and is maintained by CBOE Global Markets. The VIX is one of the most important indices in the trading and investing world because it provides a quantifiable measure of market risk and investor sentiment.

### Key Takeaways

- The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) is a real-time market index representing the market's expectation of 30-day forward-looking volatility.
- Investors use the VIX to measure the level of risk, fear, or stress in the market when making investment decisions.
- Traders can also trade VIX-linked options and exchange-traded products, or use VIX values to price derivatives.
- The VIX generally rises when stocks decline and falls when stocks advance.

## How the CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) Works

The VIX is designed to measure the magnitude of price movements in the S&P 500 -- in other words, its volatility. The greater the price swings in the index, the higher the level of volatility, and vice versa.

Beyond serving as a volatility gauge, traders can trade VIX futures, options, and exchange-traded funds (ETFs) to hedge against or speculate on changes in volatility.

Volatility can generally be measured using two different methods. The first approach is based on historical volatility, using statistical calculations on historical prices over a defined period. This process involves computing various statistics such as the mean, variance, and ultimately the standard deviation.

The second method -- the one employed by the VIX -- derives its value from implied volatility inferred from options prices. Options are derivative instruments whose prices depend on whether a given stock's current price will move sufficiently within a specified timeframe to reach a particular level known as the strike price.

Because the probability of such price movement over a given timeframe is captured by the volatility factor, various options pricing models (such as the Black-Scholes model) incorporate volatility as a key input parameter.

Since options prices are publicly available, investors can use them to derive the implied volatility of the underlying security. This market-implied or inferred volatility is known as forward-looking implied volatility (IV).

## Extending Volatility to the Market Level

The VIX was the first benchmark index introduced by the CBOE to measure the market's expectation of future volatility.

As a forward-looking index, it is constructed from the implied volatilities of S&P 500 index options and represents the market's expectation of 30-day future volatility for the S&P 500, which is widely regarded as a leading indicator of the broad U.S. stock market.

Since its introduction in 1993, the VIX has become a globally recognized benchmark for U.S. equity market volatility. It is calculated in real time based on live S&P 500 index prices from 3:00 a.m. to 9:15 a.m. ET and from 9:30 a.m. to 4:15 p.m. ET. CBOE began disseminating VIX values outside U.S. trading hours in April 2016.

## Calculation of VIX Values

VIX values are calculated using CBOE-traded standard SPX options that expire on the third Friday of each month, as well as weekly SPX options that expire on every other Friday. Only SPX options with more than 23 days and fewer than 37 days to expiration are considered.

Although the formula is mathematically complex, the theoretical approach works as follows: it estimates the expected volatility of the S&P 500 by aggregating the weighted prices of multiple SPX puts and calls across a wide range of strike prices.

All qualifying options must have valid, non-zero bid and ask prices that reflect the market's view of which strike prices are likely to be reached by the underlying before expiration.

For detailed calculations and worked examples, refer to the "VIX Index Calculation: Step-by-Step" section in the VIX White Paper.

## Evolution of the VIX

When it was first introduced in 1993, the VIX was calculated as the weighted measure of implied volatility from eight S&P 100 at-the-money put and call options, at a time when the derivatives market was still in its early growth stage.

As the derivatives market matured, CBOE partnered with Goldman Sachs in 2003 to update the VIX methodology.

The revised calculation began using a broader set of options based on the wider S&P 500 index, an expansion that provided a more accurate view of investors' expectations for future market volatility. This methodology has remained in effect ever since and is also used to calculate several other volatility index variants.

When markets decline, volatility, investor fear, and VIX values all tend to rise. Conversely, when markets advance, index values, fear, and volatility decline.

The price action of the S&P 500 and the VIX frequently exhibit an inverse relationship: sharp declines in the S&P 500 are accompanied by VIX spikes, and vice versa.

**Note:** VIX values above 30 are generally associated with heightened volatility driven by increased uncertainty, risk, and investor fear. VIX values below 20 typically correspond to stable, low-stress periods in the market.

## How to Trade the VIX

The VIX paved the way for using volatility as a tradable asset, albeit through derivative products. CBOE launched the first VIX-based exchange-traded futures contract in March 2004, followed by VIX options in February 2006.

These VIX-linked instruments allow investors to gain pure volatility exposure and have created an entirely new asset class. Active traders, large institutional investors, and hedge fund managers use VIX-based securities for portfolio diversification, as historical data demonstrates a strong negative correlation between volatility and equity market returns -- that is, volatility rises when stock returns fall, and vice versa.

Like all indices, the VIX cannot be purchased directly. Instead, investors can gain exposure through futures or options contracts, or through VIX-based exchange-traded products (ETPs). Examples include the ProShares VIX Short-Term Futures ETF (VIXY) and the iPath Series B S&P 500 VIX Short-Term Futures ETN (VXX), both of which track VIX variant indices and hold positions in related futures contracts.

**Note:** Options and futures based on VIX products trade on the CBOE and CFE platforms.

Active traders who employ proprietary strategies and advanced algorithms use VIX values to price derivatives on high-beta stocks. Beta measures the degree to which a particular stock's price fluctuates relative to the broader market index.

For example, a stock with a beta of +1.5 is theoretically 50% more volatile than the market. Traders placing bets through options on such high-beta stocks use VIX volatility values in the appropriate proportions to correctly price their options trades.

As the VIX has grown in popularity, CBOE now offers several other variants that measure broad market volatility.

Examples include the CBOE Short-Term Volatility Index (VIX9D), which reflects the 9-day expected volatility of the S&P 500; the CBOE S&P 500 3-Month Volatility Index (VIX3M); and the CBOE S&P 500 6-Month Volatility Index (VIX6M). Products based on other market indices include the Nasdaq-100 Volatility Index (VXN), the CBOE DJIA Volatility Index (VXD), and the CBOE Russell 2000 Volatility Index (RVX).

## What Does the VIX Tell Us?

The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) signals the level of fear or stress in the stock market -- using the S&P 500 as a proxy for the broad market -- and is therefore widely known as the "Fear Index." The availability of real-time news coverage can amplify irrational investor behavior. The higher the VIX, the greater the level of fear and uncertainty in the market, with readings above 30 generally indicating significant uncertainty.

## How Can Investors Trade the VIX?

Like all indices, the VIX cannot be bought directly. However, investors can gain exposure through futures contracts, exchange-traded funds (ETFs), and exchange-traded notes (ETNs) that hold these futures contracts.

## Does the VIX Level Affect Options Premiums and Prices?

Yes. Volatility is one of the primary factors affecting stock and index options prices and premiums. Because the VIX is the most widely watched measure of broad market volatility, it has a substantial impact on options prices. A higher VIX translates into higher options prices (more expensive premiums), while a lower VIX means lower options prices (cheaper premiums).

## How Can I Use the VIX to Hedge Downside Risk?

Downside risk can be effectively hedged by purchasing put options that respond to market volatility. Sophisticated investors tend to buy options when the VIX is relatively low and put premiums are cheap. When markets slide, protective puts typically become expensive; therefore, like insurance, they are best purchased when the need for protection is not immediately apparent.

## What Is a Normal VIX Level?

The long-term average of the VIX is approximately 21. Elevated readings, typically above 30, may indicate increased market volatility and fear, often associated with bearish conditions.

## Conclusion

The CBOE Volatility Index (VIX) quantifies the market's expectations for volatility, providing investors and traders with insight into market sentiment. It helps market participants assess potential risks and make informed trading decisions, such as whether to hedge or take directional positions. While the VIX itself is an index and cannot be traded directly, a range of funds and notes enable investors and traders to gain exposure.
