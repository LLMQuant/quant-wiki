# What Is Venture Capital (VC)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
Venture capital (VC) is a form of private equity and a type of financing provided to startups and small businesses with long-term growth potential. Venture capital generally comes from investors, investment banks, and financial institutions. It can also be provided in the form of technical or managerial expertise.

### Key Takeaways

- Venture capital (VC) is a form of private equity and a type of financing for startups and small businesses with long-term growth potential.
- Venture capitalists provide backing through financing, technological expertise, or managerial experience.
- VC firms raise money from limited partners (LPs) to invest in promising startups, or even in larger venture funds.

## Understanding Venture Capital (VC)

VC provides financing to startups and small companies that investors believe have significant growth potential. This financing typically takes the form of private equity (PE), with ownership sold to a small number of investors through standalone limited partnerships (LPs). Venture capital tends to focus on emerging companies, while private equity tends to fund established companies seeking an equity infusion. VC is an important source of funding, particularly when startups lack access to capital markets, bank loans, or other debt instruments. [1]

Harvard Business School professor Georges Doriot is generally regarded as the "father of venture capital." He founded the American Research and Development Corporation in 1946 and raised a $3.58 million fund to invest in companies seeking to commercialize technologies developed during World War II. [2] The corporation's first investment was in a company that planned to use X-ray technology for cancer treatment. The $200,000 that Doriot invested turned into $1.8 million when the company went public in 1955. [3]

VC became synonymous with the growth of technology companies on the West Coast in Silicon Valley. By 1992, 48% of all investment dollars went to West Coast companies; Northeast Coast industries accounted for just 20%. [4] In 2022, West Coast companies accounted for more than 37% of all deals, while the Mid-Atlantic region accounted for only about 24%. [5]

## Types of Venture Capital

- Pre-Seed: This is the earliest stage of business development, when founders are trying to turn an idea into a concrete business plan. They may enroll in a business accelerator to secure early funding and mentorship.
- Seed Funding: This is the point at which a new business seeks to launch its first product. Since there are no revenue streams yet, the company will need VC to fund all of its operations.
- Early-Stage Funding: Once a business has developed a product, it will need additional capital to ramp up production and sales before it can become self-funding. The business will then need one or more funding rounds, typically designated incrementally as Series A, Series B, and so on.

**$285 billion:** The amount raised by VC-backed companies globally in 2023. [6]

## How to Secure VC Funding

- Submit a business plan: Any business seeking venture capital must submit a business plan to a VC firm or an angel investor. The firm or investor will conduct due diligence, which includes a thorough investigation of the company's business model, products, management, and operating history.
- Investment pledge: After completing due diligence, the firm or investor will pledge investment capital in exchange for equity in the company. These funds may be provided in one lump sum but more typically are disbursed in rounds. The firm or investor then takes an active role in the funded company, advising and monitoring its progress before releasing additional funds.
- Exit: The investor exits the company after a period of time, typically four to six years after the initial investment, by initiating a merger, acquisition, or initial public offering (IPO).

**Important:** Many venture capitalists have prior investment experience, often as equity research analysts. VC professionals tend to specialize in a particular industry. A venture capitalist specializing in healthcare, for example, may have previous experience as a healthcare industry analyst.

## Advantages and Disadvantages of Venture Capital

Venture capital provides funding for new businesses that do not have sufficient cash flow to take on debt. This arrangement can be mutually beneficial, as businesses get the capital they need to launch operations and investors gain equity in promising companies. VCs often provide mentoring and networking services to help their portfolio companies find talent and advisors. Strong VC backing can be leveraged into further investment.

However, a business that accepts venture capital support may lose creative control over its future direction. VC investors are likely to demand a large share of company equity and may make demands on company management. Many VCs are only seeking a fast, high-return payoff and may pressure companies toward a quick exit.

#### Advantages

- Provides early-stage companies with the capital to launch operations
- Companies do not need cash flow or assets to secure VC funding
- VC-backed mentoring and networking help new companies secure talent and grow

#### Disadvantages

- Demands a large share of company equity
- Companies may find themselves losing creative control as investors demand immediate returns
- VCs may pressure companies to exit investments rather than pursue long-term growth

## Angel Investors

Venture capital may be provided by high-net-worth individuals (HNWIs), often called angel investors, or by venture capital firms. The National Venture Capital Association is an organization composed of VC firms that fund innovative enterprises. [7]

Angel investors tend to be a diverse group who have accumulated their wealth through a variety of sources. However, they tend to be entrepreneurs themselves, or executives recently retired from business empires. Most look to invest in well-managed companies that have a fully developed business plan and are poised for substantial growth.

These investors may also offer funding to ventures involved in industries or business sectors they are familiar with. Another common occurrence among angel investors is co-investing, where one angel investor funds a venture alongside a trusted friend or associate, often another angel investor.

## Venture Capital Success

Because of the industry's proximity to Silicon Valley, the overwhelming majority of deals funded by venture capitalists are in the technology industry -- including internet, healthcare, computer hardware and services, and mobile and telecommunications. In 2023, San Francisco still ranked highest in VC investment. [8] Other industries have also benefited from VC funding, including Staples and Starbucks (SBUX).

Google and Intel both have venture funds to invest in emerging technology. [9] In 2019, Starbucks also announced a $100 million venture fund to invest in food startups. [11] VC has matured over time, and the industry comprises a range of players and investor types who invest at various stages of a startup's evolution.

## Why Is Venture Capital Important?

New businesses are often high-risk and cost-intensive ventures. As a result, external capital is often sought to spread the risk of failure. In return for taking on this risk through investment, investors in new companies can obtain equity and voting rights for cents on the potential dollar. Venture capital thus allows startups to get off the ground and founders to fulfill their vision.

## What Is Late-Stage Investing?

Late-stage financing has become increasingly popular, as institutional investors prefer to invest in less risky ventures rather than early-stage companies with higher failure rates. [12]

## How Did Regulatory Changes Boost VC?

The Small Business Investment Act (SBIC) of 1958 boosted the VC industry by providing tax breaks to investors. In 1978, the Revenue Act was amended to reduce the capital gains tax from 49% to 28%. [13] In 1979, a change to the Employee Retirement Income Security Act (ERISA) allowed pension funds to invest up to 10% of their assets in small or new businesses. [14] In 1981, the capital gains tax was lowered to 20%. [15] These developments catalyzed the growth of VC, and the 1980s turned into a boom period for the industry, with funding levels reaching $4.9 billion in 1987. [16]

## Conclusion

Venture capital represents a core part of the life cycle of a new business. Before a company can start earning revenue, it needs startup capital to hire employees, rent facilities, and begin designing a product. This funding is provided by VCs in exchange for a share of the new company's equity.
