# T+1 Settlement: Understanding Securities Trade Settlement Periods
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
T+1 (as well as T+2 and T+3) are shorthand notations that refer to the settlement date of securities transactions. The "T" stands for the transaction date -- the day the trade is executed. The numbers 1, 2, or 3 indicate how many business days after the transaction date the settlement -- meaning the transfer of money and security ownership -- takes place.

### Key Takeaways

- T+1 (or T+2, T+3) are abbreviations that refer to a trade's settlement date.
- The letter "T" represents the transaction date; the numbers 1, 2, or 3 indicate how many days after the transaction date settlement occurs.
- Stocks settle on a T+1 basis, while bonds, mutual funds, and money market funds vary between T+1, T+2, and T+3.

## Understanding T+1 (T+2, T+3)

When determining the T+1 (T+2, T+3) settlement date, only days on which the stock market is open count as business days. For example, T+1 means that if a trade occurs on a Monday, settlement must be completed by Tuesday. Under T+3, a trade executed on Monday must settle by Thursday, assuming no holidays intervene. However, if you sell a security with a T+3 settlement on a Friday, the transfer of ownership and funds does not need to occur until the following Wednesday.

Understanding a stock's settlement date is important for investors focused on dividend-paying companies or for strategic traders, because the settlement date determines which party receives the dividend. In other words, a trade must settle before the record date for the stock buyer to be eligible for the dividend.

Historically, securities transactions were conducted manually rather than electronically. Investors had to wait for physical delivery of a security certificate and did not pay until receipt. Because delivery times were variable and prices could fluctuate, market regulators established a defined window within which securities and cash had to be delivered. Over the years, the standard settlement period for stocks has been progressively shortened -- from T+5, to T+3, to T+2 in 2017, and finally to T+1 in 2024.

Settlement periods vary by security type. Currently, all stocks settle on a T+1 basis; however, bonds, mutual funds, and certain money market funds may settle on T+1, T+2, or T+3.

**Tip:** The settlement date is the date on which the investor becomes the shareholder of record. Weekends and public holidays are not counted. Investors should be aware of the settlement date because it determines when they officially become the shareholder of record, which may affect their eligibility to receive dividends.

## The T+1 Settlement Cycle for Stocks

In May 2024, U.S. equity markets officially transitioned to the T+1 settlement cycle, meaning securities trades now settle one business day after the trade date. This change, initially proposed by the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) in February 2023, shortened the settlement cycle from the T+2 standard that had been in place since 2017. The last time U.S. equity markets operated on a one-day settlement cycle was approximately a century ago.

The new T+1 settlement cycle applies to listed equities, bonds, municipal securities, exchange-traded funds, certain mutual funds, and limited partnerships traded on exchanges. Under the new rules, if an investor sells stock on a Monday, the trade settles on Tuesday -- meaning the formal transfer of securities to the buyer's account and cash to the seller's account occurs one business day after execution.

The SEC noted that the shorter settlement cycle may create a short-term risk of marginally higher failed trades, requiring dealers to make adjustments. In rare cases, investors may need to deliver security certificates to their broker in advance or through alternative means, and investors purchasing securities may need to fund the trade one business day ahead. The change may also affect certain margin agreement provisions, so investors should consult their broker about any specific impacts on their accounts.

The transition to T+1 settlement also took effect on the same date in Canada and Mexico. The change is expected to bring U.S. equity markets in line with modern information and communications technology, enabling faster settlement times.

**Important:** Note that the time between trade and settlement does not mean the investor can back out of the trade during that period. The trade is completed on the transaction date; only the transfer has yet to occur.

## T+1 Settlement Example

Suppose that on Monday, June 3, an investor named Sarah decides to sell 100 shares of XYZ Company stock at $50 per share. She places the order through her broker, and the trade is executed that day.

Under the T+1 settlement cycle, the trade settles on Tuesday, June 4 (assuming no holidays). This means the securities are formally transferred to the buyer's account and the cash is transferred to Sarah's account on that date.

Had the trade been executed under the T+2 cycle, the settlement date would have been Wednesday, June 5, meaning the transfer of securities and cash would have occurred two business days after the trade date rather than one.

## History of U.S. Stock Settlement Cycles

A century ago, stock settlement operated on a T+1 basis, but everything was done manually. As interest in stock trading and the volume of shares grew rapidly, so did the need to streamline the settlement process. The SEC originally set a standard settlement cycle of five business days (T+5) for most securities transactions to allow adequate time for physical delivery of stock certificates and corresponding payment. That standard was shortened to T+3 in 1993, then to T+2 in 2017, and finally to T+1 in 2024.

## Are There Exceptions to the T+1 Settlement Cycle?

Yes. Certain types of securities and transactions may be exempt from the T+1 cycle. For instance, some primary issuances such as initial public offerings (IPOs) may have different settlement periods depending on the listing exchange's rules. Additionally, certain fixed-income securities -- such as U.S. Treasuries and many money market instruments -- already settle on a T+1 or even T+0 (same-day) basis.

## What Is Settlement Risk?

Settlement risk is the possibility that one or more parties fail to deliver on the terms of a contract by the agreed-upon time. It is a form of counterparty risk associated with default and timing mismatches between parties. Settlement risk is also known as delivery risk or Herstatt risk. The period between trade and settlement is designed to mitigate this risk by ensuring that all parties have gathered the necessary information and payments.

## Conclusion

Securities trade settlement periods -- abbreviated as T+1, T+2, T+3, and so on -- refer to the number of business days after the transaction date on which actual settlement occurs. As of 2024, U.S. equity markets have moved to a T+1 settlement cycle, meaning most stock trades now settle within one business day of execution. This change is intended to reduce settlement risk and align with modern technology and practices. However, investors should note that certain securities and transactions in various markets or regions may have different settlement periods.
