# An Inverted Yield Curve Indicates Long-Term Rates Are Lower Than Short-Term Rates

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

In an inverted yield curve, yields decline as maturity increases. Sometimes referred to as a negative yield curve, an inverted curve has historically proven to be a reliable indicator of impending recession.

### Key Takeaways

- A yield curve graphically represents the yields of similar bonds across various maturities.
- An inverted yield curve occurs when short-term debt instruments offer higher yields than long-term instruments of the same credit quality.
- An inverted yield curve is unusual; it reflects bond investors' expectations that long-term interest rates will decline, a development typically associated with recessions.
- Market participants and economists use various yield spreads as proxies for the yield curve.

## Understanding an Inverted Yield Curve

A yield curve graphically represents the yields of similar bonds across different maturities. It is also known as the term structure of interest rates. For example, the U.S. Treasury publishes Treasury bill and bond yields daily, which can be plotted as a curve. [1][2]

Analysts typically distill yield curve signals into the spread between two maturities. This simplifies the task of interpreting a yield curve where inversion may exist between some maturities but not others. The drawback is that there is no universal consensus on which spread is the most reliable recession predictor. [3]

Normally, the yield curve slopes upward, reflecting the greater risk assumed by holders of longer-term debt.

When long-term rates fall below short-term rates, the yield curve inverts, indicating that investors are shifting capital from short-term bonds into long-term bonds. This signals that the market as a whole has become more pessimistic about the near-term economic outlook.

In the modern era, yield curve inversions have become a relatively reliable recession indicator. [4][5] Because inversions are relatively rare yet have typically preceded recessions, they attract close scrutiny from financial market participants.

**Important:** An inverted Treasury yield curve is one of the most reliable leading indicators of recession.

## Choosing Your Spread

Academic research on the relationship between inverted yield curves and recessions has tended to focus on the spread between the 10-year U.S. Treasury yield and the 3-month Treasury bill, while market participants tend to watch the spread between the 10-year and 2-year notes more closely. [4]

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell stated in March 2022 that he prefers to gauge recession risk by looking at the difference between the current 3-month Treasury bill rate and the derivatives market pricing that forecasts where the same rate will be 18 months ahead. [6][7]

## Historical Examples of Inverted Yield Curves

The 10-year/2-year Treasury spread has been a broadly reliable recession indicator since a false positive in the mid-1960s. [8] This has not prevented a long list of senior U.S. economic officials from discounting its predictive power over the years. [9]

In 1998, the 10-year/2-year spread briefly inverted following Russia's debt default. Swift interest rate cuts by the Federal Reserve helped avert a U.S. recession.

**Note:** While inverted yield curves have generally preceded recessions in recent decades, they do not cause recessions. Rather, bond prices reflect investors' expectations that longer-term yields will decline, which is what typically occurs during a recession.

In 2006, the spread was inverted for much of the year. Long-term Treasuries outperformed equities in 2007. The Great Recession began in December 2007.

On August 28, 2019, the 10-year/2-year spread briefly turned negative. The U.S. economy experienced a two-month recession in February and March 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic -- a development that could not possibly have been priced into bonds six months earlier.

In late 2022, the yield curve inverted once again amid elevated inflation.

As of December 4, 2023, Treasury yields were as follows:

- Three-month Treasury bill: 4.31%
- Two-year Treasury note: 4.56%
- 10-year Treasury note: 4.22%
- 30-year Treasury bond: 4.40%

As the data show, the 10-year yield was 0.26 percentage points below the 2-year yield. Although the inversion that began in 2022 had narrowed, it persisted.

The shape of the yield curve reflects investors' views on economic conditions and whether the Federal Reserve will continue raising rates -- or perhaps not.

Many economists believed the U.S. economy might still be heading toward recession. Others, however, argued that as of December 2023, the narrowing of the inverted yield curve was telling a different story. Some suggested that the yield curve may have been signaling investor confidence that runaway inflation had been tamed and that normality would return, rather than portending economic turmoil.

## What Is a Yield Curve?

A yield curve is a line that plots the yields (interest rates) of bonds with identical credit quality but different maturities. The most closely watched yield curve is that of U.S. Treasuries.

## What Can an Inverted Yield Curve Tell Investors?

Historically, prolonged inversions of the U.S. yield curve have foreshadowed recessions. An inverted yield curve reflects investors' expectations that longer-term rates will fall due to deteriorating economic performance.

## Why Is the 10-Year/2-Year Spread Important?

Many investors use the spread between the 10-year and 2-year U.S. Treasury yields as a proxy for the yield curve. In recent decades, it has been a relatively reliable leading indicator of recession. Some Federal Reserve officials have argued that focusing on shorter-term maturities provides better insight into recession probability.

## The Bottom Line

Regardless of which yield spread you use as a proxy, a sustained inversion of the yield curve appears to be a more reliable recession signal than a brief one.

Fortunately, recessions are rare events, and there have not been enough of them to draw definitive conclusions. As one Federal Reserve researcher has observed, "It is hard to predict recessions. We have not had very many of them, and we do not fully understand the causes of those we have had. Nonetheless, we keep trying." [3]
