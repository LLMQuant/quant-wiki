# What Is the Producer Price Index (PPI)?
![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)
The Producer Price Index (PPI) measures the average change over time in the prices domestic producers receive for their output. It serves as a gauge of wholesale-level inflation and is composed of thousands of indices that measure producer prices by industry and product category. The index is published monthly by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS).

The PPI differs from the Consumer Price Index (CPI), which measures changes in the prices consumers pay for goods and services.

### Key Takeaways

- The Producer Price Index measures the change in prices paid to U.S. producers of goods and services.
- The PPI is a measure of wholesale inflation, whereas the Consumer Price Index measures prices paid by consumers.
- The index is published monthly by the Bureau of Labor Statistics.
- PPI indices are calculated based on products and services, industries, and the buyer's economic identity, which are used to compute the overall monthly change in final demand PPI.
- These indices track price changes in private contracts based on suppliers' input prices.

## Understanding the Producer Price Index (PPI)

The PPI measures inflation (or, less commonly, deflation) from the standpoint of the product manufacturer or service provider. Price trends for producers and consumers are unlikely to diverge over the long run, since producer prices heavily influence the prices charged to consumers and vice versa. In the short term, wholesale- and retail-level inflation can differ due to distribution costs as well as government taxes and subsidies. [1]

The BLS releases the PPI and its component industry and product indices during the second week following the survey reference date. It is based on approximately 100,000 monthly price quotes voluntarily reported online by more than 25,000 systematically sampled producer establishments. [2][3]

**2.2%:** The unadjusted PPI reading for the 12 months through April 2024. The final demand index rose 0.5% in April from March. [4]

The survey covers the entirety of U.S. goods output and approximately 69% of the value of services. Component product and service indices are weighted by the value of that category's output to calculate the overall change in producer prices. [5]

The PPI is used to forecast inflation and to calculate escalation clauses in private contracts based on the prices of key inputs. It is also essential for tracking industry price changes and comparing wholesale and retail price trends. [5]

## Producer Price Index (PPI) vs. Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Both the PPI and the CPI are important economic indicators because they point to monthly price changes. But they reflect different perspectives on pricing. As noted above, the PPI measures prices based on the first commercial transaction for a product or service. [5]

This contrasts with the Consumer Price Index, which measures price changes encountered by consumers. [6]

The CPI focuses on final sales. But the two indices differ not only in the type of prices they measure. There are also important compositional differences between the PPI and CPI, based on what is included and excluded from each index.

**Important:** Before 1978, the PPI was known as the Wholesale Price Index (WPI). In 1982, the BLS reset all producer price indices to a base of 100. [7]

For example, the PPI does not measure price changes in overall housing costs, whereas the CPI's shelter category -- which includes imputed owners' equivalent rent -- accounts for about one-third of the total index.

Meanwhile, the PPI incorporates a health care services weighting of nearly 16.6%, not far from the industry's roughly 20% share of U.S. GDP. By comparison, health care carries a weight of 8% in the CPI. This is because the CPI does not measure third-party health care reimbursements. [8][9]

Another key distinction is that, unlike the CPI, the PPI does not include the prices of imported goods. Instead, the PPI includes export prices, which the CPI does not. [10]

## How PPI Data Are Presented

The BLS generates more than 10,000 individual product and industry price indices each month, which are then used to compute the PPI. They are released in both seasonally adjusted and unadjusted form and fall into three categories: industry-level classification, commodity classification, and final demand-intermediate demand. [11]

The PPI includes producer price indices received across more than 500 industry categories, based on the output each industry sells outside the industry. These categories are compatible with those used to report industry-level data on production, employment, earnings, and productivity in other releases. [11]

The commodity classification disregards the producer's industry and instead groups output by the nature of the product or service. The PPI release publishes more than 3,800 commodity price indices for goods and approximately 900 for services. [11]

Final demand-intermediate demand indices use commodity indices organized by product to measure producer prices based on the buyer's economic identity and whether the goods sold require further processing.

The PPI release publishes more than 600 FD-ID indices. Final demand indices (as opposed to intermediate demand indices) are then used to derive the headline PPI figure, which reflects the overall PPI for final demand. [11]

## What Is Included in the Producer Price Index?

The Producer Price Index "measures the average change over time in the selling prices received by domestic producers for their output. The prices included in the PPI are from the first commercial transaction for many products and some services." [5]

## What Is the Difference Between the Producer Price Index and the Consumer Price Index?

Both the CPI and the PPI measure inflation. The PPI measures inflation from the producer's perspective -- the average selling prices they receive for their output over a period of time. The CPI measures inflation from the consumer's perspective -- the value of a basket of goods and services purchased by consumers over a period of time. [10]

## What Does the Producer Price Index Predict?

The Producer Price Index examines inflation from the perspective of industries and businesses. This approach measures price changes before consumers purchase final goods and services. As a result, many analysts regard it as a predictor of inflation ahead of the CPI.

## Conclusion

The Producer Price Index measures the changes in prices received by domestic producers for their goods and services. The index serves as a measure of wholesale inflation and as a gauge of economic health.
