# What Is Neoliberalism?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

Neoliberalism is a political and economic policy model that favors private enterprise and seeks to transfer control of economic factors from the public sector to the private sector.

Many neoliberal policies relate to the efficient functioning of free-market capitalism and focus on limiting government spending, government regulation, and public ownership.

Neoliberalism is commonly associated with the leadership of Margaret Thatcher, Prime Minister of the United Kingdom from 1979 to 1990 (and leader of the Conservative Party from 1975 to 1990), and Ronald Reagan, the 40th President of the United States, who served from 1981 to 1989. [1][2]

More recently, neoliberalism has been linked to austerity policies and efforts to reduce government spending on social programs.

### Key Takeaways

- Neoliberal policies generally support fiscal austerity, deregulation, free trade, privatization, and reduced government spending.
- Neoliberalism is commonly associated with the economic policies of Margaret Thatcher in the United Kingdom and Ronald Reagan in the United States.
- Critics of neoliberalism argue that it poses potential dangers to democracy, undermines workers' rights, and structures society and the economy around markets that are far less rational than assumed.
- It has also been accused of granting corporations excessive power and exacerbating economic inequality.

## Understanding Neoliberalism

Neoliberalism is a political and economic philosophy that emphasizes free trade, deregulation, globalization, and reduced government spending. It is related to laissez-faire economics -- a school of thought advocating minimal government intervention in the economic affairs of individuals and society.

Laissez-faire economics holds that minimal state involvement in the economy maximizes economic growth and prosperity.

Neoliberalism is sometimes confused with libertarianism. However, neoliberals generally advocate for more government involvement in economic and social matters than libertarians do. For example, while neoliberals typically favor progressive taxation at low rates, libertarians tend to advocate for flat taxes or the elimination of income tax altogether. The two philosophies also diverge on other dimensions. Libertarians focus primarily on economic freedom and individual rights, whereas neoliberals are more concerned with free-market economics as a means to achieve broader growth objectives.

Moreover, neoliberals are generally not opposed to measures such as bailouts of major industries -- actions that libertarians find objectionable.

## Characteristics of Neoliberalism

Neoliberalism is grounded in the belief that greater economic freedom leads to greater economic and social progress for individuals. It supports:

- The importance of free enterprise, competition, deregulation, and individual responsibility
- Opposition to the expansion of government power, state welfare programs, and inflation
- Minimizing government control over industry and promoting private-sector ownership of businesses and property
- Free-market capitalism and the efficient allocation of resources
- Free trade rather than heavily regulated markets and protectionism
- Reduced government spending and lower taxes
- Diminished government control over economic activity to improve economic efficiency
- An increased role for the private sector in the economy
- Reduced union power and greater labor market flexibility
- Government intervention only when necessary to implement, sustain, and protect free-market activity

**Note:** President Jimmy Carter's 1978 deregulation of the airline industry is one example of neoliberal policy. The Airline Deregulation Act removed government control over fares, routes, and market entry. [3]

## Liberalism vs. Neoliberalism

At its core, liberalism is a broad political philosophy that holds individual liberty to a high standard and defines the social, economic, and political dimensions of society, including the proper role of government.

Neoliberalism is fundamentally an economic ideology. Its policy prescriptions are narrower in scope, primarily focused on markets and the policies and measures that affect the economy.

## Criticism of Neoliberalism

Neoliberalism has attracted substantial criticism.

A common objection is that advocating a free-market approach in areas such as healthcare and education is misguided, because these are public services. Public services are not subject to the same profit motive that governs other industries.

More importantly, applying free-market principles to healthcare and education can exacerbate inequality and lead to underfunding of resources -- healthcare and education -- that are essential to the long-term health and viability of the economy.

The adoption of neoliberal policies in the Western world has coincided with rising wealth and income inequality. While highly skilled workers may command higher wages, lower-skilled workers are more likely to see their wages stagnate.

Some argue that policies associated with neoliberalism encourage the formation of monopolies, increasing corporate profits at the expense of consumers. [4]

Contrary to what neoliberal proponents often claim, capital deregulation has not necessarily promoted economic development. Instead, it has contributed to increased financial instability, including economic shocks that reverberate globally.

An International Monetary Fund (IMF) report on neoliberalism found that increased capital flows are a factor in heightening the risk of adverse economic cycles. [5]

Research indicates that neoliberal policies tend to exacerbate inequality, which can in turn impede long-term economic growth. On one hand, those with lower incomes have limited spending power. On the other hand, those who become wealthier have a higher propensity to save. [6]

In the latter case, wealth does not "trickle down" as neoliberal advocates suggest.

Finally, neoliberalism's emphasis on economic efficiency has encouraged globalization, which opponents say has led to factory closures and the offshoring of jobs.

Critics also contend that neoliberalism's call to replace government-owned enterprises with private corporations can create various forms of social harm, such as reduced access to essential services and the erosion of workers' rights.

Furthermore, opponents argue that neoliberalism is antidemocratic and may lead to exploitation and social injustice. They assert that it can enable corporations to use their power and resources to override the will of the majority, and that it has led to support for nondemocratic regimes that implement neoliberal economic frameworks.

## What Is Neoliberalism in Simple Terms?

Neoliberalism is an economic model or philosophy holding that in a free society, greater economic and social progress is achieved when government regulation is minimized, government spending and taxes are reduced, and the government does not exercise tight control over the economy. Neoliberalism does not oppose all government intervention; however, it seeks to limit such intervention to situations where it is necessary to support free markets and free enterprise.

## What Are the Effects of Neoliberalism?

Potential effects include freer markets, greater access to products and services to meet consumer demand, higher incomes, and greater profits. Lower prices resulting from increased competition may also be an outcome. More efficient resource allocation can generate savings, and better organization of the labor force -- including the ability to hire the talent needed for specific roles -- may result from neoliberal policies. Others point to perceived negative effects associated with neoliberalism, including economic inequality, the growth of monopolies, job insecurity, job losses due to outsourcing, and growing indifference to individual needs and well-being.

## What Is an Example of Neoliberalism?

The North American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA) is one example. Under this agreement, Canada, Mexico, and the United States agreed to eliminate trade restrictions among themselves to open up commerce and increase each nation's economic benefit.

## The Bottom Line

Broadly speaking, neoliberalism is an economic policy stance holding that government should play a limited role in the economy and that all functions where the private sector can operate effectively should be privatized. Like many economic policy frameworks, neoliberalism has both advantages and disadvantages.
