# What Was the Great Depression?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The Great Depression was a devastating and prolonged economic downturn that followed the U.S. stock market crash of 1929. It lasted until 1941, the year the United States entered World War II. The period was marked by several economic contractions, including the 1929 stock market crash, the banking panics of 1930 and 1931, and the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, which devastated world trade. Other events and policies also contributed to the prolonged depression of the 1930s.

Economists and historians often cite the Great Depression as the largest -- if not the most catastrophic -- economic event of the 20th century.

**Important:** It is difficult to pinpoint a single cause of the Great Depression, but economists and historians generally agree that several mitigating factors contributed to this period of economic decline. These include the 1929 stock market crash, the gold standard, a decline in lending and tariffs, banking panics, and contractionary monetary policy by the Federal Reserve.

### Key Takeaways

- The Great Depression was the greatest and longest economic recession in modern world history.
- The Great Depression lasted from 1929 to 1941.
- Investment in speculative markets during the 1920s led to the stock market crash of 1929, which wiped out a great deal of nominal wealth.
- Other factors also contributed to the Great Depression, including Fed inaction followed by overreaction.
- Both Presidents Hoover and Roosevelt tried to mitigate the impact of the Depression through government policies.

## The Stock Market Crash

During the brief depression known as the Forgotten Depression, which lasted from 1920 to 1921, the U.S. stock market fell by nearly 50% and corporate profits declined by over 90%. The U.S. economy subsequently enjoyed robust growth during the rest of the decade. The American public discovered the stock market and dove in headfirst during the Roaring Twenties. [1]

Speculative frenzies affected both the real estate market and the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE). Loose money supply and high levels of margin trading by investors fueled an unprecedented rise in asset prices. [2]

In the period leading up to October 1929, stock prices rose to all-time highs of more than 19 times after-tax corporate earnings. [3] Combined with the benchmark Dow Jones Industrial Average (DJIA) rising 500% in just five years, this ultimately caused the market crash. [4]

The NYSE bubble burst violently on October 24, 1929, a day that came to be known as Black Thursday. [5] A brief rally occurred on Friday the 25th and during a half-day session on Saturday the 26th, but the following week brought Black Monday (October 28) and Black Tuesday (October 29). The DJIA fell more than 20% over those two days. [6] The stock market would eventually fall nearly 90% from its 1929 peak. [4]

Ripples from the crash crossed the Atlantic to Europe, triggering other financial crises such as the collapse of the Boden-Kredit Anstalt, Austria's most important bank. In 1931, the economic catastrophe hit both continents in full force. [7]

## The U.S. Economic Spiral

The 1929 stock market crash wiped out nominal wealth for both corporations and individuals, sending the U.S. economy into a downward spiral. In early 1929, the U.S. unemployment rate was 3.2%. By 1933, it had soared to over 25%. [8][9]

Despite unprecedented interventions and government spending by both the Hoover and Roosevelt administrations, the unemployment rate remained above 18.9% in 1938. Real per capita gross domestic product (GDP) was below 1929 levels by the time Japan bombed Pearl Harbor in late 1941.

While the crash likely triggered the decade-long recession, most historians and economists agree that it alone did not cause the Great Depression. Nor does it explain why the severity and duration of the downturn were so extreme. A series of specific events and policies contributed to the Great Depression and helped prolong it through the 1930s.

## Errors of the Young Federal Reserve

According to monetarists such as Milton Friedman and as acknowledged by former Fed Chair Ben Bernanke, the relatively new Federal Reserve mismanaged the supply of money and credit before and after the 1929 crash. [10]

Created in 1913, the Fed remained fairly inactive throughout its first eight years of existence. After the economy recovered from the 1920-1921 depression, the Fed allowed significant monetary expansion. [11]

Total money supply grew by $28 billion, a 61.8% increase, between 1921 and 1928. [12] Bank deposits rose by 51.1%, savings and loan shares increased by 224.3%, and net life insurance policy reserves grew by 113.8%. All of this occurred after the Federal Reserve cut required reserves to 3% in 1917. Gold reserves gained through the Treasury and Fed amounted to only $1.16 billion. [13]

By increasing the money supply and keeping interest rates low throughout the decade, the Fed instigated the rapid expansion that preceded the collapse. Much of the excess money supply inflated the stock market and real estate bubbles.

After the bubbles burst and the market crashed, the Fed took the opposite course by cutting the money supply by nearly a third. This reduction created severe liquidity problems for many small banks and strangled hopes of a quick recovery.

**Note:** Trade routes established during World War II remained open during the Depression and helped the market recover.

## The Fed's Tight Fist

As Bernanke noted in a November 2002 address, before the Fed existed, bank panics were typically resolved within weeks. Large private financial institutions would lend money to the strongest smaller institutions to maintain system integrity. [10] That kind of scenario had occurred two decades earlier, during the Panic of 1907.

When frantic selling sent the NYSE spiraling downward and led to bank runs, investment banker J.P. Morgan stepped in and rallied Wall Street denizens to move significant amounts of capital to banks lacking funds. [14] Ironically, it was that panic that led the government to create the Federal Reserve in order to reduce reliance on individual financiers such as Morgan.

After Black Thursday, the heads of several New York banks had tried to instill confidence by prominently purchasing large blocks of blue-chip stocks at above-market prices. [15] While these actions caused a brief rally on Friday, panicked sell-offs resumed on Monday. In the decades since 1907, the stock market had grown beyond the ability of such individual efforts. Only the Fed was large enough to prop up the U.S. financial system.

The Fed failed to do so with a cash injection between 1929 and 1932. Instead, it watched the money supply collapse and let thousands of banks fail. The resulting collapse of the financial sector led to deflation and fueled the subsequent depression. At the time, banking laws made it difficult for institutions to grow and diversify enough to survive a massive wave of deposit withdrawals or bank runs.

The Fed's harsh reaction may have stemmed from a fear that bailing out careless banks would encourage future fiscal irresponsibility. Some historians argue that the Fed created the conditions that caused the economy to overheat and then exacerbated an already dire economic situation.

## Hoover's Artificially Propped-Up Prices

Herbert Hoover is often characterized as a "do-nothing" president, but he did take action after the crash. Between 1930 and 1932, he implemented three major changes:

- Federal spending increased by 42%, funding massive public works projects such as the Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC) [16]
- Taxes were imposed to pay for new programs
- Immigration was banned in 1930 to prevent low-skilled workers from flooding the labor market [17]

Hoover's primary concern was that wages would be cut in the wake of the economic downturn. He reasoned that prices needed to stay high to ensure high wages across all industries. To sustain high prices, consumers needed to pay more.

But the public had been hit hard by the crash and many people lacked the resources to spend lavishly on goods and services. Nor could companies count on overseas trade, as foreign nations were no more willing to buy overpriced American goods than Americans were.

**Important:** Many of the other interventions Hoover and Congress implemented after the crash -- including wage, labor, trade, and price controls -- impaired the economy's ability to adjust and reallocate resources. [17]

## U.S. Protectionism

This bleak reality forced Hoover to use legislation to prop up prices and wages by choking off cheaper foreign competition. Following the tradition of protectionists, and over the protests of more than 1,000 of the nation's economists, he signed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 into law.

The act was initially intended to protect agriculture but ballooned into a multi-industry tariff, imposing enormous duties on more than 880 foreign products. Nearly three dozen countries retaliated, and imports fell from $7 billion in 1929 to $2.5 billion in 1932. By 1934, international trade had declined by 66%. Not surprisingly, economic conditions worsened worldwide. [18]

Hoover's desire to maintain jobs and individual and corporate income levels was understandable, but he encouraged businesses to raise wages, avoid layoffs, and keep prices high at a time when they naturally should have fallen. With previous cycles of recession and depression in the United States, the economy experienced one to three years of low wages and unemployment before declining prices led to a recovery.

Unable to sustain these artificial levels with international trade effectively cut off, the economy deteriorated from a recession into a depression.

## The New Deal

President Franklin Roosevelt promised massive change when he was elected in 1933. The New Deal he initiated was an innovative, unprecedented series of domestic programs and acts designed to support American businesses, reduce unemployment, and protect the public. [19]

Loosely based on Keynesian economics and the idea that government can and should stimulate the economy, the New Deal set ambitious goals to create and maintain the national infrastructure, full employment, and healthy wages. The government set about achieving these goals through price, wage, and even production controls. [20]

Some economists claim that Roosevelt continued many of Hoover's interventions, just on a larger scale. He maintained a rigid focus on price supports and minimum wages and removed the country from the gold standard, forbidding individuals from hoarding gold coins and bullion. [21] He banned monopolistic business practices and established dozens of new public works programs and other job-creation agencies.

The Roosevelt administration also paid farmers and ranchers to stop or cut back production. One of the most heartbreaking conundrums of the period was the destruction of surplus crops, despite the need of thousands of Americans for affordable food. [20]

Federal taxes tripled between 1933 and 1940 to pay for these initiatives, as well as new programs like Social Security. These increases included hikes in excise taxes, personal income taxes, inheritance taxes, corporate income taxes, and an excess profits tax. [19]

## Successes and Failures of the New Deal

The New Deal led to measurable results, including financial system reform and stabilization, as well as a boost to public confidence. Roosevelt declared a bank holiday for an entire week in March 1933 to prevent institutional collapse due to panic withdrawals. A construction program followed that built a network of dams, bridges, tunnels, and roads. These projects launched federal work programs employing thousands of people.

The economy showed some recovery, but the rebound was far too weak for the New Deal's policies to be unambiguously credited with pulling America out of the Great Depression. Historians and economists disagree on the reasons:

- Keynesians blame insufficient federal spending, arguing Roosevelt did not go far enough with his government-centered recovery plan.
- Others contend that Roosevelt may have prolonged the Depression, much as Hoover did before him, by trying to force immediate improvement rather than letting the economic and business cycle follow its usual two-year course to bottom out.

A study by two UCLA economists estimated that the New Deal extended the Great Depression by at least seven years. [22] Yet it is also possible that the relatively quick recovery characteristic of other post-depression recoveries may not have occurred as rapidly after 1929, because it was the first time ordinary citizens -- not just Wall Street elites -- lost substantial amounts of money in the stock market.

American economic historian Robert Higgs argued that Roosevelt's new rules and regulations came so fast and were so revolutionary that businesses became afraid to hire or invest. [23] Philip Harvey, a professor of law and economics at Rutgers University, suggested that Roosevelt was more interested in addressing social welfare concerns than creating a Keynesian-style macroeconomic stimulus package.

**Note:** Social Security policies enacted by the New Deal created programs for unemployment, disability insurance, and benefits for the elderly and widows.

## The Impact of World War II

Looking at employment and GDP figures, the Great Depression appeared to end abruptly around 1941-1942. This was when the United States entered World War II. The unemployment rate fell from eight million in 1940 to just over one million in 1943. [24] However, more than 16 million Americans were conscripted to serve in the armed forces. [25] In the private sector, the real unemployment rate grew during the war.

The standard of living declined due to wartime rationing, and taxes rose dramatically to fund the war effort. Private investment dropped from $17.9 billion in 1940 to $5.7 billion in 1943, and total private sector production fell by nearly 50%. [26]

Although the notion that the war ended the Great Depression is a broken window fallacy, the conflict did put the United States on the road to recovery. The war opened international trading channels and reversed price and wage controls. Government demand opened up for inexpensive products, and the demand created a massive fiscal stimulus.

In the first 12 months after the war ended, private investment rose from $10.6 billion to $30.6 billion. The stock market broke into a bull run in a matter of a few years.

## When Did the Great Depression Start?

The Great Depression began following the stock market crash of 1929, which wiped out both private and corporate nominal wealth. This sent the U.S. economy into a downward spiral that eventually spread beyond U.S. borders to Europe.

## When Did the Great Depression End?

The Great Depression ended around 1941, roughly the same time the United States entered World War II. Most economists cite this as the end date because it marks the point at which unemployment fell and GDP began to grow.

## How Did the Great Depression End?

The conventional view holds that the United States emerged from the Great Depression because of the jobs created by the New Deal and the massive private-sector investments made in preparation for the country's entry into World War II. Some economists dispute this, arguing that the depression would have ended sooner with less government intervention.

## The Bottom Line

The Great Depression was the result of an unfortunate confluence of factors, including an erratic Fed, protectionist tariffs, and inconsistently applied government interventionist efforts. It could have been shortened or even avoided by a change in any one of these factors.

While the debate over whether the interventions were appropriate continues, many of the reforms from the New Deal remain in place to this day. These include Social Security, unemployment insurance, and agricultural subsidies. The presumption that the federal government should act in times of national economic crisis now has strong support. This legacy is one of the reasons the Great Depression is considered one of the seminal events in modern American history.
