# What Is the Future Value of an Annuity?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The future value of an annuity is the amount that a series of periodic payments will be worth at a specific date in the future, given a particular rate of return or discount rate. The higher the discount rate, the greater the annuity's future value. As long as the relevant variables -- payment amount, expected rate of return, and number of periods -- are known, the future value of an annuity can be calculated.

### Key Takeaways

- The future value of an annuity is a method for determining how much a series of payments will be worth at a future date.
- By contrast, the present value of an annuity measures how much money is required today to produce a series of future payments.
- In an ordinary annuity, payments are made at the end of each period; in an annuity due, payments are made at the beginning. [1]
- Calculating the future value of an annuity requires knowledge of the payment amount, the number of periods, and the expected rate of return.
- Because payments in an annuity due benefit from an additional compounding period, their future value is typically higher than that of an otherwise identical ordinary annuity.

## Understanding the Future Value of an Annuity

Because of the time value of money, a dollar received or paid today is worth more than the same dollar in the future. This is because money can be invested and allowed to grow over time. By the same logic, a single lump-sum payment of $5,000 today is worth more than the cumulative value of five $1,000 annuity payments spread over five years.

## Formula for Calculating the Future Value of an Annuity

The future value formula for an ordinary annuity is as follows (an ordinary annuity pays interest at the end of a given period, whereas an annuity due pays at the beginning):

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{P} = \text{PMT} \times \frac { \big ( (1 + r) ^ n - 1 \big ) }{ r } \\ &\textbf{where:} \\ &\text{P} = \text{Future value of the annuity stream} \\ &\text{PMT} = \text{Dollar amount of each annuity payment} \\ &r = \text{Interest rate (also known as the discount rate)} \\ &n = \text{Number of periods} \\ \end{aligned} $$

**Important:** Ordinary annuities are more common, but an annuity due will have a higher future value, all else being equal.

## Future Value of an Annuity Due

With an annuity due, payments are made at the beginning of each period. The formula is therefore adjusted by multiplying the ordinary annuity formula by $(1 + r)$:

$$ \begin{aligned} &\text{P} = \text{PMT} \times \frac { \big ( (1 + r) ^ n - 1 \big ) }{ r } \times ( 1 + r ) \\ \end{aligned} $$

## Example: Future Value of an Ordinary Annuity

Suppose an individual decides to invest $125,000 per year for the next five years in an annuity expected to compound at 8% per year.

In this example, the payment stream is an ordinary annuity, with payments made at the end of each period. Using the formula above, the expected future value is:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Future Value} &= \$125,000 \times \frac { \big ( ( 1 + 0.08 ) ^ 5 - 1 \big ) }{ 0.08 } \\ &= \$733,325 \\ \end{aligned} $$

## Example: Future Value of an Annuity Due

Using the same figures but treating the payments as an annuity due -- where each $125,000 payment is made at the beginning of the period -- the future value is calculated as follows:

$$ \begin{aligned} \text{Future Value} &= \$125,000 \times \frac { \big ( ( 1 + 0.08 ) ^ 5 - 1 \big ) }{ 0.08 } \times ( 1 + 0.08 ) \\ &= \$791,991 \\ \end{aligned} $$

All else being equal, the future value of an annuity due exceeds that of an ordinary annuity because the money has an additional period to accumulate compound interest. In this example, the annuity due is worth $58,666 more than the ordinary annuity.

## What Is the Future Value Factor?

When calculating future value, one component is known as the future value factor. The future value factor represents the total growth of a lump-sum payment or a series of cash flows. For example, if $1,000 grows to a future value of $1,100, the future value factor is 1.1. A future value factor of 1.0 means the cash flows are worth the same in the future as they are today.

## What Is the Difference Between an Annuity and an Annuity Due?

Annuity payments are typically made at the end of each period, whereas annuity due payments are made at the beginning. While the distinction may seem minor, it can produce a meaningful difference in accumulated value once interest compounding is taken into account.

## What Is the Relationship Between Present Value and Future Value?

Present value and future value represent the value of an investment looking backward or forward in time. The two concepts are directly related: the future value of a series of cash flows also has a corresponding present value. For example, a present value of $1,000 today might correspond to a future value of $1,200.

Typically, investors and analysts know one value and solve for the other. For instance, if you purchase a stock today for $100 that earns 2% annually, you can calculate its future value. Alternatively, if you want $10,000 available as a down payment for a car next year, you can solve for the present value required today.

## Conclusion

An annuity is a series of payments made over a period of time, typically in equal amounts. Investors can determine the future value of their annuity by considering the payment amount, expected rate of return, and number of periods. Whether payments are made at the beginning or end of each period also affects the future value calculation.
