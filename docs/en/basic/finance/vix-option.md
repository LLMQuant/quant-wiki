# What Are VIX Options?

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

VIX options are non-equity index options whose underlying asset is the Cboe Volatility Index.

### Key Takeaways

- VIX options are based on the S&P 500 Volatility Index.
- VIX call options serve as a natural hedge against sudden price declines.
- VIX put options can be more difficult to use effectively because the S&P 500 rarely rises sharply.
- VIX options trade as European-style options.

## Understanding VIX Options

VIX options are available in both call and put varieties. Call options can hedge a portfolio against sudden market declines, while put options can hedge against a rapid reversal of short positions on the S&P 500 Index. These options enable traders and investors to speculate on future changes in volatility.

Since their introduction in 2006, VIX options were the first exchange-traded options that allowed individual investors to trade market volatility. [1] VIX options trading is a useful tool for investors. By purchasing a VIX call option, a trader can profit from a rapid increase in volatility.

Sharp spikes in volatility typically coincide with short-term price shocks in equities. A rise in volatility frequently -- though not always -- accompanies a downward trend in the market. As a result, call options on the VIX act as a natural hedge and can be used strategically over longer time horizons as well as tactically in the short term. In many cases, they are more effective than equity index options.

VIX volatility tends to exhibit a pattern of gradual decline and rapid ascent. Consequently, VIX call options can be highly effective hedges when timed correctly; however, VIX put options are more difficult to use profitably. Put options may benefit traders who can accurately anticipate a market transition from a downtrend to an uptrend.

VIX options are cash-settled and trade in the European style. European-style options restrict exercise to the expiration date only. Traders may, however, sell an existing long position or purchase an equivalent option to close a short position at any time before expiration.

For advanced options traders, VIX options can be used to construct a variety of sophisticated strategies, such as bull call spreads and butterfly spreads. Calendar spreads, however, can be problematic because different expiration series do not track each other as closely as equity options do.

## VIX Explained

The Cboe Global Markets (Cboe) Volatility Index trades under the symbol VIX. However, the VIX differs from other traded instruments. It does not represent the price of a commodity, interest rate, or exchange rate; rather, it shows the market's expectation of 30-day stock market volatility. [2]

The VIX is an index calculated from the prices of S&P 500 options. The estimated volatility between the current date and the options' expiration dates forms the basis of the VIX. Cboe combines the prices of multiple options to derive an aggregate volatility value that the index tracks. [3]

Since its introduction in 1993, the Volatility Index (VIX) was initially a weighted measure of the implied volatility of eight S&P 100 at-the-money put and call options. A decade later, in 2004, it was expanded to use options based on the broader S&P 500 index. [1] This expansion provided a more accurate view of investors' expectations for future market volatility. VIX values above 30 are generally associated with significant volatility driven by investor fear or uncertainty. Values below 15 typically correspond to calmer, less stressful -- or even complacent -- periods in the market.

Due to its tendency to rise sharply during periods of market fear and uncertainty, the VIX is also known as the "Fear Index."
