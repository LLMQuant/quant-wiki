## Executive Summary

Macro hedge funds typically establish positions in currencies, bonds, equities, and commodities -- either directional or relative value -- driven by **fundamental analysis and qualitative judgment**. Investment decisions are generally guided by the portfolio manager's **top-down views on economic and political developments**, including expectations around economic growth, interest rates, inflation, government policy, and geopolitics. Relative valuations within or across asset classes may also play a role in the investment process.

In this article, we examine macro trading and provide an in-depth look at the most common macro strategies. For each strategy, we offer a description, illustrative trade examples, and a discussion of historical performance across different market regimes.

> **Notable macro fund: Bridgewater Associates**
> Macro managers tend to be tactical risk-takers, dynamically adjusting portfolio exposures as conditions evolve.

## What Is a Macro Hedge Fund?

Macro hedge funds focus on trading asset classes driven primarily by **macroeconomic factors**, with a particular emphasis on fixed income and, to a lesser extent, currencies and commodities. Because bottom-up, idiosyncratic factors play a larger role in equities and credit, macro funds are generally less active in those markets. When they do express views on equities or credit, they typically do so through aggregate instruments such as index futures, credit default swaps, and ETFs.

Macro managers are **tactical in their risk-taking** and favor dynamic portfolio adjustments. Their need for high liquidity in traded instruments leads to a primary focus on developed markets, unless the fund specializes in emerging markets. A hallmark of macro funds is the extensive use of derivatives -- futures, options, and swaps -- which not only provide leverage but also allow managers to express investment views more precisely during periods of elevated volatility.

Macro trades can be either directional or relative value in nature. While quantitative analysis increasingly supports macro trading, final risk decisions tend to remain **discretionary**. Typical holding periods range from several days to several months.
> **As short-term trading has become increasingly contested by quantitative strategies, macro funds have generally shifted away from shorter time horizons.**

## Common Macro Strategies

Macro funds can be categorized in several ways. The four most prevalent sub-strategies are:

1. **Global Macro**
2. **Fixed Income Relative Value (FIRV)**
3. **Emerging Market Macro**
4. **Commodities**

Global Macro is the largest sub-strategy, accounting for more than half of total macro AUM. Fixed Income Relative Value represents roughly one-quarter, Emerging Market Macro approximately one-fifth, and Commodities is the smallest macro sub-strategy by assets under management.

## Global Macro

### Description

Global macro funds possess the broadest investment mandate, trading **across asset classes, financial instruments, and geographies**. The strategy is typically driven by **top-down macroeconomic themes**, such as:

- Economic growth trajectories
- Interest rate expectations
- Inflation dynamics
- Capital flows
- Policy shifts and geopolitical developments

Because interest rates and currencies are most sensitive to these factors, global macro funds tend to concentrate on **fixed income** with selective currency exposure. Equity and credit views are more commonly expressed through aggregate instruments (ETFs, index futures, credit derivatives).

Global macro managers typically exhibit:

- **Tactical risk-taking**: Nimble portfolio adjustment in response to shifting conditions;
- **Strong liquidity preference**: A natural tilt toward developed markets;
- **Extensive derivatives usage**: Futures, options, and swaps are core tools.

#### "Old School" vs. "New Macro"

- **Old School Macro (Macro 1.0)**: Characterized by large, concentrated directional bets held for weeks to months, generating "lumpy" return profiles.
- **New Macro (Macro 2.0)**: Emphasizes relative value trades, employs more sophisticated derivative structures, diversifies across many smaller positions, and targets smoother returns -- potentially at the expense of upside convexity.

### Illustrative Trades

- **Directional**: A classic "risk-off" trade during the early stages of a pandemic -- going long government bonds, USD, precious metals, and volatility while shorting equities and crude oil.
- **Relative Value**: A yield curve flattener, buying and selling different maturities along the same sovereign curve.

### Performance Characteristics

- Opportunities tend to increase during **market dislocations and policy regime changes** (e.g., financial crises, pandemic shocks, inflationary episodes).
- Returns may be muted during **calm, low-volatility** environments.
- The strategy exhibits **contrarian and value-oriented** tendencies, demands high liquidity, and typically displays positive skew with low correlation to traditional assets.

### Risk/Return Profile

- Fund-level dispersion is significant, but the strategy as a whole tends to deliver a **high proportion of alpha** with relatively low market beta.
- Global macro often exhibits **long volatility** or **tail-hedging** properties, making it a valuable diversifier within a broader portfolio.

## Fixed Income Relative Value ("FIRV")

### Description

FIRV funds seek to exploit **pricing inefficiencies within fixed income markets** by taking simultaneous long and short positions in highly correlated bonds, targeting market-neutral or near-market-neutral exposure. The primary instruments are **government bonds and their derivatives**.

- **Sources of pricing dislocations**: Market events, supply-demand imbalances, capital flows, and regulatory changes.
- **High leverage**: Because individual trade returns are small, leverage is essential to generate meaningful P&L.

Historically, FIRV trading was dominated by bank proprietary desks. In recent years, **hedge funds -- particularly multi-strategy platforms** -- have become the primary vehicles. Barriers to entry include the need for specialized talent, prime brokerage relationships, and large-scale technology and risk management infrastructure.

### Illustrative Trades

- **Basis trade**: Going long cash treasuries against short futures contracts (or vice versa) to capture convergence in the basis.
- **Curve steepener/flattener**: Taking opposing duration positions across different maturities, typically via interest rate swaps.

### Performance Characteristics

- Opportunities tend to be more abundant when **interest rates are moderately rising or volatility is elevated**.
- Severe market dislocations -- such as the 2008 Global Financial Crisis or the March 2020 COVID-19 liquidity event -- can trigger substantial drawdowns, particularly if funding pressures force involuntary deleveraging.
- The strategy's reliance on high leverage creates exposure to extreme tail events; the collapse of LTCM in 1998 remains the canonical example.

### Risk/Return Profile

- Returns are typically **steady but moderate**, with low realized volatility and often attractive Sharpe ratios.
- Frequently described as "picking up pennies in front of a steamroller" -- the strategy can suffer severe losses when liquidity or funding conditions deteriorate sharply.
- Correlations with traditional assets and other hedge fund strategies tend to be low, providing strong diversification benefits.

## Emerging Market Macro

### Description

This strategy applies the same top-down framework as global macro but focuses on **emerging economies**, which present distinct challenges: **lower liquidity, less developed derivatives markets**, and heightened sensitivity to external shocks.

- A tendency toward **directional positioning** with a **structural long bias**; shorting EM currencies and equities is feasible, but shorting EM fixed income is more difficult.
- Emerging market cycles tend to be shorter and more volatile, requiring managers with deep regional expertise and experience navigating turbulent conditions.
- The investable universe spans sovereign bonds, quasi-sovereign debt, local currencies, equities, and commodities, roughly in order of trading activity. Credit opportunities are comparatively limited.

### Illustrative Trades

- Going long a country's bonds or currency based on structural reform expectations, an IMF lending program, or improving macro fundamentals.
- Shorting the currency or government debt of a country experiencing political instability or fiscal deterioration.

### Performance Characteristics

- **Risk-on environments** are generally favorable for emerging markets; conversely, risk-off episodes tend to produce significant drawdowns.
- Performance is highly sensitive to global risk appetite; external events such as recessions or geopolitical conflicts can have an outsized impact on EM assets.

### Risk/Return Profile

- **High risk, high return** -- the most volatile among macro sub-strategies.
- Typically exhibits **medium-to-high beta** to emerging market benchmarks, with elevated vulnerability during crises.
- Downside hedging is difficult to implement fully, and risk-adjusted returns (Sharpe ratios) may trail those of global macro.

## Commodities

### Description

Commodity-focused funds trade **futures, options**, and occasionally related equities across energy, metals, and agricultural markets.

- The approach is heavily discretionary, incorporating fundamental analysis and increasingly data science techniques.
- Two primary styles are commonly distinguished:
  - **Directional**: Positioning long or short based on supply-demand imbalances and macroeconomic drivers;
  - **Relative Value**: Including **calendar spreads** (different contract maturities), **cross-commodity spreads** (e.g., crack spreads in energy, spark spreads in power markets), and other basis trades.
- Some funds operate with a long-only or long-flat mandate, while others are fully market-neutral.

### Illustrative Trades

- **Trend/fundamental trade**: Positioning in agricultural commodities following weather-driven supply disruptions.
- **Crack spread trade**: Simultaneously trading crude oil against refined products (gasoline, heating oil) to capture refining margin moves.
- **Cross-commodity arbitrage**: Trading the gold-silver ratio, or platinum versus palladium.

### Performance Characteristics

- Commodities exhibit high volatility and are significantly influenced by geopolitical events and weather patterns, creating pronounced trending opportunities:
  - Strong performance during periods such as 2003--2007, 2009--2010, and the post-2020 inflationary surge;
  - Subdued returns during low-volatility, range-bound markets.

### Risk/Return Profile

- High volatility with variable returns; some funds achieve attractive Sharpe ratios, though most fall below 1.0.
- **Low correlation** with other asset classes and hedge fund strategies.
- Capable of generating substantial returns but subject to meaningful tail risk, particularly from concentrated directional exposure or crowded trades.
