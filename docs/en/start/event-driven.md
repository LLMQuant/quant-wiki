## Executive Summary

Event-driven hedge funds seek to exploit pricing dislocations arising from corporate events such as mergers, acquisitions, restructurings, bankruptcies, and other significant corporate actions. The underlying thesis rests on differentiated views regarding value-unlocking catalysts, event probabilities, and post-event valuations, with the goal of identifying mispriced securities that offer attractive risk/reward profiles. Success in these strategies hinges on a manager's ability to accurately forecast the outcomes of corporate events and effectively manage the associated risks.

## What Are Event-Driven Hedge Funds?

Event-driven hedge funds employ investment strategies designed to capitalize on opportunities triggered by specific corporate events. These events include mergers and acquisitions (M&A), spin-offs, restructurings, bankruptcies, and other material corporate actions. The approach involves analyzing the potential outcomes of these events and their likely impact on the prices of related securities, seeking to exploit market inefficiencies and idiosyncratic risks associated with such events.

Event-driven managers require deep expertise in event dynamics, regulatory environments, and potential market reactions to make informed investment decisions. This typically demands specialized knowledge in fundamental analysis, legal considerations, credit analysis, market microstructure, and corporate strategy. While event-driven strategies are predominantly expressed through equities, credit instruments such as corporate bonds also feature prominently in credit-oriented scenarios including bankruptcies, restructurings, and catalyst-driven credit rating upgrades or downgrades.

Event-driven strategies face risks specific to their target events, including event delays or failures due to regulatory hurdles, shareholder opposition, or unexpected shifts in corporate strategy. Broader market conditions and macroeconomic factors also influence performance, particularly in volatile or uncertain environments that may affect the feasibility of event completion. Liquidity assessment is critical, as event-driven opportunities can attract numerous managers, leading to crowded trades. This risk has intensified in recent years with the proliferation of event-driven sub-strategies within multi-strategy fund structures.

## Common Event-Driven Strategies

Event-driven funds can be classified in various ways. The sub-strategy taxonomy used in this primer aligns with the classification employed by Aurum's Hedge Fund Data Engine, as follows:

- Activism
- Merger Arbitrage
- Event - Multi-Strategy
- Event - Opportunistic

**Activism** managers are classified as event-driven because they actively seek to create catalysts that will enhance shareholder value. They typically acquire substantial stakes in companies to influence management decisions and drive corporate actions such as restructurings, spin-offs, or governance reforms.

**Merger Arbitrage** funds focus on capturing the spread between a target company's current market price and the agreed-upon acquisition price in an M&A transaction. This spread reflects the market's assessment of deal completion risk and timing, providing arbitrageurs with an opportunity to profit from successful deal closure.

**Event - Multi-Strategy** and **Event - Opportunistic** funds are characterized by their diversified approach, investing across multiple event-driven sub-strategies. Beyond activism and merger arbitrage, these funds may allocate capital to event-driven credit and special situations. The latter is a broad category encompassing a range of less obvious catalyst events, typically focusing on companies undergoing significant changes to their corporate structure or capital structure. Special situations may also include more arbitrage-oriented strategies such as share class arbitrage, dual-listed arbitrage, capital structure arbitrage, and holding company/stub value arbitrage. These funds may also deploy liquidity provision strategies, including index rebalancing and equity capital markets (ECM) strategies.

While both fund types can dynamically allocate capital across sub-strategies, the defining characteristic of Event - Opportunistic funds is their highly flexible and opportunistic approach. These funds may concentrate the majority or even all of their risk in a specific area based on the prevailing opportunity set. This contrasts with Event - Multi-Strategy funds, which typically maintain allocations across multiple sub-strategies at all times, with shifts occurring less frequently or less dramatically.

These funds may also allocate a portion of their capital to distressed credit situations, which can also be viewed as a form of event-driven investing. However, if the majority of a fund's risk or P&L is consistently driven by this strategy, such funds would be classified under Aurum's Credit - Distressed Credit category.

Event-driven hedge fund strategies represent a relatively modest share of the hedge fund universe. According to Aurum's Hedge Fund Data Engine, these strategies account for approximately 10% of total hedge fund industry assets. However, event strategies are also a major component of the broader multi-strategy space, typically comprising 5-15% or more of overall allocations.

## Activism

### Description

Activist hedge funds acquire significant equity positions in publicly traded companies with the objective of influencing corporate decisions and driving change to enhance shareholder value. Unlike other hedge fund strategies that may also engage with management -- such as distressed investing and certain special situations -- activist managers are distinguished by their explicit focus on serving as catalysts for change. They typically employ a value-oriented framework, with investment decisions grounded in rigorous fundamental analysis and a proactive approach to influencing corporate strategy. Activist strategies generally target:

- Operational improvements
- Capital structure optimization
- Strategic alternatives (e.g., M&A)
- Corporate governance reforms
- Shareholder-friendly policies (e.g., buybacks or dividends)

**Target Companies**

Activist hedge funds tend to target companies where they believe active engagement can unlock shareholder value. These are typically firms making suboptimal strategic decisions, deploying capital inefficiently, compensating executives excessively, or operating with suboptimal governance structures. Conversely, they generally avoid companies where their ability to effect change is limited or the pathway to value creation is unclear.

**Engagement Methods**

Activist managers typically accumulate substantial equity positions to gain influence, launching either public campaigns or private negotiations to advocate for their proposed changes. The time horizon for activist investments tends to be longer than other hedge fund strategies, often spanning several years, as the process of implementing change and realizing value can be protracted.

**Risks and Challenges**

While the activist approach is associated with notable successes and high-profile campaigns, it carries significant risks. Companies may resist change, and public confrontations can be costly and time-consuming. Moreover, markets may not react favorably to activist interventions if proposed changes are perceived as too aggressive or potentially detrimental to the company's long-term prospects.

**Historical Evolution**

Activist investing has evolved considerably over the decades, resulting in a spectrum of styles and approaches. Early activist investors of the 1980s and 1990s, often labeled "corporate raiders," were known for hostile tactics and aggressive public campaigns. By contrast, modern activists tend to adopt more collaborative and constructive approaches, seeking mutually beneficial outcomes through engagement with management. This evolution has led activist funds to diversify their playbooks, blending traditional activism with elements of private equity and strategic advisory roles.

**Activist Short Sellers**

A distinctive sub-strategy worth highlighting involves **activist short sellers** -- managers who seek to profit from anticipated declines in a company's share price. They typically publish their research findings, drawing attention to issues such as accounting irregularities, misleading disclosures, or unsustainable business models. Their reports can trigger immediate price declines in the target company's stock, potentially undermining its market position and investor confidence.

While activist managers frequently generate controversy through their public criticism of management or other aggressive tactics, activist short sellers are particularly contentious due to their emphasis on short selling. These managers may face allegations of market manipulation and disseminating negative information to depress company valuations. Such practices can spark legal and ethical debates about their impact on market stability and corporate integrity. Proponents argue, however, that activist short selling enhances market efficiency by exposing fraud or unethical conduct, thereby protecting investors and promoting transparency and price discovery.

### Example Trade

A prominent activist campaign in recent years was **Elliott Investment Management L.P.'s** ("Elliott") engagement with technology giant Twitter in 2020. The campaign attracted widespread attention due to the high-profile nature of the target company and its influential CEO, Jack Dorsey.

**Campaign Overview:**

- **Stake Accumulation**: Elliott initiated the campaign by disclosing a substantial stake in Twitter, reportedly approximately 4%, making it one of the largest shareholders.
- **Strategic Intent**: Elliott publicly advocated for changes aimed at enhancing shareholder value, including the potential replacement of Jack Dorsey as CEO. They argued that Dorsey's dual role as CEO of both Twitter and Square (another company he co-founded) was not in the best interests of Twitter's shareholders.
- **Engagement Approach**: The hedge fund reportedly conducted private discussions with Twitter's board and management, advocating for operational improvements and a more focused leadership structure, while nominating four directors to Twitter's board.
- **Public Scrutiny**: Given Twitter's prominence in social media and technology, combined with the high-profile CEO leadership challenge, the campaign received extensive media coverage.
- **Outcome**: The campaign ultimately contributed to significant changes at Twitter. In November 2021, Jack Dorsey stepped down as CEO. The leadership transition was viewed as a partial success for Elliott, aligning with their objectives of improving Twitter's governance and operational focus.

Elliott's activist campaign targeting Twitter exemplifies how hedge funds can strategically leverage their equity positions to influence corporate governance and management decisions, with the aim of unlocking shareholder value.

### Performance Across Market Environments

Activist strategies do not exhibit a clear-cut distinction between favorable and unfavorable environments. However, the strategy's inherent long bias means that broad equity market direction plays a significant role in activist manager performance. A broadly rising equity market can support appreciation in target company shares, enhancing the strategy's return potential. Conversely, declining equity markets can erode the value of activist holdings, making it more difficult to achieve target returns and potentially extending the time required for the market to recognize and reward activist-driven changes.

That said, driving change in rising markets also presents challenges, as identifying targets where activism can unlock meaningful value becomes more difficult, and shareholders are less likely to be dissatisfied with current performance. Activist opportunities tend to be more abundant during periods of corporate distress, when companies may be more vulnerable to activist pressure and more receptive to demands for strategic change.

Finally, sector-specific dynamics also influence activist strategy performance. Industries undergoing rapid technological change or regulatory disruption (e.g., technology, healthcare, consumer goods) typically offer activists more opportunities to advocate for strategic pivots. Conversely, more stable or heavily regulated sectors (e.g., utilities, financials, real estate) may pose greater challenges for activist intervention, as stringent regulations or policies can protect incumbent management and constrain activists' ability to drive change.

### Risk/Return Profile

Activist funds exhibit risk and return expectations that generally sit at the higher end among event-driven strategies and the broader hedge fund universe. Key characteristics include:

- **Long Bias**: The strategy's inherent long bias results in historically elevated correlation with equity markets.
- **Concentrated Portfolios**: Activist funds typically manage high-conviction, concentrated portfolios. The substantial equity stakes required to drive change at target companies generally result in fewer positions and lower diversification. This concentration can lead to higher volatility, as the performance of a small number of key holdings can have a material impact on overall fund returns.
- **Extended Investment Horizons**: Holding periods spanning multiple years are not uncommon, as realizing value from positions typically requires time. This extended horizon exposes activist funds to prolonged market and company-specific risks, making the strategy susceptible to elevated short- to medium-term volatility and larger drawdowns.
- **Value Orientation**: The strategy tends toward a value-oriented investment approach. This value tilt means the strategy may underperform during periods when value is out of favor -- as observed during the post-GFC decade, when growth stocks significantly outperformed value.
- **Headline Risk**: Activist funds are exposed to headline risk, where negative media coverage or public scrutiny of their campaigns can affect the perception and performance of their investments. This risk can contribute to increased volatility and potential drawdowns, as market sentiment can shift rapidly on adverse news.
- **Crowding**: Activist funds often share similar investment universes, leading to potentially overlapping positions. This overlap can amplify the risks discussed above and contribute to increased volatility in stocks that attract multiple activist investors.

In summary, while activist strategies offer the potential for substantial returns by driving meaningful change at target companies, the combination of directional equity exposure, portfolio concentration, extended holding periods, and value orientation can result in elevated volatility and larger drawdowns. Furthermore, due to the strategy's long bias, a meaningful portion of activist fund returns is attributable to beta rather than alpha generation.

## Merger Arbitrage

### Description

Merger arbitrage hedge funds specialize in capturing the spread between a target company's current market price and the agreed-upon acquisition price in an M&A transaction. The strategy typically involves purchasing the target company's stock following an acquisition announcement and selling upon deal completion, thereby capturing the price differential. This spread reflects the market's perception of deal completion risk and timing, providing arbitrageurs with an opportunity to profit from successful deal closure. Key factors influencing the spread include:

- **Deal Completion Risk**: The spread compensates for the possibility that a deal may not close as planned. Factors such as regulatory approvals, shareholder votes, and antitrust considerations affect this risk. Higher perceived risk translates to wider spreads.
- **Time Value of Capital**: Since merger arbitrage involves waiting for deal completion, the spread incorporates a premium for the time value of capital. The longer the expected time to close, the wider the spread to compensate for the opportunity cost of tied-up capital. Prevailing interest rates can significantly influence this component.
- **Market Sentiment and Volatility**: Market conditions and sentiment toward specific deals or the broader market can influence spreads. Increased market volatility or negative sentiment toward a deal can widen spreads due to heightened uncertainty.
- **Competing Bids and Deal Revisions**: The possibility of competing bids or revisions to deal terms can affect spreads. If rumors emerge of another company potentially making a higher offer, the target's stock price may rise, narrowing the spread or even exceeding the initial offer price -- a condition known as "trading through the terms."
- **Arbitrageur Demand and Market Composition**: Once a deal is announced, the composition of buyers and sellers in the target stock shifts materially. Arbitrageurs become the primary trading participants, while "real money" investors tend to step aside. This shift in market participants makes the target stock price highly sensitive to changes in arbitrageur supply and demand. When numerous merger arbitrage managers target the same stock, elevated demand can compress spreads, while lower interest can widen them. Consequently, the primary buyers and sellers -- predominantly arbitrageurs -- exert significant influence on post-announcement price dynamics.

The financing structure of an M&A transaction -- cash, stock, or a combination -- determines how merger arbitrage funds construct their positions. In an all-cash deal, the manager typically establishes a long position in the target company's stock, purchasing shares at the current market price with the expectation that the acquisition will close at the higher agreed-upon cash price, thereby capturing the spread as profit. In an all-stock deal, the merger arbitrage fund establishes a long position in the target and a corresponding short position in the acquirer's stock. This short position hedges against fluctuations in the acquirer's share price that could affect the deal's value. By shorting the acquirer's stock, the arbitrageur aims to lock in the spread value based on the exchange ratio specified in the acquisition agreement.

Historically, merger arbitrage was predominantly practiced by proprietary trading desks at large financial institutions. However, following regulatory changes -- particularly the Volcker Rule, which restricted proprietary trading -- hedge funds have emerged as the primary practitioners, operating as standalone merger arbitrage funds or as sub-strategies within broader event-driven or multi-strategy platforms.

Geographically, merger arbitrage funds tend to focus primarily on the US market, or globally with a US emphasis, due to robust M&A activity levels, a transparent regulatory framework, and deep liquidity. While some managers specialize in European markets, dedicated Asia-focused merger arbitrage funds remain relatively rare.

Given the strategy's mature and formulaic nature, merger arbitrage is increasingly implemented using quantitative, rules-based frameworks. These frameworks identify and engage with deals based on predefined characteristics such as deal size, acquirer rationale, market capitalization, geography, and sector. As a result, the strategy has become somewhat commoditized over time, with returns often viewed as an alternative risk premium best captured systematically. However, despite this trend toward systematization, merger arbitrage remains predominantly discretionary. The complexity of evaluating unique deal characteristics -- such as corporate synergies or antitrust risk -- typically requires discretionary judgment and specialized expertise.

### Example Trade

A notable recent merger arbitrage trade involved Microsoft's acquisition of Activision Blizzard. In January 2022, Microsoft announced its proposed $68.7 billion acquisition of the gaming giant, making it one of the largest transactions in technology sector history. Microsoft agreed to acquire Activision Blizzard at $95 per share in an all-cash structure, aiming to strengthen its gaming portfolio and solidify its position in the rapidly growing gaming industry.

- **Merger arbitrage funds** reportedly purchased Activision Blizzard shares at market prices below the agreed $95 per share acquisition price. The objective was to profit from the spread between the market price and the acquisition price, contingent on deal completion.
- **The transaction faced** intense regulatory scrutiny from antitrust authorities in the US, EU, and UK, with concerns over the potential impact on competition in the gaming industry. This regulatory uncertainty contributed to significant volatility in Activision Blizzard's stock price.
- **Arbitrageurs closely monitored** the regulatory approval process, market reactions, and legal challenges. The uncertainty and associated volatility in Activision Blizzard's stock price presented opportunities for arbitrage profits.
- **Despite** initial regulatory hurdles, the transaction received approval from all relevant authorities and closed in October 2023.

This example highlights the complexities inherent in merger arbitrage, particularly the challenge of navigating regulatory risk and continuously reassessing deal completion probability in highly scrutinized sectors.

### Performance Across Market Environments

Merger arbitrage is one of the most enduring hedge fund sub-strategies. Some specialists have successfully operated merger arbitrage funds for over 30 years, a testament to the persistence of the phenomenon the strategy seeks to monetize.

As a strategy dependent on the completion of corporate transactions, merger arbitrage fund performance is largely driven by the ability to accurately assess and manage idiosyncratic risks associated with individual deals -- principally the probability of completion and potential delays or deal failures. However, the strategy's opportunity set is heavily influenced by the level of M&A activity. Merger arbitrage funds thrive in environments characterized by high M&A volumes, as an increased number of deals not only provides more opportunities to deploy capital but also enables better portfolio diversification, potentially reducing the impact of any single deal failure on overall portfolio performance. Conversely, the strategy can face headwinds during economic downturns or periods when external shocks disrupt the M&A landscape. Fewer deals lead to fewer arbitrage opportunities, increasing crowding within existing deals and potentially compressing spreads.

Prevailing interest rates also create interesting dynamics for the strategy. Higher rates increase corporate borrowing costs, making deal financing more expensive and generally suppressing M&A activity. However, higher rates also tend to widen spreads, as investors typically demand a greater risk premium above the risk-free rate to compensate for the increased opportunity cost of capital and elevated perceived risk. Conversely, low-rate environments generally encourage M&A activity, but spreads tend to compress as the required premium above the risk-free rate diminishes. The net effect of interest rates on strategy performance is therefore multifaceted, shaped by the interplay between M&A volume and spread levels.

Finally, heightened regulatory and political scrutiny can create a challenging environment for the strategy. Antitrust concerns are particularly influential, as regulators such as the US Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the UK's Competition and Markets Authority (CMA), and the European Commission have become increasingly active in reviewing and potentially blocking mergers they deem anticompetitive or harmful to consumers. This increased scrutiny can result in longer approval processes, higher deal-break probabilities, and wider spreads to compensate for elevated risk. Geopolitical factors can further complicate the landscape, as shifts in trade policy, tariffs, and economic sanctions between nations can affect the feasibility and attractiveness of cross-border transactions. This has been particularly evident in US-China relations, where regulators on both sides have blocked or challenged several high-profile deals in recent years (e.g., Xilinx/AMD in 2022, Qualcomm/Broadcom in 2018).

### Risk/Return Profile

The risk and return characteristics of merger arbitrage are primarily driven by the outcomes of individual M&A transactions rather than broader market movements. As such, the strategy is generally considered market-neutral, though the underlying trade structure is typically long-biased, particularly in cash deals involving only a long position in the target company without a corresponding short leg. Merger arbitrage can therefore maintain low correlation with equity markets during periods of market volatility or decline, as performance is more closely tied to M&A outcomes. However, extreme market sell-offs and elevated volatility can materially impact the strategy, as the viability and completion of certain deals may be threatened, and mark-to-market swings in spreads can become large enough to necessitate further de-risking, potentially amplifying losses.

Compared to activist funds, merger arbitrage funds typically target more conservative returns but generally experience lower volatility and smaller drawdowns. This can result in superior risk-adjusted performance. As with many arbitrage strategies, merger arbitrage may exhibit negatively skewed return profiles, as small, steady gains from deal completions are pursued against the risk of significant potential losses from adverse events such as deal breaks or regulatory intervention. This asymmetry underscores the importance of deal selection and position sizing to ensure portfolios remain diversified and reduce the impact of any single deal failure on overall performance.

Crowding is also a significant risk factor for the strategy. Given the well-defined deal universe, merger arbitrage funds often share similar investment portfolios, potentially leading to position crowding and common factor exposures. In adverse deal scenarios, simultaneous liquidation by multiple funds can amplify selling pressure and exacerbate losses. Moreover, the increasing deployment of the strategy within multi-strategy fund pod structures, operating under tighter risk management frameworks, has made forced liquidations more prevalent. While these risk-reduction actions are necessary for managing overall portfolio volatility, they can cause significant spread dislocations during pod liquidation episodes.

The well-defined deal universe also results in a high degree of homogeneity among merger arbitrage funds, with intra-strategy correlations ranking among the highest relative to other hedge fund strategies.

Managers can differentiate themselves from peers in several ways. Some are more inclined to actively trade around deal and event milestones, such as regulatory approval announcements, shareholder vote outcomes, or other milestones that may affect deal completion. Others specialize in more complex or less well-known jurisdictions, navigating unique regulatory and market environments to identify opportunities overlooked by competitors. Additionally, some managers are willing to short deal positions -- an uncommon stance in merger arbitrage given the historically high completion rate of announced transactions. However, shorting can be an attractive feature, offering upside asymmetry and serving as a hedge during deal breaks or broader spread widening events.

## Event - Multi-Strategy

### Description

Event - Multi-Strategy funds are characterized by their diversified approach, investing across multiple event-driven sub-strategies. While activism and merger arbitrage are well-defined components within this spectrum, these funds typically allocate across a broader set of event-oriented sub-strategies, most notably merger arbitrage, event-driven credit, and special situations. The latter is a broad category encompassing a range of less obvious catalyst events, typically focusing on companies undergoing significant changes to their corporate or capital structure. Special situations also frequently include more arbitrage-oriented strategies such as share class arbitrage, dual-listed arbitrage, capital structure arbitrage, and holding company/stub value arbitrage. Event - Multi-Strategy funds may also deploy liquidity provision strategies, including index rebalancing and equity capital markets (ECM) strategies.

Event - Multi-Strategy funds are typically structured around multiple portfolio managers or portfolio management teams ("pods"), each specializing in a distinct event-driven sub-strategy. While capital allocation across strategy areas can be dynamically adjusted based on the prevailing opportunity set, shifts tend to be less frequent or less dramatic than those observed in funds classified under the Event - Opportunistic category (see below).

#### Deployed Strategies

Event - Multi-Strategy hedge funds invest across numerous event-driven sub-strategies. Beyond activism and merger arbitrage, which are covered in detail elsewhere in this primer, additional strategies include:

##### Event-Driven Credit

Strategies focused on exploiting opportunities arising from specific credit-related catalyst events, typically in stressed or technical credit situations. Positions are generally centered on balance sheet repair events such as bond refinancings, bond covenant provisions, recapitalizations (equity issuances, debt-to-equity conversions), and restructuring events (asset divestitures), as well as catalyst-driven credit rating upgrades or downgrades.

##### Special Situations

Strategies encompassing a range of trade types focused on companies undergoing significant changes to their corporate structure (spin-offs, asset sales/divestitures, management changes) or capital structure (rights issues, special dividends, share buybacks). These situations are typically complex, requiring deep understanding of the specific event and its potential implications for the company's future.

##### Share Class Arbitrage

A strategy that seeks to profit from price discrepancies between different classes of stock issued by the same company. Companies may issue distinct share classes to retain control (e.g., non-voting shares vs. common stock), accommodate investor preferences (e.g., non-voting shares with higher dividends), or address regulatory and tax considerations. These share classes may trade at different prices due to their unique characteristics and/or temporary supply-demand imbalances, with price convergence typically occurring around corporate events. A key risk of this strategy is the potential for prolonged dislocations or further price divergence due to the non-fungibility of different share classes, particularly in the absence of a clear convergence catalyst.

##### Dual-Listed Arbitrage

A strategy that seeks to profit from price discrepancies in shares of the same company listed on different stock exchanges. These discrepancies may arise from differences in supply and demand between the two markets, currency fluctuations, market sentiment, and transaction cost differentials. Executing different legs of the trade across different time zones is typically non-simultaneous, introducing "leg risk" -- the possibility that market prices for one or more intended components may move adversely during the interval required to complete all orders.

##### Capital Structure Arbitrage

A strategy that exploits valuation discrepancies among different securities within a company's capital structure, spanning debt and equity. The strategy targets situations where the market prices of these securities are perceived to be mispriced relative to their respective risk and return characteristics. Price convergence typically occurs around corporate events such as debt restructurings, equity issuances, or credit rating changes that may realign the valuation of the company's securities.

##### Holding Company / Stub Value Arbitrage

A strategy that seeks to profit from price discrepancies between a holding company's market value and the combined value of its underlying subsidiaries. The strategy typically focuses on the "stub" value -- the portion of the holding company's valuation not accounted for by its publicly listed assets -- targeting situations where the holding company's stock trades at a discount to its sum-of-the-parts valuation. This is often catalyzed by upcoming corporate events such as spin-offs, asset sales, or divestitures that may unlock the stub value or realign valuations.

##### Index Rebalancing

A strategy designed to exploit the predictable price movements associated with additions, deletions, or adjustments of stocks within major equity indices (e.g., S&P 500, FTSE 100, Russell 2000). Index providers periodically update their constituents based on criteria such as market capitalization, liquidity, and sector representation. The strategy involves anticipating the buying and selling pressure generated by passive investors (e.g., index funds) adjusting their portfolios to reflect changes in index composition. Stocks being added to an index or receiving increased index weight typically experience price appreciation due to heightened demand, while those being removed or receiving reduced weight typically decline due to anticipated selling. The strategy requires precise timing and execution to manage risks associated with market impact, factor exposures (particularly momentum), and crowding, as hedge funds themselves may become marginal buyers of target stocks, potentially diminishing the strategy's effectiveness.

##### Equity Capital Markets (ECM)

Strategies focused on opportunities created by equity capital markets activities such as initial public offerings (IPOs), follow-on offerings, and block trades. These events can create short-term price dislocations and inefficiencies, providing hedge funds with alpha-generating opportunities. Strong capital markets relationships are essential for accessing IPO allocations, participating in secondary offerings, and engaging in block trades (i.e., off-market sales of large share positions at a discount). Additionally, some funds seek to short stocks in anticipation of secondary offerings or block trades, aiming to close their positions after price declines resulting from equity dilution or temporary supply overhang.

### Performance Across Market Environments

By allocating capital across multiple event-driven strategies, Event - Multi-Strategy funds aim to deliver diversified returns with relatively low volatility across various market environments. Many of the underlying sub-strategies, particularly those focused on liquidity provision, can be highly cyclical. They have the potential to generate strong uncorrelated returns when market conditions are favorable, but may struggle to produce alpha during quieter periods (e.g., when ECM deal flow is subdued or outside major index rebalancing windows).

Moreover, special situations and event-arbitrage-oriented sub-strategies tend to be highly opportunistic in nature. These strategies typically seek to exploit transient opportunities, and the potential deal universe for dual-listed arbitrage, share class arbitrage, and holding company/stub value arbitrage has diminished in recent years as companies have taken steps to simplify their capital structures. Consequently, the broad coverage of Event - Multi-Strategy funds is designed to capitalize on a wide range of event-driven situations, somewhat mitigating the cyclicality and opportunistic nature of individual strategies.

### Risk/Return Profile

Event - Multi-Strategy funds generally deliver moderate but consistent returns with low volatility, owing to their diversified approach across multiple event-driven sub-strategies. Differentiation within this category typically depends on the selection and weighting of event-driven sub-strategies, the level of leverage employed, and the fund's tolerance for broader equity market beta exposure.

That said, the risk/return characteristics of these funds are primarily driven by their ability to generate alpha from idiosyncratic events. Consequently, a significant portion of Event - Multi-Strategy fund returns is attributable to alpha rather than beta.

## Event - Opportunistic

### Description

Event - Opportunistic hedge funds share similarities with the Event - Multi-Strategy classification but are distinguished by their highly flexible and opportunistic approach. These funds dynamically adjust their capital allocation across multiple event-driven strategies and may concentrate the majority or even all of their risk in a specific area -- including event-driven credit, equity capital markets, or index rebalancing -- based on the prevailing opportunity set. This approach differs from Event - Multi-Strategy funds, which typically maintain allocations across multiple sub-strategies at all times, with shifts occurring less frequently or less dramatically.

These funds are typically (though not exclusively) managed by a single chief investment officer or portfolio manager who engages with various strategies as opportunities emerge. The defining characteristic is their ability to swiftly redeploy capital to the most attractive areas while maintaining low allocations or avoiding less favorable strategies entirely.

### Performance Across Market Environments

Event - Opportunistic hedge funds, like their Event - Multi-Strategy counterparts, aim to deliver diversified returns across various market environments. However, their more flexible and concentrated approach produces distinct performance characteristics.

By dynamically adjusting capital across multiple event-driven strategies, Event - Opportunistic funds seek to capitalize on the most attractive opportunities available at any given time. This flexibility can potentially generate stronger returns relative to Event - Multi-Strategy funds when market conditions favor the specific sub-strategies they overweight. For instance, these funds might concentrate their risk in event-driven credit during post-crisis periods, or in ECM during periods of elevated capital markets activity, aiming to fully exploit and maximize returns from these opportunities.

### Risk/Return Profile

Event - Opportunistic hedge funds generally offer potentially higher returns with moderate to elevated volatility, reflecting their highly flexible and at times concentrated approach to specific event-driven strategies. As a result, these funds' Sharpe ratios can vary significantly depending on prevailing market conditions and the fund's ability to capitalize on specific opportunities. However, due to their higher tolerance for strategy concentration, these funds may experience greater volatility and larger potential drawdowns when situations do not develop as expected. This results in a more variable risk/return profile relative to Event - Multi-Strategy funds, reflecting their opportunistic nature and dynamic strategy allocation.
