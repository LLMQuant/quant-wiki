# Nine Popular Portfolio Strategies: Finding the Right Approach for Your Investment Style

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

In the world of investing, the notion of a **"perfect portfolio"** is something of a myth. Both novice and seasoned investors often search for that elusive combination that simultaneously maximizes returns and minimizes risk. In reality, such a portfolio does not exist — it belongs in the same category as unicorns and perpetual motion machines.

After studying countless investment strategies, reading widely across financial literature, and engaging with practitioner communities, one observation stands out: there is remarkably little consensus on how to construct the ideal portfolio. Some advocate a single-fund approach, others prefer a two- or three-fund core, while still others build elaborate multi-fund allocations spanning ten or more holdings. This article examines nine of the most widely discussed portfolio strategies, highlighting the trade-offs inherent in each.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img3@main/2024/12/14/1734217048552-0fe77ac6-45a6-4c47-9495-f04972ba7b15.png)

## 1. The Single-Fund Strategy

The simplest possible approach: allocate your entire portfolio to a single broad equity index fund — either an S&P 500 index fund (e.g., Vanguard S&P 500 Index Fund, VFIAX) or a total U.S. stock market index fund (e.g., Vanguard Total Stock Market Index Fund, VTSAX). Every dollar, across retirement and taxable accounts, goes into this one fund.

This may sound extreme, but a surprising number of investors have built multi-million-dollar portfolios using exactly this method. The advantages are clear: simplicity, minimal costs, and broad diversification (the S&P 500 alone encompasses roughly 500 large-cap companies, while a total market fund covers thousands). The primary drawback is that a 100% equity allocation can produce significant drawdowns as you approach retirement, potentially forcing you to sell at unfavorable prices.

## 2. The Two-Fund Strategy: Equities Plus Bonds

Building on the single-fund foundation, this approach introduces a fixed-income allocation alongside a broad equity index fund. A typical implementation pairs a total stock market fund (VTSAX) with a total bond market fund (VBTLX).

The equity-to-bond ratio can be adjusted to match your risk tolerance — for example, 90/10, 80/20, or 60/40. This is notably similar to the approach Warren Buffett outlined in his will for his wife's trust: 90% in an S&P 500 index fund and 10% in short-term government bonds. The two-fund strategy is straightforward to implement and easy to adjust over time as your risk profile evolves with age.

## 3. The Three-Fund Portfolio: U.S. Equities, International Equities, and Bonds

Adding an international equity fund (e.g., Vanguard Total International Stock Index Fund, VTIAX) to the two-fund approach yields the well-known **Three-Fund Portfolio**, popularized by the Bogleheads community. This strategy aims for broader global diversification by combining U.S. stocks, international stocks, and bonds.

The merits of international diversification remain debated. Proponents argue that non-U.S. markets offer uncorrelated return streams and exposure to different economic cycles. Skeptics counter that most large-cap U.S. companies already derive substantial revenue from global operations, making a separate international allocation redundant. Regardless of where one falls on this debate, the three-fund portfolio provides a well-balanced, globally diversified starting point.

## 4. Three-Fund Plus REITs

This approach augments the classic three-fund portfolio with a real estate investment trust (REIT) fund, such as the Vanguard Real Estate Index Fund (VGSLX). REITs provide exposure to commercial real estate — office buildings, hotels, residential properties, retail centers — without the operational complexity of direct property ownership.

For investors seeking additional asset-class diversification beyond equities and bonds, but who prefer not to manage physical real estate, a REIT allocation offers a practical solution.

## 5. The Dave Ramsey Four-Fund Strategy

Personal finance commentator Dave Ramsey has advocated a four-fund allocation, split equally (25% each) across four categories: Growth & Income, Growth, Aggressive Growth, and International. Using index fund proxies, this might look like:

- 25% Vanguard 500 Index Fund (VFIAX) — Growth & Income
- 25% Vanguard Mid-Cap Index Fund (VIMAX) — Growth
- 25% Vanguard Small-Cap Index Fund (VSMAX) — Aggressive Growth
- 25% Vanguard Total International Stock Index Fund (VTIAX) — International

Compared to the three-fund portfolio, this strategy segments the U.S. equity allocation by market capitalization. A notable omission, however, is any fixed-income component, leaving the portfolio fully exposed to equity market volatility. Additionally, Ramsey often references actively managed funds, whereas many evidence-based investors prefer low-cost passive index funds.

## 6. Bill Bernstein's "No-Brainer" Portfolio

Bill Bernstein — a neurologist turned investment theorist and author of *The Intelligent Asset Allocator* — proposed a straightforward four-fund portfolio with equal 25% allocations:

- 25% Total Bond Market (VBTLX)
- 25% European Equity (VEUSX)
- 25% U.S. Small-Cap (VSMAX)
- 25% S&P 500 (VFIAX)

This allocation diversifies across both geography and market capitalization, with the bond component providing a stabilizing anchor. The strategy is designed for long-term, low-maintenance investing with broad exposure to multiple return drivers.

## 7. The Ivy League Endowment-Style Portfolio

The endowment funds of Harvard, Yale, Stanford, and other elite institutions have long attracted attention for their sophisticated allocation models. These funds typically invest across equities, fixed income, international stocks, real estate, commodities, private equity, and venture capital.

A simplified, retail-accessible approximation of this approach might be:

- 35% U.S. Equities (VTSAX)
- 28% Bonds (VBTLX)
- 15% International Equities (VTIAX)
- 11% Commodities (e.g., VCMDX)
- 11% Real Estate (VGSLX)

The inclusion of commodities introduces complexity — commodity fund selection, higher expense ratios, and potential active management — but the overall framework provides multi-asset-class diversification inspired by institutional best practices.

## 8. The Coffeehouse Portfolio

Investment author Bill Schultheis introduced a seven-fund portfolio in *The Coffeehouse Investor*, featuring a heavy bond allocation combined with diversified equity exposure across styles, sizes, and geographies:

- 10% VFIAX (S&P 500)
- 10% VVIAX (Large-Cap Value)
- 10% VSMAX (Small-Cap)
- 10% International Small-Cap (e.g., VFSVX or equivalent)
- 10% VGSLX (REITs)
- 10% VTIAX (International Equity)
- 40% VBTLX (Bonds)

While this portfolio achieves broad diversification, the seven-fund structure requires more effort to rebalance periodically. The 40% bond allocation reflects a conservative posture suitable for investors prioritizing capital preservation.

## 9. The Ultimate Buy-and-Hold Portfolio

Advocated by Paul Merriman, this strategy takes diversification to its logical extreme, spanning 11 asset classes including the S&P 500, large-cap value, small-cap, small-cap value, REITs, international developed, international small-cap, international value, emerging markets, and short-term bonds. A representative allocation:

- Approximately 6% each across various U.S. and international equity sub-classes, including emerging markets and international small-cap value
- Approximately 40% in short-term bonds (e.g., Vanguard Short-Term Bond Index Fund, VBIRX)

The thesis is that granular exposure to every documented risk premium — size, value, international — will compound into superior long-term returns. The trade-off is significant operational complexity: monitoring, rebalancing, and tax management across 11 holdings requires meaningful effort and discipline.

## Conclusion: There Is No Perfect Portfolio — Only the Right One for You

These nine strategies span a wide spectrum of complexity, from a single fund to eleven or more holdings. Some investors thrive with minimalism; others believe that broader diversification yields more resilient outcomes. But regardless of the approach you choose, one truth remains constant: no portfolio is perfect, and no one can predict with certainty which allocation will outperform over the next decade.

What matters most is selecting a strategy you understand, one you can commit to over the long term. Avoid the temptation to chase performance by constantly switching approaches or reacting to short-term market noise. Investing is a marathon, not a sprint. With a sound framework and the discipline to stay the course, you can achieve your financial objectives — even without a "perfect" portfolio.

We hope these examples serve as a useful reference. If you are still defining your investment approach, consider starting simple and refining your allocation over time as your risk tolerance and convictions evolve.
