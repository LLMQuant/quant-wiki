## Executive Summary

Multi-strategy hedge funds seek to maximize risk-adjusted returns by investing across a broad range of underlying strategies — or across distinct sub-strategies within a single primary strategy. They are typically characterized by capital flexibility, dynamically allocating resources toward the most attractive opportunity set while scaling down or entirely avoiding strategies and asset classes that offer less compelling prospects. For investors seeking consistent performance across varying market regimes, multi-strategy funds are a frequent allocation choice.

Scalability is another driver of their popularity, particularly among large institutional allocators. Capital can be rapidly deployed to successful portfolio managers, and the platform model allows firms to onboard additional managers running similar books. This scalability can produce very large organizations — a feature some investors view as a concentration risk, while others consider it a structural advantage. The multi-strategy space exhibits a significant size bias, dominated to a considerable degree by a handful of well-established, large-scale platforms.

When examining alpha/beta return decomposition, multi-strategy funds stand out: the highest proportion of their returns is attributable to alpha. The diversified nature of their investment activity also makes them one of the least volatile primary strategy groups. Multi-strategy Sharpe ratios tend to be among the highest in the hedge fund industry, paired with some of the lowest volatility profiles. That said, multi-strategy hedge funds are far from homogeneous — they can differ substantially in business model, area of focus, risk parameters, concentration, liquidity requirements, and fee structures.

The scale and complexity inherent in multi-strategy hedge funds mean that conducting thorough investment and operational due diligence demands significant experience and resources. It is not a task for the faint-hearted.

## Classifying the Multi-Strategy Landscape — Three Archetypes

Aurum classifies multi-strategy funds into three principal business-model archetypes, each exhibiting distinct characteristics. These classifications are fluid; a fund may evolve from one category to another over time.

1. **Opportunistic / Dynamic ("Multi-Opportunistic")**
2. **Multi-Manager — Focused ("Multi-Focused")**
3. **Multi-Manager — Diversified ("Diversified")**

---

### Opportunistic / Dynamic ("Multi-Opportunistic")

- These funds feature a single portfolio manager — or a very small number of managers — who dynamically allocate capital across strategies based on where the best opportunities reside.
- The fund may be run by one manager trading opportunistically across strategies, or by a Chief Investment Officer (CIO) who allocates capital to sub-managers or specialists responsible for executing different strategies.
- The defining characteristic is the dynamic reallocation of capital toward the most attractive opportunity sets, with unfavored strategies receiving reduced allocations or none at all.
- Typically structured with traditional management and performance fees, meaning the manager is compensated based on the overall success of the business.
- This model is well suited to cyclical strategies that perform differently across market environments. It also appeals to managers who value collaboration and prefer compensation tied to firmwide outcomes rather than individual performance.
- There is no pressure to deploy capital during periods with a poor opportunity set. However, a manager performing well individually may be under-compensated if the firm as a whole underperforms.

---

### Multi-Manager — Focused ("Multi-Focused")

- These funds employ multiple portfolio managers running **independent books**, but the strategies deployed are typically similar or concentrated within a narrower domain.
- A classic example is an equity platform where numerous sector or regional specialists each run individual equity market-neutral portfolios.
- Another example is a fund that deploys many different strategies but only within a single region — for instance, an Asia-focused multi-strategy fund.
- These funds can be categorized as "Multi-Focused" because their strategy breadth is narrower than that of "Diversified" platforms, yet broader than "Multi-Opportunistic" funds.

---

### Multi-Manager — Diversified ("Diversified")

- Typically very large funds that organize strategy groups into **distinct business units**.
- Each business unit has its own management structure and underlying portfolio managers; collectively, they constitute the multi-strategy hedge fund.
- For example, a fund may operate separate **equity long/short, credit, fixed income/macro, and arbitrage** business units.
- Capital allocation across business units is generally relatively stable. At the individual portfolio manager level, allocation is typically determined jointly by risk teams and capacity considerations.
- These funds tend to employ **pass-through fee structures**, ensuring that individual teams are compensated based on their own performance rather than on firmwide results.
- The model is highly scalable — the firm can add more managers within an existing strategy or expand horizontally into new strategies and geographies.

## Characteristics at a Glance

| Dimension | Multi-Opportunistic | Multi-Focused | Diversified |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Portfolio Managers** | 1 to a few | Multiple PMs | Multiple PMs |
| **Collaboration** | Highly collaborative | Relatively independent pods | Independent business units; occasional top-level collaboration |
| **Fees** | Traditional management + performance fees | Predominantly pass-through | Pass-through |
| **Capital Allocation** | Dynamic | Less frequently adjusted | Less frequently adjusted |
| **Ability to Resize or Tilt Portfolios** | Flexible, managed by CIO | Via central risk book | Via central risk book |
| **Leverage** | Low | Higher | Highest |
| **Liquidity** | Strategy-dependent | Strategy-dependent | Strategy-dependent |
| **Management Structure** | CIO-led | CIO + risk management | Business unit heads, committees, multi-layered management |
| **PM Compensation** | Based on firmwide performance | Based on individual performance | Based on individual performance |
| **AUM Range** | Broad range, small to large | Medium to large, <$1 billion | Large to very large, >$5 billion |
| **Headcount** | Typically <100 | 100–1,000 | >1,000 |
| **Risk Management** | CIO or a single CRO | CRO + dedicated team | Large, multi-layered risk team |
| **Office Footprint** | Single / few offices | Central hub + regional offices | Multiple global centers |

## Strategies Deployed

Multi-strategy hedge funds typically invest across a wide array of hedge fund strategies. The most prominent include:

- **Macro and Fixed Income**
- **Equity Long/Short and Fundamental Equity Market Neutral**
- **Credit**
- **Event-Driven**
- **Quantitative / Systematic**
- **Commodities**
- **Arbitrage** (including relative value)
- **Other** (e.g., reinsurance, trade finance, cryptocurrency trading)

## Portfolio Construction

### Capital Allocation

Capital allocation depends on the fund's total AUM and each manager's deployable capacity. As a manager grows their team and builds additional capacity, their allocation generally increases. Underperformance or low utilization leads to reduced allocations.

In **Multi-Opportunistic** funds, allocation is more dynamic, shifting in response to the prevailing opportunity set. In **Diversified** platforms, allocation tends to be more stable, governed by risk teams and capacity assessments.

### Central Risk Book

Multi-strategy hedge funds may operate a **central risk book** that overlays positions on top of individual manager portfolios.

- **Replication Book**: Replicates the aggregate positions of underlying managers, typically at higher leverage.
- **Alpha Capture**: Systematically generates incremental alpha by leveraging historical and real-time trade data from individual managers.
- **Hedging**: Neutralizes unwanted concentration risks at the firm level.

### Risk Management

Multi-strategy funds generally maintain some of the largest and most sophisticated risk management operations in the hedge fund industry:

- **Manager Level**: Pre-defined investment mandates with constraints on leverage, exposure, and drawdown limits.
- **Aggregate Level**: Monitoring of total leverage, unencumbered cash, VaR, DV01, CS01, factor exposures, and scenario-based stress testing.
- **Central Book Intervention**: The ability to adjust positions at the central risk book level.

Multi-strategy funds require substantial middle- and back-office resources to manage multi-asset, multi-counterparty operations.

## Multi-Strategy Hedge Fund Structure

### Management Structure

- **Smaller funds**: Typically run by the founder or a small group of partners.
- **Larger funds**: Feature multi-layered management hierarchies, often spanning multiple offices and business units across the globe.
- **Internal vs. External Teams**: The traditional model houses all managers in-house. Increasingly, however, funds partner with external teams through **separately managed accounts (SMAs)**, combining the advantages of internal oversight with the breadth typically associated with funds of funds.

### Culture and Collaboration

- Under **pass-through fee** structures, portfolio managers are compensated based on their own P&L, which can foster independence or competition.
- Under **traditional management + performance fee** arrangements, there is typically a stronger emphasis on collaboration and maximizing firmwide results.
- At the firm level, management generally seeks to encourage information-sharing and collaboration while guarding against groupthink.

### Scale and Expansion

- Diversified platforms are generally larger than Multi-Focused funds, which in turn tend to be larger than Multi-Opportunistic funds, though exceptions exist.
- **Multi-Opportunistic** funds face natural limits on scalability.
- **Diversified** platforms are the most scalable, but growth requires parallel expansion of both investment capabilities and infrastructure — carefully balancing fundraising with strategy capacity.
- The most successful multi-strategy funds can raise or return capital on demand to maintain an optimal size. Most, however, must weigh these trade-offs carefully.
- **Talent Acquisition**: Multi-strategy platforms attract top talent by offering competitive pass-through economics, guaranteed compensation packages, equity stakes, or partnership tracks.

---

### Key Terms

#### Fees

- Smaller or single-/few-manager multi-strategy funds typically charge traditional management and performance fees.
- Diversified, multi-manager platforms predominantly employ **pass-through fee** structures to ensure that individual manager incentives are directly aligned with their own performance.

#### Liquidity

- Underlying liquidity varies by strategy, but multi-strategy funds often impose stricter redemption terms than their portfolios strictly require, providing a buffer against forced liquidation during periods of market stress.
- Investor dissatisfaction with restrictive liquidity terms may lead to redemptions over time. Fund managers must strike the right balance when setting these terms.

## Advantages of Multi-Strategy Hedge Funds

1. **Diversification**: Exposure across numerous risk-takers and strategies reduces dependence on any single manager or market.
2. **Consistent Returns**: The ability to generate alpha across a wide range of market environments.
3. **Collaboration**: A broad footprint across markets and asset classes facilitates information-sharing and cross-pollination of ideas.
4. **Economies of Scale**: Access to superior infrastructure, sophisticated risk management, and stronger counterparty relationships.
5. **Scalability**: The flexibility to add managers within existing strategies or expand horizontally into new strategies and geographies.
6. **Best-in-Class Resources**: Pass-through economics attract top talent and support comprehensive operational infrastructure.
7. **Risk Flexibility**: Sophisticated risk management frameworks enable the firm to lever up attractive opportunities or hedge out unwanted exposures as conditions evolve.
