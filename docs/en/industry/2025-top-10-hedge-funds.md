# Industry Insider: 10 Hedge Funds to Watch in 2025

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

*This article is based on industry sources and publicly available information, compiled by LLMQuant.*

## Introduction

AI was one of the hottest topics in hedge funds during 2024, with many prominent firms actively deploying artificial intelligence. Looking ahead to 2025, **LLMQuant** has identified the hedge funds that deserve the most attention based on industry intelligence. The list includes Walleye Capital, Lone Pine, Renaissance Technologies, and a new fund with ties to OpenAI.

The hedge fund industry delivered strong performance in 2024, though "middle-class" funds faced mounting operational pressures. Despite the industry managing trillions of dollars and producing dozens of billionaires, many large allocators continued to push funds to control costs. According to industry tracker PivotalPath, the average hedge fund returned over 10% through November 2024. Even so, the average fund could not match the S&P 500, which was on pace to gain over 20% for the second consecutive year. Markets proved resilient through the volatility of Trump's re-election, and top managers continued to charge eye-watering fees while demanding multi-year lock-up periods.

Yet, as in any industry, hedge funds are experiencing a widening "winner-take-most" dynamic. After speaking with over a dozen industry insiders, we have compiled the 10 hedge funds most worth watching in 2025. The list spans established giants, startups, struggling managers, and firms focused on overseas markets.

## 1. Walleye Capital

![Walleye Capital Logo](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img10@main/2024/12/31/1735693242915-2391e0b8-861f-4394-928c-d0ef0c831a16.png)

> **AUM: $7.4 billion**
> **Founder: Will England**

Walleye Capital aims to become the leading mid-sized multi-strategy fund in the industry. In 2024, the firm underwent a major reorganization, absorbing star talent from larger hedge funds -- including former Citadel executives and investors Matt Giannini and Rory Murphy, former ExodusPoint business development lead Anil Jethanandani, former Balyasny PM Anil Gondi, and WorldQuant's head of options Rupert Graham. At the same time, some leadership departed, including macro and fixed income head Raj Sethi and Anuraj Dua, who had managed part of the firm's systematic macro business.

Walleye's ambitions continue to escalate. What began as a Minnesota-based options market-making firm now has offices across the United States as well as in London and Dubai, with former Citadel executive Tom DeAngelis serving as president. The fund posted strong 2024 results, returning 15.4% through November, and is poised to attract continued attention in 2025.

## 2. Pinpoint Asset Management

![Shanghai](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img6@main/2024/12/31/1735693331285-233f8f23-b6eb-4663-a237-ac60be6221a8.png)

The Asian hedge fund market has been robust, with many new funds emerging in Singapore and Hong Kong as portfolio managers from large multi-strategy firms like Millennium have launched their own shops. Regional players such as Dymon Asia have also expanded. However, while many Asian funds focus on Japan, Korea, or pivot westward toward the Middle East, Pinpoint stands out for its unapologetic focus on the Chinese market.

Headquartered in Hong Kong with an office in Shanghai, Pinpoint's $1.2 billion multi-strategy product returned 8.6% through November 2024 -- its best annual performance since 2020. More notably, its China-focused product, currently in soft close, was up 17.3% through November (per HSBC's Hedge Weekly report). Pinpoint believes its China expertise will be a significant competitive advantage in 2025.

## 3. Viking Global Investors

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img17@main/2024/12/31/1735693371802-67675fde-0a81-4fce-8113-b12650c75ac0.png)

> **AUM: $48 billion**
> **Founder and CEO: Andreas Halvorsen**

Viking Global has once again experienced leadership turnover. The long/short equity fund, founded by billionaire co-founder Andreas Halvorsen, has seen four CIOs or co-CIOs depart since 2015, most recently Ning Jin in August.

Yet Viking has repeatedly demonstrated its ability to develop new investment teams and maintain operations after executive departures. With Ning Jin following in the footsteps of previous Viking investment heads by launching his own firm, Viking posted 12.2% returns through November 2024. The firm appointed PM Justin Walsh as its latest CIO. According to a speech Walsh gave at Harvard Business School earlier this year, his greatest concern is the risk of some form of systemic "multi-strategy blowup."

## 4. Eisler Capital

![Eisler Capital logo](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/D4E22AQHAcPXP6TtQ1Q/feedshare-shrink_800/feedshare-shrink_800/0/1718287008350?e=2147483647&v=beta&t=gCRiIANzdvyZhUg1K3Hc0q0AqTK24EWOtBKbVDazkMc)

> **AUM: $4 billion**
> **Founder: Ed Eisler**

Ed Eisler's eponymous hedge fund failed to keep pace with most multi-strategy peers in 2024. While competitors were on track for returns of roughly 10% or higher, Eisler was set to close the year with less than 5%. According to a person close to the fund, returns through November were just slightly above 2%.

It was not for lack of effort. Another person familiar with the fund said Eisler had required portfolio managers to be "fully invested" during the year -- even when suitable opportunities were scarce. A single poor year does not spell doom. Schonfeld's turnaround proved that a strong stretch of performance can catapult a fund back to the top tier. Whether Eisler can stage a comeback in 2025 remains an open question.

## 5. Marshall Wace Asset Management

![Paul Marshall](https://encrypted-tbn0.gstatic.com/images?q=tbn:ANd9GcSGGjStZvo9MRBWzhwhcPR8aWbhbdbOvRPJiA&s)

> **AUM: $70 billion**
> **Co-founders: Paul Marshall, Ian Wace**

One of London's premier hedge funds, Marshall Wace continued its push toward institutionalization in 2024. The firm has been relentless in the talent war -- despite its co-founder publicly opposing exorbitant compensation, Marshall Wace has poached aggressively from competitors and imposed a so-called "talent surcharge" on top of base management fees.

This has created internal challenges, with some friction between new hires and legacy teams. Overall, however, Marshall Wace's foundation remains solid. The firm continued to expand in the Middle East, opening a new office in Abu Dhabi and recruiting a PM from Citadel. According to people with knowledge of the matter, Marshall Wace's flagship multi-strategy product, Eureka, was up 14.5% through the end of October.

## 6. Situational Awareness LP

![OpenAI CEO Sam Altman](https://deadline.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/10/Sam-Altman-openai.jpg)

> **Founder: Leopold Aschenbrenner** (former OpenAI employee)

A new San Francisco-based hedge fund is set to launch in the new year, backed by heavyweight Silicon Valley talent and capital. Its founder, Leopold Aschenbrenner, previously worked at OpenAI and graduated from Columbia University at age 19. He plans to use AI technology for investing and has secured funding from Stripe co-founders Patrick and John Collison, former Y Combinator partner Daniel Gross, and former GitHub CEO Nat Friedman, among other prominent Silicon Valley figures.

Named after Aschenbrenner's early blog series on the transformative potential of AI, "Situational Awareness" will be a compelling test case for whether a "pure AI" fund can succeed in the fiercely competitive hedge fund industry.

## 7. Ilex Capital

![London skyline](https://www.thetradenews.com/wp-content/uploads/2024/09/iStock-2112419626.jpg)

The biggest new fund debut of 2024 -- the vehicle launched by former UBS and Credit Suisse co-head Bobby Jain -- underperformed expectations. But another high-profile launch from 2023 has thrived, attracting fresh capital.

Ilex Capital, founded in London by former Citadel employees Jonas Diedrich and Dave Sutton, focuses on equity multi-strategy. By year-end, the fund's AUM exceeded $3 billion, adding $1.5 billion over the prior year. Like Taula Capital, established by former Millennium PM Diego Megia, Ilex is among the most prominent recent spinoffs from major multi-strategy firms.

For giants like Millennium, which must constantly find new deployment channels for their ever-growing capital base, partnering with alumni-founded funds represents an attractive option.

## 8. Lone Pine Capital

![Steve Mandel](https://cdn.assetmg.info/dims4/default/6edcf33/2147483647/strip/true/crop/620x363+0+0/resize/1000x585!/quality/90/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fk2-prod-in-investor-prod.s3.us-east-1.amazonaws.com%2Fbrightspot%2F11%2Fd6%2F3abb60189fdceb8cc004fa2c195e%2F2017-04-alpha-stephen-taub-lone-pine-captial-stephen-mandel-newsletter.jpg)

> **AUM: $16 billion**
> **Founder: Steve Mandel (retired)**

Lone Pine Capital, one of the original Tiger Cub funds, is staging a resurgence. Its long/short hedge fund Cypress gained 9% in November alone, bringing year-to-date returns to 34.3%.

Beyond strong investment performance, Lone Pine has invested in its business operations, including marketing and institutional relationships. Since founder Steve Mandel retired five years ago and star manager Mala Gaonkar departed in 2022, the 27-year-old Connecticut-based fund experienced turbulence and capital outflows. To reverse course, Lone Pine spent the past two years focused on improving performance and, for the first time in its history, established a business development function: Pat Cronin was hired as chief business development officer in 2022, the website received a complete overhaul, and the firm participated in the January iConnections conference in Miami to meet prospective investors.

Some funds fade after their founders step back. Lone Pine chose to accelerate, delivering two consecutive years of standout performance.

## 9. Renaissance Technologies

![Jim Simons](https://news.stonybrook.edu/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/jim-simons-remembering.jpg)

> **Founder: Jim Simons (deceased)**
> **Flagship Product: Medallion Fund (internal employees only)**

This legendary quantitative fund had already been transitioning to next-generation management well before founder Jim Simons passed away in May, but his death nonetheless marked a significant milestone. Renaissance is best known for its internal Medallion Fund, which has maintained staggering returns.

After years of lackluster performance and partial redemptions from its largest external-investor products, 2024 brought a rebound. According to sources, Renaissance's largest external product returned approximately 24% through November. Unlike quantitative peers such as Two Sigma and D.E. Shaw, Renaissance has not attempted to expand into non-quantitative strategies. As the Long Island-based quant giant navigates its first full year without its legendary founder, whether it can sustain its edge remains a critical question.

## 10. Whale Rock Capital Management

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img5@main/2024/12/31/1735693668896-4d584284-deec-43d9-8267-f575e6fafb53.png)

> **AUM: $9 billion**
> **Founder: Alex Sacerdote**

Boston-based Whale Rock has been on a roller coaster in recent years. After declining 40% in 2021 and another 9% in 2022, the fund surged 32% in 2023. In 2024, Whale Rock climbed even higher, with the flagship fund up 51% through November.

Whale Rock's portfolio is heavily concentrated in technology stocks, encompassing both leading U.S. tech companies and select international opportunities. According to the latest reports, Sacerdote's fund plans to reopen to external investors, targeting $200 to $300 million in new capital. For a fund managing $9 billion overall, this reflects continued optimism about the technology sector and a commitment to sustained investment.
