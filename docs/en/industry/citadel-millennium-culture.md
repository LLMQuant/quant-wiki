# Citadel, Millennium, or Something Else? Inside the Cultures of Multi-Strategy Hedge Funds

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

There was a time when the gold standard in finance was **Goldman Sachs**. Then came hedge funds built by brilliant individual managers like Paul Marshall (the British hedge fund manager now striving to become a media mogul). Today, we live in the era of the large multi-strategy hedge fund.

![Paul Marshall](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img16@main/2024/11/25/1732559781157-08cf7e2d-9bfd-411c-9a5f-d1cb68ea6578.png)

By 2024, multi-strategy funds have become the most prestigious -- and often the most lucrative -- workplaces in finance. They are expanding rapidly: some of the largest funds (ranked by size) -- Millennium, Citadel, Point72, and Balyasny -- now collectively employ 11,595 people. But as regular readers of these pages know, working at each fund can be an entirely different experience.

Insiders at these firms tend to be secretive. Like banks, large funds carefully curate their public image. They do not like discussing current or former employees, nor do they appreciate loose-lipped headhunters. The information in this article therefore comes from **non-public** sources.

"All of these funds are very different," said a New York headhunter who works with large multi-strategy funds. "They have different DNA and different operating models." A London headhunter agreed: "These places are not cookie-cutter. They all have their quirks and idiosyncrasies. The difference between teams can be as big as the difference between firms."

As hedge funds grow and recruit, perceptions of their culture become increasingly important. Every fund is chasing the best portfolio managers. Nobody wants to work in a toxic environment.

The godfather of the multi-strategy hedge fund is Citadel's Ken Griffin. Three decades ago, Griffin conceived the idea of a fund with multiple investment pods, each running a different strategy, designed to keep the overall fund market-neutral. As one insider put it: "The multi-strategy structure is designed to minimize month-to-month volatility. It's a market-neutral, multi-manager platform."

![Citadel, godfather of the multi-strategy hedge fund](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img14@main/2024/11/25/1732559703864-086ea6f2-2ab7-41d5-97b8-40150f062eb3.png)

Will England, CEO and co-CIO of Walleye Capital -- a Minnesota-based firm managing $5 billion that is transforming itself into a multi-strategy fund -- described multi-strategy funds as (ideally) "pure alpha vehicles." All their costs are passed through to investors. They combine multiple strategy types, including "fundamental and quantitative long/short equity, various forms of macro, and various forms of volatility trading," in pursuit of profits regardless of market direction.

Within these constraints, some things are obvious: suffer a massive loss and you will not survive. But there are also meaningful differences across firms.

## Investment Pods: Autonomy Versus Centralization

Insiders say the biggest distinction -- particularly between the two giants, Citadel and Millennium -- lies in the autonomy of investment pods. Citadel is relatively centralized; Millennium is not.

"At Millennium, it's like you're running your own mini hedge fund, and your experience varies enormously from pod to pod," said one insider. "At Citadel, you're joining Citadel. The experience is more uniform. Citadel has a formula, and you're expected to follow it."

That said, Citadel is known to operate less in silos than its peers, encouraging people to share ideas across the firm. By contrast, Millennium grants its portfolio managers autonomy to pursue their own strategies, while collaborating when necessary.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img18@main/2024/11/25/1732559739537-8c0599a5-3321-432d-9d38-278cb080c7e4.png)

"Millennium's pods are very distinct," said one headhunter. "Joining a large, established pod like Numerus Partners is a completely different experience from joining a smaller one."

Because Millennium's pods operate autonomously, some complain that pods compete with each other. One headhunter claimed: "They have pods doing similar things. If five are making money and two are losing, they cut the two."

## Technology -- or Lack Thereof

As Walleye's England pointed out, the best portfolio managers do not need to work. They are not interested in funds that offer ping-pong tables or "fancy stuff." When choosing an employer, PMs want technology and data platforms that help them make money.

This is where Citadel and Millennium excel. It is why Millennium built a technology hub in Miami. It is why Citadel has a program called NXT to recruit experienced engineers. It is what other funds attempt to replicate -- sometimes without success. And it is why systematic quantitative trading managers leave funds with inferior technology.

Technology is not just about trading systems. At Citadel, technology powers its centralized proprietary risk factor model, led by Chief Risk Officer Joanna Welsh. The model is visualized on a 35-foot by 8-foot screen displaying stress test results, performance, and risk analytics in a comprehensive narrative. Earlier this month, Ken Griffin noted that Citadel was built on research "driven primarily by quantitative analysis that was not common at the time." Today, he said, Citadel supplements those decades-old analytics with "deep fundamental research" by "talented market specialists."

None of this technology comes cheap. When joining a pod as a portfolio manager, a key consideration is the extent to which you access and pay for centralized technology resources versus building your own. This factor can be decisive for profitability.

## Stop-Losses: Rigid or Flexible

Another key distinction is how funds handle losses and risk limit breaches. Some funds are considerably stricter than others.

Millennium has a reputation for abruptly terminating underperforming portfolio managers, though some argue this is an "outdated misconception." Similarly, there have been complaints that smaller competitor Eisler Capital has limited tolerance for losses, though Eisler's COO Chris Milner called this characterization unfair. Milner told us: "Risk limits are a source of discussion, not a gilded cage. It's about someone's process, whether they followed it, and whether the outcome was predictable given their environment. Some people have hit their stop-loss or come very close, but we understood their process and believed in their strategy. We gave them a break and then reloaded."

Some funds are known for greater leniency. Brevan Howard, which manages $34 billion, has a higher tolerance for stop-loss breaches and does not fire traders immediately for violations. This approach benefits from CEO Aron Landy being Brevan's former Chief Risk Officer. The risk team, led by current CRO Alex Assouline, works with traders to coach them through difficulties rather than abruptly letting them go.

![Brevan Howard headquarters](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img13@main/2024/11/25/1732559847361-fc3d6af0-4f9a-4785-b67f-4b884d79b5f0.png)

Schonfeld -- a fund that has been considering a partnership with Millennium -- is known for its lenient approach. Schonfeld CEO Ryan Tolkin said four years ago: "Some of our most successful portfolio managers might have been fired by competitors because they initially struggled." Whether this approach will continue under a Millennium partnership remains to be seen.

## Growing Talent Versus Hiring Externally

Funds' different tolerance for losses is partly driven by sunk costs. Some funds develop junior talent and "invest" in turning them into portfolio managers; others hire seasoned professionals directly.

Point72, founded by Steve Cohen, is the most advanced in training. Cohen himself frames his strategy as building rather than buying champions. While many hedge funds run graduate programs and internships, Point72's Academy is among the most sophisticated. For this reason, CIO Harry Schwefel says they do not simply discard junior PMs who make mistakes: "We don't put them in a binary, loss-and-you're-out outcome in their early years, because they're still figuring things out." A headhunter described this as Point72's biggest differentiator: "They want most portfolio managers to be promoted from junior positions."

Similarly, Citadel invests heavily in graduate training and recruits only the most exceptional candidates. One source complained: "It's more elitist, with more hoops to jump through, which creates a more arrogant culture." But if you are good enough, advancement is rapid. Another source said: "Citadel wants you taking risk as soon as possible." Nearly 50% of Citadel's portfolio managers started as assistants or analysts within the firm, and half of its investment business leaders were developed internally.

![BAM CIO Dmitry Balyasny](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/11/25/1732559906641-9e583451-cabd-4789-8938-9cb02e8571b0.png)

At Balyasny Asset Management, Dmitry Balyasny said he recruits people with a few years of experience elsewhere and develops them into portfolio managers over two years. "Typically, they'll know how to pick stocks but not how to build a portfolio. They may not have much trading experience. They may not have managed many people before. So we mentor them in all of these areas."

Headhunters say that smaller funds like Verition and Walleye hire junior PMs and "give them a huge book." This contrasts with Millennium, which they say prefers experienced PMs with "ten years of track record."

## Staff Turnover

All multi-strategy funds have a propensity to let people go when loss limits are breached, but some carry a bigger reputation for it than others. "If you're underperforming, both Citadel and Millennium will cut you," said one headhunter. Both firms would deny they do so unfairly.

Citadel is sometimes portrayed as having a demanding culture. Reviews on the forum site Blind give it a two-star culture rating, with complaints about pressure and intensity -- though in a separate survey, 92% of employees said it was a great place to work. Millennium has no comparable Blind profile. Both firms, however, retain long-tenured employees: at Citadel, the management team's average tenure exceeds 10 years.

When portfolio managers leave, funds must urgently recruit replacements to deploy capital. The resulting competition for talent is fierce.

## Smaller and Friendlier

There is no such thing as a friendly multi-strategy hedge fund, but insiders suggest that those seeking a more collegial environment should consider smaller firms like Walleye and Verition. A portfolio manager at Verition said: "Verition has a very humble culture. They are very supportive of their people."

Ultimately, though, it comes down to personal preference. "When you dig into culture, the question becomes whether you like the personalities of the people," said one headhunter. "In hedge funds, you always hear gossip."

As funds work to smooth their rougher edges, a new type of multi-strategy manager may be emerging in the mold of Bobby Jain, Millennium's former co-CIO. Jain is seen as more of a risk manager and organizer than a risk-taker. He is currently recruiting for his new multi-strategy fund, Jain Global, expected to launch next year. Still, after seven years at Millennium, his approach is unlikely to differ dramatically from his peers.
