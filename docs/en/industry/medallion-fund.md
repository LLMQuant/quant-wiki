# The Medallion Fund: Renaissance Technologies' Unparalleled Money Machine

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

The **Medallion Fund** -- an employee-only vehicle created by Renaissance Technologies for its quantitative analysts -- is widely regarded as the most mysterious black box in finance.

Sixty miles east of Wall Street, a whale-tail-shaped strip of land separates Long Island Sound from Conscience Bay. Here, mansions with long gated driveways and million-dollar views belong to a small village called Old Field. Locals have another name for these affluent streets: **Renaissance Riviera**.

The wealthiest residents are almost all scientists, employed by Renaissance Technologies in nearby East Setauket. These scientists are the creators and stewards of the Medallion Fund -- perhaps the greatest money-making machine the world has ever known. Medallion is open only to Renaissance's approximately 300 employees (of whom roughly 90 hold PhDs) and select individuals with deep ties to the firm.

Over the past 28 years, this legendarily secretive fund has generated approximately $55 billion in profits. According to data compiled by Bloomberg, that exceeds the gains of funds managed by billionaires Ray Dalio and George Soros by some $10 billion -- and Medallion achieved those returns in a shorter time frame while managing far less capital. The fund almost never loses money, with a maximum five-year drawdown of just 0.5%.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img3@main/2024/11/28/1732831535826-247f57a6-2f69-4711-b93a-0bddd080ef7d.png)

Few companies have generated as much interest, rumor, or speculation as Renaissance. Everyone has heard of it, but almost no one knows what happens inside. (The firm also operates three external hedge funds managing roughly $26 billion, though their performance is far less remarkable than Medallion's.) Apart from Jim Simons, who retired in 2009 to focus on philanthropy, remarkably little is known about this small group of scientists whose collective wealth surpasses the GDP of many nations and who are increasingly influential in American politics. Renaissance's owners and executives declined to comment through spokesman Jonathan Gasthalter. The following account is based on extensive research and interviews with more than twenty people who know them, work with them, or compete against them.

Renaissance stands apart even among hedge funds -- not just for its genius employees, but for their eccentricities. Co-head Peter Brown typically sleeps on a Murphy bed in his office. His partner Robert Mercer rarely speaks; in meetings, you are more likely to hear him whistling "The Star-Spangled Banner" than his voice. Twin scientists appear to produce their best work through fierce arguments. Turf wars among employees are not uncommon: one power struggle reportedly gave a Russian scientist a larger role in the highly profitable equity business, becoming a battleground between old guard and new.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img1@main/2024/11/28/1732831287513-5fceeab9-45e9-40d4-aa68-c85d969fe438.png)

For outsiders, how Medallion can sustain annualized returns near 80% while almost never losing money remains an enigma. "Even over the years, they've managed to fend off imitators," said Philippe Bonnefoy, a former Medallion investor who went on to found Swiss quantitative macro firm Eleuthera Capital. Competitors have identified several possible explanations for the fund's success. Renaissance's computers are among the most powerful in the world. Employees have access to more and better data. They have discovered more predictive signals and possess superior capital allocation models. They also pay meticulous attention to transaction costs and to how their own trading affects markets.

Yet as computing power grows cheaper and competitors' skills improve, whether Medallion can maintain its profitability remains an open question.

Quantitative analysts have seemed like saviors for those disillusioned with conventional money managers. In 2016, clients poured $21 billion into quantitative hedge funds while withdrawing $60 billion from other types. Two Sigma, a notable quant firm, saw its assets surge from $5 billion during the financial crisis to $37 billion. Even traditional traders like Paul Tudor Jones and Steve Cohen began hiring computer scientists to boost returns.

Renaissance's success is attributable to the people who built, refined, and maintain the Medallion model. Many of them met in the 1980s at IBM, where they used statistical analysis to tackle formidable language challenges. This is their story.

Simons was already famous: a mathematical genius, MIT and Harvard professor, recipient of the Oswald Veblen Prize in Geometry, and co-creator of Chern-Simons theory. He was also a codebreaker at the Institute for Defense Analyses (IDA), where he focused on extracting information from noise.

Quantitative trading pursues a similar goal: building models that find signals amid market noise. These signals are often faint but sufficient to predict the price movements of stocks, bonds, or a barrel of oil. The problems are complex, with price movements driven by fundamentals, capital flows, and irrational human behavior.

Though Simons lost his IDA position after publishing a letter opposing the Vietnam War, the network he built through cryptography work helped create Renaissance and, a few years later, the Medallion Fund. Over the following decade, while chairing the mathematics department at Stony Brook University, Simons dabbled in commodity futures trading. In 1977, he left academia entirely to devote himself to money management.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img6@main/2024/11/28/1732831319517-bec9514f-392b-478b-92f8-6302bd1e7655.png)

Initially, he bought and sold commodity futures based on fundamental factors like supply and demand. Finding the experience exhausting, he turned to his codebreaking and mathematics colleagues for help in finding statistical methods to predict prices. "Maybe there are some statistical ways of predicting prices," Simons said in a 2015 Numberphile interview. "We gradually built models."

These models generally fall into two categories: trend-following and mean reversion. Renaissance's system incorporates elements of both. The fund's early years were uneven: up 8.8% in 1988, down 4.1% in 1989. But in 1990, after shifting to short-term trading, Medallion returned 56% net. "I was sure the models would perform better," said Berlekamp, who returned to academia in 1991 and is now professor emeritus at UC Berkeley. "I didn't think they would be this good."

Eventually, the scientists developed an internal programming language for their models. Today, Medallion runs dozens of "strategies" operating as a single system. The fund's code contains millions of lines, and the various teams responsible for specific research areas can effectively participate in all aspects of the work. Tuesday meetings are held weekly to discuss and refine new ideas.

In the early 1990s, Renaissance began posting exceptional annual returns: 39.4%, 34%, 39.1%. Prospective investors clamored to invest in Medallion, but the firm was indifferent -- and unaccommodating. Bonnefoy recalls dialing a Manhattan phone number only to hear a recording of monthly returns; Renaissance's legal department doubled as an unfriendly customer service desk. (To this day, the company's website, rentec.com, looks like a relic from the Netscape era.) In 1993, Renaissance stopped accepting outside capital and raised its fee structure from 5% of assets and 20% of profits to 5% and 44%. "They raised the fees to astronomical levels, but they were still miles ahead of everyone else," said Bonnefoy, who, along with all external investors, was eventually removed from Medallion in 2005.

Emboldened by Medallion's success, Simons began recruiting additional researchers in the mid-1990s. Resumes with Wall Street experience or even finance backgrounds were firmly rejected. "We hire people who have done excellent science," Simons once said. The next wave of talent came largely from a team of mathematicians at the IBM Thomas J. Watson Research Center who had been working on speech recognition and machine translation.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img13@main/2024/11/28/1732831364972-a615e3fc-d58f-4eaa-b51b-5e135d671b48.png)

In the early days of tackling these problems, computer scientists collaborated with linguists to write grammar-based code. At IBM, a group that included Mercer and Brown concluded that using statistics and probability was more effective. (Their supervisor, Frederick Jelinek, was fond of saying: "Every time I fire a linguist, the system gets better.") The team fed enormous amounts of data into computers -- for example, bilingual Canadian parliamentary documents in English and French. (According to sources, Mercer disappeared for months to personally enter French verb conjugations into the computer.) This data enabled them to write an algorithm that found the most probable phrase matches, such as translating "Le chien est battu par Jean" into "John does beat the dog." A similar approach was applied to speech recognition: given auditory signal x, the speaker most likely said y.

"Speech recognition and translation are at the intersection of mathematics and computer science," said Ernie Chan, who worked at the research center in the mid-1990s and now heads quantitative firm QTS Capital Management. The scientists were not only working on academic problems but developing theory and writing software to implement solutions -- work that ultimately paved the way for Google Translate and Apple's Siri.

In 1993, Mercer and Brown reportedly proposed to IBM management that they be allowed to build models to manage the company's then-$28 billion pension fund. IBM was skeptical, questioning what computational linguists knew about investment management. But the duo's interest in financial markets was only beginning.

That same year, former British and American intelligence codebreaker Nick Patterson joined Renaissance and reached out to Brown and Mercer, whom he knew. "IBM was in serious trouble, and morale was low, so it was a recruiting opportunity," said Patterson, who worked at Renaissance until 2001 and now conducts genetics research at the Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard. Brown and Mercer decided to join, attracted by a 50% salary increase. They shared an attic apartment in Setauket and often dined together. When the check arrived, they would pull out a special calculator to generate random numbers. Whoever got the higher number paid.

"Renaissance was founded by a handful of mathematicians," Brown said at a 2013 conference for computational linguists. "They had absolutely no idea how to program. They learned to program by reading computer manuals, which is not a particularly good way to learn." He and Mercer knew how to build large systems -- multiple people working simultaneously -- a skill they brought to bear at Renaissance. Their new domain, however, posed its own challenges. "Everything in finance is noise," he said.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img10@main/2024/11/28/1732831387527-4528b4b9-ca17-40b0-920f-ba07f58e3b02.png)

More former IBM employees followed, including Stephen and Vincent Della Pietra, twin string theorists; Lalit Bahl, who had created algorithms for recognizing human speech; Mukund Padmanabhan, a digital signal processing specialist; David Magerman, a programmer; and Glen Whitney, who had written software as a summer intern. "The lesson from IBM was that the whole is greater than the sum of its parts," said Chan. "They all worked together."

The atmosphere at Renaissance was starkly different from what they had left behind at IBM. "We quickly learned that the financial world is different from IBM," Brown said at the conference. "It is ruthless. Either your model is better than someone else's and you make money, or it's not, and you go bust. That kind of pressure really focuses your attention."

Renaissance also amassed, organized, and cleaned vast quantities of data, making it accessible to researchers. "If you have an idea, you want to test it quickly. If you have to clean data, it slows the process enormously," Patterson said.

Intellectual challenge was not the only motivator for Renaissance's data-obsessed scientists. They also enjoyed something more elusive: a sense of family.

James Simons, chairman and president of Renaissance Technologies Corp., was a benevolent father figure. People who know him and the company say no other Renaissance senior executive possessed his team spirit or his ability to inspire these super-geniuses to pull together. "It's an open atmosphere," Simons said in a 2010 MIT talk. "We make sure everyone knows what everyone else is doing as early as possible. That energizes people."

When the IBM team arrived, Medallion was already returning at least 30% annually, almost entirely through futures trading. In the early days, anomalies were easy to spot and exploit. One Renaissance scientist noticed that S&P options and futures had closing times 15 minutes apart and turned this detail into a profit engine for a period. The system was full of such anomalies, and the scientists mined them thoroughly, according to a former investor. Add them all together, and they initially made millions, then billions.

But as finance grew more sophisticated and more quants began decoding markets, inefficiencies started to disappear. When Mercer and Brown joined, they were assigned to different research areas but quickly realized they were far more effective together. They complemented each other: Brown was the optimist, Mercer the skeptic. "Peter is very creative, comes up with a lot of ideas, and Bob says, 'I think we need to take a hard look at that,'" Patterson said. They took over the equity group, which was reportedly losing money. "It took them four years to get the system working," Patterson said. "Jim was very patient." The investment paid off handsomely. Today, the equity group accounts for the majority of Medallion's profits, primarily using derivatives and four-to-five-times leverage, according to filings with the U.S. Department of Labor.

"You need to build a layered system," Simons said in a 2000 Institutional Investor interview, explaining the philosophy behind the company and the Medallion model. "Each time you have a new idea, you need to determine: Is this truly new, or is it already embedded in what we've done before?" Once determined, the team decides how much weight to assign. Signals may fade over time but are usually retained, as they sometimes re-emerge -- or removing them can produce unintended consequences. According to sources, holding periods range from seconds to quarters.

At the 2013 conference, Brown mentioned an example they had once shared with external Medallion investors: by studying cloud cover data, they found a correlation between sunny days on the New York-to-Tokyo route and rising markets. "It turns out that when it's cloudy in Paris, the French market is less likely to go up than when it's sunny," he said. Although the profitability of this approach was only slightly above 50%, he continued: "The point is that if there were a lot of strong signals that seemed meaningful, they would have been traded away by arbitrageurs long ago. What we do is look for a large number of signals. We have about 90 PhDs in mathematics and physics who sit there all day looking for these signals. We have 10,000 processors running constantly, looking for signals."

Beyond linguists, astrophysicists have also played an enormous role in the system's success. These scientists excel at sifting through noisy data. String theorists have been equally important -- the Della Pietra brothers, who rejoined their former IBM boss in the equity group, were the first of many employees with that background. The twins, now 56, have never strayed far from each other: a science honors program at Columbia in high school; physics at Princeton as undergraduates; PhDs from Harvard in 1986.

"They always sat together," recalled Steven Strogatz, a Cornell mathematics professor, remembering their Princeton freshman abstract algebra class. "Their discussions were full of fierce arguments. They were always correcting the teacher or explaining something to each other." At IBM, Chan recalled them screaming at one another -- but never at anyone else, treating everyone with kindness and humility. Their twin bond added another dimension. "They almost have telepathy," he said.

At Renaissance, the Della Pietra brothers share adjacent offices connected by an internal window to facilitate discussion. "They're creative people, very competitive with each other," said Patterson, to whom they reported for a period.

The IBM team focused on improving system performance and efficiency. Because Renaissance's models are primarily short-term, they invested time studying execution costs and how their own trading affected markets -- an especially difficult problem. According to other quants, they also ensured that trades and profits aligned with the system's predictions, since a single bad price or other glitch could compromise the entire operation.

## Rewards and Culture

![Simons](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img15@main/2024/11/28/1732831442687-e90040da-4006-455b-a214-f3ac1d74fb8d.png)

Employees' wealth in Medallion depends on their overall contribution to the firm -- collaboration is the key to earning larger allocations. Employees receive equity allocations available for purchase. Additionally, one-quarter of compensation is deferred and invested in Medallion, remaining locked for four years. Employees also pay fees of up to "5 and 44."

Simons established early on that the fund's overall size affects performance: too much capital destroys returns. Renaissance currently caps Medallion's assets at $9 billion to $10 billion, roughly double the level of a decade ago. Profits are distributed every six months.

Thanks to Medallion, Simons -- who retained up to 50% of the firm -- built a net worth of $15.5 billion, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index. Laufer holds the next-largest share (possibly up to 25%), while Brown, Mercer, and other employees are worth hundreds of millions each.

In some ways, money binds the firm as tightly as its family culture. Beyond those who have left for academia or philanthropy, employees rarely leave Renaissance. Why would they? The problems are complex, the colleagues world-class, and the pay extraordinary.

As everyone grew rich from Medallion, lifestyles changed. Train commutes to Manhattan gave way to helicopter rides. Scientists traded Hondas for Porsches. Extravagant hobbies became the norm. Simons's cousin Robert Lourie, who headed futures research, built a riding arena for his daughter with arches so large that bridges to New York City had to be shut at night for transport. There are yachts, too. Mercer commissioned a series named Sea Owl. Simons's 222-foot Archimedes features a wood-burning fireplace. Both vessels have propulsion systems so novel they require no anchors. As ringmaster of festivities, Simons organized company trips -- to Bermuda, the Dominican Republic, Florida, Vermont -- and encouraged employees to bring their families. Company lore holds that on one ski trip, the lifelong smoker Simons purchased insurance for a restaurant so he would not have to give up his beloved tobacco.

## Challenges and Outlook

Money, however, also threatens the family atmosphere. In 2001, Renaissance hired Alexander Belopolsky, a Russian scientist who, like many of his peers, had migrated west after the Soviet Union's collapse. Patterson opposed his hire because of his recent Wall Street stints and frequent job changes. His concerns were soon validated. In 2003, Belopolsky and another Russian, Pavel Volfbeyn, announced they were leaving for hedge fund Millennium Partners, where they had negotiated rich bonuses and the right to keep most of their profits. Renaissance sued them and Millennium, fearing the researchers would carry away trade secrets. The parties later settled privately.

Meanwhile, another Russian-born researcher, Alexey Kononenko (who earned his PhD from Penn State in 1997 and had briefly worked on Wall Street), rose within the equity group. Senior equity group members discussed Kononenko's promotion during a routine dinner. One person with knowledge of the situation said the scientists were simply questioning why he had been promoted ahead of longer-tenured colleagues. But others familiar with the firm said Kononenko's promotion was a pivotal moment in Renaissance history -- that the Russian scientist had effectively executed a power play.

Regardless of the reasons behind Kononenko's promotion, the outcome preserved the source of Renaissance's wealth: since that dinner, the Medallion Fund has averaged over 40% annual returns.

When competitors and former investors are asked how Renaissance sustains such extraordinary performance, the answer is consistent: they run faster than everyone else. Yet all that running does not always keep them standing when others fall.

In August 2016, rising mortgage default rates caused turmoil at several of the largest quantitative hedge funds, including Goldman Sachs, which managed $30 billion. Fund managers were forced to deleverage, amplifying the market chaos. Insiders say the storm cost Medallion nearly $1 billion in a matter of days -- roughly one-fifth of the fund. Renaissance executives, fearing continued turmoil would wipe out their own fund, prepared to reduce risk and began selling positions. When markets bounced back, they nearly gave up -- but Medallion ended the year up 85.9%, recovering its losses. The executives learned a crucial lesson: do not interfere with the model.

Another perhaps more important lesson: beware the damage others can inflict. That same month, Simons wrote to investors in his public institutional equity fund: "While we believe we have an excellent set of predictive signals, some of them are undoubtedly shared by many long-short hedge funds."

No system can last forever, quants say. They wonder how long Medallion's magic will endure. But seven years after Simons's retirement, the fund's ability to generate profits persists. Even in the first half of 2016, when many hedge funds struggled, it returned over 20%. Renaissance's wealth and influence continue to grow.

## Conclusion

Renaissance Technologies and its Medallion Fund stand out in the fiercely competitive financial world thanks to a world-class team of scientists, extraordinary computing power, vast data resources, and obsessive attention to detail. Jim Simons's vision and leadership, combined with the brilliance and collaboration of his team, made Medallion a financial legend. Whether Renaissance can maintain its dominance as markets evolve and competition intensifies remains an open question.

The Renaissance story demonstrates not only the immense potential of quantitative analysis in finance but also reminds us that the fusion of technology and finance demands continuous innovation and adaptation. As computing advances and data science progresses, how Renaissance and its Medallion Fund will meet new challenges and continue writing their legend remains to be seen.
