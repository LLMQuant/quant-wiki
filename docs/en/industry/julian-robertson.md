# From $8 Million to $22 Billion: How Julian Robertson Built the Tiger Hedge Fund Dynasty

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

When the collapse of Archegos Capital Management inflicted over $10 billion in losses across multiple banks, the background of its founder, Bill Hwang, drew considerable attention: he was an alumnus of Tiger Management.

Before his private investment firm imploded in March that year, Hwang was a relatively obscure figure, best known for his 2012 guilty plea on wire fraud charges. Yet industry insiders were stunned that he had managed to secure $50 billion in financing from top-tier banks to amplify bets that ultimately failed.

## The Power of the Tiger Pedigree

Hwang's status as a former Tiger fund manager placed him in the elite tier of hedge fund professionals. He worked at Julian Robertson's Tiger Management in New York from 1996 to 2001, becoming a protege of the 1980s hedge fund pioneer. The Archegos debacle underscored a remarkable fact: even decades after Robertson closed Tiger's outside investments, his so-called "Tiger Cubs" still commanded enough prestige to open doors at the world's most prominent banks and investment firms.

![Hedge fund pioneer Julian Robertson](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img13@main/2024/11/30/1732967440594-0d7e6a05-4c1c-4f9c-9516-baa8f65e67c0.png)

"Once you have the Tiger pedigree, it helps convince anyone to do business with you," said a prime broker at a bank that lost money in the Archegos collapse.

## Tiger Cubs: Success and the Occasional Exception

Hwang's case is an outlier. Within this group of managers who have collectively earned billions for clients, some have become the most respected investors in the world.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img5@main/2024/11/30/1732967482274-8307e270-9fbc-45c2-95bc-6ed5453148cf.png)

"No firm in the history of investment management has produced more exceptional talent," said Dixon Boardman, CEO of Optima Asset Management, who worked alongside Robertson at brokerage firm Kidder, Peabody & Co. before Tiger's founding in 1980.

## Tiger's Influence Across Global Finance

Tiger's influence spans the global financial landscape. According to investor LCH Investments, nearly 200 hedge fund firms can trace their origins to Tiger Management -- either through direct employment at the original Tiger fund, through the roughly 50 firms that Robertson seeded with startup capital, or through "Tiger grandchildren" that spun off from those alumni firms. Only George Soros's fund management operation comes close to having produced as many industry pivotal figures, according to LCH.

Even now, ties between Robertson and his proteges remain tight, and they frequently hold similar positions. "Bill is a good friend of mine, and I know him well. I think he made a mistake, and I expect him to come out of it and move on," Robertson, then 88, told the Financial Times in a rare interview.

When asked whether Hwang's Tiger background helped him secure substantial bank financing, Robertson replied: "I don't know. I can't answer that." Hwang declined to comment.

## Tiger's Remarkable Track Record

Tiger's performance was extraordinary -- 14 consecutive years of beating the U.S. market, fueled by high-profile trades such as shorting copper in 1996 and betting against the Thai baht the following year. From 1980 to 2000, the fund delivered annualized net returns exceeding 25%. Robertson ultimately returned capital to investors because he refused to participate in the late-1990s internet stock mania. Although Tiger lost 19% in 1999, his skepticism was ultimately vindicated.

"In a sense, Julian Robertson influences trillions of dollars in assets under management, because so many people worked for him either directly or indirectly," said Daniel Strachman, author of *Julian Robertson: A Tiger in the Land of Bulls and Bears*.

## Criticism of the Tiger Cubs

Some observers argue that the aura surrounding the Tiger Cubs has been overstated, or has faded in recent years, and that many of the methods Tiger Management pioneered have since become commonplace. For instance, many of these managers have recently been bullish on U.S. growth stocks -- a consensus trade during an era of monetary easing.

Still, few can question the success of some of the original Tiger Cubs.

"The overall success rate of the group is not that different from the hedge fund community at large, but those who have succeeded over the long term have achieved extraordinary things and shown resilience through changing markets," said Jim Neumann, CIO of Sussex Partners, which advises clients on hedge fund investments.

## Shaping "The King's Table"

Many Tiger Cubs remain close friends with one another and with Robertson, having developed similar investment styles rooted in his teaching. The approach appears deceptively simple: use fundamental research to buy the best companies and short the worst.

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img5@main/2024/11/30/1732967512948-1c9fd192-8f3d-4539-a910-07ebd3d7807f.png)

Robertson told the Financial Times that a rigorous hiring process was key: "I think they are talented people, and we went about finding them in a very planned and deliberate way. They were really very carefully picked."

Philippe Laffont, founder of Coatue Capital, described Tiger's focus on hiring "well-rounded people rather than specialists" as the "secret sauce," creating a culture of competitive, curious, and outgoing individuals.

A central figure in this process was Dr. Aaron Stern, a psychoanalyst who held multiple roles at the firm -- including Chief Operating Officer -- for 30 years. Dr. Stern, who passed away in April at age 96, was a leading expert on narcissistic personality disorder and authored the influential 1979 book *Me: The Narcissistic American*.

"Aaron was a great man," said Boardman. "Saying he was responsible for Julian's success might be going too far... but he was the person who picked out who should be there, who should sit at the king's table."

Robertson used Dr. Stern to develop a systematic method for replicating Tiger's early success in hiring talented young analysts -- a process that had previously relied on Robertson's intuition. Testing applicants involved approximately 450 questions over more than three hours.

"He was very important because he really refined this recruiting technique we use," Robertson said.

Questions might include Rorschach inkblot tests, originally developed in 1921 to detect psychological imbalances. Stern also designed exams that prevented applicants from gaming results by providing answers they thought Tiger wanted to hear.

"Some questions were open-ended," recalled a former portfolio manager who took the test. "Things like: Is it more important to get along with a team or to challenge them? Would you rather be intellectually right but lose money, or intellectually wrong but save the trade?"

The goal was to understand how applicants think, their appetite for risk, and their ability to work as part of a team. The process reflected its era: the vast majority hired were men, in an industry that remains male-dominated to this day. Still, the mindset was distinctive. Tiger was not merely looking for the smartest people -- it sought those who were intensely competitive and excelled in areas like athletics.

"Once a person has a certain IQ, IQ is really not as important to their success as you might think," said Alex Robertson, Julian's son and current president of Tiger Management.

## Two Sides of the Coin

Young analysts worked alongside more experienced colleagues, learning skills on the job. Everyone worked intensely -- some up to 14 hours a day, including Saturdays. The firm built a gym on the top floor of its 101 Park Avenue headquarters, which Julian used daily, to help employees manage stress.

"We were young, super-competitive analysts competing against people who worked seven-hour days and were used to two-martini lunches," said Lee Ainslie, founder of Maverick Capital.

The firm also encouraged analysts to share and challenge ideas informally, frequently walking into each other's offices to discuss views on stocks. Julian's own office occupied the top floor and had no door, to encourage employees to approach him with ideas.

"Early on, Julian gave you the ability to focus on honest thinking," said Laffont.

"For anything, he would force you to see both sides of the argument. There were never zealots -- every coin has two sides. If Julian thought you had dismissed the other side of the story, you would be seen as unprepared, because you were considered too narrow or short-sighted in your approach."

Dr. Stern also played a key role in resolving disputes among analysts and interpreting how Julian managed the firm. "He was a great solution provider and problem solver," said Alex Robertson. "He made sure everyone stayed together as a team."

## The Long View

Tiger Management and its Cubs are known for their long-term outlook and deep company research, often speaking with customers and competitors to gain additional insight. Robertson aimed to find the 20 best stocks to buy long and the 20 worst to short. Tiger typically employed approximately 100% leverage, meaning total market exposure could reach as high as 230%.

While valuation mattered, it was often secondary. Factors such as a company's position within its industry and barriers to entry were more critical. "The investment thesis was never built around the company doing well or poorly next quarter," said Laffont. "It was always a long-term focus, a three-to-five-year time horizon."

This approach has been adopted by Tiger Cubs such as Chase Coleman of Tiger Global, Laffont, and Ainslie of Maverick, who invested in technology companies that appeared expensive on traditional valuation metrics but delivered enormous returns. All three expanded into private company investing in pursuit of broader opportunities.

Those who invested in Coleman's Tiger Technology (now Tiger Global) when it launched in 2001 -- when Coleman was just 25 -- would have seen their initial investment grow roughly 43 times over. Coleman has said that Robertson's guidance gave the Tiger Cubs confidence to take risks when they identified unique opportunities.

David Goel's low-profile firm Matrix Capital followed a similar trajectory. According to people familiar with the firm, it raised only about $1 billion from investors and grew to approximately $7 billion through investment performance alone.

"Julian Robertson's ability to recruit young analysts, train them in equity investing, and allow them to flourish as spinoff firms is unique, given the success that many Tiger Cubs have achieved," said Pierre-Henri Flamand, senior investment advisor at Man GLG. "That will be his legacy."

## Timeline: The Rise of Tiger Management

- **1980**: Julian Robertson founded Tiger Management with approximately $8 million in assets. First-year return: 54.9%.
- **1987**: After years of double-digit gains, Tiger experienced a difficult stretch, declining 1.4% for the year.
- **1991**: Dr. Aaron Stern joined Tiger.
- **1997**: Following years of strong growth, Tiger's assets reached approximately $23 billion.
- **1998**: Tiger lost approximately $1.8 billion due to USD/JPY market turmoil, ending the year down 4%.
- **1999**: Tiger refused to invest in internet and technology stocks. As performance suffered again and the fund shrank to $8 billion, it ended the year down 19%.
- **2000**: Robertson announced the closure of Tiger's hedge fund.

---

The story of Tiger Management illustrates how Julian Robertson built a generation of elite hedge fund managers through rigorous hiring, distinctive corporate culture, and a long-term investment perspective. These Tiger Cubs have not only achieved remarkable success in finance but have also carried forward Robertson's investment philosophy. Their influence extends across global financial markets, creating enormous value for investors. This will be Robertson's enduring legacy to the world of finance.
