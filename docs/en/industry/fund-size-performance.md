# Does Bigger Mean Better? The Relationship Between Hedge Fund Size and Performance

![](https://fastly.jsdelivr.net/gh/bucketio/img9@main/2024/10/20/1729465031968-b3c8959e-1d37-4b8a-91b1-b0b0dfe25143.png)

There is substantial academic and practitioner evidence that increasing assets under management (AUM) negatively impacts the performance of long-only funds. For example, a widely cited study by Jeffrey A. Busse et al. concluded that larger long-only funds underperform their smaller peers, primarily because they hold fewer small-cap stocks. From a practical standpoint, Warren Buffett articulated this vividly in his 2003 letter to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders:

> "The investment manager benefits far more from gathering assets than from managing them well. So when someone tells you that increasing funds won't hurt his investment performance, step back: his nose is about to grow."

The rationale for why increasing assets may impede performance is straightforward: as assets grow, the investment opportunity set narrows. Larger managers are confined to the most liquid investments and find it harder to trade nimbly in and out of positions. Smaller stocks offer the potential to discover under-researched gems, as they receive less attention from professional investors. Buffett put it this way in a 1999 Business Week interview:

> "If I were managing $1 million today, or even $10 million, I'd be fully invested. The highest returns I've ever achieved came in the 1950s. I was beating the Dow. But I was investing very little money. Having a tiny amount of money is a huge structural advantage. I could have made 50% a year."

At the same time, trading offers the possibility of profiting from the "process" as well as the "outcome" -- and the ability to exit positions rapidly during periods of market stress may be particularly valuable.

But do diseconomies of scale also apply to hedge funds? After all, hedge fund managers often take pride in being unconventional and do not necessarily follow the rulebook. Perhaps the hedge fund fee structure -- charging performance fees in addition to management fees -- creates greater discipline in asset gathering compared to long-only funds, which charge only management fees. Moreover, research on size and long-only fund performance has tended to focus on equity funds, whereas hedge funds invest across asset classes.

## Growth of the Hedge Fund Industry

Before addressing the main question, it is worth considering the hedge fund industry's recent growth, particularly as it relates to fund size. The number of active hedge funds more than doubled between 2010 and 2018, rising from approximately 1,700 to 3,800. Similarly, assets managed by these funds more than doubled during this period, from $1.4 trillion to $3.2 trillion.

Fund counts and AUM are divided into five size categories: less than $50 million; $50 million to $250 million; $250 million to $1 billion; $1 billion to $5 billion; and greater than $5 billion. These groupings are analogous to micro-cap, small-cap, mid-cap, large-cap, and mega-cap stocks, and are labeled accordingly throughout this article.

Interestingly, while the industry has grown substantially, the overall size distribution has remained remarkably stable -- both in terms of the proportion of funds and the share of assets within each category. For example, funds with more than $5 billion in assets represented 3.5% of all funds in 2010 and 3.1% in 2018, while their share of total assets edged up from 41.0% to 41.5%.

This stability is somewhat surprising and even counterintuitive. Given the significant overall industry growth, one might expect larger size categories to grow disproportionately. Instead, the industry's size structure has remained highly consistent, with growth distributed fairly evenly across categories.

## Size and Hedge Fund Performance

Turning to the central question of how size affects hedge fund performance, data over the past nine years across the five size categories reveals a clear overall trend: smaller funds outperform their larger peers. The micro-cap group averaged 7.7% annual returns, compared to 6.6% for small, 5.7% for mid, 5.6% for large, and 5.1% for mega-cap.

While the trend is evident, the magnitude of the return differential may be smaller than expected -- particularly for funds above $250 million in AUM, which represents a practical threshold for many institutional investors given their typical allocation sizes and operational due diligence standards. The average annual excess return of the $250 million-to-$1 billion group over the $5 billion-plus group is a relatively modest 0.6% (5.7% versus 5.1%).

Why might the size effect be less pronounced than initially anticipated? Perhaps the presence of performance fees does impose some asset-gathering discipline on hedge funds. Furthermore, hedge funds' raison d'etre largely involves participating in smaller, more niche markets, which may make managers more attuned to capacity constraints. Additionally, some of the largest funds comprise hundreds of underlying portfolio managers, and could theoretically be viewed as collections of many smaller funds. The founder of one large, well-known multi-strategy fund likened his operation to a fleet of small, agile boats, as opposed to a single large vessel that struggles to change course. It should also be noted that while subject to investment diseconomies of scale, larger funds typically enjoy operational economies in areas such as technology and infrastructure, regulatory compliance, and financing.

Another interesting feature emerging from the data is that the size "factor" has weakened noticeably over the past three years. During this period, average annual returns across size groups have been very similar, and portions of the size "curve" have actually inverted, with the mid-cap group's average annual return falling below that of the mega-cap group (3.9% versus 4.4%). Whether this is a temporary anomaly or the beginning of a more enduring trend is too early to say, but it warrants monitoring. It is difficult to ignore the parallel with small-cap stocks' recent underperformance in equity markets. The two phenomena may even be causally linked, as smaller equity funds tend to have greater small-cap exposure than their larger counterparts.

## Risk

Returns, however, tell only half the story. What about risk? Do different size categories exhibit similar trends in their risk profiles? To answer this, we examine volatility and beta to global equities.

On the volatility front, a clear pattern emerges: smaller funds are more volatile than larger ones. The micro-cap group has average annual volatility of 11.3%, compared to 9.4% for small, 8.8% for mid, 8.3% for large, and 8.0% for mega-cap. This trend is notably consistent, in contrast to the weakening of the return trend. Higher returns from smaller funds clearly come at the cost of greater volatility.

The equity beta picture is quite interesting: a "concave" relationship emerges, with the smallest and largest funds exhibiting the highest betas and mid-sized funds showing the lowest. Specifically, the micro-cap and mega-cap groups each have an average annual beta of 0.38, while small and large are 0.33 and 0.34, respectively, and mid-cap comes in at 0.31.

A possible explanation is that smaller funds run higher market exposure because their managers are less experienced, or because lower AUM affords the luxury of trading more aggressively and bearing greater risk. Larger funds may also run higher exposure due to the difficulty of managing hedges at scale and the longer-term nature of their positions. Mid-sized funds appear to occupy a sweet spot: their managers have matured enough to move away from beta, yet their fund size is not so large as to create the constraints faced by mega-funds.

## Return Dispersion

While smaller funds exhibit higher average returns than larger peers, what about the range of outcomes within each size group? The standard deviation of annual returns across the five categories (as a measure of variability) follows a very clear trend: smaller funds show significantly greater return dispersion. The micro-cap group has an average standard deviation of 16.5%, versus 13.7% for small, 11.6% for mid, 10.6% for large, and 9.7% for mega-cap. The smallest category's return dispersion is on average 1.7 times that of the largest (16.5% versus 9.7%).

This is not entirely surprising. Smaller funds tend to be more entrepreneurial, producing a wider range of outcomes, while larger funds have less room for differentiation.

While smaller funds offer the greatest potential returns, they also carry the greatest risk -- not only through higher volatility and equity beta (as shown in the previous section), but also through higher "execution" risk given the wider range of return outcomes. There is clearly a greater chance of finding "diamonds" among smaller funds (just as equity investors tend to find more opportunities among small-cap stocks), but also a higher risk of encountering poor performers, increasing reputational risk.

## Conclusion

Hedge fund managers may pride themselves on being different, but when it comes to scale, they are not immune to the diseconomies of scale that are well-documented among their long-only counterparts. That said, the impact of size on returns is not as large as one might expect -- particularly for funds above $250 million in AUM, a practical threshold for many institutional allocators. The notable weakening of the size factor over the past three years also deserves attention. Furthermore, while smaller funds offer the greatest return potential, they also bring greater risk -- higher volatility, higher equity beta, and wider return dispersion. For allocators willing and able to move down the size curve, there are potential rewards, but also greater execution and reputational risk. Consequently, a robust investment and operational due diligence process becomes paramount when considering smaller funds.

In other words, every hedge fund is unique, and each must be evaluated on its own merits, considering both general and fund-specific factors. Nevertheless, size is clearly a factor that cannot be ignored. While it may be more of an echidna in the room than an elephant, size is a consideration that hedge fund investors would be wise to weigh carefully in their allocation decisions.

## Further Research

Does the size factor differ across hedge fund strategies? It is reasonable to hypothesize that certain strategies are more sensitive to asset growth -- for example, strategies associated with less liquid assets, high leverage, or high turnover. Do returns for highly leveraged fixed-income relative value funds and high-turnover statistical arbitrage funds decline more rapidly as assets increase? Conversely, strategies associated with more liquid assets, such as global macro and CTA, may be less sensitive to asset growth. In the case of multi-strategy funds, a reverse relationship might even exist, given the particular importance of operational economies of scale for these vehicles. Further work is needed to investigate these relationships.

Another area worthy of further study is the relationship between fund age and performance. Does the hungry ambition of "young guns" confer an advantage, or does the experience of "old hands" matter more? Since smaller funds tend to be newer, perhaps it is age rather than size that is the true differentiator of performance.
